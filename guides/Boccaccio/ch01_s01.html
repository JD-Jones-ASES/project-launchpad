Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron (Proem and Introduction)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the opening sections of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. It explores the author's stated purpose, the historical and social context of the Black Death in Florence (1348), and the formation of the brigata—the group of ten young people whose storytelling forms the core of the work.

Part I: Key Concepts and Narrative Overview

The Proem and Authorial Intent

Boccaccio begins the Decameron by dedicating his work to "gentle ladies" who are in love. He argues that while men have many diversions—such as hunting, fishing, and trade—to relieve the "melancholy" of love, women are often confined to their chambers by social expectations and the authority of family members. Consequently, he intends for his hundred stories to provide both "solace and diversion" as well as "good counsel" on what to pursue and what to avoid.

The Black Death in Florence (1348)

Boccaccio’s description of the plague is a landmark of medieval literature, blending clinical observation with moral reflection.

  • Symptoms: The disease was marked by gavoccioli (tumors in the groin or armpits) and black or livid spots on the skin. Death usually occurred within three days of the appearance of symptoms.
  • Transmission: The contagion was highly virulent, spreading not just through association with the sick, but through contact with their clothing or belongings. Boccaccio recounts witnessing two hogs die almost instantly after sniffing the rags of a deceased man.
  • Societal Breakdown: The plague led to the total dissolution of both human and divine laws. Key indicators of this collapse included:
    • Abandonment of Kin: Parents abandoned children, and siblings forsook one another to avoid infection.
    • Loss of Modesty: Sick women, regardless of rank, allowed male servants to attend to them out of necessity, leading to a perceived loss of modesty in survivors.
    • Funeral Rites: Traditional mourning and burial customs were replaced by the use of becchini (hired corpse-carriers) and mass burial trenches.

Responses to the Pestilence

The text identifies four distinct ways the citizens of Florence responded to the crisis:

  1. Seclusion and Temperance: Living in isolated groups, eating moderately, and avoiding news of the outside world.
  2. Excess and Debauchery: Drinking heavily, frequenting taverns, and ignoring property rights in a spirit of reckless nihilism.
  3. The Middle Course: Walking abroad while carrying fragrant herbs or spices to "comfort the brain" against the stench of death.
  4. Flight: Abandoning the city entirely for the countryside, believing that God’s wrath might be confined to the city walls.

The Formation of the Brigata

Amidst the chaos, a group of seven ladies and three young men meet at the church of Santa Maria Novella. They decide to flee the city for an estate in the hills to live honorably and preserve their lives through structured companionship.

  • The Seven Ladies: Pampinea (the eldest), Fiammetta, Filomena, Emilia, Lauretta, Neifile, and Elisa.
  • The Three Men: Pamfilo, Filostrato, and Dioneo.
  • The Governance: To ensure order, Pampinea proposes a daily rotating monarchy. Each "king" or "queen" has absolute authority for one day to plan the group's activities.

Part II: Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. To whom does Boccaccio dedicate the Decameron, and why?
    • Answer: He dedicates it to ladies who love, because they are restricted by social conventions and have fewer outlets for relief from their "melancholy" than men.
  2. What were the specific physical symptoms of the plague in Florence as described by Boccaccio?
    • Answer: The emergence of gavoccioli (tumors) in the groin or armpits, followed by black or livid spots on the arms, thighs, or elsewhere.
  3. How did the "becchini" change the nature of funerals during the plague?
    • Answer: They were members of the "baser ranks" who carried corpses for hire, often taking them to the nearest church rather than the deceased's chosen one, with minimal or no religious ceremony.
  4. What is the "moral foundation" of the Decameron as highlighted in the Introduction?
    • Answer: The contrast between the chaos and social destruction caused by the plague and the brigata’s creative, ordered response through companionship and storytelling.
  5. Which member of the brigata was chosen as the first queen?
    • Answer: Pampinea.
  6. Where did the brigata go after leaving Florence?
    • Answer: To a palace on a hill two miles outside the city, featuring gardens, meadows, and wells of cool water.

Part III: Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Clinical vs. Moral Narrative: Analyze how Boccaccio uses the physical symptoms of the Black Death as a metaphor for the moral decay of Florentine society. Does the breakdown of the body reflect the breakdown of the "body politic"?
  2. Gender and Agency: Explore the reasoning behind the formation of the brigata. Why does Filomena insist that the women need the company of men to succeed in their plan, and how does this reflect the social commentary Boccaccio makes in the Proem?
  3. Order vs. Chaos: Discuss the significance of the "rotating signory" (monarchy) established by Pampinea. How does the group’s emphasis on rules, roles for servants, and scheduled activities serve as a direct rebuttal to the anarchy described in the streets of Florence?

Part IV: Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition from Source Context
Albeit Although; even though.
Becchini Hired corpse-carriers from the lower classes who performed burials during the plague.
Brigata The company of ten young people who fled the plague to tell the hundred tales.
Canonical hours Fixed times of daily prayer in monasteries (e.g., Matins, Vespers).
Courtesy Refined behavior expected of the nobility; a central virtue in the text.
Discourse Conversation or a formal discussion on a subject.
Fortune (Fortuna) Fate or chance, often personified as a goddess who influences human affairs.
Gavoccioli The tumors in the groin or armpits that were the first sign of the plague.
Indulgence A remission of punishment for sin granted by the Church.
Novella A short prose tale; the literary form used by Boccaccio.
Pestilence The plague; specifically the Black Death of 1348.
Prudence The ability to judge wisely and act accordingly; one of the cardinal virtues.
Requital A return or repayment for a favor or an injury.
Seneschal An official in a large household in charge of domestic arrangements and the hall.
Wont Accustomed or in the habit of.
