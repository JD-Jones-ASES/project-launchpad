Study Guide: Patient Griselda (The Decameron, Day X, Novel 10)

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the final tale of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. It explores the narrative of Griselda and Gualtieri, the socio-political context of the story’s narrator, and the broader conclusion of the Decameron framework.

1. Narrative Overview

The hundredth tale of the Decameron is told by Dioneo, the most independent member of the brigata. Set in the Marquisate of Saluzzo, the story follows the Marquis, Gualtieri, and his peasant wife, Griselda. The narrative is defined by a series of "tests" Gualtieri imposes on Griselda to prove her absolute obedience and patience.

The Conditions of Marriage

Gualtieri, pressured by his vassals to marry and produce an heir, chooses Griselda, the daughter of a poor farmer named Giannucolo. He establishes strict conditions before the union:

  • Absolute Obedience: Griselda must study to comply with his wishes.
  • Stoicism: She must never be angry or complain, regardless of his words or actions.
  • Public Consent: She must agree to these terms in the presence of her father.

The Trials of Griselda

Gualtieri subjects Griselda to a progression of increasingly cruel psychological trials to "put her patience to the proof."

Trial Phase Gualtieri's Actions Griselda’s Response
The First Child He pretends the vassals are dissatisfied with their daughter and sends a servant to imply the child must be killed. She kisses and blesses the child, then hands her over without a word of protest.
The Second Child He repeats the process with their son, claiming the vassals will not accept a grandson of Giannucolo as their lord. She remains composed, stating that her pleasure is whatever pleases him.
Annulment He fakes a papal dispensation to "put her away" and take a noble bride, sending her back to her father. She returns her ring and clothing, asking only for a "shift" (a simple garment) to cover her body.
The "New Bride" He forces her to return to his house as a servant to prepare the wedding chamber for his "new wife." She performs the menial labor with a "cheerful" countenance and a "lady-like" mien.

2. Character Analysis

Gualtieri, Marquis of Saluzzo

Gualtieri is characterized by Dioneo as a man of "mad folly." Though he is a ruler, he initially prioritizes hunting and hawking over the duties of marriage. His treatment of Griselda is described as "intolerably harsh" and "insensate." His motivation is a desire to ensure "perpetual peace" by proving his wife's character beyond any doubt.

Griselda

A "poor peasant girl" who displays "spirits divine." Griselda undergoes a transformation in "mind and mien" upon her marriage, earning the love of Gualtieri’s vassals. Her primary attribute is her constancy; she views her high status not as a gift but as a "loan" to be returned at the lender's whim.

Dioneo (The Narrator)

Dioneo is the most irreverent member of the brigata. It is deeply ironic that he tells the most "moral" tale of the collection. He distances himself from the Marquis’s actions, calling them "nought magnificent" and counseling others not to copy Gualtieri’s behavior.

3. The Framing Narrative: The End of the Decameron

The conclusion of Tale 10 marks the end of the ten days of storytelling.

  • The Return to Florence: After fifteen days away from the city to escape the "season of pestilence" (the Black Death), the king of the day proposes they return to avoid scandal and weariness.
  • Final Reflections: The king notes that despite the telling of "gay stories" that might "stimulate concupiscence," the group maintained "seemliness" and the "sweet intimacy of brothers and sisters."
  • The Departure: The three young men and seven ladies return to Santa Maria Novella in Florence, where they depart to their respective homes and diversions.

4. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who is the narrator of the story of Griselda, and what is his reputation among the brigata?
    • Answer: Dioneo; he is the most irreverent, independent, and subversive member of the group.
  2. What condition does Gualtieri demand of Griselda before they marry?
    • Answer: That she obey him absolutely and never complain or show anger, regardless of his actions.
  3. How does Gualtieri justify his decision to marry a peasant rather than a noblewoman?
    • Answer: He argues that children do not always resemble their parents and that he would rather choose his own wife so he has no one else to blame if the marriage fails.
  4. What happens to Griselda's children after they are taken from her?
    • Answer: They are sent to Bologna to be raised and educated by Gualtieri's kinswoman, though Griselda is led to believe they might be killed.
  5. What is Griselda’s final request when she is sent back to her father's house?
    • Answer: She asks for a single "shift" (a basic garment) to cover her body, in exchange for the virginity she brought to the marriage.
  6. Who is the "new bride" Gualtieri brings to the house at the end of the story?
    • Answer: She is actually Griselda's own daughter, now twelve years old.
  7. What does Dioneo suggest would have happened if Griselda had been a different kind of woman when she was turned out in her shift?
    • Answer: He suggests she might have "dusted the pelisse" of another man to get herself a fine robe (implying she would have found a new lover).
  8. According to the king’s closing remarks, how many days have passed since the group left Florence?
    • Answer: Fifteen days.

5. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of the Test: Analyze Gualtieri’s "tests" from a moral and political standpoint. Is he a wise ruler ensuring the stability of his household, or a "cruel tyrant"? Use specific examples of his behavior to support your argument.
  2. Irony and the Narrator: Why does Boccaccio choose Dioneo, the most subversive narrator, to tell the most "moral" tale? Discuss how Dioneo’s introduction and conclusion frame the story as a "piece of mad folly" rather than a straightforward lesson in virtue.
  3. Class and Nobility: Dioneo concludes that "spirits divine" can fall into the "cots of the poor" while "souls... fitter to tend hogs" are found in palaces. Explore how the tale of Griselda challenges or reinforces the social hierarchy of the 14th century.
  4. Griselda as a Symbol: Is Griselda’s submission admirable or appalling? Compare her "divine" patience with the reactions of the brigata, who "diversely inclined" to either censure or commend the characters.

6. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Ay: Yes (used as an affirmative response).
  • Brigata: The company of ten young people (seven women, three men) who flee Florence to tell stories.
  • Certes: Certainly; assuredly.
  • Concupiscence: Strong sexual desire; lust.
  • Dissemble: To conceal one's true feelings or intentions; to pretend.
  • Dowry: Property or money brought by a bride to her husband on their marriage.
  • Guerdon: A reward or recompense.
  • Mien: A person's look or manner, especially one of a particular kind indicating their character or mood.
  • Palfrey: A docile horse used for riding, especially by women.
  • Seneschal: A steward or major-domo in a medieval noble household.
  • Shift: A simple, basic undergarment or dress.
  • Tierce: A canonical hour of prayer, roughly 9:00 a.m. (Half tierce refers to the time midway through this period).
  • Vassals: Persons granted the use of land, in return for rendering homage, fealty, and usually military service or its equivalent to a lord.
