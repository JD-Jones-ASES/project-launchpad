Study Guide: The Tale of Ser Ciappelletto (Day I, Novel 1)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the first tale of Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron, as narrated by Panfilo. It explores the narrative’s themes of deception, the fallibility of human judgment, and the complexities of religious devotion.

Key Concepts and Narrative Overview

The Protagonist: Ser Ciappelletto

The story centers on Ser Cepparello da Prato, known by the French diminutive "Ciappelletto" (meaning "garland"). He is depicted as perhaps the most wicked man to ever live. His character is defined by a complete lack of moral scruples:

  • Professional Malice: A notary by trade, he took pride in forging legal documents and bearing false witness.
  • Social Chaos: He delighted in fomenting enmities and dissension among friends and family.
  • Moral Depravity: He was a blasphemer, a glutton, a practiced gamester who used false dice, and a man who "held all the sacraments vile things."
  • Violence: He was irascible and willing to commit murder or other foul crimes with alacrity.

The Central Conflict

The merchant Musciatto Franzesi hires Ciappelletto to collect debts from "wily knaves" in Burgundy, believing only a man of Ciappelletto's wickedness can outmatch them. However, while in Burgundy, Ciappelletto falls terminally ill at the home of two Florentine usurers. The brothers face a dilemma: if Ciappelletto dies without confession, he will be denied a Christian burial, leading to a public scandal that could result in the brothers being robbed or killed by the local populace.

The False Confession

Ciappelletto, overhearing the brothers' concerns, decides to perform one final act of deception to save them. He requests a "holy and discreet" friar and proceeds to deliver a brilliantly crafted false confession. He presents his greatest vices as virtues and his minor lapses as soul-crushing sins:

  • Chastity: He claims to be a lifelong virgin.
  • Gluttony: He laments enjoying water too much after a fast or craving simple herbs.
  • Avarice: He claims to have shared all his earnings with the poor.
  • Anger: He describes his frequent anger as "righteous wrath" against those who break God’s commandments.

Appearance vs. Reality

The core theme of the tale is the massive gap between Ciappelletto’s true nature and the friar’s perception of him. While the reader and the Florentine brothers know Ciappelletto is a "reprobate," the friar is so moved by Ciappelletto’s apparent contrition—especially his "tearful" confession of cursing his mother as a child—that he declares him a saintly man.

The Paradox of Sanctity

After his death, Ciappelletto is venerated by the people. He is interred in a marble tomb, and the public begins to pray to "San Ciappelletto." The narrator, Panfilo, suggests a profound theological irony: while Ciappelletto is likely in Hell, God may still grant the prayers of the faithful because He rewards the sincerity of the believer's faith rather than the actual merit of the deceased intercessor.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who is the narrator of this tale, and what does his name mean?

  • Answer: The narrator is Panfilo, whose name means "all-loving" in Greek. He is described as the most serious and philosophical of the male narrators.

2. Why does Musciatto Franzesi choose Ciappelletto for the mission in Burgundy?

  • Answer: Musciatto knows the Burgundians are violent, faithless, and ill-conditioned. He believes Ciappelletto is the only man bad enough to successfully oppose their guile with his own.

3. What is the significance of the name "Ciappelletto"?

  • Answer: It is a French diminutive of "Chapelet" (garland), applied to him because the French misunderstood his real name, Cepparello, and noted his short stature. The instability of his name mirrors his false identity.

4. Why are the two Florentine brothers worried about Ciappelletto dying in their house?

  • Answer: They fear that if he dies without confession (due to his wickedness), he will be cast into a ditch like a dog. This would provoke the local people to riot against the "Lombard" usurers, potentially costing the brothers their property and lives.

5. How does Ciappelletto frame his "sin" of anger during the confession?

  • Answer: He claims he only feels anger when he sees young men following vanity, swearing, avoiding church, and failing to observe God’s commandments.

6. What "sin" does Ciappelletto claim is so great that God might never forgive him?

  • Answer: He claims that as a small child, he once cursed his mother.

7. How do the Florentine brothers react while listening to the confession?

  • Answer: They stand behind a wooden partition and can "scarce refrain their laughter," amazed that Ciappelletto remains committed to his wicked ways even on the threshold of death.

8. What happens to Ciappelletto’s body after his funeral?

  • Answer: It is interred in a marble tomb in a chapel where people light tapers, pay vows, and seek his intercession for miracles.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Nature of Deception: Analyze how Ciappelletto uses the friar’s own values and the structure of the Catholic confession to construct his false persona. What does this suggest about the vulnerability of "holy" men to secular guile?
  2. Divine Grace vs. Human Error: Panfilo concludes that God may answer prayers directed at a sinner if the believer is sincere. Discuss the implications of this argument. Does it undermine the authority of the Church, or does it emphasize the "superabundance of the goodness of God"?
  3. The Comedy of Knowledge: Much of the humor in this story is derived from the "gap between our knowledge and the friar's credulity." Explore how Boccaccio uses dramatic irony to critique social and religious institutions of his time.
  4. The "Worst Man" as a Tool of Providence: Musciatto Franzesi uses Ciappelletto's evil for business, and the Florentine brothers use his lies for safety. Finally, God (according to Panfilo) might use his "sanctity" to perform real miracles. Explore the theme that even the worst sinners may serve a purpose in a larger, divine or social plan.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Avarice Extreme greed for wealth; one of the seven deadly sins in medieval theology.
Extreme Unction A ritual of anointing the sick with oil, administered by a priest to a person who is near death.
False Witness The act of giving untruthful testimony; Ciappelletto’s specialty and a source of "boundless delight" for him.
Friar A member of a religious order who, unlike a monk, travels and works among the laypeople.
Lombard A term often used in Northern Europe to refer to Italian merchants and usurers.
Notary A person authorized to perform certain legal formalities, especially to draw up or certify contracts and deeds.
Penance A punishment or task (such as prayer or fasting) imposed by a priest after confession to atone for sins.
Ser A title for a notary or a man of professional standing, ranking below a knight.
Usury The practice of lending money at unreasonably high rates of interest; the trade of the two brothers hosting Ciappelletto.
Vernacular The native language of a specific region (in this case, Tuscan Italian) as opposed to Latin.
