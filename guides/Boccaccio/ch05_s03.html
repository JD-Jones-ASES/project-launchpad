Study Guide: The Tragedy of Ghismonda and Guiscardo (Decameron, Day IV, Novel 1)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the first tale told on the Fourth Day of Boccaccio’s Decameron. Narrated by Fiammetta, this story is considered a pinnacle of medieval tragic literature, exploring themes of love, social hierarchy, and paternal duty.

I. Narrative Overview and Key Themes

Plot Summary

Tancred, the Prince of Salerno, holds a possessive and stifling affection for his daughter, Ghismonda. After she is widowed at a young age, Tancred fails in his paternal duty to arrange a second marriage, preferring to keep her near him. Ghismonda, recognizing her father's selfishness and acknowledging her own natural desires, chooses a secret lover: Guiscardo, a page of humble origin but immense personal virtue.

The lovers meet in secret via a hidden grotto connected to Ghismonda’s suite. Their affair is discovered when Tancred accidentally falls asleep in Ghismonda’s room and witnesses their tryst. Distraught, Tancred has Guiscardo arrested and later murdered. He sends Guiscardo’s heart to Ghismonda in a golden chalice. Ghismonda, having already prepared a poisonous distillation, adds the toxin to the cup, drinks from it while holding her lover's heart, and dies. Following her death, a repentant Tancred buries the lovers together in a single tomb.

Central Themes

  • True Nobility (Virtue vs. Birth): A major intellectual pillar of the story is the argument that nobility is determined by a person's character and actions ("virtue") rather than their social class or lineage ("blood").
  • The Natural Force of Love: Ghismonda argues that love is an irresistible natural power. To deny it is to do violence to human nature.
  • Paternal Failure: Medieval audiences viewed Tancred’s refusal to remarry his daughter as a violation of his duty. His "humane" nature is eclipsed by a possessive devotion that leads to tragedy.
  • Renaissance Humanism: Ghismonda’s speech anticipates humanist ideals by prioritizing individual merit and the human experience over inherited status.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why did Tancred refuse to let Ghismonda remarry after her first husband died? He loved her with a "possessive devotion" and was reluctant to part with her, choosing to keep her near him for his own satisfaction rather than fulfilling his paternal duty.

2. How did Ghismonda first communicate her instructions for a secret meeting to Guiscardo? She wrote a letter and hid it inside a cane, which she gave to Guiscardo with the instructions to use it as a bellows for his fire.

3. What physical location served as the meeting place for the lovers? A disused grotto hewn into the rock, accessible via a secret stairway from Ghismonda’s room and an exterior orifice choked with brambles.

4. How did Tancred discover the affair? He entered Ghismonda's room to speak with her while she was in the garden. He fell asleep on a divan behind bed curtains and awoke to see Ghismonda and Guiscardo together.

5. What was Guiscardo’s sole response when Tancred accused him of dishonoring the prince? He stated, "Love is more potent than either, you or I."

6. What was Tancred’s primary grievance regarding Ghismonda's choice of lover, aside from the secrecy? He was outraged that she chose a man of "lowest condition" and "humble birth" rather than a nobleman.

7. How did Ghismonda prepare for the possibility of her lover's death? She distilled a poisonous water from herbs and roots to have ready if Tancred took a harsh course of action.

8. What was the "goodly present" Tancred sent to Ghismonda? Guiscardo’s heart, placed inside a large, beautiful golden cup.

9. What was Ghismonda’s final request to her father? She requested that she be buried side by side with Guiscardo in a common tomb so they could be united in death as they were not allowed to be in life.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. Analysis of Ghismonda’s Defense

Ghismonda’s speech to Tancred is described as the intellectual center of the tale. Analyze how she utilizes the concepts of "flesh and blood" and "natural laws" to justify her actions. In what ways does her argument that "merit made the first distinction between us" challenge the social structures of the Middle Ages?

2. The Duality of Tancred

Tancred is described initially as a lord "most humane and kind of heart," yet he commits an act of extreme violence. Discuss the transformation of Tancred’s character. Is his violence a result of his love for his daughter, or a result of his injured pride and social standing? Support your argument with his reactions to both the discovery and the final tragedy.

3. Symbolism of the Golden Chalice

The heart of Guiscardo is delivered in a "beautiful cup of gold." Examine the symbolism of this vessel. Consider Ghismonda’s remark that "sepulture less honourable than of gold had ill befitted heart such as this." How does the use of the cup highlight the conflict between the "noble" quality of the lover and the "humble" status he held in life?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Gentilesse Nobility of spirit or character; true worth regardless of social rank.
Grotto An artificial or natural cavern; in this tale, it serves as the secret passage for the lovers.
Humanism A philosophical stance emphasizing the value and agency of human beings, often prioritizing virtue over lineage.
Imbrued To stain or soak, specifically used in the text to describe Tancred staining his hands with blood.
Noble (by Birth) Status derived from lineage and social class; Tancred’s primary metric for worth.
Noble (by Virtue) Status derived from merit and character; Ghismonda’s primary metric for worth.
Orifice An opening; specifically the bramble-covered opening Guiscardo used to enter the grotto.
Paternal Duty The medieval social expectation that a father provides for his children's future, particularly by arranging suitable marriages.
Sepulture Burial or a place of burial; a tomb.
Tragic Theme The "direful theme" of Day IV, focusing on stories where love ends in disaster or sorrow.
