# Study Guide: Day IV of Boccaccio’s Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of Day IV of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*, often referred to as the "dark heart" of the work. It explores the shift from comedy to tragedy, the philosophical arguments regarding love and nature, and the specific narratives that define this pivotal section of the text.

---

## Overview of Day IV: Love and Tragedy

Day IV marks a significant structural shift in the *Decameron*. After three days defined by wit, fortune, and comic triumph, Boccaccio introduces a darker tone to demonstrate that love—previously celebrated as a source of delight—can also be a destructive force.

### The Conflict of Authority vs. Desire
The central pattern of Day IV involves lovers separated not by their own failures or by fortune, but by the intervention of social authority. This authority typically manifests as:
*   **Fathers:** As seen in the tale of Tancred and Ghismonda.
*   **Brothers:** As seen in the tale of Lisabetta and Lorenzo.
*   **Husbands:** As seen in the "eaten heart" tradition.

In these narratives, love is crushed by social power, parental control, or commercial calculations rather than moral failings of the lovers themselves.

---

## Key Philosophical Concepts

### Love as a Natural Force
Boccaccio presents love as an irresistible force of nature. This view suggests that nature plants love in the human heart, and no human authority can remove it without violating the natural order. This perspective anticipates Renaissance arguments regarding human dignity and natural rights.

### Nobility of Virtue vs. Blood
A central intellectual debate in fourteenth-century Italy involved the definition of true nobility (*nobiltà*). Boccaccio argues that virtue and character, rather than lineage or birth, determine true nobility. By placing this argument in the mouth of Ghismonda—a woman facing death—Boccaccio provides the idea with significant rhetorical force.

### The Author’s Artistic Creed
In the Preface to Day IV, Boccaccio speaks in his own voice to defend his work against three specific criticisms:
1.  **Excessive Devotion:** That he is too devoted to pleasing women.
2.  **Theme Selection:** That he writes about love too frequently.
3.  **Lack of Seriousness:** That he should "seek his bread among the wise" rather than writing frivolous tales.

Boccaccio defends the right of literature to treat the full range of human experience, including desire, and asserts that his tales follow the tradition of the greatest poets inspired by the Muses.

## Summary of Tales: Day IV

| Novel | Characters | Summary of Plot and Theme |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **IV.1** | Tancred & Ghismonda | Tancred kills his daughter's lover, Guiscardo. Ghismonda delivers a powerful speech defending love as natural and Guiscardo as noble in virtue before taking her own life. |
| **IV.2** | Fra Alberto & Madonna Lisetta | A corrupt friar pretends to be the Angel Gabriel to seduce a woman. This is the only comic tale of the Day, serving as an ironic counterpoint. |
| **IV.3** | Three Couples in Crete | Three Florentine couples elope; jealousy and misfortune eventually lead to the destruction of all three relationships. |
| **IV.4** | Gerbino | A Sicilian prince attacks a ship to rescue the daughter of the King of Tunis. The princess is killed by her own attendants, and Gerbino is executed for treaty violations. |
| **IV.5** | Lisabetta | Lisabetta’s brothers murder her lover, Lorenzo. She buries his head in a pot of basil, watering it with her tears until her brothers steal it, leading to her death from grief. |
| **IV.6** | Andreuola & Gabriotto | Gabriotto dies of a sudden seizure in a garden. Andreuola is accused of poisoning him but is proven innocent; both lovers are ultimately mourned. |
| **IV.7** | Simona & Pasquino | Pasquino dies after rubbing a sage leaf on his teeth. Accused of murder, Simona demonstrates his actions with the same leaf and dies, proving her innocence through accidental death. |
| **IV.8** | Girolamo & Salvestra | Separated by his mother, Girolamo returns to find Salvestra married. He dies of grief at her bedside, and she dies of remorse upon the discovery of his body. |
| **IV.9** | Guillaume de Roussillon | Based on the "eaten heart" legend, a jealous husband feeds his wife the heart of her murdered lover. She commits suicide in defiance, declaring she will never eat again. |
| **IV.10** | The Physician’s Wife | A lover falls unconscious from a potion and is nearly lost in a stolen chest. He is eventually recovered alive, ending the Day on a lighter, comic note. |

## Literary Traditions and Comparisons

### The "Eaten Heart" and Provençal Tradition
Tale IV.9 draws from the *vidas* (biographies) of Provençal troubadours, specifically the legend of Guilhem de Cabestanh. In medieval literature, the eaten heart was a symbol of absolute, all-consuming devotion—where the lover is literally incorporated into the body of the beloved.

### Boccaccio vs. Chaucer
While both authors explore the power of love to overwhelm reason, their approaches differ:
*   **Boccaccio:** His tragic tales are direct, passionate, and emotionally intense, demanding the reader feel the injustice of the situation.
*   **Chaucer:** In works like *Troilus and Criseyde*, Chaucer is more meditative and philosophical, pulling back from raw emotion to contemplate the nature of suffering.

---

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Cornice:** The "frame" story of the *Decameron*; the narrative involving the storytellers fleeing the plague.
*   **Natural Law:** The philosophical principle that certain moral truths are inherent in nature and discoverable by reason, independent of human laws or social conventions.
*   **Nobiltà (Nobility):** In the context of Day IV, the state of being noble, argued by Boccaccio to be a product of character and virtue rather than social rank or bloodline.
*   **Vidas:** Brief prose biographies of Provençal troubadours that often blended fact with legend; the source of the "eaten heart" motif.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Conflict of Natural Law and Social Order:** Analyze how Boccaccio uses the concept of "Natural Law" to challenge the social authorities of the fourteenth century. Does he suggest that natural desire should always supersede social duty, or is he highlighting a specific type of injustice?
2.  **Ghismonda as a Heroine:** Evaluate Ghismonda’s speech in Tale IV.1. To what extent does she represent a modern or "Renaissance" view of human dignity? Consider her arguments regarding virtue, birth, and the consequences of her actions.
3.  **Imagery and Emotion in Tale IV.5:** Discuss the use of symbolism in the story of Lisabetta and the pot of basil. How does Boccaccio use imagery rather than rhetorical argument to create emotional power in this specific narrative?
4.  **The Authorial Voice:** Examine Boccaccio’s decision to speak in his own voice in the Preface to Day IV. Why might he have chosen this specific moment—between the comedy of Day III and the tragedy of Day IV—to defend his artistic choices and his focus on love?
5.  **The Extremity of Tale IV.9:** The story of the "eaten heart" is often disturbing to modern readers. Analyze this tale within its medieval context. How does the wife’s suicide transform a "grotesque" act into a statement of ultimate fidelity and defiance?
