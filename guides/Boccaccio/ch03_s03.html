Study Guide: The Tale of Andreuccio da Perugia (Decameron, Day II, Novel 5)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fifth tale of the second day of Boccaccio’s Decameron. Narrated by Fiammetta, the story serves as a quintessential example of medieval comic adventure, focusing on the themes of fortune’s reversals and the loss of innocence.

Story Overview

The narrative follows Andreuccio di Pietro, a naive horse-dealer from Perugia, who travels to Naples with five hundred gold florins. Over the course of a single night, Andreuccio experiences a series of harrowing misfortunes and narrow escapes, transitioning from a vulnerable provincial outsider to a man capable of thinking on his feet. The story is structured into three distinct, increasingly dangerous episodes: the swindling by a prostitute, the incident at the well, and the robbery of the Archbishop’s tomb.

Key Characters

Character Role and Description
Andreuccio di Pietro A young, "raw and unwary" horse-dealer from Perugia. Initially naive, he displays a "great conceit of his person" before being humbled by experience.
Fiordaliso (The Sicilian Girl) A "fair as fair could be" prostitute who uses her wits and a detailed knowledge of Andreuccio’s family history to swindle him.
The Old Sicilian Woman A former servant of Andreuccio’s father who inadvertently provides the Sicilian girl with the information needed to execute her ruse.
Scarabone Buttafuoco The "bully" or protector living in the Sicilian girl’s house who intimidates Andreuccio after the robbery.
The Two Thieves Men who encounter Andreuccio after his fall and recruit him to help rob the tomb of an Archbishop.
Messer Filippo Minutolo The late Archbishop of Naples, whose tomb and ruby ring become the target of the night's final adventure.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What initial mistake does Andreuccio make upon arriving at the Neapolitan market? He repeatedly draws out his purse of five hundred gold florins in view of everyone to show he "means business," alerting criminals to his wealth.

2. How does the Sicilian girl convince Andreuccio that she is his long-lost sister? She uses specific details provided by an old servant, including his father Pietro’s time in Palermo, the names of his kinsfolk, and the claim that her mother was a widow who had an affair with Pietro.

3. Describe the physical trap that leads to Andreuccio’s first misfortune. While attempting to use the "stool" (latrine) in a corner of the girl’s house, Andreuccio steps on a plank that has been detached from its joist. He falls through the floor into a foul sewer/alleyway.

4. Why do the city patrols flee the well, accidentally saving Andreuccio? The patrols are thirsty and begin hauling up the rope, expecting a bucket of water. When Andreuccio—who had been lowered into the well to wash off the sewer filth—appears at the brink, the patrols are terrified by the sight of him and flee, leaving their arms behind.

5. How do the thieves ensure Andreuccio enters the Archbishop’s tomb? When Andreuccio refuses to enter, the thieves threaten him with an iron crowbar, telling him they will beat him until he is dead if he does not comply.

6. What "insurance" does Andreuccio take while inside the tomb? Suspecting his companions will abandon him once they have the treasure, he hides the Archbishop's ruby ring on his own finger, handing up the other valuables (the mitre, crosier, and gloves) first while claiming he cannot find the ring.

7. How does Andreuccio eventually escape the locked tomb? When a group of priests and others open the tomb to rob it themselves, a priest attempts to climb in feet-first. Andreuccio grabs the priest’s leg, causing the entire group to flee in terror, believing the corpse has come to life or a devil is present.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Transformation of Andreuccio

Analyze the character arc of Andreuccio di Pietro. Contrast his behavior at the beginning of the tale—specifically his "great conceit" and lack of caution—with his actions inside the Archbishop’s tomb. To what extent does his survival depend on luck versus a newly acquired "quick-wittedness"?

2. Naples as a Labyrinthine Character

The document describes Naples as "dark, labyrinthine, dangerous, and full of opportunity." Discuss how the setting of the city influences the plot and tone of the story. How do the specific locations—the "Evil Hole," the Ruga Catalana, and the Cathedral—reflect the shifting nature of Andreuccio’s peril?

3. The Role of Deception and Narrative

The Sicilian girl’s swindle relies on a carefully constructed narrative that Andreuccio "took for gospel." Examine the elements that made her story "congruous" and "consistent." How does Boccaccio use the power of storytelling within the tale to explore the vulnerability of those who are "raw and unwary"?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Archbishop Filippo Minutolo: The Archbishop of Naples whose burial with a ruby ring and fine trappings serves as the final target for the thieves.
  • Contrive: To devise or plan cleverly; to bring about through ingenuity or schemes.
  • Evil Hole (Malpertugio): The disreputable quarter of Naples where the Sicilian girl’s house is located.
  • Fiammetta: The narrator of this tale, described as "Little Flame" and one of the liveliest storytellers of the Decameron.
  • Florin: A gold coin from Florence, highly trusted in medieval Europe. Andreuccio carries five hundred, a sum sufficient to buy several fine horses.
  • Guelf: A political faction; the Sicilian girl’s husband is described as a "staunch Guelf" to add legitimacy to her false life story.
  • Naples: A major commercial city in the 14th century, portrayed as a dangerous and sophisticated environment compared to the provincial Perugia.
  • Perforce: By necessity; inevitably.
  • Perugia: A provincial city in Umbria, central Italy, and the home of Andreuccio.
  • Ruga Catalana: A street in Naples that Andreuccio traverses while attempting to find the sea to wash himself.
  • Vespers: A sunset prayer service; the time of day when Andreuccio first arrives in Naples and later when the little girl finds him at his inn.
