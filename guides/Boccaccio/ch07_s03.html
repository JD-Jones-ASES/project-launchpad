Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day VI, Novel 1

This study guide examines the first tale of Day VI in Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron, narrated by Filomena. Though it is the shortest complete tale in the collection, it serves as a critical manifesto on the art of narrative and the social importance of wit.

Narrative Overview and Key Concepts

The Art of Narrative as a Social Skill

The tale of Madonna Oretta is presented as a "miniature manifesto" on storytelling. Boccaccio uses this story to highlight that narrative skill is not just a form of entertainment but a social grace. Conversely, poor storytelling is framed as a "social offense." The narrative suggests that the arrangement of incidents—a principle echoed in Aristotle's Poetics—is what determines a story’s power.

The Horseback Metaphor

The central metaphor of the tale is the Italian expression portare a cavallo con una novella, which translates literally to "to carry on horseback with a story." This metaphor establishes a link between storytelling and travel:

  • A Good Story: Like a well-bred horse, it carries the listener effortlessly to their destination, making the journey pleasant.
  • A Poor Story: Like a horse that "trots too hard," it causes the listener physical and mental distress.

Elements of Failed Storytelling

Through the character of the knight, Boccaccio identifies specific faults that ruin a narrative:

  • Repetition: Using the same words or phrases multiple times (e.g., repeating a word "three, four or six times").
  • Confusion and Inconsistency: "Harking back" or correcting oneself mid-story by saying "I said not well."
  • Factual Errors: Mishandling the names of characters or setting one in place of another.
  • Inappropriate Delivery: A mode of delivery that does not match the character of the persons or the incidents being described.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who is the narrator of the first tale of Day VI? Answer: Filomena.

2. What is the literal meaning of the Italian expression portare a cavallo con una novella? Answer: "To carry on horseback with a story."

3. According to the text, what specific physical reaction does Madonna Oretta have while listening to the knight's poorly told tale? Answer: She begins to sweat and feels as if she might faint or is "at the point of death."

4. How does Madonna Oretta use wit to end the knight's story? Answer: She uses a metaphor related to his original offer, saying, "Sir, this horse of yours trots too hard; I pray you be pleased to set me down."

5. How does the knight respond to Madonna Oretta’s request to "set her down"? Answer: He is "quick of apprehension" and understands her meaning. He takes it in good humor, leaves the story unfinished, and moves on to other stories.

6. In the introduction to the tale, what does Filomena complain about regarding the ladies of her day? Answer: She laments that few or no ladies are left who are able to use "apt speech" or "bright sallies" of wit, and many cannot even understand such wit when they hear it.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Metaphor of Travel and Narrative Flow Analyze the metaphor of the "horseback journey" in the context of the knight’s failure. How does Boccaccio use the physical sensation of a "hard trot" to describe the cognitive experience of a poorly structured narrative? In your response, consider how the "arrangement of incidents" impacts the listener's journey.

2. Wit as a Social Tool for Women Filomena’s introduction suggests that brief, witty speech is particularly "proper for ladies." Discuss how Madonna Oretta uses wit to navigate a socially delicate situation. How does her "sally" allow her to correct the knight without causing a breach of courtesy or social offense?

3. Boccaccio’s Self-Awareness as an Author The text describes this tale as Boccaccio "showing us what not to do." Compare the faults of the knight—repetition, confusion, and mismatched delivery—with the stylistic goals of the Decameron as a whole. Why would Boccaccio choose to include a story about a "spoiled" tale within his own collection of stories?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Apt Speech Language that is appropriate, clever, and well-suited to the specific occasion.
Brevity The quality of being brief and concise; the text contrasts this with "prolixity."
Madonna A title of respect for a woman of rank, meaning "My lady." In the context of the Decameron, it is a social rather than a religious title.
Messer A title of respect used for men of rank or status (e.g., Messer Geri Spina).
Prolixity The state of being tediously wordy or lengthy in speech.
Sallies Quick, witty, or bright remarks; often referred to as "sallies of wit."
Wit Intellectual cleverness and the ability to use "ready speech" to navigate social situations or provide entertainment.
