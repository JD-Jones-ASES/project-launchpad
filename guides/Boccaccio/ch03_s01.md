# Study Guide: Fortune’s Reversals in Boccaccio’s Decameron (Day II)

This study guide examines the themes of fate, intelligence, and human agency as presented in the second day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It focuses on the interplay between the capricious force of *fortuna* and the human quality of *ingegno*.

## Key Concepts and Philosophical Foundations

### The Concept of Fortuna
In the medieval worldview, **Fortuna** (Fortune) was a quasi-divine, personified goddess who was viewed as capricious, irresistible, and morally indifferent. The term carries a dual meaning from the Latin *fortuna*: both wealth and fate/chance. 

The primary metaphor for this concept is **Fortuna's Wheel**, a concept inherited from **Boethius’s *Consolation of Philosophy*** (c. 480–524). On this wheel, mortals are raised to prosperity only to be dashed down again. In the *Decameron*, Boccaccio uses Fortune as the "great plot-maker," creating the shipwrecks, reversals, and unexpected encounters that test the characters.

### Ingegno vs. Fortuna
The tales of Day II explore the relationship between external forces (*fortuna*) and the internal human response (*ingegno*). 
*   **Ingegno:** Refers to intelligence, resourcefulness, and native wit. It is the ability to think quickly and adapt to circumstances to turn a misfortune into an advantage.
*   **The Narrative Tension:** The "heroes" of these tales are not those who avoid the wheel of Fortune, but those who learn to "ride" it through the use of their *ingegno*.

## Analysis of Primary Tales

### Andreuccio da Perugia (II.5)
This tale is considered a prototype of the **picaresque** genre—episodic adventure stories featuring a resourceful protagonist navigating a dangerous social world. The story follows Andreuccio’s transformation from a "fool" to an "improviser" over a single night in Naples.

**The Three-Part Structure:**
1.  **The Sicilian Woman’s Trap:** Andreuccio is lured by a prostitute pretending to be his sister, robbed, and thrown into a foul alley. This represents his first lesson in worldly duplicity.
2.  **The Well and the Watchmen:** After falling into a well while trying to clean himself, he is recruited by two men for a crime. Here, he begins to adapt to his changing circumstances.
3.  **The Archbishop’s Tomb:** Andreuccio enters the tomb of the Archbishop of Naples to steal a ruby ring. When his companions attempt to trap him inside, he uses their own trick against them to escape with the treasure.

**Setting as Character:**
The city of Naples is presented with vivid sensory detail, reflecting Boccaccio’s personal experience at the Angevin court (c. 1327–1340). Its narrow streets and hidden dangers function as a character that facilitates Andreuccio’s growth.

### Alatiel (II.7)
In contrast to Andreuccio’s growing agency, Alatiel’s tale explores extreme passivity. A beautiful royal woman, Alatiel is passed between multiple men by force of circumstance and desire without ever choosing her fate or resisting it.

*   **Social Satire:** The tale concludes with Alatiel being returned to her father and married off "as a virgin." This is a pointed irony, suggesting that in her society, appearances alone determine social reality.
*   **The Closing Proverb:** The narrator observes that "kissing-lips lose no flavor, but renew themselves like the moon." This suggests a world where women are reduced to objects of exchange, valued only for beauty and perceived virginity.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Evolution of Agency:** Using Andreuccio da Perugia as a case study, track his transformation from a "naive provincial" to a "resourceful survivor." How do the three specific episodes of his night in Naples facilitate this growth?
2.  **The Blindness of Fortune:** The text suggests that Fortune is "morally indifferent." Examine the tales of Landolfo Rufolo and Rinaldo d'Asti to argue whether Boccaccio believes there is a moral order behind the chaos of chance, or if he accepts that the wicked and innocent are rewarded with equal indifference.
3.  **Gender and Autonomy:** Compare and contrast the agency (or lack thereof) of Alatiel in II.7 and Zinevra (the wife of Bernabò) in II.9. What do their stories suggest about the limitations placed on women in Boccaccio’s world and the strategies they must use to navigate them?
4.  **The Satirical Nature of Alatiel:** Some read the tale of Alatiel as a celebration of her adventures, while others see it as a satire of a society that values women only as objects of exchange. Based on the closing proverb and the irony of her "renewed" virginity, construct an argument for the tale's true intent.

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Angevin Court:** The royal court in Naples where Boccaccio lived from approximately 1327 to 1340; the source of his intimate knowledge of the city.
*   **Boethius (c. 480–524):** A Roman senator and philosopher whose work, *The Consolation of Philosophy*, introduced the metaphor of Fortune's wheel to medieval thought.
*   **Fortuna:** A quasi-divine, capricious force governing human affairs; represents both wealth and fate.
*   **Ingegno:** Intelligence, native wit, and resourcefulness; the ability to adapt and turn misfortune into advantage.
*   **Picaresque:** A genre of fiction featuring a clever, low-born protagonist (a *picaro*) who survives by their wits while navigating a corrupt or dangerous society.
*   **Sicilian Vespers (1282):** A historical rebellion in Sicily against French Angevin rule, resulting in significant political upheaval used as a plot device in Day II.
