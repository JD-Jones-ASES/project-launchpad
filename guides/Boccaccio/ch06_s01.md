# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day the Fifth – Love Rewarded

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the themes, narrative structures, and critical interpretations of the fifth day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It explores the complexities of courtly love, the evolution of the novella, and the diverse ways in which romantic devotion is "rewarded."

---

## Key Concepts and Literary Context

### The Tradition of Courtly Love
Courtly love (*fin’amors* in Provençal or *amor cortese* in Italian) is a medieval literary convention originating in 12th-century Provence. It is characterized by:
*   **The Idealized Lady:** A woman of higher social station who is the object of intense devotion.
*   **The Suffering Lover:** A nobleman who serves his lady with humility, patience, and generosity.
*   **The Ennobling Power of Devotion:** The belief that love makes the lover a better person, regardless of whether the love is returned.
*   **Codification:** Andreas Capellanus's *De Amore* (c. 1185) established the formal rules for courtly behavior.

While Boccaccio was deeply familiar with this tradition, he treated it with ambiguity, celebrating its noble ideals in some tales while questioning its darker implications in others.

### The Novella Form
Boccaccio perfected the **novella**, a short prose tale that differs from other medieval forms:
*   **Realism:** Unlike the *romance*, which features supernatural marvels and idealized heroes, the novella focuses on recognizable people in realistic settings.
*   **Human Behavior:** Unlike the *exemplum*, which is designed to illustrate a specific moral lesson, the novella explores the complexities of human behavior and often leaves the reader to draw their own conclusions.

## Analysis of Primary Tales

### Federigo degli Alberighi (V.9)
Critics since the Renaissance have regarded V.9 as one of the most perfectly constructed short narratives in Western literature. Its brilliance is defined by three key elements:

| Element | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Narrative Economy** | As noted by critic Erich Auerbach, every detail serves the plot. Federigo's loss of fortune, his retreat to a farm, and his devotion to his falcon all lead inexorably to the central sacrifice. |
| **Irony** | Tension arises from the gap in knowledge between characters and the reader. Federigo kills his falcon to honor Monna Giovanna, unaware that the bird is the very thing she has come to request. |
| **Reversal** | The resolution occurs not through years of courtly service, but through a singular, devastating act of sacrifice. Monna Giovanna marries Federigo because his sacrifice reveals a character truly worthy of love. |

### Nastagio degli Onesti (V.8)
This tale serves as the "dark mirror" to the idealism of Federigo’s falcon. It utilizes a supernatural vision—the "infernal hunt"—to resolve a suitor’s unrequited love.

*   **Contrapasso:** The punishment in the vision mirrors the sin. A woman who was cruel to her suitor is eternally hunted, while the suitor who died of despair pursues her.
*   **The Moral Dilemma:** Nastagio uses this divine vision as a tool of manipulation, staging a dinner so his beloved will witness the horror and be frightened into marriage.
*   **Interpretations:** While medieval audiences might have viewed a woman’s persistent refusal of a suitor as a form of cruelty (*crudeltade*), modern readers often find the use of terror to compel consent deeply disturbing.

## Summary of Additional Tales

| Tale | Title/Characters | Theme/Plot Summary |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **V.1** | Cimone and Efigenia | Love transforms a boorish man into an intelligent, refined individual. It celebrates love’s power to "civilize" the lover. |
| **V.2** | Gostanza and Martuccio | Believing her lover dead, Gostanza sets out to die at sea but is carried to Tunis. She finds Martuccio alive and prosperous; they are reunited by fortune. |
| **V.3** | Pietro and Agnolella | After eloping, the couple is separated by bandits in a forest. After various misadventures, they are reunited and married with family blessings. |
| **V.4** | Ricciardo and Caterina | Lovers caught together on a balcony are forced into marriage by the girl’s father, resolving a scandalous situation with pragmatic humor. |
| **V.5** | Guidotto and Giacomino | Two rivals for the same woman cease their violence when her noble parentage is revealed, leading to a resolution through marriage and family identity. |
| **V.6** | Gianni and Restituta | Lovers captured by pirates and condemned to death are saved when a high-ranking official recognizes the man's noble blood. |
| **V.7** | Teodoro and Violante | A servant is condemned to death for impregnating his master’s daughter, only to be saved when he is revealed to be the lost son of Armenian nobility. |
| **V.10** | Pietro di Vinciolo | A comic *beffa* (trick) involving a neglected wife and her lover. It provides an earthy, cynical counterpoint to the Day’s more romantic themes. |

## Comparative Perspective: Boccaccio and Chaucer

Chaucer’s *The Franklin’s Tale* shares themes of generosity with Boccaccio's V.9. Both explore how noble actions can overcome self-interest.
*   **Chaucer’s Approach:** Presents a chain of generosity (husband to wife, suitor to wife, magician to suitor) and asks the reader, "Which was the most generous?"
*   **Boccaccio’s Approach:** In the case of Federigo, generosity is absolute and uncalculating. He sacrifices his last treasure without any hope of reward, representing the supreme act of courtly love.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Where did the conventions of courtly love originate?**
    *   *Answer:* In the troubadour poetry of 12th-century Provence.
2.  **What is the name of the classic 1185 text that codified the rules of courtly behavior?**
    *   *Answer:* *De Amore* by Andreas Capellanus.
3.  **In V.9, what does the falcon symbolize for Federigo?**
    *   *Answer:* His former wealth, his courtly identity, and his last remaining possession.
4.  **How does the literary critic Erich Auerbach describe Boccaccio’s narrative style in V.9?**
    *   *Answer:* He praised it for its "narrative economy," or the ability to convey complex situations in a few swift strokes.
5.  **What is "contrapasso"?**
    *   *Answer:* A punishment that mirrors the sin committed, a concept familiar from Dante’s *Inferno*.
6.  **In V.8, what specific day of the week does the supernatural hunt occur?**
    *   *Answer:* Every Friday.
7.  **What is the primary difference between a novella and a romance?**
    *   *Answer:* The novella is realistic and concerned with human behavior, while the romance deals with idealized heroes and supernatural marvels.
8.  **In V.1, what is the initial character of Cimone before he falls in love?**
    *   *Answer:* He is a boorish and unrefined young man.
9.  **Which character tells the final, more cynical tale (V.10) of the Fifth Day?**
    *   *Answer:* Dioneo.
10. **What frequently resolves the "impossible" class divides or legal crises in tales like V.6 and V.7?**
    *   *Answer:* The revelation of hidden noble birth or "noble blood."

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ethics of Reward:** Compare the "happy endings" of V.8 (Nastagio) and V.9 (Federigo). Both men are eventually "rewarded" with the women they love. Is one ending more morally justifiable than the other? Does Nastagio’s use of terror negate the "happy" nature of the resolution?
2.  **The Function of Sacrifice:** Analyze Federigo’s decision to kill the falcon. Is this act noble or foolish? Discuss whether Boccaccio presents Federigo’s extreme devotion as an admirable ideal or as a cautionary tale about the dangers of excess.
3.  **Gender and Autonomy:** Discuss the role of female consent in Day Five. In tales like V.4 (Caterina) and V.8 (Nastagio), women are moved toward marriage by paternal pragmatism or supernatural fear. Contrast these with Monna Giovanna’s decision in V.9. What does Boccaccio suggest about a woman’s agency in the courtly love tradition?
4.  **Structural Juxtaposition:** Why might Boccaccio have placed the "perfect" courtly tale of Federigo (V.9) immediately after the "disturbing" tale of Nastagio (V.8)? Consider how these two stories serve to define the boundaries of the Day’s theme.

## Glossary of Terms

*   **Beffa:** A comic trick or prank, often featured in Boccaccio’s more realistic or cynical tales.
*   **Contrapasso:** A punishment that mirrors the sin; used to describe the supernatural vision in Nastagio’s tale.
*   **Courtly Love (Fin'amors/Amor Cortese):** A medieval literary tradition of idealized, often unrequited romantic devotion.
*   **Crudeltade:** A medieval term for "cruelty," specifically used to describe a lady’s refusal to return the affection of a faithful suitor.
*   **Exemplum:** A short story told to illustrate a moral point or provide an example of proper behavior.
*   **Novella:** A short prose narrative focused on realistic characters and human behavior, a form perfected by Boccaccio.
*   **Provence:** A region in southeastern France where troubadour poets invented the conventions of courtly love.
