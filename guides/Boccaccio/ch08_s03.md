# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron – Day VII, Novel 2 (The Tale of Peronella)

This study guide provides an in-depth analysis of the second tale of the Seventh Day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. Told by Filostrato, the story follows Peronella, a quick-witted Neapolitan wife who successfully deceives her husband when he returns home unexpectedly.

---

## Key Concepts and Narrative Analysis

### The Setting and Social Context
Unlike many tales set in Florence, this story takes place in **Naples**, specifically on the street called **Avorio**. The characters belong to the "urban poor" or the working class. The husband is a **mason**, and Peronella contributes to the household by **spinning**. Their "slender means" are central to the plot; the economic reality of their lives makes the sale of a household object—a large wine jar or tun—a significant financial event.

### The Concept of the *Beffa*
The tale is a "masterclass in the *beffa*" (a trick or practical joke). In Boccaccio's framework, the *beffa* often involves:
*   **Improvisation:** Peronella must react with "breathtaking speed" when her husband knocks.
*   **Verbal Brilliance:** She does not just hide her lover; she uses language to put her husband on the defensive.
*   **The Triumphant Performance:** By the end of the story, the husband is not only deceived but is actually grateful to his wife for her "superior" business skills.

### The Symbolic and Material Role of the Jar
The large tun (jar) serves three distinct purposes in the narrative:
1.  **A Hiding Place:** It physically conceals the lover, Giannello Sirignario.
2.  **A Commercial Object:** It represents a "valuable commodity" in a poor household, worth several gigliats.
3.  **A Comic Stage:** It provides the physical setting for the story’s climax, where the husband is literally inside the jar cleaning it while the wife and lover interact just outside the vent.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. What is the husband’s profession, and how does Peronella contribute to the household income?**
The husband is a poor mason. Peronella earns money by spinning.

**2. Why does the husband return home earlier than expected on the day of the story?**
He returns early because it is the feast of San Galeone, which is a holiday.

**3. What is the name of Peronella’s lover?**
His name is Giannello Sirignario.

**4. How does Peronella immediately react when her husband knocks at the door?**
She instructs Giannello to hide in a large tun (jar) and then goes to the door to berate her husband, accusing him of being lazy and not providing for the family.

**5. For what price did the husband claim to have sold the tun, and what price did Peronella claim she had secured?**
The husband claimed to have sold it for five gigliats. Peronella claimed she had already sold it to a man for seven gigliats.

**6. What "defect" does Giannello pretend to find in the tun to justify his presence inside it?**
He claims the tun is sound but "encrusted" with dry lees (sediment) and refuses to buy it unless it is scoured clean.

**7. Describe the husband's actions while Giannello and Peronella are together at the end of the tale.**
The husband strips to his vest, takes a light and a rasp, and climbs inside the jar to scrape the lees off the sides.

**8. How does Peronella participate in the "scouring" of the jar?**
She thrusts her head and arm into the vent of the tun to "direct and admonish" her husband, telling him exactly where to scrape.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Strategy of Verbal Attack
Analyze Peronella’s use of language upon her husband's return. Rather than acting guilty or fearful, she immediately assumes the role of the aggrieved party. How does her speech about her own "honesty" and her husband's perceived laziness serve to distract him? Discuss how Boccaccio uses "high verbal style" to contrast with the "low material reality" of the characters' lives.

### 2. Class and Economics in the Decameron
The source context describes this as one of Boccaccio’s "great class-conscious tales." Explore how the poverty of the mason and the spinner drives the plot. Would the trick be as effective or the humor as sharp if the characters were wealthy aristocrats who did not care about the price of a used wine jar?

### 3. The Performance of Fidelity
Peronella mocks other women who have "two or three" lovers and claim "the moon is the sun." Ironically, she is doing exactly what she decries. Discuss the theme of performance in this tale. How does Peronella use the social expectations of a "good and honest girl" to facilitate her adultery?

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Beffa** | A trick, joke, or piece of deception, often involving a display of wit or verbal dexterity. |
| **Betimes** | Early in the morning; promptly. |
| **Gigliats** | A form of currency (gold or silver coins) mentioned as the payment for the tun. |
| **Lees** | The sediment or dregs found at the bottom of a wine container; the "encrustation" the husband must scrape away. |
| **Mason** | A person who works with stone or brick; the profession of Peronella’s husband. |
| **San Galeone** | The feast day/holiday that serves as the plot device for the husband’s early return. |
| **Tun** | A large cask or jar used for holding liquids, in this case, wine; the central prop of the story. |
| **Vent** | A small opening or hole in the tun, used by Peronella to look inside and direct her husband. |
