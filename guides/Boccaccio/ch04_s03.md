# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Tale of Masetto da Lamporecchio (The Decameron, Day III, Novel 1)

This study guide explores the first tale of the third day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*, narrated by Filostrato. The story centers on Masetto da Lamporecchio, a young man who uses a clever ruse to gain employment at a convent, leading to a satirical exploration of human nature, religious hypocrisy, and the power of silence.

## Key Concepts and Thematic Analysis

### 1. Ingegno (Cleverness/Wit)
The primary theme of Day Three is "cleverness triumphant." In this tale, *ingegno* is defined as the ability to recognize an opportunity others miss and act decisively upon it. Masetto’s cleverness is not found in complex schemes but in the "brilliant insight" that silence is the key to opportunity. By pretending to be a deaf-mute, he eliminates the social and moral barriers that would normally prevent a young man from living among nuns.

### 2. Anticlerical Satire
Boccaccio targets institutional hypocrisy rather than religious faith itself. The satire highlights the gap between:
*   **Professed Virtue:** The nuns’ vows of virginity and their "repute for sanctity."
*   **Actual Behavior:** Their "all too human" desires and their willingness to abandon vows when presented with a risk-free opportunity.
*   **The "Nature of a Stone":** The narrator mocks the foolish belief that taking a veil causes a woman to cease having natural human cravings.

### 3. Nature vs. Social Constraints
The text argues that neither religious garb nor manual labor can eradicate "carnal propensities." It challenges the notion that "tillers of the soil" (the working class) lack wit or that nuns lose their humanity upon entering a convent. The story suggests that "idleness and solitude" in the convent actually intensify these natural desires.

### 4. Distribution of Sympathy and Irony
The humor is derived from situational irony. Masetto is portrayed as a "shrewd" opportunist rather than a predator, as the nuns are depicted as willing participants who often initiate the encounters. The ending complicates moral judgment, as Masetto’s "wisdom" leads him to a wealthy and comfortable retirement.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

| Question | Answer Summary |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Why did Nuto, the original gardener, leave the convent?** | He was unhappy with the low wages and complained that the young nuns were constantly "worrying" him and interfering with his work. |
| **What specific ruse does Masetto use to get hired?** | He pretends to be a deaf-mute, believing the convent will admit him because he cannot "blab" about what happens there. |
| **Why does the steward recommend Masetto to the Abbess?** | He sees Masetto is strong and capable, and believes a mute gardener poses no threat of "cracking jokes" or gossiping with the young nuns. |
| **What reasoning does the first nun use to convince her friend to approach Masetto?** | She argues that many vows are made but not performed, and that Masetto is the perfect candidate because he is a "simpleton" who cannot speak of their actions. |
| **Why does Masetto eventually decide to break his silence?** | He becomes physically exhausted by the "exorbitant demands" of the nine women and realizes his pretense is becoming a "burden quite beyond [his] power to bear." |
| **How do the nuns and the Abbess explain Masetto's sudden ability to speak?** | They tell the neighbors that he recovered his speech through their prayers and the merits of their patron saint. |

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Role of Silence as Power:** Analyze how Masetto’s feigned disability serves as his greatest strength. In what ways does his lack of speech grant him access, safety, and power within the hierarchical structure of the convent?
2.  **Satire and Institutional Hypocrisy:** Boccaccio states that his target is not religious life itself but the "gap between professed virtue and actual behavior." Using specific examples from the text, discuss how the author uses the characters of the nuns and the Abbess to critique the institutional Church of his time.
3.  **The Narrator's Perspective on Human Nature:** At the beginning of the tale, Filostrato challenges the idea that "the carnal propensities are utterly eradicated" by religious vows or hard labor. How does the progression of the story support or complicate this argument?
4.  **Moral Ambiguity in the Resolution:** Masetto returns home "rich and a father," having been supported by the convent for years. Discuss the moral implications of this ending. Does Boccaccio reward "shrewdness" over "virtue"?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Abbess:** The female head of a community of nuns. In this tale, she is the final one to succumb to Masetto’s presence, eventually promoting him to steward to keep their secrets safe.
*   **Contadino:** A peasant or husbandman; Masetto is described as a "hardy fellow" and "handsome for a contadino."
*   **Ingegno:** Italian for "wit," "genius," or "cleverness." It represents the ability to use one's intellect to overcome obstacles or seize opportunities.
*   **Lamporecchio:** The home village of Masetto and Nuto.
*   **Masetto da Lamporecchio:** The protagonist of the tale; a young gardener who successfully deceives a convent by pretending to be a deaf-mute.
*   **Nuto:** The gardener who preceded Masetto at the convent; his complaints about the nuns provide Masetto with the information needed to form his plan.
*   **Steward:** The official in charge of managing the convent's estate and labor. Masetto eventually takes over this position after the original steward dies.
*   **The Decameron:** The larger work by Giovanni Boccaccio from which this tale is taken, consisting of 100 stories told over ten days.
