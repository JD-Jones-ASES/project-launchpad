Study Guide: The Tale of the Eaten Heart (Decameron, Day IV, Novel 9)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the ninth tale of the fourth day of Boccaccio’s Decameron. It explores the historical origins, thematic depth, and narrative structure of one of the most extreme tragic stories in the collection.

Overview and Historical Context

The ninth tale of Day IV, narrated by Filostrato, is characterized by its "stark, unflinching brevity." While it is one of the shortest tales in the fourth day, its impact is immense due to its use of the "eaten heart" motif—a tradition of extreme passion found in medieval literature.

  • Literary Tradition: The story draws from Provençal literature, specifically the legend of the troubadour.
  • Historical Source: The primary source is the vida (biographical sketch) of the troubadour Guilhem de Cabestanh, a figure from the late 12th century. Legend states he was killed by a jealous husband who then served his heart to his wife.
  • The Moral Exemplum: In medieval literature, extreme and horrifying scenarios were often used as a moral exemplum—an illustration pushed to its furthest limit to reveal fundamental truths about human nature, specifically regarding jealousy and honor.

Plot Summary and Key Entities

Entity Role and Description
Filostrato The narrator of the tale and the "King" of Day IV.
Sieur Guillaume de Roussillon A noble knight of Provence; husband to the lady and former close friend of Cabestaing.
Sieur Guillaume de Cabestaing A noble knight and "complete" warrior; Roussillon's brother-in-arms who becomes his wife's lover.
The Lady Roussillon’s wife, who returns Cabestaing’s love with "fierce, terrible dignity."
The Cook The servant instructed to prepare Cabestaing’s heart as a "boar's heart" ragout.

Narrative Arc

  1. The Bond: Roussillon and Cabestaing are inseparable friends and knights who share the same device in tournaments.
  2. The Affair: Cabestaing falls in love with Roussillon’s wife. She returns his passion, and they meet with "little discretion."
  3. The Betrayal and Murder: Upon discovering the affair, Roussillon's love for Cabestaing turns to "mortal enmity." He lures Cabestaing into an ambush by inviting him to discuss an upcoming tournament in France. Roussillon kills him with a lance and cuts out his heart.
  4. The Gruesome Feast: Roussillon has his cook mince the heart with "plenty of good seasoning" and serves it to his wife as a delicacy. She eats the entire dish with pleasure.
  5. The Revelation: Roussillon reveals that she has consumed the heart of her lover.
  6. The Resolution: The lady declares that "no food shall ever follow so noble a morsel" and throws herself out of a high window to her death.
  7. The Aftermath: Roussillon flees the country in fear of the Count of Provence and the local folk. The lovers are buried together in a single tomb with an inscription detailing their death.

Key Concepts for Analysis

1. The Eaten Heart Motif

This motif represents the logical extreme of jealousy and passion. For a medieval audience, the horror was not merely for sensationalism but served to confront the reader with the destructive reality of jealousy. The heart, once the center of the lovers' "inordinate" passion, becomes the literal substance that consumes the lady.

2. Passivity vs. Dignity

Initially, the lady is a figure of courtly love, waiting for Cabestaing to make his suit. However, her response to the revelation of her husband's crime transforms her. She does not weep or rage; instead, she makes a calculated declaration of loyalty and takes her own life. This final act shifts her from a passive object of the men's conflict into a figure of "fierce, terrible dignity."

3. Narrator Influence

The fact that Filostrato—the King of Day IV—chooses to tell this story himself is significant. As the leader of a day dedicated to tragic love, his selection of the most "extreme and disturbing" tale reveals his own preoccupation with the darker, more monstrous aspects of human emotion.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the specific historical source for the characters in this tale?
    • Answer: The vida (biographical sketch) of the late 12th-century troubadour Guilhem de Cabestanh.
  2. How did Roussillon disguise the heart when giving it to the cook?
    • Answer: He told the cook it was a "boar's heart."
  3. Why did the lady decide to jump from the window?
    • Answer: She declared that no other food should ever follow a meal as noble as the heart of her lover.
  4. How did the public respond to the deaths of the lady and Cabestaing?
    • Answer: They bore the bodies with "grief and lamentation," buried them in the same tomb, and inscribed the tomb with the story of their deaths.
  5. What was the relationship between Roussillon and Cabestaing before the affair?
    • Answer: They were noble knights, close friends, and brothers-in-arms who wore the same device to tournaments.
  6. Why did Roussillon flee after the lady’s death?
    • Answer: He was "conscience-stricken" and feared the vengeance of the country folk and the Count of Provence.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Retribution: Analyze Roussillon’s transition from a "brother" to a "mortal enemy." Does the text present his revenge as a restoration of honor or as a descent into monstrosity? Use specific details from his actions in the woods and the kitchen to support your argument.
  2. The Lady’s Transformation: Examine the character of the wife. Contrast her role during the secret affair with her behavior in the final scene of the story. How does her suicide function as a reclamation of her own agency?
  3. Brevity and Impact: This tale is noted for its "stark, unflinching brevity." Discuss how the short length of the narrative contributes to the horror of the "eaten heart" motif. Would the story be more or less effective if Boccaccio had included more dialogue between the lovers or the husband?
  4. Moral Exemplum in Medieval Context: Define the concept of the moral exemplum and apply it to this tale. What "truth about human nature" is revealed by pushing the scenario of jealousy to such a gruesome limit?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Banderole: A small flag or streamer; in this context, the cloth used by Roussillon to wrap and carry Cabestaing’s heart.
  • Moral Exemplum: A story told to illustrate a moral point or to serve as a model of behavior, often using extreme scenarios to reveal human truths.
  • Porringer: A small bowl, often with a handle, used for serving soup or stews (ragouts).
  • Provençal: Relating to Provence, a region in southern France known for its tradition of troubadours and courtly love.
  • Ragout: A highly seasoned meat stew; the form in which the heart was served to the lady.
  • Troubadour: A poet-musician of southern France (11th–13th centuries) who composed lyrics regarding courtly love.
  • Vida: A brief prose biography of a troubadour, written in Occitan, which often blended fact with legendary elements.
  • Yclept: An archaic term meaning "named" or "called."
