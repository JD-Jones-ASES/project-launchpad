Study Guide: The Decameron, Day the First

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the introductory sections of Day the First of Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. It explores the transition of the ten young protagonists from the chaos of plague-stricken Florence to the structured environment of a country estate, establishing the framework for the one hundred tales that follow.

Key Concepts and Narrative Framework

The Transition from Chaos to Order

The narrative begins with a sharp contrast between the "disordered world" of plague-stricken Florence and the civilized retreat at a country estate in the hills of Fiesole. The move to the countryside is not merely an escape for survival, but an attempt to re-establish social order. This order is formalized through:

  • The Election of a Leader: Pampinea, the eldest woman and the one who originally proposed the retreat, is elected as the first queen.
  • The Establishment of Rules: The group (the brigata) agrees on a set of rules for their daily conduct and storytelling to ensure their time is spent in a healthy, virtuous, and pleasurable manner.

The Setting: Fiesole

Fiesole is described as an ancient Etruscan town situated north of Florence. Traditionally identified with the Villa Palmieri (now Crawford), the estate is characterized by:

  • Natural Beauty: Lush meadows, green grass, and olive trees.
  • Atmosphere: A "fair prospect" with cool air and light breezes, providing a refuge from the intense midday heat and the "sun high" in the sky.

The Purpose of Storytelling

Pampinea proposes storytelling as the primary afternoon activity to pass the "hot hours." She argues that storytelling is superior to games like chess or dice because:

  • Collective Solace: The invention of one person provides enjoyment for the entire company.
  • Emotional Stability: Games like dice often result in the loser being "vexed" and the winner or onlookers being no better off, whereas stories provide a shared benefit.

The Scope of Day I

Unlike most other days in the work, Day I has no prescribed theme. This freedom allows the narrators to speak on any subject they choose.

  • Themes Introduced: Religious hypocrisy, interfaith wisdom, royal rebuke, and the wit of men and women.
  • The "Overture": Collectively, the ten tales of the first day serve as an introduction to the broader themes Boccaccio develops throughout the entire work.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does the brigata choose Fiesole as their retreat? It is a beautiful country estate in the hills that offers a civilized retreat from the plague-stricken chaos of Florence.

  2. Who is elected as the first queen of the company, and why? Pampinea is elected queen because she is the eldest of the women and was the individual who initially proposed the retreat.

  3. According to the Queen, why is sleeping long in the daytime discouraged? She asserts that sleeping for long periods during the day is "injurious to health."

  4. How many "Days" in the Decameron are free from a prescribed theme? Two: Day I and Day IX.

  5. What alternative pastimes does Pampinea reject in favor of storytelling? She rejects playing with dice or chess.

  6. Who is the first person called upon to tell a story? Pamfilo, who sat on the queen's right hand.

  7. What does the text suggest is the etymological origin of the implement used to drag corpses during the plague? The term is likely derived from a "pronged or hooked implement" used for that specific purpose.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Architecture of Order

Analyze how the election of Pampinea and the establishment of rules for storytelling serve as a "first act of order in a disordered world." How does the structured environment of the Fiesole estate function as a thematic and moral counterpoint to the plague described in the opening of the work?

2. Storytelling as a Social Remedy

Pampinea suggests that storytelling provides "solace to all the company." Discuss the role of narrative as a communal activity in The Decameron. Contrast this with the "vexation" Pampinea associates with games of chance. Why is the "invention of one" considered more valuable for the group's health and harmony than individual competition?

3. Day I as a Thematic Overture

The text describes the first day’s tales as an "overture to the entire work." Based on the subjects mentioned—religious hypocrisy, interfaith wisdom, and wit—explore how these initial stories set the stage for Boccaccio’s broader commentary on 14th-century society.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Brigata: The company of ten young people (seven women and three men) who flee the plague.
  • Cicalas: Insects (cicadas) found among the olive trees, noted for their sound during the heat of the day.
  • Fiesole: An ancient Etruscan town overlooking Florence, known for its villas and rural retreats.
  • None: A specific time of day (traditionally the ninth hour or mid-afternoon) when the queen roused the company.
  • Pampinea: The eldest woman of the group and the queen of Day I.
  • Pamfilo: The narrator chosen by Pampinea to lead the storytelling on the first day.
  • Vesper: The evening hour; the time by which the storytelling was expected to conclude.
  • Villa Palmieri: The estate traditionally identified as the location where the brigata first stayed.
