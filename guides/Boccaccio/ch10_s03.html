Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Tale of the Abbess and the Breeches (The Decameron, Day IX, Novel 2)

This study guide explores Section 3 of Chapter 10, focusing on Elissa’s narration of the second tale from the ninth day of Giovanni Boccaccio's The Decameron. This story serves as a quintessential example of medieval anticlerical satire, blending narrative comedy with a sharp critique of religious hypocrisy.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. Anticlerical Satire

The tale belongs to a long tradition of literature that mocks the clergy. While such satire was common in the Middle Ages—appearing in Latin goliardic poems and the works of Chaucer—Boccaccio’s specific contribution is the marriage of biting satire with narrative comedy. The story is designed to "demolish the pretense of monastic chastity."

2. Comic Economy

The text describes this tale as a "masterpiece of comic economy." In just two pages, Boccaccio establishes a complex situation, executes a visual punchline, and delivers a moral resolution that exposes the gap between public authority and private behavior.

3. The Shift in Power Dynamics

A central theme is the sudden inversion of power. The Abbess begins the scene as a figure of absolute moral authority, delivering a "severe reprimand" to the young nun, Isabetta. However, once the "points" of the priest’s breeches are noticed on the Abbess's head, the authority shifts. The accused becomes the accuser, and the Abbess is forced to abandon her moral high ground to protect her own interests.

4. The Role of Fortune

The narrator, Elisa, frames the story as an example of how "Fortune at times justly puts [foolish folk] to shame." The story suggests that those who take it upon themselves to govern others are often the most deserving of being exposed for their own follies.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who is the narrator of this specific tale? Answer: Elissa (also spelled Elisa).

2. In what region is the convent located? Answer: Lombardy.

3. What is the name of the young nun who is caught with her lover? Answer: Isabetta.

4. Why does the Abbess, Madonna Usimbalda, have a priest’s breeches in her room? Answer: She frequently has a priest brought to her room in a chest for her own secret sexual encounters.

5. What does the Abbess accidentally place on her head instead of her veil? Answer: The priest’s breeches (trousers).

6. What specific visual detail on the Abbess's head gear does Isabetta notice that gives her the courage to speak up? Answer: The "points" (laces or ties) that were hanging down on either side of the Abbess's face.

7. How does the Abbess change her "language" once she realizes she has been caught? Answer: She stops the reprimand and argues that it is impossible to "withstand the assaults of the flesh," concluding that all the nuns should enjoy themselves as long as they remain discreet.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Visual Punchline as Moral Commentary

The image of the Abbess wearing a priest's breeches on her head has been a favorite subject for illustrators since the fourteenth century. Discuss how this specific visual image serves as both a comedic device and a devastating moral critique. How does the physical presence of the breeches strip away the Abbess’s religious "veil" of sanctity?

2. Hypocrisy and Authority

Examine the character of Madonna Usimbalda. Before the discovery of the breeches, she is described as a "good and holy woman" by those who know her. Analyze the disconnect between her public reputation and her private actions. What does Boccaccio’s resolution—allowing all the nuns to pursue their pleasures—suggest about his view of monastic life and human nature?

3. The Community of Women

The "watchful sisters" initially act as a collective force for enforcement, hoping to catch Isabetta "in the act." By the end of the story, the entire convent "enjoys the revelation" and eventually adopts a lifestyle of secret pleasures. Trace the evolution of the convent's social dynamic from one of "close espial" and judgment to one of mutual complicity.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Anticlerical Satire A genre of literature aimed at mocking or criticizing the clergy, often highlighting corruption, hypocrisy, or the failure to uphold religious vows.
Breeches Short trousers fastened just below the knee; in this story, they belong to the priest and are mistakenly used as headgear.
Chapter-house A building or room that is part of a monastery or cathedral, used as a meeting place for the governing body of the religious community.
Comic Economy A literary style characterized by brevity and efficiency, achieving a complete narrative and thematic impact in a very short space.
Goliardic Poems Twelfth-century Latin poems, often satirical or ribald, written by traveling scholars known as Goliards.
Madonna Usimbalda The Abbess of the convent in Lombardy; the primary antagonist who is exposed as a hypocrite.
Points The ties or laces used to fasten medieval clothing; specifically the parts of the priest's breeches hanging from the Abbess's head.
Psalter In the context of this story, the specific term used for the veil or head-covering worn by the nuns.
