Study Guide: Giovanni Boccaccio and the Literary Landscape of the Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the literary, historical, and scholarly contexts surrounding Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. It is designed to assist students in understanding Boccaccio’s broader body of work, his relationship to contemporary authors, the historical forces that shaped his writing, and the primary scholarly interpretations of his masterpiece.

I. Key Literary Works by Giovanni Boccaccio

While the Decameron is Boccaccio’s most famous work, his literary output was vast and influential across several genres.

Work Description and Significance
The Decameron A collection of one hundred tales set against the backdrop of the Black Death. Notable translations include J.M. Rigg (1903), G.H. McWilliam (1995), and Wayne A. Rebhorn (2013).
The Life of Dante A biography of Dante Alighieri, whom Boccaccio revered. It provides insight into the literary culture of 14th-century Italy.
The Fates of Illustrious Men A collection of biographical sketches tracing the downfall of great figures from Adam to Boccaccio’s time. It heavily influenced Geoffrey Chaucer’s "The Monk’s Tale."
On Famous Women The first Western collection of biographies devoted exclusively to women, ranging from the biblical Eve to Queen Joanna of Naples.
The Genealogy of the Pagan Gods A massive encyclopedia of classical mythology that includes a significant defense of poetry and fiction.

II. Comparative Literary Context

To understand the Decameron, one must view it in relation to the works of Boccaccio's predecessors and contemporaries.

Geoffrey Chaucer: The Canterbury Tales

Chaucer’s work serves as the English companion to the Decameron. Key points of comparison include:

  • The General Prologue: Features a "brigata" or gallery of pilgrims similar to Boccaccio’s, though Chaucer’s group is more socially diverse.
  • The Clerk’s Tale: A retelling of Boccaccio’s story of Patient Griselda, featuring an ironic "envoy."
  • The Franklin’s Tale: Echoes the themes of competing generosity found in Decameron X.5.
  • The Pardoner’s Prologue and Tale: Explores religious hypocrisy, comparable to the character of Ser Ciappelletto.

Dante Alighieri: Inferno

Dante was Boccaccio’s great predecessor. While Dante treats themes of love and free will with theological seriousness (notably in Cantos V and X), Boccaccio deliberately avoids such gravity in the Decameron.

Francesco Petrarch: Canzoniere

A close friend of Boccaccio, Petrarch focused on the lyric tradition. While Petrarch’s sonnets to Laura portray an idealized love in exquisite verse, Boccaccio’s prose focuses on "messy, real love" in earthy terms.

III. Historical and Scholarly Perspectives

Historical Background

The 14th century was a period of profound upheaval, often described as "calamitous." Understanding this era is essential for contextualizing Boccaccio’s work.

  • The Black Death (1347–1351): The plague pandemic provides the framing for the Decameron. It had devastating social and psychological effects.
  • Mercantile Society: Boccaccio’s Florence was a hub of trade and urban life, moving away from medieval feudal certainties.
  • Political Context: The era was marked by the Hundred Years' War, the Great Schism, and the rise of Italian city-states.

Scholarly Approaches

Modern critics have analyzed the Decameron through various lenses:

  • The Ludic Perspective: Giuseppe Mazzotta views the work as a meditation on "play" and fiction's role in the social order.
  • Literary Self-Consciousness: Millicent Marcus explores how the Decameron reflects on its own nature as a work of fiction and the act of storytelling.
  • Sacred vs. Profane: Robert Hollander argues there is a moral seriousness and a tension between sacred and profane love beneath the comic surface.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Which modern translation of the Decameron is noted for being fluent, accurate, and richly annotated?
  2. What is the significance of Boccaccio’s On Famous Women in Western literature?
  3. How does the social composition of Chaucer’s pilgrims in The Canterbury Tales differ from Boccaccio’s brigata?
  4. In which of Boccaccio's works can a defense of poetry and fiction be found?
  5. Which specific Cantos of Dante’s Inferno are most relevant to the Decameron’s treatment of love and free will?
  6. What historical event serves as the primary psychological and social backdrop for the Decameron’s introduction?
  7. Which Boccaccio work served as a primary influence for Chaucer’s "The Monk’s Tale"?
  8. How does Boccaccio’s portrayal of love differ from Petrarch’s portrayal in the Canzoniere?
  9. According to Giuseppe Mazzotta, what is the relationship between the Decameron and the social order?
  10. Who wrote A Distant Mirror, a narrative history of the "calamitous" 14th century?

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Narrative: Compare and contrast the use of the "frame story" in Boccaccio’s Decameron and Chaucer’s The Canterbury Tales. How do the different social backgrounds of the storytellers influence the tone and themes of the tales?
  2. Idealism vs. Realism: Analyze the transition from the theological seriousness of Dante and the idealized love of Petrarch to Boccaccio’s "earthy prose." How does the Decameron represent a shift in the literary portrayal of human experience?
  3. The Impact of Catastrophe: Discuss the role of the Black Death as a literary device. How does the "Great Mortality" serve as a catalyst for the storytelling in the Decameron, and how does Boccaccio use fiction to respond to social collapse?
  4. Moral Ambiguity: Evaluate Robert Hollander’s argument that the Decameron maintains a "moral seriousness" beneath its comic surface. Use specific examples of religious hypocrisy or competing generosities to support your analysis.

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Brigata: The group of storytellers in the Decameron (and by extension, the pilgrims in The Canterbury Tales).
  • Canzoniere: Francesco Petrarch’s collection of sonnets, representing the lyric tradition of idealized love.
  • De casibus virorum illustrium: Boccaccio’s work The Fates of Illustrious Men, focusing on the fall of great figures.
  • De mulieribus claris: Boccaccio’s On Famous Women, the first Western biographical collection devoted to women.
  • Genealogia deorum gentilium: Boccaccio’s encyclopedia of classical mythology.
  • Ludic: Relating to play; a term used by scholars like Mazzotta to describe the Decameron as a meditation on fiction and play.
  • Mercantile Society: The urban, trade-based social structure of 14th-century Florence portrayed in Boccaccio’s stories.
  • The Great Mortality: Another term for the Black Death pandemic of 1347–1351.
  • Trattatello in laude di Dante: Boccaccio's biography of Dante, known as The Life of Dante.
