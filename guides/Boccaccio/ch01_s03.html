Study Guide: The Life and Times of Giovanni Boccaccio

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the life, works, and historical context of Giovanni Boccaccio (1313–1375). It is designed to facilitate a deep understanding of Boccaccio’s development as a writer, the impact of the Black Death on his masterpiece, the Decameron, and his role as one of the "Three Crowns" of Italian literature.

Key Concepts and Biographical Overview

Early Life and the Neapolitan Influence

Giovanni Boccaccio was born in 1313, likely in Certaldo or Florence, as the illegitimate son of Boccaccino di Chellino, a merchant associated with the powerful Bardi banking house. Despite his illegitimacy, his father provided for an education that allowed him to transition from the world of commerce to the world of letters.

In 1327, Boccaccio moved to Angevin Naples to apprentice in banking. While he showed little interest in trade, the vibrant cultural crossroads of the Neapolitan court introduced him to French romances and classical Latin literature. This period also saw the emergence of Fiammetta, a woman who served as Boccaccio’s literary muse and the inspiration for his early narrative poems, such as the Filostrato and the Filocolo.

Return to Florence and the Black Death

Financial instability forced Boccaccio back to Florence around 1340. Though he initially found the merchant-driven city provincial compared to Naples, Florence was an international center of trade and intellectual life.

The defining event of Boccaccio’s life occurred in 1348: the Black Death. The plague killed between 50,000 and 80,000 Florentines—more than half the population. Boccaccio witnessed the total collapse of social, religious, and moral structures, a devastation he later documented in the harrowing introduction to the Decameron. This atmosphere of social upheaval provided the backdrop for his collection of 100 tales.

Intellectual Maturity and the Humanist Movement

Boccaccio’s later life was defined by his friendship with Francesco Petrarch, which began in 1350. Under Petrarch’s influence, Boccaccio moved toward humanist scholarship, producing encyclopedic Latin works like the Genealogy of the Pagan Gods and delivering the first public lectures on Dante’s Divine Comedy. Despite his earlier "profane" writings, Boccaccio’s final years were marked by increased religious devotion and a spiritual crisis triggered by a warning of damnation from a Carthusian monk.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Where was Boccaccio likely born, and where did he spend his final years?
  2. What was the significance of the Bardi family in Boccaccio’s early life?
  3. Describe the literary significance of "Fiammetta."
  4. How did Boccaccio’s view of Florence change upon his return from Naples in 1340?
  5. According to the text, what was the estimated population of Florence before and after the Black Death?
  6. What are the linguistic origins of the title Decameron?
  7. Identify three different types of sources Boccaccio drew upon when composing the Decameron.
  8. How did Francesco Petrarch influence Boccaccio’s perception of his own Italian prose works?
  9. What was the purpose of Boccaccio’s Genealogy of the Pagan Gods?
  10. What does the phrase tre corone refer to in the context of Italian literature?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Crucible of the Plague

Analyze how the social and moral breakdown caused by the Black Death in 1348 served as the necessary catalyst for the Decameron. Discuss how Boccaccio uses the contrast between the "world turned upside down" and the structured storytelling of his ten protagonists to comment on human resilience and morality.

2. The Conflict of Language and Legacy

Examine the intellectual relationship between Boccaccio and Petrarch. How did Petrarch’s preference for Latin and lyric poetry create a tension in Boccaccio’s career? Discuss the irony of Boccaccio’s legacy: while he turned to Latin scholarship in his later years to satisfy humanist ideals, he is primarily remembered for his Italian prose.

3. From Naples to Florence: A Cultural Synthesis

Boccaccio’s writing is often described as covering the "full range of human experience." Argue how his early exposure to the courtly romances of Naples combined with his later experiences in the pragmatic, merchant-driven society of Florence allowed him to bridge the gap between "the sacred and the profane."

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Angevin Naples The Kingdom of Naples under the French House of Anjou; a brilliant cultural crossroads where Boccaccio spent his youth.
Bardi A major Florentine banking family whose collapse in the 1340s impacted Boccaccio’s family and forced his return to Florence.
Certaldo A Tuscan hill town where Boccaccio was likely born and where he died in 1375.
Decameron Boccaccio’s masterpiece (c. 1349–1353), consisting of 100 tales told over ten days by ten young people.
Fabliau A short, often bawdy, humorous verse tale from medieval France; Boccaccio adapted many of these into prose for the Decameron.
Fiammetta "Little flame"; the name Boccaccio gave to his beloved and muse, possibly based on Maria d'Aquino.
Humanist A scholar devoted to the studia humanitatis (grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and philosophy), focusing on classical Greek and Roman texts.
Pestilence The Black Death of 1348, caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, which killed roughly a third of Europe's population.
Tre Corone "Three Crowns"; the collective title for the three greatest early Italian writers: Dante, Petrarch, and Boccaccio.
Yersinia pestis The modern scientific name for the bacterium responsible for the bubonic, pneumonic, and septicemic plagues.
