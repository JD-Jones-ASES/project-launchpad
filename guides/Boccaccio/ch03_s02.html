Study Guide: The Decameron, Day the Second

This study guide explores the transition into the second day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. It focuses on the shift in leadership, the introduction of the central theme of Fortune, and the structural relationship between the storytelling company’s environment and the narratives they share.

Key Concepts and Contextual Overview

The Transition of Power

Following the conclusion of the first day, the crown is passed from Pampinea to Filomena. As the queen of Day II, Filomena is responsible for setting the tone and the specific subject matter for the company’s storytelling.

The Theme of Fortuna

Unlike Day I, which had no prescribed theme, Day II centers on the concept of Fortuna (Fortune). In medieval thought, Fortune was personified as a goddess who turned a great wheel, arbitrarily raising some individuals to prosperity while casting others into ruin.

  • Literary Origins: This imagery is heavily influenced by Boethius’s Consolation of Philosophy (c. 524).
  • The Narrative Focus: The stories of Day II specifically concern individuals who, after experiencing diverse misadventures and being tossed about by forces beyond their control, ultimately arrive at a happy ending they had not expected.

Narrative Style and Structure

The tales told under Filomena’s reign are noted for being among the longest and most elaborately plotted in the entire collection. They are characterized by:

  • Adventure and Surprise: Frequent use of shipwrecks, disguises, and mistaken identities.
  • The World of Action: Midnight escapades and reversals of fate that test a character's luck, wit, or endurance.

The Cornice and Setting

The "cornice," or frame story, undergoes a subtle shift as Filomena leads the company to a new estate. This movement from the first villa to a second location is a deliberate literary device by Boccaccio. The physical movement and discovery of the company mirror the themes of movement and unexpected journeys found within the tales themselves.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who succeeds Pampinea as the ruler of the second day?

Answer: Filomena.

2. What specific theme does the queen establish for the stories of Day II?

Answer: Tales of those who, after various misadventures, achieve a happy ending they had not hoped for (the theme of Fortuna).

3. What ubiquitous medieval image is used to represent Fortune?

Answer: A goddess turning a great wheel, which raises some mortals up and casts others down.

4. To which 6th-century literary work is the medieval personification of Fortune traced?

Answer: Boethius’s Consolation of Philosophy.

5. How does the physical setting of the company change at the start of Day II?

Answer: The company moves from the gardens of the first villa to a new estate.

6. According to the text, what activities does the company engage in on the morning of the second day before storytelling begins?

Answer: They walk through dewy meadows, cull flowers, weave garlands, eat breakfast in the shade, dance, and sleep until noon.

7. Who is the first storyteller called upon by the queen on the second day?

Answer: Neifile.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Mirror of the Cornice

Analyze Boccaccio’s use of the "cornice" (the frame story) in Day II. How does the physical movement of the ten storytellers to a new villa serve as a metaphor for the narratives they are about to share? Discuss how the transition in setting reinforces the themes of adventure and discovery.

2. The Dynamics of Fortune and Agency

The theme of Day II involves characters "tossed about by forces beyond their control" who still reach a "happy issue." Explore the balance between external fate (Fortuna) and internal traits (luck, wit, or endurance). In the context of Day II, is a happy ending a result of a character's own merit, or is it merely another turn of the goddess's wheel?

3. Plot Complexity and Medieval Realism

Day II is described as having some of the most "elaborately plotted" tales, featuring shipwrecks, disguises, and mistaken identities. Discuss how these complex plot devices contribute to the overarching theme of Fortune's reversals. How do these elements shift the tone of The Decameron from the unstructured nature of Day I?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Boethius The author of The Consolation of Philosophy (c. 524), whose work popularized the image of the Wheel of Fortune in medieval culture.
Cornice The "frame" or setting of The Decameron, involving the company’s movements and daily routines between storytelling sessions.
Filomena The queen of Day II who chooses the theme of Fortune's reversals.
Fortuna The medieval personification of Fortune as a goddess; a force of unpredictable change and reversal in human life.
Laurel Wreath The crown worn by the reigning king or queen of the day to signify their authority over the company.
Neifile The first member of the company to tell a story on the second day at the behest of Queen Filomena.
Pampinea The ruler of Day I who preceded Filomena.
