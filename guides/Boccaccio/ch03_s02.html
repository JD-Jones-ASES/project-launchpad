Study Guide: The Decameron, Day the Second

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the transition into the second day of Boccaccio’s Decameron. It explores the shifts in leadership, the thematic focus on Fortune, and the structural relationship between the frame narrative and the stories themselves.

Key Concepts and Contextual Overview

The Reign of Filomena

Following the conclusion of the first day, the company undergoes its first change in leadership. Pampinea, the queen of Day I, passes the crown to Filomena. This transition marks the beginning of a more structured approach to storytelling, as Filomena is the first to prescribe a specific theme for the company’s tales.

The Theme of Fortuna

The central theme of Day II is Fortuna (Fortune). In medieval thought, Fortune was not merely a concept of chance but was personified as a goddess.

  • The Wheel of Fortune: Drawing from Boethius's The Consolation of Philosophy (c. 524), Fortune is depicted as a goddess who turns a great wheel. This wheel raises some individuals to prosperity while casting others down into ruin.
  • The Nature of the Tales: The stories told under Filomena’s rule focus on characters who, after suffering "divers misadventures," eventually reach a "happy issue" or ending that they had not expected.
  • Narrative Elements: Because of the focus on reversal and adventure, the tales of Day II are characterized by:
    • Elaborate plots and significant length.
    • Shipwrecks and midnight escapades.
    • The use of disguises and mistaken identities.
    • A focus on luck, wit, and human endurance in the face of uncontrollable forces.

The Cornice and Setting

The cornice, or frame story, undergoes a subtle but significant change at the start of Day II. Filomena leads the company from the gardens of the first villa to a new estate. This physical movement serves as a literary device:

  • Mirroring Themes: The transition between villas mirrors the themes of movement, discovery, and the unpredictable nature of the stories being told.
  • Morning Rituals: The day begins with the company rising at dawn, singing with the birds, and wandering through "dewy meads" to weave garlands of flowers. They follow a routine of breakfasting in the shade, dancing, and resting until noon before the storytelling begins.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who succeeds Pampinea as the ruler for the second day?

Answer: Filomena.

2. What is the specific theme prescribed for the stories of Day II?

Answer: Tales of individuals who, after various misadventures, achieve an unexpected happy ending.

3. Which historical text is credited with the ubiquitous medieval image of the "Wheel of Fortune"?

Answer: Boethius's The Consolation of Philosophy (c. 524).

4. How does the physical setting change at the beginning of Day II?

Answer: The company moves from the first villa to a new estate.

5. Who is the first person called upon by the Queen to tell a story on the second day?

Answer: Neifile.

6. What qualities allow characters in Day II to survive the "misadventures" of Fortune?

Answer: Luck, wit, or simple endurance.

7. Describe the narrative style of the tales in Day II compared to Day I.

Answer: They are among the longest and most elaborately plotted tales, featuring high-adventure elements like shipwrecks and mistaken identities.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Role of the Goddess Fortuna

Analyze the medieval personification of Fortune as a goddess with a wheel. Based on the introductory text, how does this imagery inform the structure of the stories told on Day II? Discuss how the concept of "reversal" serves as the primary engine for these plots.

2. The Mirroring Effect of the Cornice

Explore the significance of the company’s move to a new estate. How does Boccaccio use the physical movements of the ten storytellers to reinforce the thematic content of their tales? Discuss the "subtle element of the cornice" that links the setting to the stories of adventure and discovery.

3. Wit and Endurance vs. Fate

While the characters of Day II are "tossed about by forces beyond their control," the text suggests they reach happy endings through "luck, wit, or simple endurance." Compare the role of external "Fortune" with the role of internal human agency in navigating the misadventures described in the tales.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Boethius The author of The Consolation of Philosophy, whose work popularized the image of the Wheel of Fortune in medieval culture.
Cornice The "frame" or outer narrative of The Decameron involving the ten young people who flee the plague.
Dewy Meads Wet meadows; part of the idyllic setting where the company spends their morning before storytelling.
Divers Misadventures Various unfortunate events or hardships experienced by characters before their fortunes change.
Filomena The queen of Day II who chooses the theme of Fortune.
Fortuna The medieval personification of Fortune, often depicted as a goddess who controls the unpredictable shifts in human life.
Happy Issue A positive or successful conclusion to a series of events.
Neifile The first storyteller of Day II, chosen by Queen Filomena.
Pampinea The queen of Day I who passes the laurel wreath (the crown) to Filomena.
The Wheel of Fortune A ubiquitous medieval symbol representing the rise and fall of mortals as governed by the goddess Fortune.
