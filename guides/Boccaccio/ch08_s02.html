Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day VII

This study guide provides an overview of the thematic and literary elements found in the introduction to the seventh day of Boccaccio’s Decameron. It examines the shift in narrative leadership, the specific themes of domestic deception, and the atmospheric setting of the Ladies’ Vale.

Key Concepts and Narrative Framework

The Role of Dioneo

Day VII marks the beginning of the reign of Dioneo as king. Dioneo is characterized as the most playful and irreverent of the ten narrators. Throughout the Decameron, he holds a "standing privilege": he always tells the final story of each day and is permitted to choose any subject he desires, regardless of the theme assigned by that day’s king or queen. His appointment as king for Day VII signals a shift toward a more relaxed, merry, and provocative atmosphere where propriety is tested through the storytelling game.

The Central Theme: Domestic Deception

The theme of Day VII focuses on "the tricks wives play on their husbands." These deceptions are performed for two primary motivations:

  • For Love: Acting out of genuine passion.
  • For Preservation: Acting out of self-interest or necessity.

These stories center on the beffa—a practical joke or elaborate deception. While these tales draw on the medieval tradition of the fabliaux, Boccaccio introduces psychological realism. He frequently portrays the husband as deserving of deception due to traits such as jealousy, foolishness, or inattentiveness, thereby shifting the audience's sympathy toward the wives.

The Setting: The Ladies’ Vale

The company moves to a new location to begin the seventh day. The environment is described with vivid sensory details:

  • Atmosphere: The day begins at dawn, with the star Lucifer still glowing in the sky as the group departs.
  • Nature: The valley is filled with singing nightingales and other birds that seem to "swell the chorus" of the human company.
  • Amenities: The seneschal prepares the area with tables under a "living canopy of trees" beside a lake filled with shoals of darting fish.
  • Leisure: Before the storytelling begins, the company enjoys wine, comfits, and singing. The seneschal provides beds equipped with "French coverlets" for those wishing to rest after breakfast.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What unique privilege does Dioneo possess among the ten narrators? Dioneo always tells the last tale of the day and has the freedom to choose any subject he likes, regardless of the day’s assigned theme.

2. According to the day’s rubric, what are the two main reasons wives deceive their husbands? Wives act either "for love" or for their "own preservation."

3. How does Boccaccio’s portrayal of the "clever wife" differ from traditional medieval verse tales? Boccaccio distinguishes his work through psychological realism and a clear sympathy for the wives, often depicting the husbands as foolish or jealous individuals who deserve to be outwitted.

4. What is a beffa? A beffa is a practical joke, trick, or elaborate deception that serves as a central comic motif in the Decameron.

5. Describe the scene at the Ladies’ Vale before the storytelling begins. The company arrives at dawn, accompanied by birdsong. They eat breakfast under trees by a lake, watch fish, sing, and some take naps on beds furnished with French coverlets before the storytelling starts on carpets spread on the sward.

6. Who is the first person to tell a story on Day VII? Emilia is the first to begin the storytelling at the king’s request.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Subversion of the Exemplum

In the medieval tradition, the exemplum was a short story used in sermons to teach a moral lesson. Discuss how Boccaccio uses and subverts this form in Day VII. Based on the source context, how does the shift from moral instruction to the beffa change the purpose of the narrative?

2. Dioneo as a Narrative Catalyst

Analyze the selection of Dioneo as king for a day focused on domestic tricks and deceptions. How does his reputation as "irreverent" and "playful" influence the tone of the day? Consider how the environment of the Ladies’ Vale and the relaxation of propriety serve Dioneo’s specific storytelling style.

3. Sympathy and Justice in Domestic Deception

The text suggests that the husbands in Day VII are often "deceived but deserve to be deceived." Explore the criteria Boccaccio uses to justify these deceptions. To what extent does the source material frame these tricks as a form of social or domestic justice for the wives?

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Beffa A practical joke, trick, or elaborate deception; a central comic motif ranging from harmless fun to calculated cruelty.
Blithe Happy, carefree, or showing a casual indifference.
Dioneo The narrator known for being playful and irreverent; he holds the privilege of storytelling last and choosing his own themes.
Exemplum (Plural: Exempla) A short illustrative story used in medieval sermons to teach a moral lesson; Boccaccio often subverts this form.
Fabliaux Short, humorous, and often bawdy verse tales popular in medieval France that influenced the Decameron.
Lucifer The name used in the text to refer to the morning star present in the whitening dawn.
Seneschal The official responsible for the company’s logistics, baggage-train, and the preparation of the Ladies’ Vale.
Sward An expanse of short grass where the company sits to tell their stories.
