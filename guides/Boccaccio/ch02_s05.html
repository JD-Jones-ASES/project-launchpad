Study Guide: The Decameron, Day I, Novel 9 (The King of Cyprus and the Lady of Gascony)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the ninth tale of the first day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. Narrated by Elissa, this story is noted for its extreme brevity and its focus on the transformative power of wit.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Power of the Motto

In the context of the Decameron, a "motto" is a pointed, witty remark or bon mot. It serves as a social weapon capable of achieving what physical force or humble supplication cannot. In this tale, a single sentence from a wronged woman acts as a catalyst for profound personal and political change, demonstrating Boccaccio’s belief in the potency of well-aimed language.

2. Speculum Principis (Mirror for Princes)

The tale functions as a miniature version of the speculum principis, a medieval literary genre intended to instruct rulers on proper conduct and morality. While traditional works in this genre are often lengthy and moralizing, Boccaccio achieves the same instructional end—the reformation of a king—through a brief, sharp interaction.

3. Indolence vs. Justice

The King of Cyprus is initially characterized as "faineant" (lazy/spiritless) and "apathetic." He is so "reprehensibly tame" that he ignores not only the wrongs done to his subjects but also the insults directed at himself. The transition from this state of "sluggish" indifference to becoming a "rigorous" administrator of justice is the central arc of the narrative.

4. The Geography of the Tale

The story broadens the geographical reach of the Decameron by moving the setting beyond Italy. It involves a lady from Gascony (a region in southwestern France under English control in the 14th century) who travels to Jerusalem on a pilgrimage to the Holy Sepulchre before landing in Cyprus during the reign of its first king, following the conquest by Godfrey de Bouillon.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who narrates the ninth tale of Day I?

  • Answer: The tale is told by Elissa, who is the final narrator of the day.

2. What happens to the lady of Gascony on her return from Jerusalem?

  • Answer: While in Cyprus, she suffers "brutal outrage" at the hands of "certain ruffians."

3. Why is the lady initially discouraged from seeking justice from the King of Cyprus?

  • Answer: She is told the King is so spiritless and indolent that he refuses to avenge affronts to others and even endures insults to himself with "reprehensible tameness."

4. Does the lady approach the king expecting her wrong to be redressed?

  • Answer: No. Despairing of redress, she approaches him specifically to "make the king sensible of his baseness" as a way to alleviate her own grief.

5. What is the specific "motto" or remark the lady delivers to the king?

  • Answer: She asks the king to teach her how he suffers the wrongs done to him with such patience, so that she might learn to endure her own—jokingly adding that she would gladly transfer her burden to him since he is so "well fitted" to bear them.

6. What was the immediate result of the lady’s remark?

  • Answer: The king was aroused "as it were from sleep." He immediately redressed the lady's wrong and became a rigorous enforcer of justice from that point forward.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Language as a Social Weapon: Analyze how Boccaccio uses the "motto" in this tale to challenge traditional power dynamics. How does the lady’s use of wit succeed where "force and supplication" might have failed?
  2. Gender and Authority: Explore why Boccaccio might have chosen to give the power of the transformative "motto" to a woman in this specific story. Consider the lady's status as a pilgrim and a victim, and how those roles contrast with her eventual influence over a monarch.
  3. The Brevity of Virtue: Elissa notes that the tale is "of the briefest" and that its brevity is part of its point. Discuss how the structure of the story reflects the efficiency of the "motto" itself. Does the short length enhance or detract from the moral weight of the King’s reformation?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Bon Mot A witty remark or "motto"; used in the text as a form of social weapon to shame or correct others.
Faineant Spiritless, idle, or lazy; used to describe the King of Cyprus’s initial state of governance.
Gascony A region in southwestern France; the home of the lady who challenges the king.
Godfrey de Bouillon The leader mentioned in the text as having conquered the Holy Land, setting the historical timeframe for the tale.
Motto In the Italian context of the Decameron, a pointed, witty remark capable of shaming or delighting the listener.
Obdurate Stubborn or resistant to change; used to describe men who are often reclaimed by casual words rather than formal punishment.
Speculum Principis "Mirror for princes"; a medieval genre of literature intended to teach rulers how to govern justly.
The Holy Sepulchre The site in Jerusalem that was the destination of the lady of Gascony's pilgrimage.
