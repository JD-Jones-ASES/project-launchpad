# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s The Decameron, Day IX, Novel 6

This study guide explores the sixth tale of the ninth day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *The Decameron*. Narrated by Panfilo, this story is a seminal example of a "bed-trick" narrative—a comedic farce built upon darkness, spatial confusion, and mistaken identity. Set in a provincial inn, the tale demonstrates Boccaccio’s mastery of timing and the realistic constraints of medieval life.

---

### Key Concepts and Themes

#### 1. The Bed-Trick Device
The "bed-trick" is one of the oldest plot devices in Western literature. It involves characters sleeping with the wrong partners due to darkness or deception. The source context notes its appearance in:
*   **The Bible:** The story of Jacob and Leah in Genesis 29.
*   **Classical Comedy:** Ancient Greek and Roman theatrical traditions.
*   **Medieval Fabliaux:** Short, humorous, and often ribald medieval verse tales.

#### 2. Spatial Choreography
The success of the tale depends on the precise physical layout of the room. Boccaccio functions like a stage director, detailing the positions of three beds, a door, and a mobile infant’s cradle. The movement of the cradle serves as the "anchor" for the characters’ spatial orientation; when the cradle is moved, the characters’ internal maps of the room fail, leading to the chain of misunderstandings.

#### 3. Medieval Privacy and Realism
The story highlights a specific historical reality: privacy was a luxury of the wealthy. In medieval inns, it was common for travelers and the host’s family to share a single room for sleeping. This lack of private space is the essential catalyst for the plot’s "comedy of bodies."

#### 4. The "Discreet Woman"
A central theme is the "quick apprehension" of the host's wife. Despite being a victim of the confusion, her ability to recognize the danger of a scandal and invent a face-saving lie (attributing the events to Pinuccio’s "dreaming") restores order and protects the reputations of all involved.

### Plot Summary and Mechanics of Confusion

The story follows two young Florentines, **Pinuccio** and **Adriano**, who travel to the plain of Mugnone to stay at an inn. Pinuccio is in love with the host’s daughter, **Niccolosa**.

**The Room Configuration:**
*   **Bed 1:** Pinuccio and Adriano.
*   **Bed 2:** Niccolosa (the daughter).
*   **Bed 3:** The Host and his Wife, with a **baby cradle** placed at the foot/side.

**The Chain of Errors:**
1.  **The Tryst:** Pinuccio steals away to Bed 2 to be with Niccolosa.
2.  **The Disturbance:** A cat knocks something over, and the wife gets up to investigate in the dark.
3.  **The Cradle Move:** Adriano gets up to "answer the call of nature." Finding the cradle in his path, he moves it next to his own bed (Bed 1).
4.  **The First Mistake:** The wife returns in the dark. She feels for the cradle to find her husband's bed. Finding it next to Bed 1, she climbs in and sleeps with Adriano, thinking he is her husband.
5.  **The Second Mistake:** Pinuccio leaves Niccolosa to return to his bed. He finds the cradle where it *used* to be (by the host's bed) and assumes that is his own bed. He climbs into Bed 3 and whispers a detailed account of his sexual encounter with Niccolosa to the person he thinks is Adriano—but is actually the host.
6.  **The Resolution:** The host reacts with rage. The wife, realizing she is in bed with Adriano, quickly moves the cradle back to her daughter’s bed and pretends she has been with Niccolosa all night, claiming Pinuccio is merely sleepwalking and dreaming aloud.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Who is the narrator of this tale?**
*Answer:* Panfilo.

**2. Where does the story take place?**
*Answer:* At an inn in the plain of Mugnone, near Florence/Romagna.

**3. Why do Pinuccio and Adriano use valises filled with straw?**
*Answer:* To maintain the ruse that they are weary travelers who were forced to stop at the inn because they could not reach Florence before nightfall.

**4. What specific object is moved that causes the wife to enter the wrong bed?**
*Answer:* The cradle containing the host’s infant son.

**5. How does the host’s wife explain Pinuccio’s confession to her husband?**
*Answer:* She claims Pinuccio is dreaming and walking in his sleep due to drinking too much the night before.

**6. What is the daughter's name in the story?**
*Answer:* Niccolosa.

**7. How does Adriano assist in the deception once the host becomes angry?**
*Answer:* He pretends that Pinuccio has a well-known habit of "walking about at night" and relating his dreams as truth, urging him to return to their bed.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Architecture of Farce:** Analyze how Boccaccio uses the physical constraints of the setting to drive the plot. How does the "precision of a stage director" contribute to the humor of the story?
2.  **Gender and Agency:** Compare the roles of the host’s wife and daughter. While the men (Pinuccio and Adriano) initiate the action, it is the wife’s "quick apprehension" that resolves the conflict. Discuss how Boccaccio portrays her intelligence and discretion.
3.  **Morality vs. Comedy:** The text describes the tale as "pure farce" with "no moral lesson." Do you agree with this assessment, or does the story offer a subtle commentary on social class, reputation, or the nature of medieval marriage?
4.  **The Bed-Trick Tradition:** Research the historical uses of the bed-trick in literature mentioned in the text (e.g., Jacob and Leah). How does Boccaccio’s "Italian spin" on this trope differ from its more serious or tragic uses in other contexts?

### Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Bed-trick:** A literary device involving a sexual encounter between two people where one or both are mistaken about the identity of their partner.
*   **Comport:** To behave or conduct oneself in a specific manner (e.g., Niccolosa studied how to comport herself debonairly).
*   **Debonairly:** In a cheerful, confident, and stylish manner.
*   **Discreet:** Careful and circumspect in one's speech or actions, especially in order to avoid causing offense or to gain an advantage.
*   **Fabliaux:** A metrical tale, typically a short ribald one, of a type found in early French poetry.
*   **Farce:** A comic dramatic work using buffoonery and horseplay and typically including crude characterization and ludicrously improbable situations.
*   **Gallant:** A dashing, fashionable young man; often used to describe Pinuccio.
*   **Plain of Mugnone:** The geographical setting of the inn, located near Florence.
*   **Spatial Choreography:** The planned movement of characters within a defined physical space to achieve a specific narrative or comedic effect.
*   **Valises:** Small traveling bags or suitcases.
