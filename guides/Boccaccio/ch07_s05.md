# Study Guide: The Decameron, Day VI, Novel 9

This study guide examines the ninth tale of the sixth day of Boccaccio's *Decameron*, as told by Elissa. The narrative focuses on a witty encounter between the Florentine philosopher-poet Guido Cavalcanti and a group of local socialites led by Messer Betto Brunelleschi.

## Key Concepts and Narrative Context

### Historical Context: Florentine Social Companies
The story opens with a reflection on the lost customs of Florence. In former times, the city was characterized by "excellent and commendable" customs that have since been eroded by greed. These included:
*   **Companies of Gentlemen:** Small, exclusive groups of residents who shared expenses and took turns entertaining one another.
*   **Social Duties:** These groups would honor visiting dignitaries and fellow citizens, ride through the city on notable days, and participate in tilts (jousting) during festivals or celebrations of victory.

### Character Profiles

| Character | Role & Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Guido Cavalcanti** | A renowned Florentine poet, philosopher, and logician (c. 1255–1300). He was Dante Alighieri's closest friend. Known for being wealthy, gallant, and a master of discourse, he was also famously aloof and dedicated to philosophical speculation. |
| **Betto Brunelleschi** | A historical Florentine nobleman and the leader of a fashionable social circle. He is portrayed as having a "subtle and discerning wit," being the only one in his group capable of deciphering Cavalcanti’s wordplay. |
| **Elissa** | The queen of the Sixth Day and the narrator of this specific tale. |

### The Central Conflict
Messer Betto Brunelleschi’s company frequently attempted to recruit Guido Cavalcanti into their social circle. They valued his wealth and gallantry but cared little for his logic or philosophy. Because Cavalcanti often remained "estranged from men" due to his studies, the common public (the "vulgar") rumored that he was an Epicurean seeking to prove the non-existence of God. 

The climax of the story occurs when the company corners Cavalcanti at the church of San Giovanni, attempting to "flout" or mock him for his intellectualism and his refusal to join their company.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Where specifically does the encounter between Cavalcanti and the company take place?**
    The encounter occurs among the marble tombs (ancient Roman sarcophagi) located near the church of San Giovanni (the Baptistery) and the columns of porphyry.

2.  **According to the text, why did the company want Cavalcanti to join them?**
    They sought him out because he was "without a peer for gallantry and courtesy," an excellent speaker, rich, and highly capable in all matters belonging to a gentleman.

3.  **What was the "vulgar" opinion regarding Cavalcanti’s philosophical speculations?**
    They believed he was an Epicurean and that his goal was to find logical proof that God did not exist.

4.  **What physical action did Cavalcanti take to escape the group?**
    He laid his hand on a large marble tomb and, being very "nimble," vaulted over it to move away from the riders.

5.  **What was the literal response Cavalcanti gave to the men who cornered him?**
    "Gentlemen, you may say to me what you please in your own house."

6.  **How did the majority of the company initially react to Cavalcanti’s remark?**
    Most thought he had "taken leave of his wits," believing the remark was nonsense since the piazza was public ground common to all citizens.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Houses of the Dead: Analyzing Cavalcanti's Metaphor
In the tale, Betto Brunelleschi interprets Cavalcanti’s "quip" as a "shrewd reprimand." Analyze the double meaning of the word "house" in this context. How does Cavalcanti use the physical presence of the tombs to comment on the intellectual and spiritual state of the men confronting him? Contrast the "life of the mind" (represented by Cavalcanti) with the "life of idle pleasure" (represented by the company).

### 2. The Role of the Wit in Social Hierarchy
The tale is presented as a celebration of "Quick Wit and Ready Speech." Discuss how wit serves as a tool for social navigation and defense in this story. Compare the initial attempt of the company to "flout" Cavalcanti with the final result, where the company is "abashed." What does this suggest about the power of language versus the power of social status or physical numbers?

### 3. Historical and Literary Significance of Cavalcanti
Based on the text and the provided notes, evaluate why Boccaccio might have chosen Guido Cavalcanti as the protagonist for this specific tale. Consider his historical reputation as a philosopher, his relationship with Dante, and his eventual fate (dying of malaria in exile). How does his "dense and philosophical" nature contribute to the gravity of the story’s "pregnant" quip?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Epicurean:** A follower of the philosophy of Epicurus; in the context of this tale, it refers to the belief (often attributed to Cavalcanti by the public) that the soul is mortal and God does not exist.
*   **Flout:** To mock, insult, or treat with contemptuous disregard.
*   **Logician:** A person skilled in the art of logic and formal reasoning.
*   **Messer:** A title of respect for a gentleman or nobleman, similar to "Sir."
*   **Natural Philosopher:** An early term for a scientist or someone who studies the physical laws of the universe.
*   **Piazza:** A public square or open space in an Italian city, typically the center of civic life.
*   **San Giovanni:** The Baptistery of Florence, a significant religious and historical building.
*   **Santa Reparata:** An ancient cathedral in Florence (the site where the Duomo now stands); the piazza mentioned in the text is associated with this location.
*   **Speculation:** Deep intellectual contemplation or theoretical reasoning.
*   **Vaulted:** To leap or spring over an obstacle while supporting oneself with the hands.
