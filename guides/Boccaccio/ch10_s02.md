# Study Guide: The Decameron – Day the Ninth Introduction

This study guide provides an overview of the introduction to Day the Ninth of Boccaccio's *The Decameron*. It analyzes the transition from previous thematic cycles to the "Free Theme" format, the leadership of Emilia, and the symbolic elements of the frame narrative.

---

### Key Concepts

#### The Return to Free Theme
Day the Ninth marks a significant structural shift in the storytelling rhythm. It is one of only two days—the other being Day I—where the storytellers are not bound by a prescribed theme. This "open format" allows for a varied miscellany of subjects.

#### Shift in Tone and Complexity
After the intense and often harsh cycles of Day VII (marital trickery) and Day VIII (elaborate revenge), the company enters a "looser, lighter mode." While Day VIII pushed the *beffa* (practical joke) toward cruelty, Day IX pulls it back toward laughter. The tales are described as being:
*   **Brief and Anecdotal:** Many stories are short, serving as a "breathing space" before the grand finale of Day X.
*   **Comically Varied:** The subjects revisit familiar themes—clerical foolishness, bedroom confusion, and practical jokes—but with a gentler touch.

#### The Leadership of Emilia
Emilia serves as the queen for Day IX. Her authority is characterized as "gentle," setting a tone of comic variety rather than rigid thematic unity. Under her reign, the company relaxes, reflecting the transition from the previous days' heavy subject matter.

#### The Frame Narrative and the Plague
Boccaccio uses the introduction to mark the passage of time. While the plague continues to rage in Florence, the *brigata* (the company of storytellers) has established its own sense of order through the rhythm of storytelling. This order is contrasted with the chaos of the outside world, evidenced by the behavior of wild animals (goats and stags) that no longer fear hunters, as if aware that the pestilence has halted the hunt.

### Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Which other day in the *Decameron* shares Day IX's "Free Theme" format?**
*Answer:* Day I.

**2. Who is the reigning queen of Day IX?**
*Answer:* Emilia.

**3. How does the tone of the *beffa* change from Day VIII to Day IX?**
*Answer:* In Day VIII, the *beffa* was pushed toward cruelty; in Day IX, it is pulled back toward laughter and handled with a lighter touch.

**4. What observation did onlookers make about the company as they returned to the palace garlanded with oak leaves?**
*Answer:* Onlookers might have said, "Either shall death not vanquish these, or they will meet it with a light heart."

**5. How many "ditties" (songs) were performed before the company went to the table for their meal?**
*Answer:* Six gay ditties.

**6. Who was chosen by the queen to begin the storytelling for the day?**
*Answer:* Filomena.

**7. Why do the wild creatures in the copse show no fear of the company?**
*Answer:* The text suggests they act as if they know that, during the time of pestilence, they have nothing to fear from hunters.

### Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

**1. The Order of the Frame Narrative vs. the Chaos of the Plague**
Examine how Boccaccio uses the daily rituals of the *brigata*—the singing, walking, dining, and storytelling—to create a "sheltered world." How does this internal order serve as a psychological or social counterpoint to the "pestilence" mentioned in the text?

**2. Characterizing Emilia’s Reign**
Analyze the description of Emilia’s "gentle authority." How does her leadership style influence the selection and tone of the stories on Day IX, and how does this contrast with the more "intense cycles" of previous days?

**3. Nature and Symbolism in the Introduction**
Discuss the significance of the company’s interaction with nature in the copse. Consider the description of the "eighth heaven," the specific color of the sky (*cilestro*), and the "tame" wild animals. What do these elements suggest about the state of the world and the company’s place within it?

### Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Beffa** | A practical joke; a central element of many *Decameron* tales, particularly in Days VII, VIII, and IX. |
| **Brigata** | The company of ten young people (seven women and three men) who have fled Florence to tell stories. |
| **Cilestro** | An Italian term for the intense luminosity of the sky when the sun is well up; it has no exact English equivalent but refers to a light, bright blue. |
| **Eighth Heaven** | In the Ptolemaic system, the region of the fixed stars; mentioned to describe the transition of light at dawn. |
| **Free Theme** | A storytelling day without a prescribed subject, allowing storytellers to choose their topics at will. |
| **Luminaries** | Celestial bodies (like the sun) that provide light. |
| **Pestilence** | A reference to the Black Death (plague) occurring in Florence, which serves as the backdrop for the frame narrative. |
| **Seneschal** | An officer in a medieval or Renaissance household in charge of domestic arrangements and the dining hall. |
