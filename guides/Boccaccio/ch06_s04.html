Study Guide: Federigo’s Falcon (The Decameron, Day V, Novel 9)

This study guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the ninth tale of the fifth day from Giovanni Boccaccio's The Decameron. Narrated by Fiammetta, the story of Federigo degli Alberighi is regarded as a masterpiece of European literature, distilling the essence of courtly love and sacrifice.

Key Concepts and Narrative Themes

1. The Nature of Courtly Love

The story explores the traditional medieval concept of courtly love, where a nobleman (Federigo) performs "feats of arms," "jousts," and "scatters largess" to win the affection of a virtuous lady (Monna Giovanna). The narrative transforms this concept by showing that true nobility is found in sacrifice and character rather than the mere display of wealth.

2. The Role of Fortune

Fiammetta introduces the tale by discussing the "guidance of Fortune," which often scatters gifts "without rule or measure." The characters must navigate the "adverse fortune" of poverty and illness. Ultimately, the story suggests that while Fortune may take away material wealth, it cannot diminish "nobleness of temper."

3. Sacrifice and Symbolism

The falcon serves as the central symbol of the story. It represents:

  • Federigo’s Identity: It is his last link to his noble past and his sole remaining "solace."
  • Absolute Devotion: By killing the bird to feed Monna Giovanna, Federigo demonstrates a level of devotion that transcends material value.
  • The Irony of Fate: Federigo destroys the very thing Monna Giovanna came to ask for in his attempt to honor her visit.

4. Merit vs. Wealth

The resolution of the story hinges on Monna Giovanna’s realization that Federigo’s "high desert" and "magnificent generosity" are more valuable than financial status. Her famous declaration—"I had rather have a man without wealth than wealth without a man"—underscores the theme that true merit is internal.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Question Answer
Who is the original source of the story according to the text? Coppo di Borghese Domenichi, a respected Florentine man of noble lineage and great memory.
How did Federigo lose his vast fortune? He spent it courting Monna Giovanna through jousts, entertainments, and "scattering largess."
Where did Federigo retire after being reduced to poverty? To a small estate at Campi, where he lived "very straitly."
What was Federigo’s "last resource" for a meal? His "brave falcon," which he killed and roasted because he had no money or goods to pledge for a proper meal.
Why did Monna Giovanna’s son want the falcon? He believed that having the bird would make him well from his sickness.
How did Monna Giovanna react when she learned the falcon was dead? She first chided him for killing such a bird for a breakfast, but then inwardly commended his magnanimity.
What happened to Monna Giovanna’s son? He "departed this life" not many days after his mother’s visit to Federigo.
Why did Monna Giovanna eventually marry Federigo? She was pressured by her brothers to remarry and chose him because of his "high desert" and generosity, despite his poverty.
How did the brothers react to her choice? They derided her for wanting a man "who has not a thing in the world."
How did Federigo’s lifestyle change after the marriage? He became very wealthy again and lived "keeping more exact accounts" to the end of his days.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Economy of Narrative: Fiammetta’s tale is praised for its "extraordinary economy." Analyze how Boccaccio uses the falcon to connect the two primary plot lines: Federigo’s quest for Monna Giovanna’s love and Monna Giovanna’s quest to save her son. How does the bird’s death resolve both conflicts simultaneously?

  2. The Evolution of Federigo: Compare Federigo’s "extravagance" at the beginning of the story with his behavior at the end. Does his marriage to Monna Giovanna represent a victory for courtly love, or is it a pragmatic shift toward the "exact accounts" of the rising merchant class?

  3. Monna Giovanna’s Agency: Although Monna Giovanna is the object of Federigo’s affection, she makes several pivotal decisions throughout the story. Evaluate her character in the context of medieval social expectations, focusing on her decision to visit Federigo and her eventual choice of a husband based on merit rather than wealth.

  4. The Paradox of Hospitality: Federigo’s desire to be a "good host" leads to the destruction of the very object his guest desires. Discuss how the rules of "courtesy" and "hospitality" drive the tragic irony of the story and whether Federigo’s choice was a "virtuous" one.

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Concinnity: Elegance or harmony in style, specifically regarding Coppo di Borghese Domenichi’s speech.
  • Guerdons: Rewards or compensations; in this context, the favors or "charms" bestowed by a lady.
  • Hied: To go quickly or hasten (e.g., "hied him to her with all speed").
  • Husbandman: A person who tends the land; a farmer or worker on an estate.
  • Largess: Generous spending or the giving of gifts, often used by nobles to show status.
  • Mien: A person's look or manner, indicating their character or mood.
  • Monna: A contraction of Madonna (“my lady”); the standard respectful address for a married woman in medieval Italy.
  • Straitly: In a restricted or meager manner; living with very little money.
  • Trencher: A wooden plate or platter on which food is served.
  • Wont: Accustomed to or in the habit of doing something.
