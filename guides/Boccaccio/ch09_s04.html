Study Guide: The Scholar’s Revenge (Decameron, Day VIII, Novel 7)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the seventh tale of the eighth day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. Told by Pampinea, this narrative is distinguished as the longest and one of the most disturbing tales in the collection, exploring the dark side of intelligence (ingegno) when used for ruthless retribution.

1. Narrative Overview and Key Themes

The Seasonal Mirror

The tale is meticulously structured around a seasonal inversion. The original offense occurs in the dead of winter, while the retribution takes place at the height of summer. This symmetry is designed to repay "debt" with literal physical correspondence.

  • The Winter Offense: Elena lures the scholar, Rinieri, to wait in a snow-covered courtyard for a rendezvous that never happens. He nearly dies from the cold while she and her lover mock him.
  • The Summer Retribution: Rinieri tricks Elena into standing naked on a tower roof under a July sun. She suffers from extreme dehydration, sunstroke, and insect stings, nearly dying from heat and exposure.

Central Themes

  • The Limits of the Beffa: While many tales in the Decameron use the beffa (trick) for comedy, this story pushes trickery to a point of extreme psychological and physical cruelty.
  • Justice vs. Proportionality: The story forces a confrontation with medieval concepts of vendetta (just vengeance) and excessive cruelty. While the narrator, Pampinea, suggests the retribution is "just," the text highlights the scholar’s "disproportionate" response.
  • The Danger of Ingegno: Intelligence and wit are usually celebrated in the Decameron as tools for love or survival. Here, Boccaccio explores ingegno placed in the service of hatred.
  • The Power of the Pen: The scholar explicitly threatens Elena with his ability to ruin her reputation through writing, asserting that the "might of the pen is greater far than they suppose."

2. Key Characters and Entities

Entity Description and Role
Rinieri (The Scholar) A Florentine nobleman educated in Paris. He represents the intellectual type—learned, proud, and socially awkward. His love for Elena turns into "bitter and remorseless enmity."
Elena A beautiful, high-spirited Florentine widow. She is portrayed as vain and manipulative, initially viewing Rinieri as a "gander" to be caught to flatter her own charms.
The Maid Elena's confidante who facilitates both the original trick and the scholar’s revenge. She ultimately suffers a broken thigh during the escape from the tower.
The Lover Elena’s actual romantic interest. His jealousy prompts Elena to play the initial cruel trick on Rinieri to prove her loyalty.
Pampinea The narrator of the tale. She frames the story as a cautionary lesson for women not to "flout" (mock) men, especially scholars.

3. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does the source suggest the scholar is often referred to only by his profession rather than his name? The anonymity suggests he represents a "type"—the intellectual consumed by revenge—rather than just an individual.
  2. What physical toll does the first trick take on Rinieri? He nearly dies of cold, his teeth chatter so hard he sounds like a stork, and he loses the use of his limbs for a time, requiring "drastic remedies" from physicians.
  3. How does Rinieri convince Elena to climb the tower naked? He claims to be an expert in necromancy and tells her that a specific ritual involving an image, a flowing stream, and standing naked on a tower will lure her deserting lover back to her.
  4. How does the scholar respond when Elena begs for a cup of water on the tower? He refuses, telling her she shall have as much water from him as he had fire from her to mitigate his cold during the winter night.
  5. What is the "cover story" Elena uses to explain her injuries to her family in Florence? She invents a story claiming her and her maid's injuries were the result of the "enchantments of evil spirits."
  6. What does the scholar argue is the difference between young lovers and "more mature" men? He argues that while young men are "ruddier" and better dancers, they are inconstant and "lust after as many as he sets eyes on," whereas mature men are more experienced and appreciative.

4. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

I. The Ethical Gap in Narration

The narrator, Pampinea, begins by stating that the retribution Elena suffered was "but just." However, the source context notes a "gap between the narrator's apparent approval and the tale's deeper implications." Analyze whether the scholar’s revenge is truly a "just retribution" or if it is "excessive cruelty." Use specific details of the physical suffering described to support your argument.

II. The Symbolic Use of Nature

Boccaccio uses the environment (snow, sun, insects, the river Arno) as active participants in the plot. Discuss how the seasonal inversion functions as a structural device in the story. How does the "quality" of the cold in winter compare to the "quality" of the heat in summer as described by both the scholar and Elena?

III. The Scholar’s Rhetoric

During the climax of the story, the scholar delivers a long speech justifying his actions. He claims that as a man of "spirit" and learning, his life is worth more than "a hundred thousand" like Elena. Examine the scholar’s defense of his own ego and social status. Does his rhetorical training make his cruelty more or less defensible in the context of the story?

5. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Beffa: A trick or prank, often elaborate, played upon a character; a core narrative device in the Decameron.
  • Cornemuse: A type of bagpipe; mentioned by the lady when mocking the scholar's chattering teeth.
  • Ingegno: Intelligence, cleverness, or wit. In this tale, it is used for destruction rather than creation.
  • Indite: To write or compose, particularly formal documents or literary works.
  • Necromancy: Black magic involving communication with the dead or spirits; the "lore" Rinieri pretends to use to trick Elena.
  • Orison: A prayer; specifically the "gibberish" written by the scholar for Elena to recite on the tower.
  • Tierce/None/Vespers: Traditional markers of time based on canonical hours (Tierce is morning, None is mid-afternoon, Vespers is evening).
  • Vendetta: The medieval concept of just vengeance or blood-feud, governed by specific moral and legal distinctions.
  • Withies: Flexible branches (like willow) used for binding or tying; used by the husbandman to repair the ladder.
