Study Guide: The Decameron, Day Four – Introduction and Author’s Preface

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the transition into Day Four of Boccaccio’s Decameron. It examines the author’s direct intervention in the narrative, the philosophical defense of his literary project, and the thematic shift toward tragedy under the reign of Filostrato.

I. Key Concepts and Narrative Analysis

The Author’s Intervention

At the beginning of Day Four, Boccaccio breaks the established frame narrative. Rather than allowing the ten storytellers to proceed immediately, he speaks in his own voice to address his critics. This move is a significant example of early "metafiction," where the author acknowledges the fictionality of the work and defends its purpose and style.

The Five Primary Criticisms

Boccaccio identifies specific attacks leveled against him by detractors after the circulation of the first parts of the Decameron:

  1. Lasciviousness: Critics alleged he was too fond of women and took too much pleasure in ministering to their gratification.
  2. Age Inappropriateness: Detractors argued that a man of his age should not be "discoursing of women" or seeking to please them.
  3. Neglect of High Art: Some suggested he should stay on "Parnassus with the Muses" rather than engaging in "vain dalliance" with women.
  4. Financial Practicality: Critics advised him to pursue "daily bread" and practical labor rather than wasting time on "idle toys" and fables.
  5. Historical Inaccuracy: Some claimed the events in his stories did not happen as he described them.

The Parable of the Goslings (Papere)

To counter the claim that his focus on women is a corruption, Boccaccio tells an incomplete story about Filippo Balducci.

  • The Experiment: After his wife's death, Filippo raises his son in total isolation in a cell on Monte Asinaio, exposing him only to religious teachings and "holy orisons."
  • The Encounter: When the son is eighteen, they visit Florence. Despite his father’s attempts to dismiss a group of beautiful women as "naughty things" called "goslings" (papere), the boy is instantly captivated.
  • The Moral: Boccaccio argues that love and attraction are "natural affection." If a boy raised in total ignorance of women prefers them over everything else he sees (including palaces and "painted angels"), then the impulse to love is a fundamental force of nature that cannot be suppressed by education or isolation.

The Defense of the Muses and Style

Boccaccio argues that his work is not a departure from high art:

  • Literary Precedent: He cites great poets like Dante Alighieri, Guido Cavalcanti, and Cino da Pistoia, who all wrote of love in their old age.
  • The Muses as Women: He posits that the Muses themselves are "ladies," and while women are not their peers, they bear a "likeness" to them. He credits women as the inspiration for thousands of his verses.
  • Simple Style: He defends his use of the "vulgar Florentine" (the common vernacular) and a "homely and simple style," arguing that even in this humble form, his work is subject to the "blasts of envy."

Filostrato’s Reign

Following the preface, Filostrato is crowned King of Day Four.

  • Etymology: The name Filostrato is derived from the Greek philos (lover) and the Latin/Italian strato (struck down or prostrated), meaning "vanquished by love."
  • Thematic Mandate: He decrees that the stories for the day must concern those whose love came to a "tragic conclusion." This marks a dramatic shift from the cleverness and triumphs celebrated in Day Three.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Boccaccio call his critics' advice to seek "bread" ironic?

    • Answer: He notes that poets have historically found "more bread among their fables than many rich men among their treasures," and that he is not currently in need, but if he were, he would "know how to abound and to be in want."

  2. What is the "Author's Introduction to IV" famously known for in a literary sense?

    • Answer: It is a daring move that anticipates modern "metafiction" by breaking the frame narrative to speak directly to the reader.

  3. What name does Filippo Balducci give to the women his son sees in Florence, and why?

    • Answer: He calls them "goslings" (papere) to prevent his son from feeling a "mischievous craving of concupiscence" and to make them seem insignificant or "naughty."

  4. According to Boccaccio, how does his work relate to "lofty towers or the highest tree-tops"?

    • Answer: He initially thought the "blast of envy" only struck high places, so he tried to keep a course in the "depth of the valley" with a simple style; however, he found that even his humble work was "lacerated" by envy.

  5. Which three specific Italian poets does Boccaccio name to justify his writing about love in his later years?

    • Answer: Guido Cavalcanti, Dante Alighieri, and Messer Cino da Pistoia.

  6. What does the name "Filostrato" mean, and why is it appropriate for Day Four?

    • Answer: It means "vanquished by love." It is appropriate because Day Four is dedicated to stories of love that end in tragedy.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Nature vs. Nurture in the Parable of the Goslings: Analyze Boccaccio's argument that "nature is more potent than art." How does the failure of Filippo Balducci’s extreme religious isolation serve as a broader defense for the themes of the Decameron?
  2. The Author’s Persona and Defense: Boccaccio describes himself as someone whose "body Heaven fashioned all apt for love." Explore how he uses his personal identity and age to challenge medieval social and religious expectations regarding "proper" literary subjects.
  3. The Shift to Tragedy: Compare the tone of the previous days (specifically Day Three’s "clever triumph") with the introduction to Day Four. How does the naming of Filostrato and the introduction of "unhappy endings" change the emotional landscape of the frame narrative?

IV. Glossary of Terms

Term Definition
Alms Charitable gifts given to the poor; considered a religious duty in medieval Christianity.
Confess To make a sacramental confession of sins to a priest, a requirement of medieval Church law.
Florentine Relating to Florence, the Tuscan city-state that was Boccaccio’s home.
Goslings (Papere) The term Filippo Balducci uses to misidentify women to his son; literally small geese.
Messer "My lord" or "sir"—the standard Italian title of respect for a gentleman or knight.
Metafiction A literary device where the author breaks the fictional "frame" to address the act of storytelling or the audience directly.
Parnassus A mountain in Greece which, in mythology, was the home of the Muses; represents the heights of high art and poetry.
Prithee A contraction of "I pray thee"; a polite way of making a request.
Vulgar Refers to the common vernacular language (Italian) rather than the formal Latin used for scholarly or religious texts.
