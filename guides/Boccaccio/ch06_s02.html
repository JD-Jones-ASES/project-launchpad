Study Guide: The Decameron, Day the Fifth

This study guide examines the transition into the fifth day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. It explores the thematic shift from tragedy to comedy, the structural rhythms of the work, and the literary traditions that informed Boccaccio’s storytelling.

Key Concepts and Contextual Analysis

Thematic Shift: From Tragedy to "Happy Issue"

Day IV of The Decameron was characterized by somber tragedies where love resulted in sorrow and death. These stories often featured extreme violence, such as fathers murdering their daughters' lovers or brothers killing their sisters' beloveds.

In contrast, Day V marks a deliberate change in mood. Under the queenship of Fiammetta, the brigata (the company of storytellers) focuses on lovers who "after divers direful or disastrous adventures, at last attain a happy issue." While Day IV explored the destructive power of love, Day V emphasizes:

  • Endurance: Love’s ability to withstand suffering.
  • Persistence: The capacity to overcome significant obstacles.
  • Resolution: The arrival at joy after hardship.

Structural Rhythms and Fortuna

The movement from the darkness of Day IV to the light of Day V reflects a fundamental structural rhythm in The Decameron. Boccaccio employs a deliberate alternation between:

  • Sorrow and happiness.
  • Tragedy and comedy.

This mirrors the unpredictable turning of fortuna (fortune). By arranging the days so that light follows darkness, Boccaccio suggests an effort to create an ordered world of storytelling as a counterpoint to the chaos and death caused by the plague.

Transformation of Literary Traditions

The tales of Day V are rooted in the traditions of romance and adventure Boccaccio encountered during his years in Naples. He was particularly influenced by French courtly love literature. However, Boccaccio transforms these conventions in two significant ways:

  1. Social Status: His lovers are not restricted to knights and ladies; the themes apply to a broader range of characters.
  2. Merit vs. Birth: Happy endings are not granted solely by noble birth. Instead, they are earned through suffering, sacrifice, and occasionally "sheer luck."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

Question Answer
Who is the queen of Day V, and what does her name mean? Fiammetta is the queen; her name means “Little flame.”
What is the specific theme decreed for the stories of Day V? Tales of lovers who reach a happy ending after experiencing disastrous adventures.
How does Day V serve as a structural counterpoint to Day IV? It shifts the narrative from the "destructive power of love" and tragedy to a focus on comedy, joy, and the endurance of love.
According to the text, what replaces "noble birth" as the requirement for a happy ending? Suffering, sacrifice, and sometimes sheer luck.
Where did Boccaccio read the French courtly love literature that influenced these tales? During his years in Naples.
What activities does the brigata engage in before the storytelling begins? Walking in the meadows, drinking wine and eating comfits, singing a stampita and ballads, dancing, and taking a siesta.
At what specific location does the queen reassemble the company to begin the stories? By the fountain.
Who is the first person called upon to tell a story on Day V? Pamfilo.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Emotion: Analyze the significance of Boccaccio’s decision to follow a day of tragedy with a day of "happy issues." How does this structural choice reflect the author’s perspective on the role of storytelling during times of crisis, such as the plague?
  2. Evolution of Courtly Love: Discuss how Boccaccio adapts the medieval conventions of French courtly love for his audience. How does shifting the focus from "noble birth" to "sacrifice and luck" change the moral or thematic weight of the romantic narratives?
  3. The Role of Fortuna: Explore the concept of fortuna as presented in the transition to Day V. How do the "disastrous adventures" mentioned in the theme reinforce the idea that human happiness is subject to unpredictable forces?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Balladette: A short ballad or song; one of the musical forms performed by the brigata before their meal.
  • Brigata: The company of ten young people (seven women and three men) who have fled the plague to tell stories in the Italian countryside.
  • Champaign: A broad, open plain or meadow where the company sauntered at dawn.
  • Comfits: Sweetmeats or candy consumed by the company along with wine to repair "slight fatigue."
  • Courtly Love: A medieval literary convention involving idealized, ennobling romantic love, traditionally between a knight and a noble lady.
  • Fiammetta: The queen of Day V; the name Boccaccio traditionally gave to the woman he loved in his early romances.
  • Fortuna: The personification of luck or fortune, whose "unpredictable turning" is reflected in the alternating tragic and comic themes of the stories.
  • None: A traditional time of day (mid-afternoon) shortly after which the brigata reassembled for storytelling.
  • Seneschal: An official or steward in a medieval household responsible for arranging meals and domestic order.
  • Stampita: A type of musical composition or song performed by the company before eating.
