Boccaccio and the Medieval Literary Context: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This guide explores the literary landscape surrounding Giovanni Boccaccio, the traditions he inherited, and the influence he exerted on European literature. It focuses on the transition from medieval allegory and lyric poetry to the realistic prose narrative of the Decameron.

Key Concepts and Historical Overview

The Three Crowns of Italian Literature (Tre Corone)

The Italian literary tradition is anchored by three foundational figures from the medieval period. Collectively, they established the Tuscan dialect as the literary standard of Italy.

Author Primary Contribution Key Work Significance
Dante Alighieri (1265–1321) Supreme achievement of medieval literature; developed the highest form of Italian verse. The Divine Comedy (Commedia) Utilized terza rima to explore theology, philosophy, and personal emotion through a cosmic allegory.
Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374) "Father of Humanism"; perfected the lyric tradition and the sonnet form. Canzoniere Established the conventions of European love poetry; recovered and studied classical Latin texts.
Giovanni Boccaccio (1313–1375) Master of prose narrative; creator of the first great work of European prose fiction. The Decameron Transformed earthly, comic, and tragic stories into a sophisticated literary form.

Inherited Medieval Traditions

Boccaccio did not invent his stories in isolation. He drew upon and transformed several established medieval genres:

  • The Exemplum: Originally short stories used in sermons to provide moral lessons. Boccaccio utilized this form but often subverted it by providing "morals" that were ironic or ambiguous.
  • The Fabliau: Humorous, often bawdy French verse tales featuring archetypes like cuckolded husbands and lustful clergy. Boccaccio refined these crude plots by adding psychological depth and social commentary.
  • The Romance: Narratives of chivalry and courtly love. Boccaccio shifted these from the "ideal and marvelous" to grounded, realistic settings like the markets and bedrooms of Florence and Naples.
  • Classical Literature: Boccaccio incorporated the rhetorical models and narrative crafts of Roman authors such as Ovid, Cicero, Livy, Plautus, and Terence.

The Birth of the Novella

While short prose tales existed before Boccaccio (such as the brief, paragraph-long anecdotes in the Novellino), the Decameron established the novella as a distinct literary art form. Boccaccio introduced complex plots, vivid settings, and narrative voices, creating the blueprint for the modern short story later practiced by authors like Chekhov and Maupassant.

Boccaccio and Chaucer

Geoffrey Chaucer, the English author of The Canterbury Tales, was significantly influenced by Boccaccio. While Chaucer never mentions Boccaccio by name, the parallels between their works—including the use of a frame narrative and specific story plots—suggest a direct literary lineage.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What specific rhyme scheme did Dante invent for his Divine Comedy? Dante invented terza rima, an interlocking three-line rhyme scheme.

2. How did Petrarch contribute to the intellectual foundations of the Renaissance? Petrarch was the first great humanist; he spent much of his life seeking out, copying, and studying classical Latin texts.

3. What was the Novellino, and how did it differ from Boccaccio's work? The Novellino was an anonymous collection of approximately one hundred Italian tales compiled between 1281 and 1300. These tales were very brief—often only a paragraph—and lacked the narrative sophistication and developed characters found in Boccaccio’s writing.

4. In what way does Boccaccio "subvert" the tradition of the exemplum? While a traditional exemplum is meant to teach a clear moral lesson, Boccaccio’s narrators often provide "morals" that are ironic, ambiguous, or deliberately beside the point.

5. Name three Roman authors whose works influenced Boccaccio’s humanist education. Boccaccio was influenced by the comedies of Plautus and Terence, the poetry of Ovid (Metamorphoses), the histories of Livy, and the rhetorical models of Cicero.

6. Compare the narrators in Boccaccio’s Decameron to those in Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales. Boccaccio’s narrators are a group of refined young aristocrats (the brigata), whereas Chaucer’s narrators represent a broad cross-section of English society, ranging from knights to millers.

7. Which specific story from the Decameron serves as the basis for Chaucer’s "The Clerk’s Tale"? The story of Patient Griselda (found in Decameron X.10).

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Transition from Divine to Earthly Narrative

The "Three Crowns" represent a shift in the focus of Italian literature. Compare the "cosmic allegory" of Dante’s Divine Comedy with the "earthly" focus of Boccaccio’s Decameron. How does Boccaccio’s move toward realism and prose change the reader's engagement with the characters and the moral of the story?

2. Adaptation and Subversion of Genre

Analyze how Boccaccio takes the "raw materials" of the French fabliaux and the medieval exempla to create something new in the Decameron. Use specific examples of character depth, narrative elegance, and social commentary to explain how Boccaccio elevated these popular traditions into high art.

3. The Influence of the Frame Narrative

Both Boccaccio and Chaucer utilize a frame narrative—a story within a story—to organize their collections of tales. Compare the motivations of Boccaccio’s brigata fleeing the plague to Chaucer’s pilgrims traveling to Canterbury. How do these different external settings (the plague vs. a religious pilgrimage) influence the tone and selection of the stories told within the frame?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Allegory: A narrative where characters and events represent abstract ideas or moral principles (e.g., Dante's journey representing the soul's path to God).
  • Canzoniere: Meaning "songbook," this is Petrarch’s collection of 366 poems, primarily sonnets, dedicated to his beloved Laura.
  • Exemplum (pl. exempla): A short story used by medieval preachers to illustrate a moral point during a sermon.
  • Fabliau (pl. fabliaux): A humorous, often bawdy verse tale popular in France, typically featuring tricks and common social archetypes.
  • Frame Narrative: An outer story that provides the context and motivation for a series of inner stories (e.g., the brigata fleeing the plague in the Decameron).
  • Humanism: An intellectual movement characterized by a passion for recovering and studying classical Greek and Latin texts, of which Petrarch was a founding figure.
  • Novella: From the Italian for "new" or "news," it is a short prose tale typically focused on a single incident or turning point.
  • Novellino: An anonymous 13th-century collection of very short, unsophisticated Italian tales.
  • Terza Rima: An interlocking three-line rhyme scheme invented by Dante for the Divine Comedy.
  • Tre Corone: "The Three Crowns"; the collective name for Dante, Petrarch, and Boccaccio, the three primary figures of medieval Italian literature.
