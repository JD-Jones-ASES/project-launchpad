Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day VI – The Art of the Motto

This study guide examines the sixth day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron, a section dedicated to the power of the "motto." While earlier days in the work focus on fortune, action, or love, Day VI celebrates verbal ingegno (wit) and the capacity of well-timed speech to rescue individuals from embarrassment, danger, or social loss.

Core Concepts and Themes

1. The "Motto" as Verbal Skill

In Boccaccio’s usage, a motto is more than a slogan; it is a clever, pointed verbal response delivered at the exact right moment. It represents the transition from clever action to clever speech. The tales of Day VI explore how language functions across different social strata, from refined ladies to philosophical intellectuals and theatrical entertainers.

2. Sprezzatura: Effortless Mastery

Though the term was coined later by Baldassare Castiglione in The Book of the Courtier (1528), the concept of sprezzatura is central to Day VI. It refers to the art of performing difficult tasks with an appearance of ease and nonchalance.

  • Madonna Oretta (VI.1): Delivers a devastating social critique with lightness.
  • Guido Cavalcanti (VI.9): Torts a group of antagonists while physically vaulting over a tomb.
  • Fra Cipolla (VI.10): Improvises an elaborate lie with total spontaneity when his "relic" is compromised.

3. Metaliterary Reflection

Day VI is the most metaliterary section of the Decameron—meaning it is literature that reflects on the nature of literature itself. Through the character of the clumsy knight in VI.1, Boccaccio identifies the cardinal sins of storytelling:

  • Repetition: Telling the same thing multiple times.
  • Confusion: Getting names and facts wrong.
  • Lack of Decorum: A mismatch between the style of the delivery and the subject of the tale.

4. Rhetoric vs. Truth

The figure of Fra Cipolla (VI.10) raises classical questions about rhetoric. His speech is magnificent in its invention but entirely fraudulent. His success suggests that the power of persuasion often depends more on the confidence of the speaker and the credulity of the audience than on the truth of the message.

Social Categories of Wit

The tales present a catalogue of verbal intelligence categorized by social function:

Speaker Social Level Nature of Wit Purpose
Madonna Oretta Gentildonna (Upper Class) Subtle, indirect, and graceful. To correct social behavior without causing direct insult.
Guido Cavalcanti Philosopher/Intellectual Dense, allusive, and intellectually demanding. To transcend social conventions and assert intellectual superiority.
Fra Cipolla Popular Entertainer Theatrical, broad, and inventive. To dazzle and deceive a credulous audience for personal gain.

Summary of the Tales (Day VI, 2–8)

The following stories illustrate the diversity of the motto across various scenarios:

  • VI.2 (Cisti the Baker): Cisti uses a motto ("The Arno has not yet run dry") to teach a nobleman that gifts of high-quality wine must be offered and received with proper etiquette, not demanded through servants.
  • VI.3 (Monna Nonna de’ Pulci): A woman uses a pointed retort to silence the Bishop of Florence after he makes a vulgar jest at her expense.
  • VI.4 (Chichibio and the Crane): A servant saves himself from his master’s anger by claiming cranes have only one leg; when proven wrong, his quick retort about "shouting" at the birds rescues him from punishment.
  • VI.5 (Messer Forese and Giotto): The famous painter Giotto uses wit to remind a lawyer that true worth is not found in outward appearances, especially when both are bedraggled by a rainstorm.
  • VI.6 (Michele Scalza): Uses "mock-logical eloquence" to win a bet by arguing that the ugliest family in Florence is the noblest because they were God’s "first drafts."
  • VI.7 (Madonna Filippa): Caught in adultery, she argues before a podesta that laws made without women’s consent are unjust. Her wit and courage lead to a change in the law.
  • VI.8 (Fresco da Celatico): Fresco uses a sharp remark about mirrors to criticize his niece Cesca’s habit of acting disgusted by everyone she sees.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does the Italian word motto differ from the English cognate "motto"?
  2. In the context of VI.1, what does Madonna Oretta mean when she asks to be "set down" from the knight’s horse?
  3. What are the three specific narrative faults committed by the knight in VI.1?
  4. Who is the patron saint of cooks mentioned in VI.10, and why is he associated with dark humor?
  5. What are "Truffia and Buffia," and what is the significance of their names?
  6. How does Guido Cavalcanti’s physical action in VI.9 mirror his verbal wit?
  7. What legal argument does Madonna Filippa make in VI.7 to escape punishment?
  8. According to the text, what is the primary difference between the audience of Madonna Oretta and the audience of Fra Cipolla?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Eloquence: Using Fra Cipolla (VI.10) as a case study, analyze Boccaccio’s view on the relationship between rhetoric and truth. Does Boccaccio admire Cipolla’s skill, or is the character intended as a moral warning?
  2. Sprezzatura and Social Class: Compare and contrast the "effortless" wit of Madonna Oretta and Guido Cavalcanti. How do their specific styles of speech reflect their respective positions in Florentine society?
  3. Boccaccio’s Narrative Philosophy: Using the "bad storyteller" in VI.1 as a guide, define what Boccaccio considers to be the essential virtues of a "good" story. How do these virtues (economy, clarity, and decorum) manifest in the other tales of Day VI?
  4. Gender and Wit: Analyze the role of women’s speech in Day VI (specifically Madonna Oretta, Monna Nonna, and Madonna Filippa). How do these women use language to navigate a world governed by men and male-centric laws?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Castiglione, Baldassare: The author of The Book of the Courtier (1528) who defined the concept of sprezzatura.
  • Gentildonna: A lady of good birth or a gentlewoman; expected to possess subtle and graceful wit.
  • Giotto di Bondone: A historically significant Italian painter praised by Boccaccio for introducing naturalism and "bringing back to light" the art of painting.
  • Ingegno: Italian for wit or cleverness; the internal quality that produces a motto.
  • Metaliterary: Literature that reflects on its own nature or the process of creation.
  • Motto: A clever, pointed verbal response or witticism delivered at the opportune moment.
  • Podesta: The chief magistrate of an Italian city-state, typically an outsider hired to ensure impartial justice.
  • Sprezzatura: The appearance of effortless mastery; performing difficult tasks with nonchalance.
  • St. Lawrence: A martyr roasted on a gridiron; the patron saint of cooks and a symbol of dark humor.
