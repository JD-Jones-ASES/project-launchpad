Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day VIII, Novel 3

This study guide explores the third tale of the Eighth Day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. It focuses on the character of Calandrino, the nature of the Florentine beffa (prank), and the intersection of medieval belief systems with comic realism.

Key Concepts and Narrative Overview

The Protagonist: Calandrino

Calandrino is a central figure in the Decameron, appearing as the only recurring protagonist across four different tales (VIII.3, VIII.6, IX.3, IX.5). A painter by trade, he is characterized by his "uncouth manners" and profound gullibility. His name has transitioned into the Italian language as a common noun meaning a "simpleton" or "easy mark."

The Setting: Comic Realism

Unlike the exotic locales often found in medieval romance, this tale is grounded in the everyday world of 14th-century Florence. Key locations include:

  • The Church of San Giovanni: Where the initial deception begins.
  • The Mugnone: A local stream where the search for the magical stone takes place.
  • Porta a San Gallo: The city gate where Calandrino encounters the customs officers.
  • The Macina: The neighborhood where Calandrino resides.

The Mechanism of the Beffa

The story revolves around a beffa—a complex, often cruel prank. In this instance, the prank is multi-layered, involving an initial "tall tale" told by Maso del Saggio, followed by a physical and psychological trial orchestrated by Calandrino's fellow painters, Bruno and Buffalmacco.

Narrative Summary

  1. The Deception: Maso del Saggio, a notorious wit, encounters Calandrino in the church of San Giovanni. Feigning a scholarly discussion on lapidaries (the properties of stones), Maso describes the "heliotrope," a black stone capable of making its bearer invisible.
  2. The Myth of Bengodi: To further entice Calandrino, Maso describes a fantasy land called Bengodi, where vines are tied with sausages and a mountain made of Parmesan cheese is populated by people making macaroni and ravioli.
  3. The Expedition: Calandrino recruits his friends Bruno and Buffalmacco to find the heliotrope in the Mugnone stream. Though the friends immediately recognize the absurdity of the claim, they accompany him to exploit his simplicity.
  4. The "Invisibility" Test: At the stream, Calandrino collects a massive quantity of black stones. Bruno and Buffalmacco pretend they can no longer see him. To "test" his presence, they pelt him with heavy stones, which Calandrino endures silently, convinced his invisibility is functioning.
  5. The Return to Florence: Calandrino staggers back to the city, laden with rocks. The customs officers at the gate, previously tipped off by his friends, allow him to pass without acknowledgment.
  6. The Domestic Conflict: Upon arriving home, Calandrino is greeted by his wife, Monna Tessa. Believing that a woman's presence has stripped the heliotrope of its virtue, Calandrino becomes enraged and brutally beats her.
  7. The Aftermath: Bruno and Buffalmacco arrive to find Calandrino exhausted and Tessa injured. They maintain the ruse, blaming Calandrino for failing to follow the "rules" of the stone by not keeping his wife away, thus leaving him with a house full of useless rocks and a battered spouse.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Who is Maso del Saggio and what is his role in the story? Maso is a well-known Florentine wit whose name ironically means "Maso the Wise." He initiates the prank by convincing Calandrino of the existence of the heliotrope and the land of Bengodi.

2. What are the purported powers of the "heliotrope" stone? According to Maso, the heliotrope is a black stone that renders whoever carries it invisible to everyone else.

3. Describe the mythical land of Bengodi as presented by Maso. Bengodi is described as a land in the district of the Basques where vines are bound with sausages, a mountain is made of grated Parmesan cheese, and people spend their time making macaroni and ravioli boiled in capon's broth.

4. Why did Bruno and Buffalmacco pelt Calandrino with stones at the Mugnone? They pretended they could not see him and were "throwing" at the spot where he used to be. In reality, they were taking the opportunity to physically punish him for his greed and stupidity while maintaining the illusion that he was invisible.

5. Why does Calandrino believe the stone's magic failed when he reached his house? He believes the presence of a woman destroys the magical virtues of the stone. When his wife, Monna Tessa, sees and speaks to him, he assumes she is the cause of his sudden visibility.

6. What real-world evidence suggests that Bruno and Buffalmacco were based on historical figures? The text notes that Bruno di Giovanni and Buffalmacco (Buonamico Cristofani) were real painters in 14th-century Florence, and Giorgio Vasari recorded anecdotes about their pranks in his Lives of the Artists.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Psychology of Belief: Analyze Calandrino’s reaction to being pelted with stones. How does his absolute faith in the heliotrope override his physical senses and self-preservation? What does this suggest about the nature of gullibility in Boccaccio’s world?
  2. Friendship and Cruelty: Examine the relationship between Calandrino, Bruno, and Buffalmacco. The text describes the tale as a "portrait of friendship at its most ruthless." Discuss whether the painters' actions are portrayed as harmless fun or as a malicious exploitation of a "simple soul."
  3. Social Realism and the Artisan Class: Discuss how the specific details of Florentine geography and artisan life contribute to the humor of the tale. How does the contrast between the "exotic" Bengodi and the "modest" house of Calandrino highlight the themes of the story?
  4. Gender Dynamics and Scapegoating: Explore the character of Monna Tessa. How is she used as a scapegoat for Calandrino’s failure? Analyze the shift from the lighthearted comedy of the beffa to the grim reality of domestic violence at the tale's conclusion.

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Beffa A complex prank or trick intended to embarrass or deceive a gullible person.
Bengodi A fictional "land of plenty" described by Maso del Saggio to trick Calandrino.
Calandrino A simpleton or "easy mark"; specifically, the recurring painter protagonist in the Decameron.
Forsooth An archaic intensifier meaning "in truth" or "indeed."
Heliotrope Also known as bloodstone; a real semi-precious stone which medieval lapidaries claimed could grant invisibility.
Lapidary A book or treatise describing the physical and magical properties of precious and semi-precious stones.
Mugnone A stream near Florence where Calandrino goes to search for the heliotrope.
Tall Tale An exaggerated, unreliable story told for entertainment or to deceive.
Untrussed A state of dress where the laces (trusses) holding up one's hose are undone; used to describe Calandrino’s disheveled state.
Vernaccia A type of high-quality wine mentioned as flowing in the rivulets of Bengodi.
