Study Guide: The Literary Architecture of Boccaccio’s Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the structural, thematic, and historical significance of Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron, based on the analysis of its "literary architecture."

Overview of the Project

The Decameron is characterized not just as a collection of stories, but as a "carefully designed literary architecture." It consists of one hundred tales contained within a frame narrative that provides shape, unity, and meaning to the individual stories.

The Structure and the Cornice

The work utilizes a cornice (Italian for "frame"), which refers to the narrative of ten young people fleeing plague-stricken Florence.

Key Structural Facts:

  • The Brigata: The group consists of seven women and three men of noble birth.
  • The Setting: Fleeing the chaos of the Black Death, the group retreats to a series of country villas in the hills outside Florence.
  • The Routine: To pass the time and combat despair, they tell stories. Each day, a king or queen is elected to preside over the group and set a theme for that day's tales.
  • The Total: Ten storytellers, telling one tale each per day for ten days, totaling 100 tales.

The Thematic Arc of the Ten Days

The progression of the themes is deliberate, moving from earthly concerns toward moral greatness.

Day Theme Description
I No set theme Establishes the tone and concerns of the work.
II Fortune’s reversals Tales of unexpected happiness following adversity.
III Cleverness triumphant Tales of those who use wit to obtain their desires.
IV Love and tragedy Tales of love with unhappy endings.
V Love rewarded Tales of lovers finding happiness after terrible adventures.
VI Quick wit Tales of escaping danger or mockery through clever remarks.
VII Tricks of wives Tales of tricks wives play on their husbands.
VIII General trickery Tales of tricks between various men and women.
IX No set theme A second day of free-ranging subjects.
X Magnanimity and virtue Tales of noble, generous, and morally great deeds.

The Brigata: The Storytellers

The storytellers are not real historical figures but use literary pseudonyms, some of which appear in Boccaccio's earlier works.

  • Pampinea: The eldest woman; practical, authoritative, and the first queen.
  • Filomena: Favors tales of fortune and adventure.
  • Neifile: Prefers tales of cleverness and ingenuity.
  • Fiammetta: Boccaccio’s literary alter ego of his beloved; focuses on love.
  • Filostrato: Meaning "vanquished by love"; king during the day of tragic love.
  • Panfilo: Meaning "all-loving"; tells the very first and very last tales.
  • Dioneo: The "rebel" and "provocateur." He is exempt from the daily themes and always tells his story last. Despite his irreverence, he tells the final, morally serious tale of Patient Griselda.
  • Elissa, Lauretta, and Emilia: Other members of the group with less sharply defined preferences.

Key Concepts and Themes

The Proem: Dedication to Women

In a departure from 14th-century norms, Boccaccio dedicates the Decameron to women who suffer in love. He argues that while men have external distractions (hunting, business, etc.), women are confined to their chambers. The work is intended as consolation and entertainment for them. This dedication highlights that the work takes women seriously as both an audience and as resourceful, brave characters.

Storytelling as Survival

The frame narrative suggests that storytelling is a tool for rebuilding civilization. While Florence is in a state of chaos and shattered social bonds due to the plague, the brigata creates an ordered, miniature society in the countryside. The structured storytelling asserts the value of human experience against the meaninglessness of mass death.

Historical Significance of Prose

The Decameron is considered the first great work of European prose fiction. Prior to this, prose was reserved for functional texts like legal documents or sermons, while art was the domain of verse (epic and poetry). Boccaccio proved prose could be an artful medium, paving the way for the European novel and short story.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the literal meaning of the term "cornice" in the context of this work?
  2. Why did the brigata leave the city of Florence?
  3. Which two days of the ten-day retreat do not have a set theme?
  4. How many stories are told in total, and how is this number reached?
  5. Who is the most "irreverent" member of the brigata, and what special privilege does he have?
  6. According to the Proem, why does Boccaccio specifically dedicate his work to women?
  7. What is the name of the final tale told in the collection, and who tells it?
  8. How does the thematic focus of the tales change from Day I to Day X?
  9. Who is the first member of the group to be elected queen?
  10. Before the Decameron, what were the primary uses for prose writing?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Order: Analyze how Boccaccio uses the "cornice" to contrast the chaos of the plague with the civilization of the brigata. How does the structured nature of their retreat (kings, queens, themes) reflect a desire to rebuild a broken world?
  2. Gender and Audience: Discuss the significance of Boccaccio's dedication to women in the Proem. In what ways does the text challenge or uphold the 14th-century social restrictions placed upon women?
  3. The Role of the Narrator: Examine the character of Dioneo. Why is he allowed to break the rules of the group, and what does his presence—especially his role in telling the final tale—suggest about Boccaccio’s views on humor and morality?
  4. Prose as Art: Evaluate the historical impact of the Decameron on European literature. Why was the shift from verse to prose significant for the development of fiction, and what human questions does Boccaccio explore that keep the work relevant today?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Brigata: Italian for "company" or "brigade"; the organized group of ten young people who flee the plague and narrate the tales.
  • Cornice: Italian for "frame"; the scholarly term for the frame narrative that surrounds and connects the one hundred stories.
  • Dioneo: A name derived from Dione (mother of Venus); the name of the most complex storyteller, the "rule-breaker" and "provocateur" of the group.
  • Proem: An introduction or preface; in the Decameron, this contains Boccaccio's dedication to women and his explanation for the work.
  • Pseudonym: A fictitious name; used by the ten storytellers to hide their real identities.
  • Magnanimity: Greatness of spirit or generosity; the focus of the tales on the final day of the retreat.
