Study Guide: The Project of Boccaccio’s Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the structural, thematic, and historical elements of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. It explores the "literary architecture" of the work, the characteristics of its narrators, and its significance as a landmark of European literature.

I. Structural Overview: The Cornice

The Decameron is defined by its cornice (Italian for "frame"), a narrative device that organizes one hundred individual tales into a unified whole.

  • The Setting: The story begins in plague-stricken Florence. To escape the chaos and the breakdown of social order, ten young nobles flee to a series of country villas in the hills.
  • The Logic of the Frame: The cornice represents an ordered, civilized society set in direct opposition to the death and disorder of the plague.
  • The Routine: To pass the time, the group establishes a structured routine. Each day, a king or queen is elected to oversee the company and set a specific theme for the stories to be told.
  • The Mathematical Symmetry: 10 storytellers tell 1 tale each per day over 10 days, resulting in exactly 100 tales.

II. The Thematic Arc of the Ten Days

The progression of themes throughout the ten days is deliberate, moving from earthly concerns toward moral greatness and transcendence.

Day Theme Key Characteristics
I No set theme Establishes the tone and central concerns of the work.
II Fortune's reversals Tales of individuals reaching happiness after unexpected adversity.
III Cleverness triumphant Tales of using wit to obtain desires.
IV Love and tragedy Tales of love that end unhappily.
V Love rewarded Tales of lovers finding happiness after terrible adventures.
VI Quick wit Escaping danger or mockery through clever remarks.
VII Tricks wives play on husbands Focused on domestic deception and female ingenuity.
VIII Tricks of all kinds Deceptions played between men and women or among men.
IX No set theme A second day of free-ranging subjects.
X Magnanimity and virtue Tales of noble, generous deeds and moral greatness.

III. The Brigata: The Storytellers

The brigata (the "company" or "brigade") consists of seven women and three men. While they are not fully developed modern characters, they possess distinct personalities and literary functions.

  • Pampinea: The eldest woman; practical and authoritative. She proposes the retreat and serves as the first queen.
  • Panfilo: His name means "all-loving." He is responsible for telling the very first and the very last tales of the collection.
  • Filostrato: His name means "vanquished by love." He appropriately serves as king on the day dedicated to tragic love stories.
  • Dioneo: The most complex member and a primary narrator. He is the "rule-breaker" who is always permitted to tell his tale last and choose any subject regardless of the day's theme. Though a joker and a provocateur, he tells the morally serious final tale of the collection.
  • Filomena: Favors stories of adventure and the shifts of fortune.
  • Neifile: Gravitates toward stories of ingenuity and cleverness.
  • Fiammetta: Boccaccio’s literary alter ego of his beloved; she focuses on tales of love.
  • Elissa, Lauretta, and Emilia: Additional members of the company with less sharply defined but distinct preferences.

IV. Key Literary Concepts

The Proem and the Audience

In a remarkable departure from the customs of the fourteenth century, Boccaccio dedicated the Decameron to women. He argues that while men have external outlets for their suffering—such as hunting, business, or gambling—women are confined to their chambers. The work is offered as a "consolation" for women trapped by social restrictions. This dedication underscores Boccaccio’s commitment to taking women seriously as both an audience and as resourceful, brave protagonists.

Prose as Art

Before the Decameron, prose was generally reserved for functional documents like legal papers or sermons. High art was the domain of verse (epic and lyric poetry). Boccaccio proved that prose could be artful, moving, and complex, effectively laying the groundwork for the European novel and short story.

Storytelling as Survival

The frame narrative suggests that storytelling is a tool for survival. By creating a world with rules, themes, and structured narratives, the brigata asserts the value of human experience and rebuilds a "miniature civilization" in the face of a world where death has rendered everything seemingly meaningless.

V. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the literal meaning of the Italian word cornice, and how does it apply to the Decameron?
  2. How many stories are told in total, and what is the mathematical breakdown of their delivery?
  3. Why is Dioneo considered the most important or unique member of the brigata?
  4. According to the Proem, why did Boccaccio choose to dedicate his work specifically to women?
  5. Which storyteller is the eldest and first to be elected queen?
  6. How does the theme of Day X differ from the theme of Day I?
  7. What was the status of prose fiction before Boccaccio wrote the Decameron?
  8. What does the name "Filostrato" mean, and how does it relate to his role in the story?

VI. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Arc of Morality: Analyze the deliberate progression of themes from Day I to Day X. How does the movement from "earthly" concerns to "moral greatness" reflect Boccaccio’s view of the human experience?
  2. Order vs. Chaos: Discuss the role of the frame narrative as a response to the plague. In what ways does the structure of the brigata's retreat serve as a "civilizing" force against the breakdown of society in Florence?
  3. The Portrayal of Women: Boccaccio claims his work is a consolation for women. Evaluate this claim by examining his dedication in the Proem and the types of female characters he presents throughout the tales.
  4. Dioneo as the Provocateur: Explore the paradox of Dioneo. Why would the most "irreverent" member of the group be tasked with telling the final, most "morally serious" tale? What does this suggest about the relationship between humor and virtue in the Decameron?

VII. Glossary of Terms

  • Brigata: The "company" or "brigade"; the group of ten young nobles who flee the plague and tell the tales.
  • Cornice: Italian for "frame"; the scholarly term for the narrative structure that houses the one hundred tales.
  • Magnanimity: Greatness of spirit or generosity; the central theme of the final day of storytelling.
  • Proem: An introduction or preface; specifically, the section where Boccaccio dedicates his work to women.
  • Pseudonym: A fictitious name; used by the ten storytellers to mask their real identities.
  • Verse: Writing arranged with a metrical rhythm (poetry); the dominant literary form prior to the rise of Boccaccio's prose.
