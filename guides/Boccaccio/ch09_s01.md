# Study Guide: Day VIII of Boccaccio’s Decameron

This study guide explores the themes, narratives, and ethical complexities of the eighth day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It focuses on the shift from lighthearted comedy to darker forms of revenge, the concept of the *beffa* (trick), and the realism of 14th-century Florence.

---

## Overview of Key Themes

### The Spectrum of the Beffa: Comedy vs. Cruelty
Day VIII marks a significant shift in the *Decameron*. While Day VII focused on lighthearted tricks, Day VIII introduces a spectrum of "cleverness" that ranges from harmless pranks to lethal malice. The central question of the Day is whether the brilliance of a trick’s execution justifies the suffering it causes.

*   **Harmless Comedy (Novel VIII.3):** The Calandrino tales exemplify "comic realism." These stories involve physical humor and psychological manipulation where the victim suffers no lasting financial or physical ruin.
*   **Extreme Revenge (Novel VIII.7):** The tale of the Scholar and the Widow represents the opposite pole, where revenge becomes physically dangerous and psychologically devastating.

### Ingegno and Ethics
The stories explore *ingegno* (intelligence or wit). Boccaccio suggests that intelligence is a neutral tool; its moral value depends entirely on its application. The juxtaposition of various tales asks the reader to consider the ethical limits of trickery and whether proportionality exists in the pursuit of a vendetta.

### Comic Realism
Unlike earlier literary traditions set in allegorical landscapes, Boccaccio’s tales are grounded in the actual streets of Florence, particularly the **Oltrarno** district. The characters are not archetypal royalty but recognizable artisans, painters, and merchants. This realism emphasizes human psychology—specifically vanity, gullibility, and the desire to feel special.

## Summary of the Tales of Day VIII

The following table summarizes the ten tales told on Day VIII, illustrating the variety of the *beffa*:

| Novel | Characters | Plot Summary | Tone |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **VIII.1** | Gulfardo & the Wife of Gasparruolo | A soldier tricks a merchant’s wife by using borrowed money to pay for her favors, then "returning" the loan to the husband in her presence. | Clever/Deceptive |
| **VIII.2** | The Priest of Varlungo | A priest seduces a woman by leaving his cloak as security, then tricks her into returning it without paying the promised fee. | Bawdy/Neutral |
| **VIII.3** | Calandrino, Bruno, & Buffalmacco | Friends convince the gullible Calandrino he has found an invisibility stone (heliotrope), leading to his public humiliation. | Light Comedy |
| **VIII.4** | The Provost of Fiesole | A widow and her brothers trap a lecherous church official in a compromising position to be discovered by the bishop. | Just Revenge |
| **VIII.5** | Three Judges from the Marches | Florentines humiliate three stingy provincial judges by stealing their breeches while they are presiding in court. | Humiliation |
| **VIII.6** | Calandrino and the Pig | Bruno and Buffalmacco steal Calandrino’s pig and rig a "divination test" to make it look like Calandrino stole it himself. | Comic Realism |
| **VIII.7** | The Scholar and the Widow | A scholar, humiliated by a widow, retaliates by trapping her on a tower in the scorching sun, causing severe physical torture. | Dark Revenge |
| **VIII.8** | Spinelloccio and Zeppa | Two friends discover they are each sleeping with the other’s wife and decide to share their arrangements openly. | Amicable Resolution |
| **VIII.9** | Maestro Simone | Two tricksters convince a vain doctor he is joining a secret society, only to lure him into a ditch full of filth. | Satire of Vanity |
| **VIII.10** | Salabaetto & Jancofiore | A merchant is swindled by a Sicilian courtesan but recovers his losses through an even more elaborate counter-trick. | Commercial Warfare |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **What is "comic realism," and how does Boccaccio use it in the Calandrino tales?**
    *   *Answer:* Comic realism is the use of real-world settings (like the streets of Florence and the Oltrarno) and recognizable, everyday characters (artisans and painters) rather than allegorical or royal figures. It grounds comedy in human psychology and local culture.
2.  **How does the punishment in Novel VIII.7 (The Scholar and the Widow) differ from the tricks played on Calandrino?**
    *   *Answer:* The punishment in VIII.7 is physically dangerous, psychologically devastating, and disproportionate, involving torture under the sun. Calandrino’s tricks involve humiliation and minor physical comedy but leave him financially and socially intact.
3.  **Who are Bruno and Buffalmacco?**
    *   *Answer:* They are recurring characters—based on real-life Florentine painters—who serve as the intelligent but often heartless tricksters in the Calandrino and Maestro Simone cycles.
4.  **What role does the setting of Fiesole play in Novel VIII.4?**
    *   *Answer:* Fiesole is a hilltop town and bishopric overlooking Florence; its status as an ecclesiastical site provides the backdrop for the humiliation of the lecherous provost.
5.  **What is the significance of the "heliotrope" in the Calandrino cycle?**
    *   *Answer:* It is a mythical stone of invisibility that Calandrino's friends convince him he has found, exploiting his vanity and desperate desire to be "special."
6.  **How does Boccaccio distinguish between the narrator and the author?**
    *   *Answer:* The *Decameron* uses ten different narrators (*the brigata*), each with their own biases and judgments. For example, Pampinea approves of the scholar's revenge in VIII.7, but this does not necessarily mean Boccaccio himself endorses the cruelty.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ethics of Proportionality:** Analyze the concept of *vendetta* in Day VIII. Using Novel VIII.7 as your primary evidence, argue whether the scholar’s revenge is a "just" response to Elena’s initial trick or a descent into unjustifiable cruelty.
2.  **The Architecture of the Beffa:** Compare the methods used by the tricksters in Novel VIII.3 (Calandrino) and Novel VIII.9 (Maestro Simone). How does Boccaccio use the specific flaws of his victims—gullibility vs. intellectual vanity—to drive the comedy?
3.  **Gender and Power Dynamics:** Examine the role of women in the tricks of Day VIII. Contrast the agency and outcome for the women in VIII.1 (the merchant’s wife), VIII.4 (the widow of Fiesole), and VIII.7 (Elena). How does the *beffa* function as a tool for or against female characters?
4.  **Boccaccio vs. Chaucer:** Compare Boccaccio's use of gullibility in the Calandrino stories to Chaucer's treatment of it in *The Nun's Priest's Tale*. How do both authors use the gap between a character's self-image and reality to create humor?

## Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Beffa:** A trick or prank, often involving the manipulation of a "dupe" by a more clever "trickster."
*   **Commedia dell'arte:** A later form of Italian improvisational theater featuring stock characters; Boccaccio’s Calandrino tales are seen as an early ancestor of this tradition.
*   **Fiesole:** A hilltop town overlooking Florence, historically an ancient Etruscan settlement and a medieval bishopric.
*   **Guelphs and Ghibellines:** The two primary warring factions in medieval Italian politics. Florence was a Guelph city but remained deeply divided by internal feuds.
*   **Ingegno:** Human wit, cleverness, or genius. In Day VIII, it is the primary engine of both comedy and revenge.
*   **Oltrarno:** Literally "beyond the Arno," the district of Florence on the south bank of the river, traditionally home to artisans and craftsmen.
*   **The Marches (Marche):** A region in central Italy. In the *Decameron*, characters from this region are often depicted as provincial or naive compared to Florentines.
*   **Vendetta:** The recognized social institution of personal or family revenge in medieval Italy, which was sometimes acknowledged by law.
*   **Vernaccia:** A specific white wine from the Tuscan hill town of San Gimignano, used by Boccaccio to ground his tales in local culture.
