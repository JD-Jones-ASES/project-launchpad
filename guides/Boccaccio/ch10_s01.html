Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day IX

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of Day IX of Giovanni Boccaccio’s Decameron. It explores the structural significance of the day’s "free theme," analyzes key tales and literary traditions, and provides assessment tools for mastering the material.

Overview of Day IX: The Reprise

Day IX serves as a strategic "intermission" or "reprise" within the Decameron. Following the intense and morally complex cycles of Day VII (marital trickery) and Day VIII (revenge), Day IX offers a lighter, gentler emotional register. Like Day I, it has no prescribed subject, allowing the narrators to return to familiar Boccaccian settings such as the monastery, the bedroom, and the marketplace.

Key Characteristics of Day IX

  • Tone: Notably lighter than previous days, focusing on comic relief, easier laughter, and lower stakes.
  • Brevity: Many stories are short anecdotes or brief comic scenes centered on a single reversal.
  • Structural Position: It acts as a moment of relaxation before the "grand ascent" of Day X, which focuses on themes of virtue and magnanimity.
  • Themes: Even without a mandatory theme, the tales revisit the work's central concerns: clerical hypocrisy, the comedy of fortune, female cleverness, and human gullibility.

Key Literary Concepts

Anticlerical Satire

Boccaccio’s tales frequently target the clergy, but this satire is specific in its intent. It does not attack the Christian faith itself; rather, it attacks the failure of institutional representatives to live up to their own ideals.

  • The Target: Hypocrisy—specifically the gap between vows (chastity, poverty) and actual behavior (lust, cunning).
  • The Modes of Satire:
    • Intellectual: Superior verbal dexterity (e.g., I.1).
    • Physical: Exploiting physical appetites (e.g., III.1).
    • Rhetorical: Improvisational persuasion (e.g., VI.10).
    • Visual: Slapstick or visual absurdity, such as the abbess wearing breeches on her head (IX.2).

The Fabliau and the Bed-Trick

Several tales in Day IX, most notably the story of the inn at Forlì (IX.6), belong to the fabliau tradition.

  • Definition: A short, comic, often bawdy verse tale that originated in medieval France.
  • Boccaccio’s Transformation: He converted the verse fabliau into prose novella, adding psychological depth and narrative sophistication.
  • The Bed-Trick: A classic plot device where characters end up in the wrong beds due to darkness. Boccaccio emphasizes "spatial choreography," using specific props—like a baby's cradle—to drive the confusion.

Literary Realism (Mimesis)

As argued by Erich Auerbach in Mimesis (1946), the Decameron represents a shift toward a new kind of realism. It treats the everyday world as a serious subject for art without requiring the allegorical or religious frameworks common in earlier medieval literature.

Summary of Tales

Novel Title/Subject Key Theme Synopsis
IX.1 Madonna Francesca Female Cleverness Francesca rids herself of two unwanted suitors by tricking them into a frightening encounter involving a "corpse" in a tomb.
IX.2 The Abbess’s Breeches Anticlerical Satire An abbess, rushing to catch a nun with a lover, accidentally wears a priest’s breeches on her head, exposing her own hypocrisy.
IX.3 Calandrino’s Pregnancy Gullibility The painter Calandrino is convinced by his friends that he is pregnant; he pays them for a "cure."
IX.4 Cecco di Fortarigo Reckless Behavior A historical poet, Cecco Angiolieri, gambles away his companion's money and then shamelessly blames the companion.
IX.5 Calandrino in Love Gullibility/Marriage Calandrino is tricked into believing a magic scroll will win him a young woman's love, only to be beaten by his wife.
IX.6 The Inn at Forlì The Bed-Trick A complex series of mistaken bed-hoppings at an inn, triggered by the movement of a baby's cradle.
IX.7 Talano d’Imolese Dreams/Stubbornness Talano dreams his wife is attacked by a wolf; she ignores his warning and the dream comes true.
IX.8 Biondello and Ciacco Florentine Wit Ciacco the glutton takes revenge on Biondello for a trick by arranging for Biondello to be beaten.
IX.9 Solomon’s Advice Medieval Marriage Solomon advises a man to go to the "Goose-bridge," where he learns to use violence to make his wife obedient.
IX.10 Donno Gianni’s Tail Irreverent Comedy A priest attempts a "transformation" of a woman into a mare, a bawdy trick interrupted by her husband.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Day IX differ in tone from Days VII and VIII?
  2. What is the specific visual image used to satirize the clergy in Novel IX.2?
  3. In Novel IX.6, what specific household object is used as the "key prop" to cause the confusion in the dark?
  4. According to the text, what is the difference between Boccaccio’s satire and atheism?
  5. Which recurring character is the victim of practical jokes in both Novel IX.3 and IX.5?
  6. How did medieval people generally view dreams, as illustrated in the tale of Talano d’Imolese (IX.7)?
  7. What is the significance of the "Goose-bridge" in Novel IX.9?
  8. How does Boccaccio’s version of the fabliau differ from the original Old French versions?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Anticlerical Satire: Trace the development of Boccaccio’s critique of the clergy from Day I to Day IX. How does the shift from intellectual wit (I.1) to visual farce (IX.2) change the impact of the satire?
  2. Spatial Choreography and Narrative: Analyze Boccaccio’s use of physical space in the tale of the Inn at Forlì (IX.6). How does his "stage designer" approach to the room layout contribute to the story's dramatic irony and comedic timing?
  3. The Ethics of the Trickster: Many tales in Day IX (IX.3, IX.5, IX.8) involve cruel or elaborate practical jokes. Discuss the role of the "trickster" in Florentine society as depicted by Boccaccio. Is there a moral limit to these jokes, or is gullibility portrayed as its own justification for punishment?
  4. Historical Distance and Modern Values: Novel IX.9 (Solomon’s Advice) presents a troubling endorsement of domestic violence. Discuss how a modern reader should approach this text. To what extent does the story reflect medieval attitudes toward authority and marriage versus Boccaccio's personal narrative stance?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Anticlerical Satire: A literary mode that mocks the clergy for failing to adhere to their religious vows and spiritual duties.
  • Bed-Trick: A narrative device in which characters unknowingly sleep with the wrong person due to darkness or deception.
  • Cluniac Reforms: Tenth-century movements aimed at correcting clerical corruption within the medieval Church.
  • Fabliau: A short, bawdy, comic tale in verse, originally popular in medieval France.
  • Mendicant Orders: Thirteenth-century religious orders (such as friars) that took vows of poverty; often the target of Boccaccio's satire regarding worldly cunning.
  • Mimesis: A term used by critic Erich Auerbach to describe literary realism—the representation of the everyday world as a serious subject for art.
  • Novella: A short prose tale; the form Boccaccio helped standardize by adapting earlier verse traditions.
  • Reprise: A musical or literary term for a return to earlier themes or styles; used to describe the "free theme" nature of Day IX.
