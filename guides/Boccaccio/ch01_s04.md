# Study Guide: Boccaccio and the Literary Context of the Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the literary traditions, figures, and forms that shaped Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It is designed to facilitate a deep understanding of Boccaccio’s role as one of the "Three Crowns" of Italian literature and his transformation of medieval storytelling into the modern novella.

---

## Key Concepts and Historical Context

### The Three Crowns (Tre Corone)
The Italian literary tradition is anchored by three foundational figures from the medieval period. Together, they established the Tuscan dialect as the primary literary language of Italy.

| Figure | Contribution | Key Work(s) | Focus/Legacy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Dante Alighieri** (1265–1321) | High Literary Art & Theology | *The Divine Comedy* | Mastered *terza rima* and cosmic allegory; proved Italian could rival Latin. |
| **Francesco Petrarch** (1304–1374) | Lyric Poetry & Humanism | *Canzoniere* | Perfected the sonnet and the "inner life"; recovered classical Latin texts. |
| **Giovanni Boccaccio** (1313–1375) | Prose Narrative | *The Decameron* | Created the first great work of European prose fiction; focused on earthly, human reality. |

### Medieval Narrative Traditions
Boccaccio did not invent storytelling; rather, he synthesized and transformed several existing medieval traditions:

*   **The Exemplum:** Originally short stories used by preachers to illustrate moral lessons. Boccaccio adopted this form but often subverted it by providing ironic or ambiguous "morals."
*   **The Fabliau:** Bawdy, humorous verse tales from France. Boccaccio refined these crude plots by adding psychological depth and social commentary.
*   **The Romance Tradition:** Tales of chivalry and courtly love. While Boccaccio utilized these themes, he grounded them in realistic settings like the markets and bedrooms of Florence and Naples.
*   **Classical Influence:** Boccaccio’s humanist education allowed him to incorporate rhetorical models and themes from Roman authors such as Ovid, Cicero, and Livy.

### The Evolution of the Novella
Before Boccaccio, short prose tales—known as *novelle*—existed as brief, undeveloped anecdotes, such as those found in the anonymous collection *Novellino*. Boccaccio transformed the novella into a sophisticated art form characterized by:
1.  Developed characters with psychological depth.
2.  Complex, elegant plots.
3.  Vivid, realistic settings.
4.  Narrative voices that comment on the action.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Subversion of the Moral Narrative
Analyze how Boccaccio utilizes the form of the medieval *exemplum* in the *Decameron*. Discuss how he shifts the focus from clear moral instruction to irony and ambiguity. What does this shift suggest about Boccaccio’s view of human nature compared to the traditional medieval religious worldview?

### 2. Prose vs. Verse: The Completion of the Trio
Dante and Petrarch are celebrated for their mastery of verse. Explore the significance of Boccaccio choosing prose narrative for the *Decameron*. How does the shift to prose allow Boccaccio to explore "earthly, human, comic, and tragic" themes in a way that his predecessors' cosmic or lyric styles might not have permitted?

### 3. A Comparative Study: Boccaccio and Chaucer
Compare and contrast the organizational structures of the *Decameron* and the *Canterbury Tales*. While both use frame narratives, Boccaccio organizes his tales by daily themes told by aristocrats, whereas Chaucer uses the personalities of a diverse social cross-section. How do these different structural choices reflect the cultural contexts of 14th-century Italy and England?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Allegory:** A narrative where characters and events represent abstract ideas or moral principles (e.g., Dante’s journey representing the soul’s path to God).
*   **Canzoniere:** Italian for "songbook"; Petrarch’s collection of 366 poems, primarily sonnets addressed to Laura.
*   **Exemplum (pl. exempla):** A short story used in medieval sermons to illustrate a moral point.
*   **Fabliau (pl. fabliaux):** A short, humorous, and often bawdy French verse tale featuring tricks and lustful characters.
*   **Frame Narrative:** A "story within a story" structure where an outer narrative provides the context for the inner tales.
*   **Humanism:** An intellectual movement focused on the recovery and study of classical Latin and Greek texts; Petrarch is considered its "father."
*   **Novella:** Derived from the Italian for "new" or "news"; a short prose tale focused on a realistic setting and a single turning point.
*   **Novellino:** An anonymous 13th-century collection of approximately one hundred very brief Italian tales.
*   **Terza Rima:** An interlocking three-line rhyme scheme invented by Dante for *The Divine Comedy*.
*   **Tre Corone:** "The Three Crowns"; refers to the trio of Dante, Petrarch, and Boccaccio as the pillars of Italian literature.
