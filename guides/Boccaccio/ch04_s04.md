# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron – Day III, Tale 9

This study guide explores the ninth tale told on the third day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. Narrated by Neifile, the story follows the persistent and clever Gillette of Narbonne as she navigates the rigid social hierarchies of medieval France to secure the love and recognition of the Count of Roussillon.

---

## Narrative Overview and Key Concepts

### Summary of the Tale
Gillette of Narbonne, the daughter of a renowned physician, falls in love with Beltramo (Bertrand), the Count of Roussillon, with whom she was raised. Following the death of her father and Beltramo’s father, Beltramo becomes a ward of the King of France and moves to Paris. Gillette, determined to follow him, uses her inherited medical knowledge to cure the King of a debilitating fistula that other physicians could not treat. 

As her reward, the King allows her to choose her husband. She chooses Beltramo, who is forced to marry her despite his disdain for her lower social standing. Immediately after the wedding, Beltramo flees to Florence to serve in the military, setting two seemingly impossible conditions for their marriage to be consummated: Gillette must obtain a specific ring from his finger and bear him a child. Through a series of clever maneuvers involving a "bed trick" in Florence, Gillette fulfills both conditions and eventually wins Beltramo's genuine acceptance and love.

### Core Themes

*   **Female Agency and Determination:** Gillette is a portrait of a woman who refuses to accept the role society has assigned her. She uses both her professional skills (medicine) and her wit to overcome obstacles of birth and gender.
*   **The Conflict of Class vs. Merit:** The tension in the story arises from the disparity between Gillette’s modest birth and Beltramo’s nobility. Beltramo’s initial rejection of her as a "she-doctor" highlights the social prejudices of the era.
*   **Cleverness as Triumph:** Consistent with the theme of Day III ("Cleverness Triumphant"), the story rewards the protagonist's ingenuity. Her success is not a matter of luck but of strategic planning.
*   **The "Bed Trick" Motif:** A recurring literary device where one person is secretly substituted for another in a sexual encounter. This motif serves as the turning point that allows Gillette to satisfy Beltramo’s impossible conditions.

## Key Characters

| Character | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Gillette of Narbonne** | The protagonist; a physician’s daughter of modest birth who possesses great medical skill and intelligence. |
| **Beltramo (Bertrand)** | The young Count of Roussillon; a nobleman who initially rejects Gillette due to her lower social class. |
| **The King of France** | Suffers from a fistula; he grants Gillette her choice of husband in exchange for his health. |
| **Master Gerard** | Gillette's deceased father; a famous physician from whom she inherited her medical knowledge. |
| **The Florentine Gentlewoman** | A virtuous but poor widow who assists Gillette in her plan to trick Beltramo in exchange for a dowry for her daughter. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **What specific ailment is the King of France suffering from, and how does Gillette treat it?**
    The King suffers from a fistula in his breast that had been ill-tended. Gillette treats it using a powder made from certain herbs, a remedy she learned from her father.
2.  **What reward does Gillette demand from the King for curing him?**
    She demands the right to choose her own husband from among the King's subjects, excluding any members of the royal house.
3.  **Why does Beltramo initially refuse to accept Gillette as his wife?**
    He considers her lineage to be beneath his nobility, disparagingly referring to her as a "she-doctor."
4.  **What are the two conditions Beltramo sets for accepting Gillette?**
    First, she must obtain the ring he always wears on his finger (which he believes possesses a special virtue). Second, she must have a son fathered by him.
5.  **How does Gillette discover Beltramo’s location and his activities in Florence?**
    She travels to Florence dressed as a pilgrim and stays at an inn. The hostess informs her that a foreign gentleman named Count Bertrand is in the city and is pursuing the daughter of a poor but virtuous neighbor.
6.  **What financial arrangement does Gillette make with the Florentine gentlewoman?**
    In exchange for the gentlewoman’s help in the "bed trick," Gillette provides a dowry of 500 pounds plus rare and valuable jewels for the woman's daughter.
7.  **How does Gillette eventually prove to Beltramo that she has met his conditions?**
    She appears at his palace during an All Saints' Day feast, dressed as a pilgrim, and presents him with his ring and their two twin sons.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Nature of Victory:** Analyze the ending of the tale. Does Boccaccio present the conclusion as a purely happy ending, or does Beltramo’s initial "harsh obduracy" and the nature of the "bed trick" cast a shadow over their eventual reconciliation?
2.  **Scientific Knowledge vs. Social Status:** Gillette’s medical skill is described as hovering between science and something close to magic. Discuss how her role as a "physician’s daughter" empowers her to challenge the medieval social hierarchy and how Beltramo’s rejection of her reflects the prejudices of the time.
3.  **Literary Legacy:** This tale served as the source for Shakespeare’s *All’s Well That Ends Well*. Compare the description of Gillette’s agency in Boccaccio’s text with the characteristics of a "problem comedy," focusing on how audiences might feel about a heroine who uses deception to secure a husband.
4.  **The Ethics of Deception:** Explore the moral implications of the "bed trick." Is Gillette’s use of deceit justified by the conditions Beltramo imposed, or does it complicate the reader's view of her as a virtuous protagonist?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Bed Trick:** A literary motif involving the secret substitution of one sexual partner for another. It is used to resolve plot complications in medieval and Renaissance literature.
*   **Dowry:** Money, property, or goods brought by a bride to her husband at marriage. In the tale, Gillette provides a substantial dowry to the Florentine girl to facilitate her own plan.
*   **Fistula:** A chronic, abnormal passage or ulcer. In the context of the story, it is the life-threatening ailment afflicting the King of France.
*   **Guerdon:** A reward or recompense. Gillette asks for a husband as her guerdon for curing the King.
*   **Mien:** A person's appearance or manner, especially as it indicates their character or mood. Neifile begins her narration with a "mien most gracious."
*   **Palace of Pleasure:** A 16th-century English translation of Italian tales by William Painter, which served as the likely medium through which Shakespeare encountered Boccaccio’s story.
*   **Pilgrim’s Weeds:** The traditional clothing or "habit" worn by a pilgrim. Gillette uses this disguise to travel unnoticed and observe Beltramo.
*   **Virtue (of the Ring):** A specific power or quality inherent in an object. Beltramo prizes his ring because of a "virtue" he believes it possesses.
