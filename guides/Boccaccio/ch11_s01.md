# Day X of Boccaccio's Decameron: Magnanimity and Virtue - A Study Guide

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the final Day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It explores the thematic culmination of the work, the central virtue of magnanimity (*magnificenza*), and the complex literary debates surrounding its most famous tales.

---

## Key Concepts and Thematic Overview

### The Moral Summit of the Decameron
Day X represents the culmination of the *Decameron*. While earlier days established themes of wit, fortune, and desire, Day X resolves these into a "higher key." The movement of the entire work—from the depiction of the worst of men (Ser Ciappelletto in I.1) to the most patient of women (Griselda in X.10)—parallels the structure of Dante’s *Divine Comedy*, moving from a metaphorical Hell toward a human "paradise" grounded in classical virtue.

### Magnificenza (Magnanimity)
The governing principle of Day X is *magnificenza*, or greatness of soul. Inherited from the Aristotelian tradition, this is considered the "crown virtue." It characterizes an individual who:
*   Acts with extraordinary generosity.
*   Bears misfortune with dignity.
*   Prioritizes honor above self-interest.
*   Demonstrates self-mastery and sacrifice.

### The Chain of Generosity
A recurring structural motif in Day X, particularly in Tale X.5, where one magnanimous act inspires another, creating an ascending spiral of virtue. This structure suggests that generosity is not a "zero-sum game" but a "contagion" that multiplies when demonstrated.

### The "Noble Pagan"
This medieval literary archetype refers to a non-Christian character whose moral excellence is admired despite their status outside the Christian faith. Saladin serves as the primary example in the *Decameron*, appearing as a figure of wisdom and magnificence who serves as a moral peer—or even superior—to Christian figures.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **What does the term *magnificenza* mean in the context of Day X?**
    *   *Answer:* It refers to magnanimity or "greatness of soul," characterized by selfless generosity, bearing misfortune with dignity, and placing honor above self-interest.

2.  **How does the character arc of the *Decameron* compare to Dante's *Divine Comedy*?**
    *   *Answer:* It moves from a low point of human depravity (Ser Ciappelletto) to a high point of virtue (Griselda), mirroring the movement from Hell to Paradise, though Boccaccio’s "paradise" is human and secular rather than theological.

3.  **In Tale X.5 (The Magic Garden), who are the three characters involved in the "chain of generosity"?**
    *   *Answer:* Madonna Dianora's husband, her suitor Ansaldo, and the magician.

4.  **Why is Boccaccio's portrayal of Saladin in X.9 considered remarkable for the 14th century?**
    *   *Answer:* Because the Crusades were recent history, yet Boccaccio presents a Muslim ruler as a moral superior and the supreme exemplar of the Christian virtue of magnanimity.

5.  **How does Petrarch’s reading of the Griselda story differ from Boccaccio’s?**
    *   *Answer:* Petrarch stripped away Boccaccio's ironic framing and presented the tale as a straightforward allegorical *exemplum*, where Griselda represents the soul's faithfulness when tested by God (Gualtieri).

6.  **What is "Chichevache"?**
    *   *Answer:* A legendary cow from a medieval joke that fed only on patient wives; she was said to be starving because such wives were so rare.

7.  **What comment does Dioneo make about Gualtieri’s behavior in Tale X.10?**
    *   *Answer:* He calls Gualtieri’s tests "mischievous folly" (*matta bestialità*) and suggests Gualtieri deserved a wife who would have been unfaithful to him.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ethics of Testing:** Compare the "tests" administered in Day X (such as the King of Spain's test of Ruggieri in X.1 and Gualtieri's test of Griselda in X.10). Does the intent of the tester or the severity of the test change the moral value of the virtue being demonstrated?

2.  **The Griselda Problem:** Modern readers often view Griselda as a victim of abuse, while medieval readers viewed her as a model of saintly patience. Analyze these two perspectives. Can Griselda's endurance be considered a form of *magnificenza*, or is it a "pathological submission" that falls outside the bounds of virtue?

3.  **Religious Boundaries and Virtue:** Using the tales of Saladin (I.3 and X.9), discuss Boccaccio’s argument regarding virtue and religious identity. Does the *Decameron* suggest that moral excellence is a universal human trait independent of faith?

4.  **Dioneo’s Role:** Dioneo is established as the most irreverent and bawdy narrator of the *brigata*. Why would Boccaccio choose him to tell the final, most serious, and most troubling tale of the collection? How does Dioneo’s framing affect the reader’s interpretation of Griselda's story?

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Beffa:** A tale of a trick or practical joke, common in the earlier days of the *Decameron*.
*   **Chichevache:** A satirical medieval creature that consumes patient wives; used by Chaucer’s Clerk to mock the rarity of Griselda-like obedience.
*   **De Amicitia:** Cicero's "On Friendship," a classical text that influenced Boccaccio's portrayal of the bond between Titus and Gisippus in Tale X.8.
*   **Exemplum:** A moralizing story used to illustrate a specific virtue or lesson.
*   **Ingegno:** Human wit or cleverness; a major theme in the early days of the *Decameron*.
*   **Magnificenza:** Magnanimity; the core virtue of Day X, involving greatness of soul and selfless generosity.
*   **Motto:** A sharp, witty verbal retort; a key theme in Day VI.
*   **Noble Pagan / Virtuous Infidel:** A non-Christian character in medieval literature recognized for their genuine moral excellence.
*   **The Clerk's Tale:** The section of Geoffrey Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* that retells the story of Griselda, influenced by Petrarch’s Latin translation.
