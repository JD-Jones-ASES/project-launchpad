# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day I, Novel 9

This study guide provides an analysis of the ninth tale of the first day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*, narrated by Elissa. It explores the themes of wit, the power of language, and the reform of leadership through the "motto."

---

## I. Summary and Contextual Overview

The ninth tale of Day I is one of the shortest stories in the *Decameron*. It follows a lady of Gascony who, while returning from a pilgrimage to the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem, is "brutally outraged" by ruffians in Cyprus. Seeking redress, she is discouraged by reports that the King of Cyprus is "spiritless and faineant," failing to avenge not only the insults of others but even those directed at himself.

Rather than offering a traditional plea, the lady uses a sharp, witty remark—referred to as a "motto"—to shame the king. This single sentence successfully "awakens" the king from his apathy, leading him to redress her wrong and transform into a rigorous administrator of justice.

### Key Conceptual Themes
*   **The Power of the Motto:** In the context of the *Decameron*, a motto is a pointed, witty remark (a *bon mot*). It serves as a social weapon capable of shaming, correcting, or delighting. In this tale, it accomplishes what force and supplication could not.
*   **The Mirror for Princes (*Speculum Principis*):** This tale functions as a miniature version of the medieval genre of literature that instructs rulers on proper conduct. While most such works are lengthy and moralizing, Boccaccio achieves this didactic end through a single, brief interaction.
*   **Language and Reform:** The narrative suggests that language has the power to reclaim men who are otherwise "obdurate to censures and chastisements."

## II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Who narrates the ninth tale of Day I?**
> **Answer:** The tale is told by Elissa, who is the last to speak following the story told by Lauretta.

**2. Where is the lady in the story from, and what was the historical status of that region?**
> **Answer:** She is from Gascony, a region in southwestern France that was part of the English-controlled Duchy of Aquitaine during the fourteenth century.

**3. What was the reputation of the King of Cyprus at the beginning of the tale?**
> **Answer:** He was considered notoriously indolent, spiritless, and "faineant." He was so "spiritless" that he neglected to avenge affronts to others and tamely endured those offered to himself.

**4. Why did the lady decide to approach the king if she knew he would not grant her redress?**
> **Answer:** She despaired of redress but sought an "alleviation of her grief" by making the king "sensible of his baseness."

**5. What specific remark did the lady make to the king?**
> **Answer:** She told him she did not come for redress, but to learn how he suffered the wrongs done to him with such patience, so that she might learn to endure her own "outrage" and "transfer" her burden to him, as he was so "well fitted to bear them."

**6. What was the long-term effect of the lady's words on the king's governance?**
> **Answer:** The king moved from being apathetic to meeting out "rigorous justice" to all who offended the "majesty of his crown."

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

**1. The Economy of Brevity:**
The source context notes that the brevity of this tale is "part of its point." Analyze how Boccaccio uses a "miniature" narrative to demonstrate the efficacy of the motto. Contrast the lady's single sentence with the "moralizing at length" found in traditional *speculum principis* literature.

**2. Language as a Social Weapon:**
Discuss the relationship between language and power in this tale. Why is the "well-aimed word" presented as more effective than "force and supplication"? Consider why Boccaccio might have chosen to give this specific power of correction to a woman in a period of "brutal outrage."

**3. The Transformation of the Sovereign:**
Examine the metaphor of the king being "aroused... as it were from sleep." What does this suggest about Boccaccio’s view of "faineant" or indolent leadership? Is the king’s change driven by a newfound sense of justice, or by the sting of social shame?

## IV. Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Faineant** | Spiritless, idle, or indolent; specifically used to describe the King of Cyprus's initial lack of action. |
| **Gascony** | A region in southwestern France, part of the English-controlled Duchy of Aquitaine in the 14th century. |
| **Motto** | In Italian, a pointed, witty remark or *bon mot*; used as a social weapon for shaming or correcting others. |
| **Obdurate** | Stubbornly refusing to change one's opinion or course of action; resistant to censures and chastisements. |
| **Redress** | Remedy or compensation for a wrong or grievance; what the lady initially sought but despaired of receiving. |
| **Speculum Principis** | "Mirror for princes"; a medieval literary genre intended to instruct rulers on proper conduct and morality. |
| **The Holy Sepulchre** | The site in Jerusalem where the lady of Gascony went on pilgrimage before her encounter in Cyprus. |
