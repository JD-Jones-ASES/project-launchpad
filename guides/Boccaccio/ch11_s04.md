# Study Guide: Magnanimity and Virtue in Boccaccio’s Decameron (Day X, Novel 5)

This study guide explores the tale of Madonna Dianora and Messer Ansaldo, as told by Emilia on the tenth day of Boccaccio's *Decameron*. The narrative serves as an exploration of "magnificenza" (magnificence/generosity) and the complex interplay between love, honor, and the binding nature of oaths.

## Executive Summary

Set in the region of Friuli, the story follows Madonna Dianora, who attempts to rid herself of a persistent suitor, Messer Ansaldo, by tasking him with an impossible feat: creating a blooming garden in the middle of winter. When Ansaldo employs a necromancer to fulfill the request, a chain of events is triggered that tests the virtue and generosity of the lady, her husband, the suitor, and the magician. The tale is structured as a series of renunciations, where each character attempts to outdo the other in nobility of spirit.

---

## Key Themes and Motifs

### The "Impossible Task"
A common motif in medieval literature and Arthurian romance, the impossible task is usually a stalling tactic or a way to dismiss an unwanted suitor. In this tale, the twist is that the task—producing flowers and fruit in January—is actually achieved through magic, forcing the characters to move from the realm of rhetoric to the realm of consequence.

### The Upward Spiral of Magnificenza
The story illustrates a chain reaction of generosity. 
*   **Giliberto (The Husband):** Prioritizes his wife’s honor and the sanctity of her word over his own pride and marital rights.
*   **Messer Ansaldo (The Suitor):** Moved by the husband's nobility, he renounces his "unbridled passion" and releases the lady from her oath.
*   **The Necromancer (The Magician):** Inspired by the nobility of the noblemen, he refuses his substantial monetary reward.

### Honor vs. Virtue
The narrative distinguishes between the technical fulfillment of a promise and the underlying purity of soul. Giliberto tells Dianora she may yield her "body, but not her soul," suggesting that her virtue remains intact because her intent was never to betray him, but rather to keep her word.

## Character Analysis

| Character | Role | Primary Motivation | Act of Generosity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Madonna Dianora** | A noble lady of Udine | To rid herself of an importunate suitor while maintaining her silence. | Confesses her dilemma to her husband rather than hiding it. |
| **Giliberto** | Dianora's husband | Preservation of honor and protection from the necromancer's potential malice. | Permits his wife to fulfill her oath to another man to preserve her integrity as a woman of her word. |
| **Messer Ansaldo** | A noble suitor | Ardent love and desire for Dianora. | Releases Dianora from her promise and treats her as a sister after witnessing her husband's liberality. |
| **The Necromancer** | A hired magician | Monetary gain (initially). | Forgoes his "exceeding great sum" of money after witnessing the nobility of the other characters. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **In which specific city and region does this story take place?**
    *   *Answer:* The city of Udine in the region of Friuli (northeastern Italy).
2.  **What specific conditions did Dianora set for the garden Ansaldo was to provide?**
    *   *Answer:* It had to be full of green grass, flowers, and flowering trees in the month of January, as if it were May.
3.  **How did Ansaldo manage to fulfill the impossible task?**
    *   *Answer:* He hired a necromancer (magician) for a "substantial reward" or "exceeding great sum of money" to perform spells.
4.  **What two reasons does Giliberto give for allowing Dianora to go to Ansaldo?**
    *   *Answer:* First, to ensure she is quit of her promise/oath; second, for fear that the necromancer might do them a "mischief" if Ansaldo were played false.
5.  **What was Ansaldo’s reaction when Dianora arrived at his house and explained her husband's command?**
    *   *Answer:* He was moved from "passion to compassion" and declared he would not wound the honor of a man who had shown such compassion for his love.
6.  **How long did the magical garden remain visible before the necromancer made it vanish?**
    *   *Answer:* Three days.
7.  **What was the nature of Ansaldo's affection for Dianora after these events?**
    *   *Answer:* His carnal love was spent, and he burned for her with a "flame of honourable affection," treating her as a sister.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ranking of Generosity:** At the conclusion of the tale, the narrator (Emilia) poses a question regarding who was the most generous. Analyze the sacrifices made by Giliberto, Ansaldo, and the Necromancer. Who gives up the most, and whose act of "magnificenza" is the most impressive given their social standing and original goals?
2.  **The Role of Magic and the Necromancer:** Medieval literature often blurred the lines between learned magic and demonic arts. Discuss the role of the Necromancer in this story. Why does Boccaccio include a character motivated by money in a story about noble virtue, and what does the Necromancer’s final decision say about the infectious nature of honor?
3.  **Gender and Agency:** Madonna Dianora sets the plot in motion to protect herself, but eventually, the resolution of her dilemma is handled by the men (Giliberto and Ansaldo). Examine Dianora's role in the story. Does she remain a protagonist, or does she become a pawn in a competition of male "magnificenza"?
4.  **The Concept of the Oath:** In the context of the story, why is Dianora’s "impossible" promise considered so binding that her husband would risk his own honor to see it fulfilled? Discuss the weight of the spoken word and oaths in the social fabric described by Boccaccio.

## Glossary of Key Terms

*   **Ambassages:** Messages or advertisements sent by a suitor (in this context, via a messenger woman).
*   **Brigata:** The "gay company" of storytellers in *The Decameron* (though referred to here as the company extolling Messer Gentile).
*   **Calends (of January):** The first day of the month.
*   **Friuli:** A region in northeastern Italy near modern-day Udine; chosen by Boccaccio to provide a pan-Italian scope to his work.
*   **Guerdon:** A reward or recompense.
*   **Liberality:** Generosity or the quality of being open-handed and noble in spirit.
*   **Magnificenza (Magnificence):** A high medieval virtue involving greatness of spirit, particularly in the context of being lavishly generous or noble.
*   **Necromancer:** A term used loosely here for a magician, sorcerer, or "adept" who uses spells to manipulate the natural world.
*   **Shrewd (Air):** In the context of Friuli’s climate, meaning piercingly cold or harsh.
