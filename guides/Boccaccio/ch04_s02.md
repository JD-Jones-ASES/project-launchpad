# Study Guide: The Decameron, Day the Third – Introduction

This study guide focuses on the transition to and the setting of Day III of Giovanni Boccaccio’s *The Decameron*. It explores the thematic shift to *ingegno* (wit) and the symbolic importance of the garden setting as a "pleasant place" that contrasts with the reality of the plague.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### The Theme of *Ingegno*
Under the reign of Queen Neifile, the company explores the theme of human resourcefulness. The day is dedicated to stories of individuals who use their wit to either obtain something they greatly desire or recover something they have lost. This focus on *ingegno*—intelligence and cleverness—celebrates the human ability to manipulate uncooperative circumstances through quick thinking.

### The *Locus Amoenus* (Pleasant Place)
The garden described in the introduction is a literal and literary representation of the *locus amoenus*, a convention in classical and medieval literature. It serves several purposes:
*   **Contrast:** It stands in sharp opposition to the death and chaos of plague-ravaged Florence.
*   **Symbolism:** Just as the garden imposes order on wild nature through clipped hedges and manicured lawns, the protagonists of the stories impose their will on the world through their intellect.
*   **Paradise on Earth:** The setting echoes the Garden of Eden and the enclosed gardens of courtly romance, featuring diverse flora, abundant wildlife, and sophisticated waterworks.

### The Frame Narrative (The Cornice)
The "cornice" is the frame narrative that connects the one hundred individual tales. It provides the transitions between the days of storytelling and tracks the movements of the *brigata* (the company of ten young people) as they move through various villas and gardens to escape the plague.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Who is crowned queen at the start of Day III?**
> **Answer:** Neifile is crowned queen at the beginning of the third day.

**2. What is the specific theme assigned for the stories of Day III?**
> **Answer:** The theme is tales of those who, by their wit (*ingegno*), have obtained something they greatly desired or recovered something they had lost.

**3. Describe the central feature of the lawn in the garden.**
> **Answer:** In the middle of the lawn is a basin of white marble with a central column that sends forth a powerful jet of water. This water is sufficient to turn a mill-wheel and is eventually distributed throughout the garden via hidden and visible channels.

**4. How does the garden setting mirror the thematic focus of the day’s stories?**
> **Answer:** The garden represents the imposition of beautiful order on wild nature. This mirrors how the day’s protagonists use their cleverness to impose their will on difficult or uncooperative circumstances.

**5. What types of wildlife are observed by the company in the garden?**
> **Answer:** The garden is peopled with roughly a hundred sorts of harmless creatures, including conies (rabbits), hares, goats, and fawns.

**6. What activities does the company engage in while waiting for the heat of the day to pass?**
> **Answer:** While some sleep, others stay in the garden to read romances or play games such as chess and dice.

**7. Who is the first storyteller charged by the queen to begin the tales?**
> **Answer:** Filostrato is the first to tell a story on Day III.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. The Garden as a Cultural and Literary Symbol
Analyze Boccaccio’s description of the garden in the Day III introduction. How does this setting draw upon the medieval tradition of the *locus amoenus*? Discuss how the physical elements of the garden—such as the walled perimeter, the fountain, and the variety of plants—function as symbols of both courtly romance and a restored "Eden" in the shadow of the Black Death.

### 2. The Concept of *Ingegno* vs. Fortune
While Day II focused on the "adventures of fortune," Day III shifts toward the power of *ingegno* (wit). Contrast these two forces. To what extent does Boccaccio suggest that human intelligence can overcome the random whims of fate? Use the transition between the two days to support your argument.

### 3. Architecture and Order in the Frame Narrative
Examine the role of the "seneschal" and the physical movement of the *brigata* from the first palace to the second. How does the orderly management of the group's needs (food, wine, travel, and leisure) reflect the broader themes of the *Decameron*? Discuss how the "fair and orderly manner" of their lives serves as a defense against the social disintegration caused by the plague.

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Brigata** | The company of ten young people (seven women and three men) who flee Florence to escape the plague. |
| **Cornice** | The "frame" or narrative structure that surrounds the stories told by the characters in the *Decameron*. |
| **Ingegno** | A term for wit, intelligence, resourcefulness, or cleverness; the central theme of Day III. |
| **Locus Amoenus** | Latin for "pleasant place"; a literary trope involving a standardized description of an ideal landscape, typically including shade, grass, and water. |
| **Magnifico** | A term of respect used by the company to describe the lord of the palace, implying great wealth and nobility. |
| **None** | A traditional time of day (mid-afternoon, approximately 3:00 PM) marking the end of the afternoon rest period. |
| **Seneschal** | An official or steward in a wealthy household in charge of domestic arrangements and the management of servants. |
| **Tierce** | A traditional time of day (approximately 9:00 AM). The company arrives at the new palace "well before half tierce." |
| **Vermilion** | A brilliant red color, used by Boccaccio to describe the sky at dawn. |
