# Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day II, Tale 5

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fifth tale of the Second Day of Giovanni Boccaccio's *The Decameron*. Narrated by Fiammetta, the story follows the harrowing and transformative adventures of Andreuccio di Pietro, a naive horse-dealer whose experiences in the city of Naples serve as a masterclass in medieval comic adventure and the theme of Fortune’s reversals.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Themes

### The Transformation of Andreuccio
The central arc of the story tracks the protagonist, **Andreuccio di Pietro**, as he evolves from a "raw and unwary" provincial merchant into a man who has learned to "think on his feet." 
*   **Initial State:** He arrives in Naples from Perugia with five hundred gold florins, foolishly flaunting his wealth in the public marketplace. 
*   **The Catalyst:** His education occurs through a series of "perilous scapes" involving deception, physical filth, and mortal danger.
*   **Final State:** By dawn, having survived three distinct ordeals, he returns to his inn not with horses, but with a ruby ring worth more than his original capital.

### The Character of Naples
In Boccaccio’s narrative, the city of Naples is more than a setting; it is a character in its own right. It is described as:
*   **Labyrinthine and Dark:** A place of narrow alleys and "Evil Holes" where a stranger can easily lose their way.
*   **Dangerous:** Inhabited by swindlers, prostitutes, bullies, and thieves.
*   **Full of Opportunity:** A city where the quick-witted can seize fortune even in the most desperate circumstances.

### Structural Triality
The tale is masterfully structured into three distinct episodes, each escalating in danger and stakes:
1.  **The Swindle:** Andreuccio is robbed of his money and dignity by a Sicilian prostitute.
2.  **The Well:** Andreuccio is nearly abandoned in a well by thieves and narrowly avoids capture by patrols.
3.  **The Tomb:** Andreuccio is trapped inside a sarcophagus with a dead archbishop, eventually using his wits to escape.

## II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Where is Andreuccio from, and what is his profession?**
Andreuccio is a young man from Perugia who works as a horse-dealer.

**2. Why does Andreuccio travel to Naples, and how much money does he bring?**
He travels to Naples to buy horses, carrying five hundred gold florins in his purse.

**3. How does the Sicilian girl (Fiordaliso) obtain specific information about Andreuccio’s family?**
She questions an old Sicilian woman who had previously lived with Andreuccio’s father, Pietro, in both Sicily and Perugia.

**4. What "cunning stratagem" does the girl use to lure Andreuccio to her house?**
She sends a well-trained little girl to tell Andreuccio that a "great lady" wishes to speak with him. She then pretends to be his long-lost sister, claiming to be the illegitimate daughter of his father from his time in Palermo.

**5. How does Andreuccio lose his money at the lady’s house?**
He falls through a loose plank in a private "stool" (toilet) into a foul sewer alley. While he is trapped outside, the lady steals his clothes and the money he kept on his person.

**6. Who is Scarabone Buttafuoco?**
He is the "bully" or protector of the Sicilian girl who threatens Andreuccio from the window in a "gruff and savage" voice when Andreuccio tries to reclaim his belongings.

**7. Why do the two thieves want Andreuccio to join them at the Cathedral?**
They intend to rob the tomb of Messer Filippo Minutolo, the Archbishop of Naples, who was buried that day with valuable ornaments, including a ruby ring.

**8. What happens when Andreuccio is lowered into the well?**
The thieves flee when they see a company of thirsty patrols approaching. The patrols, unaware Andreuccio is on the rope, pull him up to the brink. Terrified by the sight of him, they drop the rope and flee, allowing Andreuccio to climb out.

**9. How do the thieves trap Andreuccio inside the Archbishop’s tomb?**
After Andreuccio hands up the Archbishop's trappings (crosier, mitre, and gloves) but claims he cannot find the ring, the thieves—sensing he is trying to cheat them—pull out the prop holding the marble lid, locking him inside.

**10. How does Andreuccio finally escape the tomb?**
When a second group of grave robbers (including a priest) opens the tomb, Andreuccio grabs the priest’s leg as he enters. The terrified intruders flee in a panic, leaving the tomb open for Andreuccio to exit.

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **Fortune vs. Wit:** Analyze whether Andreuccio’s ultimate success is a result of pure luck or his developing cleverness. Cite specific moments where his character changes from "raw and unwary" to "cunning."
2.  **The Role of Setting:** Explore how Boccaccio uses the geography of Naples (the "Evil Hole," Ruga Catalana, the Cathedral) to reflect the moral and physical dangers faced by the protagonist. How does the "labyrinthine" nature of the city parallel the "labyrinthine" plot?
3.  **Deception and Identity:** Every major character Andreuccio meets is pretending to be something they are not. Discuss the theme of false identity, focusing on the Sicilian girl’s performance as a sister and the thieves’ manipulation of Andreuccio.
4.  **The Comic Macabre:** The final episode takes place in a tomb with a corpse. Discuss how Boccaccio balances the "lugubrious" and terrifying elements of the story with comic timing and irony.

## IV. Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition from Context |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Florin** | The gold coin of Florence; one of the most trusted currencies in medieval Europe. 500 florins was a substantial sum. |
| **Fiammetta** | The narrator of the tale, whose name means "Little Flame." She is identified with the woman Boccaccio loved in Naples. |
| **Vespers** | An evening prayer service; used in the text to denote the time of day (evening). |
| **Evil Hole (Malpertugio)** | A quarter in Naples known for its ill repute, where the Sicilian girl's house was located. |
| **Perforce** | By necessity or inevitably. |
| **Guelf** | A political faction; the girl’s husband is described as a "staunch Guelf" to add legitimacy to her fake story. |
| **Doublet** | A man's close-fitting padded jacket; Andreuccio strips down to this before his fall. |
| **Ordure** | Excrement or filth; Andreuccio falls into this when the plank gives way. |
| **Ruga Catalana** | A specific street in Naples that Andreuccio traverses while heading toward the sea to wash. |
| **Contrive** | To devise, plan cleverly, or bring about through ingenuity. |
| **Crosier** | A hooked staff carried by a bishop; one of the items Andreuccio loots from the tomb. |
| **Mitre** | A tall headdress worn by bishops; Andreuccio hands this up to the thieves. |
