Study Guide: The Parable of the Three Rings (The Decameron, Day I, Novel 3)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the third tale told on the first day of Boccaccio’s The Decameron. Narrated by Filomena, this story explores themes of wit, religious ambiguity, and the power of "good sense" to navigate political and financial peril.

Narrative Overview

The story is set in Alexandria and features two primary figures: Saladin, the Sultan of Egypt, and Melchisedech, a wealthy Jewish moneylender. Saladin, having depleted his treasury through wars and lavish displays of magnificence, requires a large sum of money quickly. He identifies Melchisedech as a potential source but knows the man is too miserly to lend the money willingly.

To compel him, Saladin devises a "colorably reasonable pretext" to trap the moneylender. He asks Melchisedech a dangerous question: Which of the three great religions—Judaism, Christianity, or Islam—is the true law of God? Melchisedech, recognizing the snare, avoids a direct answer by telling a parable about a father and his three sons.

The Parable of the Rings

Melchisedech tells of a wealthy man who possessed a ring of extraordinary beauty and value. The father established a tradition: whichever son he bequeathed the ring to would be his heir and the superior of the household. This continued for generations until the ring came to a father who loved his three virtuous sons equally.

Unable to choose a single heir, the father commissioned a "cunning artificer" to create two perfect copies of the ring. Before his death, he secretly gave one ring to each son. After the father died, each son claimed the inheritance, but because the rings were indistinguishable, the question of who was the true heir remained "pendent"—undecided.

Key Themes and Analysis

Wit vs. Folly

The storyteller, Filomena, frames the narrative as a lesson in how "good sense" can extricate the wise from "extremity of peril." While folly can cause a person to forfeit a happy estate, Melchisedech’s "excellent adroitness" allows him to avoid Saladin’s trap and secure a position of honor.

Religious Ambiguity

The tale suggests that while each of the three great faiths (Jews, Saracens, and Christians) believes itself to have the true inheritance and commandments of God, the truth of these claims cannot be settled by human judgment. Like the rings, the validity of each religion remains "pendent" in the eyes of the world.

Chivalric Virtue

Despite his initial intent to extort money, Saladin is portrayed as a model of chivalric virtue. Upon hearing Melchisedech’s wise response, he chooses admiration over anger. He openly confesses his original plan, and the two men establish a lifelong friendship characterized by "munificent gifts" and "amity."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why did Saladin need to approach Melchisedech for money? Saladin had spent his treasure on various wars and "lavish displays of magnificence." He faced an emergency that required a large sum of money with a "celerity adequate to his necessity."

2. What specific trap did Saladin set for Melchisedech? Saladin asked Melchisedech to judge which of the "three laws"—the law of the Jews, the Saracens, or the Christians—was the "true law." He intended to use any answer Melchisedech gave as a pretext to harass him and extort money.

3. How did the father in the parable ensure that all three of his sons felt loved? He promised the ring to each son individually. To fulfill these promises without showing favoritism, he had two copies made that were so identical that even the maker could hardly distinguish the original.

4. What was the outcome of the dispute between the three sons in the parable? The dispute remained "pendent." Because the rings were indistinguishable, the true heir could not be determined, and the suit remained unsettled.

5. How did Saladin react when he realized Melchisedech had evaded his snare? Saladin felt admiration for the Jew’s adroitness. He decided to be honest about his financial needs and his original plan. Ultimately, he repaid the borrowed money and granted Melchisedech an honorable position in his court.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Architecture of Evasion: Analyze the structure of Melchisedech’s response. Why is a story (a parable) a more effective tool for his survival than a direct philosophical or theological argument?
  2. Cross-Cultural Respect in Medieval Literature: The text describes Saladin as a "model of chivalric virtue" despite being a Muslim ruler during the Crusades. Discuss how Boccaccio uses the character of Saladin to illustrate values that transcend religious boundaries.
  3. The Concept of "Pendent" Truth: Explore the implications of the story’s conclusion that the "true law" is a question that "remains pendent." What does this suggest about Boccaccio’s perspective on religious conflict and human certainty?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Celerity Swiftness or speed of movement.
Circumspect Wary and unwilling to take risks; cautious.
Munificent Larger or more generous than is usual or necessary.
Pendent Remaining undecided or unsettled (in a legal or logical sense).
Privily Secretly; privately; without the knowledge of others.
Saracen A medieval European term for Muslims, particularly those encountered during the Crusades.
Soldan A Sultan; the ruler of a Muslim state.
Usance The practice of lending money at interest (usury).
Wit Mental sharpness and inventiveness; keen intelligence.
