Study Guide: The Decameron, Day I, Novel 3 (The Tale of the Three Rings)

This study guide provides an in-depth analysis of the third tale told on the first day of Boccaccio’s Decameron. Narrated by Filomena, this story explores the intersections of wit, religious tolerance, and the complexities of human judgment.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Power of Wit and Good Sense

The narrative serves as an illustration of how "good sense will extricate the wise from extremity of peril." While folly can cause one to forfeit a happy estate, Melchisedech demonstrates that intellectual adroitness and circumspection can transform a dangerous situation into one of "complete and assured peace."

2. Religious Pluralism and Human Judgment

The central parable of the three rings suggests that the "true" faith—whether Judaism, Christianity, or Islam—cannot be determined by human judgment. Just as the three sons could not distinguish the original ring from the perfect copies, the question of which religious law is the "true inheritance" remains "pendent" (undecided).

3. The Chivalric Virtue of Saladin

Though Saladin attempts to "entangle" Melchisedech in a trap to extort money, his reaction to being outmaneuvered is one of admiration rather than anger. Boccaccio portrays Saladin as a model of "great valour" and "magnificence," highlighting a medieval Western respect for the Sultan as a figure of chivalric virtue regardless of his faith.

4. Strategic Evasion

Melchisedech’s answer is notable for its structure: he does not answer the question directly. By using a parable, he avoids the "snare" laid by Saladin. This evasion is presented not as a lack of conviction, but as the height of wisdom in a perilous situation.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why does Saladin feel the need to "devise a colourably reasonable pretext" for taking Melchisedech’s money? Saladin has spent his treasure on "divers wars" and "lavish displays of magnificence." He needs a large sum of money quickly but does not want to use force to "constrain" the Jewish moneylender, as he prefers a seemingly legal or moral justification for the extortion.

2. Describe the physical characteristics of the rings in Melchisedech's parable. The original ring was of "extraordinary beauty and value." The two copies made by the "cunning artificer" were so similar to the original that "the maker himself could hardly tell which was the true ring."

3. What was the traditional rule regarding the descent of the original ring? The rule stated that whichever son was in possession of the ring by his father's bequest would be designated the heir and entitled to "receive from the rest the honour and homage due to a superior."

4. How does Saladin react when he realizes Melchisedech has evaded his trap? Saladin admires the Jew's "excellent adroitness" and "circumspection." Instead of proceeding with his plan to harass Melchisedech, he decides to be honest about his financial needs.

5. What is the final outcome of the interaction between Saladin and Melchisedech? Melchisedech provides Saladin with the required funds. In return, Saladin repays the loan in full, gives Melchisedech "munificent gifts," and grants him an "honourable position" near his person, establishing a lifelong friendship.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Ethics of the "Snare"

Analyze the moral landscape of the story. Saladin is described as a man of "great valour," yet he attempts to "trap" Melchisedech into a position where any answer could lead to his ruin. Discuss how Boccaccio reconciles Saladin's "chivalric virtue" with his willingness to use "extortion" and "pretext" to solve his financial problems.

2. Parable as a Shield

In the primary source, Filomena suggests the story will teach the company to be "more circumspect than you have been wont to be in answering questions." Explore the role of storytelling within the story itself. How does Melchisedech’s use of the parable function as a defensive tool, and what does this suggest about the social importance of rhetoric and "good sense" in Boccaccio’s world?

3. The Unsettled Question

The Jew concludes that the question of which law is the true law "remains pendent." Consider the historical context provided: Saladin was a Muslim sultan, and Melchisedech was a Jewish moneylender. Discuss how this narrative challenges or supports medieval views on the three great religions. Does the story advocate for religious equality, or is it merely a pragmatic take on survival in a multi-faith society?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Circumspect Cautious and prudent; wary of taking risks or giving direct answers.
Pendent Remaining undecided or unsettled; in a state of suspense.
Privily Secretly or privately; without the knowledge of others.
Saracen A medieval European term used to describe Muslims, particularly during the era of the Crusades.
Soldan A Sultan; the title for a ruler of a Muslim state, as used by Boccaccio.
Usance The practice of lending money at interest (often associated with moneylending).
Valour Great courage in the face of danger, especially in battle.
Wit Mental sharpness and inventiveness; the ability to use intelligence to solve problems or create humor.
