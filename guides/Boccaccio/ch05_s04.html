Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Tale of Lisabetta and the Pot of Basil (Decameron IV.5)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the fifth tale told on the fourth day of Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. Narrated by Filomena, this story explores the tragic intersection of romantic love, family honor, and the burgeoning merchant class values of the medieval Mediterranean.

Narrative Overview

The tale of Lisabetta is set in Messina, Sicily, an important medieval trading port. Lisabetta, a young woman living with her three wealthy merchant brothers, falls in love with Lorenzo, a Pisan youth who manages the brothers' business affairs. Upon discovering the secret affair, the brothers—motivated by concerns for family honor and commercial reputation—murder Lorenzo in secret and bury him outside the city.

Following Lorenzo’s disappearance, his ghost appears to Lisabetta in a dream, revealing the murder and the location of his body. Lisabetta locates the grave, recovers Lorenzo's head, and hides it within a large pot of basil. She spends her days weeping over the plant, which flourishes from her tears and the nutrients of the decaying remains. Eventually, her brothers grow suspicious, steal the pot, and discover the head. Fearing the discovery of their crime, they flee to Naples. Lisabetta, deprived of her only remaining connection to Lorenzo, dies of grief.

Key Concepts and Themes

1. The Merchant Class and Family Honor

Unlike the grand, rhetorical tragedy of Tancred and Ghismonda (IV.1), the story of Lisabetta is grounded in the social realism of the merchant class. The brothers do not act out of "personal cruelty" but rather a calculated desire to protect their "commercial reputation." Their decision to kill Lorenzo is a "discreet" attempt to banish a "disgrace" before it becomes public knowledge.

2. Lyrical Grief vs. Rhetorical Defiance

Boccaccio uses this tale to demonstrate his emotional range. While other tragic tales in Day IV involve grand speeches and dramatic confrontations, the story of Lisabetta is "quiet, intimate, and devastating in its simplicity." It possesses a "folk-song quality" characterized by repetitive action and haunting imagery.

3. Imagery of Growth and Decay

The central image of the pot of basil serves as a powerful symbol of the morbid union between life and death.

  • The Head: Represents the secret, gruesome reality of the brothers' violence.
  • The Basil: Specifically "basil of Salerno," it grows with "exceeding great beauty and fragrance," nourished by Lisabetta’s tears and the "richness given to the soil by the decaying head."

4. The Supernatural in Realism

The inclusion of Lorenzo’s ghost is a notable departure from the tale’s otherwise realistic social setting. The dream sequence functions as a catalyst, allowing the truth to be revealed to Lisabetta when the social structures (her brothers) have systematically silenced the truth.

Historical and Literary Context

Category Details
Geographic Setting Messina, Sicily (a major trading port).
Origin of Characters The brothers' father was from San Gimignano; Lorenzo was a Pisan.
Literary retellings Retold by John Keats in "Isabella; or, The Pot of Basil" (1818).
Artistic Influence A favorite subject of Pre-Raphaelite painters.
Musical Connection The tale concludes with a reference to a traditional Sicilian folk song regarding the stolen pot of basil.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Where do Lisabetta’s brothers move at the end of the story, and why?
    • Answer: They move to Naples to hide their crime after discovering Lorenzo’s head in the pot of basil and fearing the matter would be "bruited abroad."
  2. How does Lisabetta discover what happened to Lorenzo?
    • Answer: Lorenzo appears to her in a dream, "wan and in utter disarray," telling her he was murdered by her brothers and revealing the location of his shallow grave.
  3. What specific plant does Lisabetta grow, and what is its origin?
    • Answer: She grows basil of Salerno.
  4. How do the neighbors alert the brothers to Lisabetta's behavior?
    • Answer: They observe her "daily wont" of sitting beside the pot and weeping until her beauty is ruined and her "eyes as it were evanished from her head."
  5. What is the brothers' initial reaction to discovering the affair?
    • Answer: The eldest brother observes her in secret and, being a man of "discretion," consults his brothers to determine a way to "banish this disgrace" silently.
  6. What was Lorenzo's role in the brothers' household?
    • Answer: He was a Pisan who "managed all their affairs" in their merchant shop.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. A Critique of Merchant Values: Analyze how Boccaccio uses the characters of the three brothers to critique the values of the 14th-century merchant class. To what extent is their "discretion" actually a form of moral bankruptcy?
  2. The Nature of Lisabetta’s Grief: Examine the transition of Lisabetta’s grief from "total dejection" to a ritualistic obsession with the pot of basil. How does Boccaccio use the physical act of watering the plant with tears to illustrate the depth of her tragedy?
  3. Comparative Narrative Styles: Contrast the narrative style of Filomena’s tale (IV.5) with the more rhetorical style found in other tragedies of the Fourth Day. How does the "lyrical and haunting" tone of this story affect the reader’s perception of the brothers’ crime?

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Basil of Salerno: A high-quality variety of basil used by Lisabetta to cover Lorenzo's head.
  • Fere: A companion or lover (used in the provided folk song translation).
  • Messina: A Sicilian port city where the events of the story take place.
  • Ottava Rima: The eight-line stanza form used by Boccaccio in his earlier poetry and later adopted by John Keats for his retelling of this story.
  • Pisan: A native of Pisa; Lorenzo’s city of origin.
  • San Gimignano: The original home of the brothers' father.
  • Wight: An archaic term for a human being or person (used in the provided folk song translation).
  • Yare: An archaic term meaning quickly or readily.
