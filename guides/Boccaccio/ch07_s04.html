Study Guide: The Decameron, Day VI, Tale 10

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the tenth tale told on the sixth day of Boccaccio’s Decameron. It focuses on the character of Fra Cipolla, the power of rhetoric, and the satire of medieval religious practices.

I. Summary and Key Concepts

The Setting and Characters

The story is set in Certaldo, a small Tuscan town (and Boccaccio’s ancestral home). The central figure is Fra Cipolla ("Brother Onion"), a friar of the Order of St. Antony. Despite having no formal learning, he is a master rhetorician whose verbal skill is compared to that of Tully (Cicero) or Quintilian.

Key Supporting Characters:

  • Guccio: Fra Cipolla’s servant, also known by the derogatory nicknames Balena (Whale), Imbratta (Filth), and Porco (Hog). He is described as having nine negative qualities, including being lazy, uncleanly, and a liar.
  • Giovanni del Bragoniera and Biagio Pizzini: Two local "wags" or pranksters who are friends with the friar but decide to test his wit by stealing his relic.
  • Nuta: A coarse, misshapen kitchen maid whom Guccio attempts to woo, leading him to neglect his duties.

The Plot: The Relic and the Prank

Fra Cipolla arrives in Certaldo to collect alms. To encourage donations, he promises the townspeople they will see a sacred relic: a feather from the wing of the Angel Gabriel, left behind in the Virgin Mary’s room during the Annunciation.

While the friar is at breakfast, Giovanni and Biagio sneak into his room. They find a parrot's feather (an exotic rarity in Certaldo at the time) wrapped in silk within a casket. They steal the feather and replace it with pieces of charcoal. Guccio, tasked with guarding the room, abandons his post to flirt with Nuta in the kitchen.

The Climax: The Geography of Nonsense

When Fra Cipolla opens the casket during his sermon, he finds the charcoal. Without hesitating, he improvises a magnificent, rambling account of his travels to the "Holy Land." He describes a "Geography of Nonsense," listing fictional or satirically named locations:

  • Truffia and Buffia: Lands of cajolery and drollery.
  • Menzogna: The land of lies.
  • India Pastinaca: Where "pruning-hooks fly."

He claims that the Patriarch of Jerusalem gave him many relics, including the sound of the bells of Solomon's temple and the feather of the Angel Gabriel. He explains the presence of the charcoal by claiming he has two identical caskets—one for the feather and one for the coals used to roast St. Lawrence. He asserts that God switched the boxes so that he would show the coals just two days before the feast of St. Lawrence, to rekindle the people's devotion.

Outcome

The townspeople are awestruck and contribute more money than ever to be touched by the "blessed" charcoal. The pranksters, impressed by Cipolla’s ready wit, return the feather and share a laugh with him.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. What is the significance of Fra Cipolla’s name in the context of Certaldo? The name means "Brother Onion." The region of Certaldo was famous throughout Tuscany for its onions, making the name a fitting and humorous choice for a friar who found "good pasture" (plentiful donations) there.

2. List four of the "nine qualities" Fra Cipolla attributes to his servant Guccio. The text lists: lazy, uncleanly, a liar, negligent, disobedient, foul-mouthed, reckless, witless, and mannerless.

3. Why were the townspeople of Certaldo so easily fooled by a parrot's feather? The text explains that the luxuries of Egypt (like parrots) had not yet become common in Tuscany. The simple folk of the countryside had mostly never seen or heard of such a bird.

4. How does Fra Cipolla describe the "payment" used in the land of Menzogna? He describes people "paying in nought but unminted coin," which is a metaphorical reference to paying with false promises.

5. What miracle does Fra Cipolla claim the charcoal can perform? He tells the congregation that anyone marked with a cross from these coals will live secure for the year, protected from the touch of fire.

6. Who takes over as King of the company at the end of Day VI? Dioneo is crowned King after the Queen (Elisa) completes her reign.

7. What is the "Ladies' Vale" (Valle delle Donne)? It is a secluded, perfectly circular plain surrounded by six hills, featuring a crystal-clear lake and lush vegetation. The ladies of the company go there to bathe and relax.

8. What theme does Dioneo set for the stories of the Seventh Day? He ordains that the company shall discuss "the tricks that, either for love or for their deliverance from peril, ladies have heretofore played their husbands."

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Power of Rhetoric: Analyze how Fra Cipolla uses language not to convey truth, but to reshape reality. How does his "Geography of Nonsense" serve to both confuse and impress his audience? Discuss the idea that, in this tale, the performance of the sermon is more important than the authenticity of the relic.
  2. Satire of Medieval Religion: Boccaccio parodies the medieval trade in relics through the "feather of Gabriel" and the "charcoal of St. Lawrence." Using the text, discuss what this tale suggests about the relationship between the clergy and the "simple folk" (the gullible laity).
  3. The Role of the Servant: Contrast Fra Cipolla with his servant Guccio. While both are described in somewhat negative terms (the friar as a "shameless" improvisor and Guccio as a "rascallion"), how does their lack of "virtue, wisdom, or holiness" manifest differently in their actions?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Certaldo A small Tuscan hill town; the setting of the tale and ancestral home of Boccaccio.
Fra An abbreviation of frate (friar), a title for members of mendicant religious orders.
Relic A physical object associated with a saint or holy person, often venerated by the faithful.
Menzogna An Italian word meaning "lies"; used by Cipolla to name a fictional land in his sermon.
India Pastinaca A fictional location in Cipolla’s sermon; "Pastinaca" refers to a type of fish used as a type for the outlandish.
Tully / Quintilian Famous Roman rhetoricians to whom Fra Cipolla is compared due to his speaking ability.
St. Lawrence A Christian martyr who was famously executed by being roasted on a gridiron.
Lief An archaic term meaning "gladly" or "willingly."
Seneschal An officer in a medieval household in charge of domestic arrangements and feasts.
Cornemuse A type of bagpipe used for musical accompaniment during dances.
