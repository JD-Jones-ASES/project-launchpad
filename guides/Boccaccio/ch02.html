Study Guide: Wit and Wisdom in Day I of The Decameron

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the first day of storytelling in Giovanni Boccaccio’s The Decameron. It analyzes the central themes of wit, the mechanisms of medieval comedy, the portrayal of religious institutions, and the individual tales that serve as an overture to the entire work.

I. Overview of Day I

Following the outbreak of the Black Death in Florence in 1348, a brigata (company) of seven young women and three young men retreats to a country estate in Fiesole. To establish order amidst the chaos of the plague, they elect Pampinea as their first queen.

On Day I, Pampinea sets no specific theme, allowing the narrators to speak on any subject they please. The resulting ten tales function as a panoramic introduction to Boccaccio’s world, touching upon religious fraud, interfaith wisdom, royal justice, and social resourcefulness.

The Setting: Fiesole

Fiesole is an ancient town overlooking Florence, traditionally associated with rural retreats and villas. The transition from the "disordered world" of the plague to the "civilized order" of the estate is marked by the group’s first meal, their agreement on storytelling rules, and their gathering in a lush meadow to begin the day's entertainment.

II. Key Concepts and Mechanisms of Comedy

The comedy and social commentary of Day I are driven by three primary concepts:

Concept Definition Function in the Text
Ingegno Intelligence, wit, and resourcefulness. The supreme virtue celebrated by Boccaccio; it allows characters to overcome obstacles and escape danger.
Motto A pointed, clever remark or verbal retort. A "verbal weapon" used to shame, correct, or delight. It is often a single stroke of brilliance (e.g., Novel I.9).
Beffa A sustained practical joke or elaborate deception. The engine of comedy that relies on long-term manipulation (e.g., Ser Ciappelletto’s false confession in Novel I.1).

The Question of Religion

Religion is a pervasive theme in Day I, but Boccaccio's focus is the human institutions that represent God rather than faith itself.

  • Anticlerical Satire: The tales frequently target the hypocrisy of the clergy. In Novel I.4, a monk and abbot share in a sin to avoid punishment; in Novel I.6, a man exposes the greed of the Inquisition.
  • Religious Tolerance: Novel I.3 (The Three Rings) suggests that the "truth" of a faith cannot be settled by human argument, advocating for humility and mutual respect.
  • Comparison to Chaucer: Like Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales, Boccaccio explores the gap between profession and practice. Both authors demonstrate how truth can emerge even from the mouths of liars or corrupt clerics.

III. Summary of the Tales of Day I

Major Tales

  • Novel I.1 (Ser Ciappelletto): Ser Ciappelletto, a man described as "the worst man ever born," falls ill in Burgundy. To protect his hosts from social ruin, he performs a masterful beffa by delivering a false deathbed confession to a holy friar. He portrays himself as a saintly figure of extreme purity. After his death, the friar's sermon convinces the public of his holiness, and he is eventually venerated as "San Ciappelletto."
  • Novel I.3 (The Three Rings): The Sultan Saladin tries to trap Melchisedech, a Jewish moneylender, by asking which of the three great laws (Judaism, Islam, Christianity) is true. Melchisedech responds with a parable about a father who gave three identical rings to his three sons. Since the "true" ring could not be identified, the inheritance remained "pendent." Saladin admires the wit and rewards Melchisedech.
  • Novel I.9 (The King of Cyprus): A lady of Gascony is insulted in Cyprus. Knowing the King is too "spiritless" to seek justice, she shames him with a single motto, asking how he bears the insults to his own crown. The king is "aroused from sleep" and becomes a rigorous administrator of justice.

Other Tales of Day I

  • Novel I.2 (Abraham the Jew): A Jew converts to Christianity after visiting Rome; he reasons that if the religion can flourish despite the visible corruption of the Papal court, it must have divine support.
  • Novel I.4 (The Monk and the Abbot): A young monk caught with a woman avoids punishment by discovering the abbot is guilty of the same sin.
  • Novel I.5 (The Marchioness of Monferrato): The Marchioness uses a banquet of only chicken to rebuke the King of France's unwanted romantic advances, proving that all women are essentially the same.
  • Novel I.6 (The Good Man and the Inquisition): A man avoids a heresy charge by using a witty remark about the inquisitor’s love of wine.
  • Novel I.7 & I.8: Tales of storytellers and courtiers (Bergamino and Guglielmo Borsiere) using allegories and single words ("Generosity") to shame miserly lords into hospitality.
  • Novel I.10 (Master Alberto): An elderly physician uses a parable about leeks to defend his love for a younger woman and rebuke her mockery.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who is elected the first queen of the brigata, and why?
  2. What is the difference between a motto and a beffa?
  3. In Novel I.1, what is the specific "greatest sin" that Ciappelletto confesses to the friar?
  4. How does the setting of Fiesole contrast with the opening of the Decameron?
  5. What is the "ironic logic" found in the tale of Abraham the Jew (Novel I.2)?
  6. According to the parable in Novel I.3, why is the question of the "true" religion still "pendent"?
  7. What does the Italian term motto encompass beyond its modern English meaning?
  8. How does Master Alberto defend his feelings for a younger woman in Novel I.10?
  9. Why does the lady in Novel I.9 say she wants to learn from the King of Cyprus?
  10. What was Boccaccio's later attitude toward the Decameron in 1362, and who persuaded him not to destroy it?

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. Appearance vs. Reality in the Sacred Sphere: Analyze the tale of Ser Ciappelletto (I.1). Does the fact that Ciappelletto’s false confession leads to real miracles suggest that Boccaccio is mocking religion, or is he making a more complex point about the nature of faith and human credulity?
  2. The Power of the Word: Using Novels I.5, I.9, and I.10, discuss how language functions as a tool of social power for those who may lack physical or institutional authority (such as women or the elderly).
  3. Medieval Tolerance and the Three Rings: Examine the parable of the three rings in Novel I.3. How does this story reflect the medieval concept of religious tolerance? Compare this to the "ironic logic" of Novel I.2.
  4. The Role of the Artist: Novel I.7 and I.8 deal with storytellers and courtiers who use their wit to influence the powerful. What do these tales suggest about Boccaccio’s view of the artist’s role in a courtly or communal society?

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Allegory: A narrative where characters/events represent abstract ideas. The Decameron generally favors realism over this form.
  • Angevin: Relating to the House of Anjou, which ruled Naples; Boccaccio spent his formative years in this court.
  • Anticlerical Satire: Mockery of the clergy's corruption or hypocrisy.
  • Beffa: A sustained practical joke or deception; a primary engine of comedy in the text.
  • Black Death: The 1347–1351 bubonic plague pandemic that serves as the Decameron’s backdrop.
  • Bottega: A workshop or shop; the basic unit of Florentine commerce.
  • Brigata: The "company" of ten young people who flee the plague.
  • Certaldo: Boccaccio’s family home in Tuscany where he died in 1375.
  • Chivalry: The medieval code of knightly conduct (honor, courtesy, service).
  • Commune: A self-governing city-state like Florence.
  • Contado: The rural territory surrounding and dependent on an Italian city-state.
  • Cornice: Italian for "frame"; the outer narrative of the brigata.
  • Courtly Love: The literary convention of idealized romantic love.
  • Exemplum: A short story used in sermons to teach a moral; Boccaccio often subverts this form.
  • Fabliau: A humorous, bawdy verse tale from France that influenced Boccaccio’s beffe.
  • Fiammetta: Boccaccio’s literary muse and a member of the brigata.
  • Florence: The Tuscan city-state that is the primary setting of the Decameron.
  • Fortuna: Fate or chance, personified as a goddess.
  • Frame Narrative: A story within a story.
  • Gentildonna: A gentlewoman or lady of good birth.
  • Guild: A professional association of merchants or craftsmen in Florence.
  • Humanism: The intellectual movement emphasizing classical texts and human reason.
  • Ingegno: Intelligence, wit, and resourcefulness.
  • Interdict: A papal decree barring a community from the sacraments.
  • Irony: A technique where the surface meaning differs from the deeper significance.
  • Magnificenza: Magnanimity or greatness of soul.
  • Mendicant Orders: Religious orders (e.g., Franciscans) who lived by begging.
  • Motto: A witty remark or clever verbal retort.
  • Novella: A short prose tale; in Italian, "a little new thing."
  • Papal Schism: A period of division within the Catholic Church.
  • Picaresque: A genre featuring the adventures of a roguish hero.
  • Podestà: The chief magistrate of an Italian city-state.
  • Proem: An introductory passage or preface.
  • Realism: A literary mode aiming to represent everyday life as it is.
  • Romance: A genre featuring chivalric quests and idealized love.
  • Satire: Literature using humor or ridicule to criticize folly or vice.
  • Signoria: The governing council of Florence.
  • Sprezzatura: The art of making difficult tasks look easy.
  • Tre Corone: "Three Crowns"—the name for Dante, Petrarch, and Boccaccio.
  • Usury: Lending money at interest, often a source of moral complication in the tales.
  • Vernacular: The common spoken language (Tuscan Italian) used by Boccaccio.
