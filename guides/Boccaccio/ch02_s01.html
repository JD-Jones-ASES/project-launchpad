Study Guide: Boccaccio’s Decameron, Day I – Wit and Wisdom

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the first day of Boccaccio's Decameron. It explores the structural role of Day I as an "overture," examines the primary comedic mechanisms of the work, analyzes the treatment of religious institutions, and provides tools for self-assessment and deeper reflection.

Overview: Day I as Overture

Day I serves as a panoramic introduction to the world of the Decameron. Because the queen for the day, Pampinea, sets no specific theme, the narrators provide a wide-ranging survey of the human experience. These ten tales introduce the major motifs that recur throughout the hundred stories, including:

  • Religious fraud and institutional hypocrisy.
  • Interfaith wisdom and religious tolerance.
  • The exercise of royal justice.
  • The social power of quick-witted women.
  • The resourcefulness of courtiers and artists.

The Two Engines of Comedy

The comedy of the Decameron is driven by two principal mechanisms that celebrate ingegno (intelligence and resourcefulness):

  1. The Motto: A quick, pointed remark or cleverly turned phrase deployed at a precise moment to correct, shame, or delight. It is verbal cleverness in a single stroke (e.g., the Gascon lady’s rebuke to the King of Cyprus).
  2. The Beffa: A sustained practical joke or elaborate deception. It often involves long-term planning and execution (e.g., Ciappelletto’s false confession).

Thematic Analysis: Religion and Hypocrisy

The Decameron presents an unsettling portrait of religious institutions, though not necessarily of faith itself. Boccaccio’s satire targets human hypocrisy and the failure of the clergy to live up to their vows, rather than God or the core tenets of religion.

Key Religious Narratives

  • The Problem of Credulity (I.1): Ciappelletto, a man of profound wickedness, exploits the assumption of sincerity required for social life. By deceiving a friar during a deathbed confession, he is venerated as a saint. This highlights the gap between human perception and divine reality.
  • The Paradox of Corruption (I.2): In the story of Abraham the Jew, the corruption of the papal court in Rome becomes a logical proof of divine support. Abraham reasons that if Christianity can flourish despite such internal rot, it must be supported by God.
  • The Parable of the Three Rings (I.3): This tale argues that the "true" faith cannot be determined through human argument. It advocates for wisdom, humility, and mutual respect over dogmatism, suggesting that religious truth is a matter of inheritance and belief rather than verifiable fact.

Literary Parallel: Boccaccio and Chaucer

Boccaccio’s treatment of the clergy mirrors that of Geoffrey Chaucer in The Canterbury Tales. Specifically, Chaucer’s Pardoner—a corrupt cleric who admits his own greed—tells a morally powerful tale. Both authors explore the "disturbing possibility" that profound truth can emerge from the mouths of liars and hypocrites.

Summary of the Ten Tales

Novel Primary Figures Key Theme / Mechanism Summary
I.1 Ciappelletto The Beffa / Falsehood A wicked man makes a false confession on his deathbed and is subsequently venerated as a saint.
I.2 Abraham the Jew & Jehannot Ironic Logic A Jewish merchant converts to Christianity after seeing the corruption in Rome, believing only a divinely protected faith could survive such depravity.
I.3 Melchisedech & Saladin Wisdom / Parable A Jew avoids a trap set by a Sultan by telling a parable about three identical rings representing three religions.
I.4 Monk & Abbot Hypocrisy A monk escapes punishment for a sexual indiscretion by proving his abbot is guilty of the same sin.
I.5 Marchioness of Monferrato The Motto The Marchioness uses a banquet of chickens to rebuff the King of France’s unwanted advances.
I.6 A Good Man & Inquisitor Wit vs. Power A man uses a witty remark about wine to expose the greed and hypocrisy of an inquisitor.
I.7 Bergamino & Can Grande Storytelling as Defense A storyteller uses an allegory about a hungry poet to shame a miserly lord into providing hospitality.
I.8 Guglielmo Borsiere The Motto A courtier shames a legendary miser into reform by suggesting he paint a mural of "Generosity."
I.9 Gascon Lady & King The Motto A woman’s single-sentence rebuke transforms the character of a weak and cowardly king.
I.10 Master Alberto Defense of Maturity An elderly physician uses a parable about leeks to defend his right to love and shame the young women mocking him.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the literal meaning and function of a motto in the context of Day I?
  2. Define beffa and identify which tale serves as its "supreme" example.
  3. Why does Abraham the Jew decide to convert to Christianity after witnessing the corruption of the clergy in Rome?
  4. What is the specific target of Boccaccio's satire regarding religion?
  5. How does the Marchioness of Monferrato use a banquet of chickens to deliver a message to the King of France?
  6. In the tale of the Three Rings (I.3), what is the suggested response to the question of which religion is the "true" one?
  7. What quality does Boccaccio value "above almost all others" as demonstrated by his characters?
  8. How did Boccaccio’s friend Petrarch influence the history of the Decameron in 1362?
  9. What is the significance of the monk’s defense in tale I.4?
  10. Explain the "masterpiece of ironic logic" found in tale I.2.
  11. How does Guglielmo Borsiere shame Ermino de' Grimaldi in tale I.8?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  • The Paradox of Sanctity: Analyze the character of Ciappelletto (I.1). Does the narrator Panfilo’s framing—that God’s grace works through unlikely instruments—excuse Ciappelletto’s actions, or does it deepen the irony of his "sainthood"?
  • Religious Pluralism in the Middle Ages: Using the "Tale of the Three Rings" (I.3), discuss Boccaccio’s stance on religious tolerance. To what extent does this tale advocate for relativism versus humility in the face of the unknown?
  • The Agency of Women: Examine the use of the motto by female characters in Day I (e.g., the Marchioness in I.5 and the Gascon lady in I.9). What does their ability to "transform character" or rebuff kings suggest about Boccaccio’s view of women’s intelligence and social power?
  • Narrative as Power: Explore tale I.7 (Bergamino and the Miser). How does this story serve as a defense of the artist’s role in society and the inherent power of storytelling to influence the ruling class?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Beffa A sustained practical joke or trick, often involving elaborate deception; the primary "engine of comedy" in the Decameron.
Confession The sacramental practice of admitting sins to a priest for absolution; a requirement for all Christians after 1215.
Ingegno Intelligence, resourcefulness, and wit; the character trait most highly valued by Boccaccio.
Motto A clever, pointed remark deployed to correct or shame another; a shorter form of wit than the beffa.
Overture A term used to describe Day I, as it introduces the major themes and diverse social world of the entire collection.
Providence Divine guidance or care; ironically invoked in tale I.2 to explain the survival of a corrupt church.
Satire The use of humor or irony to expose and criticize human vices or institutional hypocrisy.
