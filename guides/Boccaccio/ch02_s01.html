Day I of the Decameron: Wit, Wisdom, and the Human Comedy

The ten tales of Day I serve as an overture to Giovanni Boccaccio's Decameron. Because the queen of the day, Pampinea, sets no specific theme, the narrators provide a panoramic introduction to Boccaccio’s world, featuring a diverse cast of characters: religious frauds, interfaith figures, royal justice, sharp-tongued women, and quick-witted courtiers.

Key Concepts and Engines of Comedy

The comedy within the Decameron is driven by two primary mechanisms that celebrate ingegno—the intelligence and resourcefulness that Boccaccio values above almost all other qualities.

Concept Definition Function in the Text
Motto A clever, pointed remark deployed at a precise moment. Used to correct, shame, or delight. It can transform character or defeat institutional power.
Beffa A sustained practical joke or deception. Involves elaborate planning and deception; acts as the primary engine of comedy throughout the work.
Ingegno Human intelligence and resourcefulness. The quality celebrated through both the motto and the beffa.

Religious Satire and Institutional Hypocrisy

Religion is a pervasive theme in Day I, but the text distinguishes between faith itself and the human institutions that represent it. Boccaccio’s target is typically hypocrisy and human credulity rather than God.

Major Religious Themes

  • The Problem of Credulity: In the tale of Ciappelletto (I.1), a wicked man exploits the assumption of sincerity required for social life. The friar who hears his confession is not depicted as stupid, but as unable to imagine a dying man would lie during a sacrament.
  • Clerical Hypocrisy: Several tales (I.4, I.6) highlight members of the clergy who fail to live up to their vows. Boccaccio suggests that those who judge others must first be capable of judging themselves.
  • The Complexity of Truth: Through the character of Abraham the Jew (I.2), Boccaccio uses ironic logic to suggest that the very corruption of the Church in Rome is proof of divine support, as the religion flourishes despite its leadership.
  • Religious Tolerance: The parable of the three rings (I.3) posits that the "true" faith cannot be settled by human argument. It advocates for humility and mutual respect over dogmatism.

Summary of the Tales of Day I

The following table summarizes the essential action and themes of the tales comprising the opening day.

Tale Primary Characters Summary of Action Core Theme/Mechanism
I.1 Ciappelletto A wicked man gives a false confession on his deathbed and is venerated as a saint. The supreme beffa; human credulity.
I.2 Abraham the Jew; Jehannot Abraham visits Rome, sees the corruption of the papacy, and converts because the religion survives despite it. Ironic logic; divine providence.
I.3 Melchisedech; Saladin Melchisedech avoids a financial trap by telling a parable about three identical rings given by a father to his sons. Wisdom and religious humility.
I.4 Monk and Abbot A monk caught with a woman avoids punishment by proving his abbot is guilty of the same sin. Exposure of hypocrisy.
I.5 Marchioness of Monferrato; King of France The Marchioness rebuffs the King’s advances by serving only chicken, noting that women are all the same regardless of status. The motto; women's wit.
I.6 A "Good Man"; An Inquisitor A man accused of heresy uses a witty remark about wine to expose an inquisitor's own greed. Wit vs. institutional power.
I.7 Bergamino; A Miserly Lord Bergamino tells an allegory about a hungry poet to shame a host into providing hospitality. The power of storytelling.
I.8 Guglielmo Borsiere; Ermino de' Grimaldi A courtier shames a miserly man into reform by suggesting he paint "Generosity." The motto; social correction.
I.9 Gascon Lady; King of Cyprus A woman’s sharp rebuke transforms a weak and cowardly king into a just ruler. The motto; transformation of character.
I.10 Master Alberto; A Young Lady An elderly physician defends his right to love by using a parable about leeks to mock a young woman's vanity. Defense of mature love; quick wit.

Short-Answer Practice Quiz

  1. What is the difference between a motto and a beffa in the context of the Decameron?
  2. Why does Abraham the Jew decide to convert to Christianity after seeing the corruption in Rome (I.2)?
  3. In Tale I.3, what do the three rings represent in Melchisedech’s parable?
  4. According to the commentary, what was Boccaccio’s target in his satirical treatment of religion?
  5. Which friend of Boccaccio reportedly talked him out of destroying the Decameron in 1362?
  6. How does the Marchioness of Monferrato use a banquet of chicken to deliver a message to the King of France (I.5)?
  7. What specific word does Guglielmo Borsiere use to shame the miser Ermino de' Grimaldi (I.8)?
  8. What is the "ironic logic" present in the tale of Abraham the Jew?
  9. In Tale I.4, why does the Abbot pardon the monk who was caught with a woman?
  10. What does the narrator Panfilo suggest about God’s grace in the introduction to the tale of Ciappelletto?

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of the Beffa: Analyze the character of Ciappelletto in Tale I.1. Does Boccaccio present him as a hero of ingegno or a warning against wickedness? Discuss how the "supreme beffa" of the false confession challenges the reader's understanding of sanctity and truth.
  2. Women and Social Power: Examine the role of women's speech in Tales I.5, I.9, and I.10. How do these women use the motto to navigate social challenges and exert power over men of higher status or different social standings?
  3. Religious Tolerance and Human Limitation: Using Tale I.3 (The Three Rings) as your primary evidence, argue whether Boccaccio is advocating for religious relativism or a more specific form of medieval humility. How does this tale contrast with the more cynical depictions of religious figures in Day I?
  4. The Gap Between Profession and Practice: Compare Boccaccio's treatment of corrupt clerics with that of Geoffrey Chaucer (as mentioned in the commentary). Discuss the narrative value of the "liar telling the truth" and what it reveals about Boccaccio’s view of human institutions.

Glossary of Important Terms

  • Beffa: A sustained practical joke or elaborate deception that serves as a primary source of comedy in the Decameron.
  • Confession: A sacramental practice involving the confession of sins to a priest for absolution; required annually for Christians by 1215.
  • Ingegno: The quality of intelligence, resourcefulness, and verbal cleverness.
  • Inquisition: A powerful human institution within the Church intended to suppress heresy, often depicted by Boccaccio as hypocritical.
  • Motto: A cleverly turned phrase or pointed remark used to correct, shame, or delight.
  • Overture: A term used to describe Day I, as it introduces nearly every major theme of the entire collection without a set theme.
  • Parable: A simple story used to illustrate a moral or spiritual lesson, such as Master Alberto’s leeks or Melchisedech’s rings.
