# Study Guide: The Tale of Nastagio degli Onesti (Decameron, V, 8)

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the eighth tale of the fifth day of Boccaccio’s *Decameron*. It includes key narrative concepts, practice questions, and a glossary of terms to facilitate a deep understanding of the text's themes, characters, and historical context.

---

## Key Concepts and Narrative Overview

### Context and Setting
The tale is narrated by Filomena and is set in the ancient city of **Ravenna** and its surrounding pine forests (**pineta**). Historically, Ravenna was an Adriatic coastal city famous for its Byzantine mosaics and as the final resting place of Dante Alighieri. The story centers on **Nastagio degli Onesti**, a wealthy young nobleman who has inherited a vast fortune but is driven to the brink of despair by his unrequited love for the daughter of **Messer Paolo Traversaro**.

### The Motif of the Infernal Hunt
A central element of the story is the **"infernal hunt,"** a medieval literary motif where the damned are condemned to an eternal cycle of pursuit and punishment. In this version:
*   **The Hunter:** Messer Guido degli Anastagi, a knight who committed suicide because of the cruelty of his beloved.
*   **The Hunted:** A naked damsel who rejected Guido’s love and exulted in his death.
*   **The Punishment:** Every Friday, Guido pursues the woman, kills her with the rapier he used to kill himself, rips out her "hard and cold heart," and feeds it to his mastiffs. She then rises to be hunted again.

### Nastagio’s Strategy
Upon witnessing this supernatural vision, Nastagio transitions from a victim of unrequited love to a strategist. Rather than viewing the vision solely as a terrifying warning of divine justice, he recognizes its potential as a **tool of persuasion**. He arranges a banquet for his beloved and her family at the exact location and time of the ghostly hunt, ensuring she witnesses the gruesome fate of a woman who was "harsh and ruthless" to her lover.

### The Resolution and its Implications
The tale concludes with the beloved woman, terrified by the vision, immediately reciprocating Nastagio’s affection to avoid a similar fate. They are married the following Sunday. The story suggests that this event had a broader social impact, making the ladies of Ravenna more "compliant with men's desires."

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Who is the narrator of this tale?**
*Answer:* The tale is told by Filomena at the behest of the queen.

**2. What causes Nastagio degli Onesti to leave Ravenna and camp in Chiassi?**
*Answer:* His kinsfolk and friends, fearing he was wasting his wealth and himself on a woman who disdained him, urged him to leave to cool his passions and reduce his spending.

**3. Describe the vision Nastagio sees in the pine forest.**
*Answer:* He sees a beautiful naked woman running through the briers, being bitten by two fierce mastiffs and pursued by a knight on a black horse who threatens her with death.

**4. Why is Messer Guido degli Anastagi condemned to this eternal hunt?**
*Answer:* He fell into despair and killed himself because of his unrequited love for the damsel; the damsel is condemned because she did not repent for her cruelty and joy at his suffering.

**5. How long must the hunt continue?**
*Answer:* Guido is to pursue her for as many years as the number of months she showed herself harsh to him.

**6. What specific action does the knight perform after killing the damsel?**
*Answer:* He opens her back with a knife, removes her heart and inward parts, and throws them to his famished dogs to be devoured.

**7. How does Nastagio use the vision to his advantage?**
*Answer:* He invites his beloved, her parents, and other ladies to a breakfast at the site of the hunt on a Friday so they will see the punishment of the cruel woman.

**8. What is the immediate reaction of Nastagio’s beloved after seeing the vision?**
*Answer:* She is so appalled and terrified that she converts her aversion into affection and sends her chambermaid to tell Nastagio she is ready to pleasure him.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

### 1. Divine Justice vs. Worldly Manipulation
Filomena introduces the story as an example of "Divine justice" punishing cruelty. However, the narrative focuses heavily on Nastagio’s tactical use of the supernatural vision. Does the story truly reflect a moral lesson about divine retribution, or is it a cynical exploration of how fear can be used to manipulate others into compliance?

### 2. The Portrayal of the "Cruel Lady"
Analyze the characterization of the daughter of Messer Paolo Traversaro. The text describes her as "haughty and disdainful" because of her beauty and birth. Compare her character and her eventual "conversion" to the ghostly woman in the forest. How does the text justify the violence used—both spectral and psychological—to change her mind?

### 3. Social Impact and Gender Dynamics
The tale concludes by stating that the ladies of Ravenna became "much more compliant" after witnessing these events. Discuss the implications of this ending. What does the story suggest about the power dynamics between men and women in the context of courtly love and marriage?

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Anon** | An archaic term meaning "soon," "shortly," or "at once." |
| **Chiassi** | A location approximately three miles from Ravenna where Nastagio pitched his tents. |
| **Dante** | A famous Italian poet who died in 1321 and is buried in Ravenna; his presence adds to the city's literary significance. |
| **Infernal Hunt** | A widespread medieval motif involving a ghostly huntsman pursuing a woman or animal as an eternal punishment for sin. |
| **Messer** | A title of respect for a gentleman or nobleman. |
| **Nastagio degli Onesti** | The protagonist of the tale, a wealthy nobleman of Ravenna. |
| **Pineta** | The celebrated pine forests south of Ravenna, used as the setting for the supernatural events in the tale. |
| **Rapier** | The weapon used by Messer Guido to kill himself and subsequently used to "slaughter" the damsel in the vision. |
| **Ravenna** | An ancient city on Italy’s Adriatic coast, known for its Byzantine mosaics and historical importance. |
| **Romagna** | The region in Italy where the city of Ravenna is located. |
