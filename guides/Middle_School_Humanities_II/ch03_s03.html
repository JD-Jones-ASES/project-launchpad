Study Guide: The Crusades & The Age of Chivalry

Overview

This section provides a historical summary of the Crusades, beginning with the spiritual significance of Jerusalem for medieval Christians and the geopolitical shifts that led to the call for holy war. It details Pope Urban II's motivations and the powerful appeal that launched the First Crusade, the subsequent establishment of Crusader States, and the emergence of military orders. The narrative continues through the disastrous Second Crusade, the rise of the formidable Muslim leader Saladin and his recapture of Jerusalem, and the grand, yet ultimately incomplete, efforts of the Third Crusade.

Key Concepts

  • The Significance of Jerusalem: For medieval Christians, Jerusalem was not merely a city but the holiest place on earth, the site of Christ's crucifixion, burial, and resurrection, making pilgrimage there a supreme act of devotion.
  • Pilgrimage as a Central Practice: Journeying to holy sites like Jerusalem, Rome, and Santiago de Compostela was a vital religious practice in Christian Europe, offering spiritual benefits to participants.
  • The Seljuk Turk Threat: The expansion of the Seljuk Turks into Anatolia, their defeat of the Byzantine Empire at Manzikert, and their proximity to Constantinople and Jerusalem, created a critical threat to Eastern Christians and pilgrim routes, prompting the Byzantine Emperor's appeal to the West.
  • Pope Urban II's Call to Crusade: At Clermont in 1095, Urban II passionately appealed to European knights to defend Eastern Christians, liberate holy sites, and promised remission of sins for those who died on crusade, igniting widespread fervor with the cry "Deus lo volt!" (God wills it!).
  • Remission of Sins: Participating in a Crusade was offered as a form of penance, granting remission of temporal sins, but not salvation by works alone.
  • Crusader States: After the First Crusade, four Latin Christian principalities (the County of Edessa, Principality of Antioch, County of Tripoli, and Kingdom of Jerusalem) were established in the Levant, requiring constant defense.
  • Complex Motivations for Crushing: The Crusades were driven by a mixture of genuine religious devotion, political ambition, economic opportunities (e.g., land for younger sons), and the prevailing culture of knightly warfare, not solely religious piety.
  • Military Orders: Institutions like the Knights Templar and Knights Hospitaller emerged, combining monastic vows with military service to permanently defend Crusader territories and pilgrims.
  • Saladin's Leadership: Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub (Saladin) was a brilliant Kurdish general who unified Muslim forces in Egypt and Syria, achieving a decisive victory at Hattin and recapturing Jerusalem. He was known for his strategic acumen and, notably, his humane treatment of defeated enemies compared to the earlier Crusaders.

Important Terms

  • Pilgrimage: A journey made to a holy place as an act of religious devotion, a crucial practice in the Middle Ages.
  • Seljuk Turks: A Central Asian Turkic people who converted to Sunni Islam, built a powerful empire, and whose conquests were a primary cause of the First Crusade.
  • Crusade: A holy war called by the Pope, in which participants wore a red cross and were promised remission of their sins if they died.
  • Knights Templar: The Poor Fellow-Soldiers of Christ and of the Temple of Solomon; a major military order of warrior-monks founded to defend pilgrims and territories, later becoming wealthy bankers.
  • Saladin: Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub; a Kurdish general and Sultan who unified Muslim forces, defeated the Crusaders at Hattin, and recaptured Jerusalem.
  • Church of the Holy Sepulchre: The church in Jerusalem built over the sites traditionally believed to be where Jesus was crucified, buried, and resurrected.
  • Battle of Manzikert (1071): A decisive battle where the Seljuk Turks defeated the Byzantine army, opening Anatolia to Turkish expansion and prompting the Byzantine Emperor's appeal for aid from the West.
  • Pope Urban II: The Pope who famously called for the First Crusade at the Council of Clermont in 1095.
  • Council of Clermont: The ecclesiastical council in France where Pope Urban II delivered his influential speech calling for the First Crusade.
  • "Deus lo volt!": Latin for "God wills it!", the enthusiastic response and rallying cry of the First Crusade participants.
  • Crusader States: The four Latin Christian principalities (County of Edessa, Principality of Antioch, County of Tripoli, and Kingdom of Jerusalem) established in the Holy Land following the First Crusade.
  • Knights Hospitaller: A military order that originated as a hospital caring for sick pilgrims in Jerusalem, later taking up arms for defense.
  • Bernard of Clairvaux: A highly influential Cistercian monk and orator who passionately preached and inspired the Second Crusade.
  • Battle of Hattin (1187): A catastrophic military defeat for the Crusader states at the hands of Saladin, leading to the loss of Jerusalem and most Crusader territories.
  • Richard I (the Lionheart): King of England, a prominent and skilled military leader of the Third Crusade, known for his campaigns against Saladin.

Detailed Notes

The Holy Land Before the Crusades

  • Jerusalem's Centrality: Jerusalem held immense religious significance for medieval Christians as the place of Jesus's crucifixion, burial, and resurrection, marked by the Church of the Holy Sepulchre.
  • Pilgrimage Tradition: For centuries, Christian pilgrimage to Jerusalem and other holy sites (Rome, Santiago de Compostela) was a deeply important act of religious devotion.
  • Muslim Rule: Since 638 CE, Jerusalem had been under Arab Muslim rule. Generally, early Arab rulers tolerated Christian pilgrims and access to holy sites, as Christianity, Judaism, and Islam all revered the city.

The Seljuk Turk Threat and Byzantine Appeal

  • Shifting Dynamics: This period of relative coexistence changed in the late eleventh century with the rise of the Seljuk Turks.
  • Seljuk Expansion: Having converted to Sunni Islam, the Seljuks built a powerful empire stretching from Persia into Anatolia (modern Turkey).
  • Battle of Manzikert (1071): The Seljuks decisively defeated the Byzantine army, leading to their rapid conquest of much of the Byzantine heartland.
  • Byzantine Appeal: By the 1090s, the Seljuks were dangerously close to Constantinople. Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos sent an urgent appeal to Pope Urban II in Rome for military assistance.

The Call: Urban II at Clermont (1095)

  • The Council of Clermont: In November 1095, Pope Urban II convened a major council in Clermont, France.
  • Urban's Speech: Addressing a vast crowd, Urban II described the suffering of Eastern Christians under Turkish rule and the desecration of holy places. He urged European knights, accustomed to internal warfare, to redirect their martial skills toward a righteous cause: freeing Jerusalem.
  • Promise of Remission of Sins: Urban offered that those who died on Crusade would receive remission of their sins. This was understood as a form of penance, not salvation by works, and was a powerful motivator for many.
  • "Deus lo volt!": The crowd's enthusiastic, unanimous response—"God wills it!"—became the battle cry of the First Crusade.

The First Crusade (1096–1099)

  • The People's Crusade: Before the official armies organized, an unstructured movement of peasants and minor knights led by Peter the Hermit set out. This People's Crusade was largely destroyed by the Seljuks in Anatolia, highlighting the difference between zealous fervor and military organization.
  • The Official Crusade: The main First Crusade assembled in 1096 under various powerful lords (e.g., Godfrey of Bouillon, Raymond of Toulouse, Bohemond of Taranto). It was a coalition, not a unified army.
  • Siege of Antioch: The Crusaders endured an eight-month siege of Antioch (1097–1098), suffering severe privation. The timely discovery of what was believed to be the Holy Lance inspired them to break out and defeat a Turkish relief army.
  • Capture of Jerusalem (1099): After a five-week siege, the Crusaders breached Jerusalem's walls. What followed was a brutal massacre of the city's Muslim and Jewish inhabitants, which medieval and modern historians agree was widespread and terrible.
  • Establishment of Crusader States: Following their victory, the Crusaders carved out four Crusader States in the Holy Land: the County of Edessa, the Principality of Antioch, the County of Tripoli, and the Kingdom of Jerusalem. These states were perpetually vulnerable and dependent on external support.
  • Complex Motivations: It's crucial to remember that Crusading was driven by a mix of genuine religious devotion, political ambition (e.g., land and power for younger sons), economic gain, and the existing culture of knightly warfare.

The Military Orders: Knights Templar and Knights Hospitaller

  • Need for Defenders: The Crusader States required permanent military forces to survive in a hostile environment.
  • Warrior-Monks: This need led to the creation of military orders, which combined monastic vows (poverty, chastity, obedience) with military service.
  • Knights Templar: Founded around 1119, these warrior-monks were sworn to protect pilgrims and Christian territories. Their distinctive white mantles with red crosses became iconic. They also evolved into powerful bankers.
  • Knights Hospitaller: Originally a hospital order caring for sick pilgrims in Jerusalem, they later took up arms.
  • Fortifications: Both orders were instrumental in building impressive stone castles across the Holy Land, such as the formidable Krak des Chevaliers.

Bernard of Clairvaux and the Second Crusade

  • Fall of Edessa (1144): The northernmost Crusader state, the County of Edessa, fell to the Muslim lord Zengi, sparking shock in Europe.
  • Preaching of Bernard of Clairvaux: The Second Crusade (1147–1149) was powerfully preached by Bernard of Clairvaux, a highly influential Cistercian monk.
  • Disastrous Outcome: The Second Crusade was a complete failure. The German and French armies failed to coordinate, attacked the wrong targets, and achieved nothing. Bernard attributed its failure to the Crusaders' sins.

Saladin and the Fall of Jerusalem (1187)

  • Rise of Saladin: The Muslim response to the Crusades was eventually unified under the extraordinary leadership of Saladin (Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub), a Kurdish general who consolidated control over Egypt and Syria.
  • Battle of Hattin (1187): Saladin inflicted a catastrophic defeat on the combined Crusader armies at the Battle of Hattin, encircling and destroying them.
  • Recapture of Jerusalem: Within months of Hattin, Saladin recaptured nearly all of the Kingdom of Jerusalem, including the city itself.
  • Saladin's Restraint: In stark contrast to the Crusader massacre of 1099, Saladin forbade a general slaughter, ransomed inhabitants, and allowed Christian pilgrims to continue visiting holy sites. This humane conduct earned him respect even among Christian chroniclers.

The Third Crusade (1189–1192)

  • European Response: The fall of Jerusalem galvanized Christian Europe, leading Pope Gregory VIII to call the Third Crusade.
  • Powerful Coalition: This Crusade attracted the most formidable European leaders: Holy Roman Emperor Frederick Barbarossa, King Philip II of France, and King Richard I (the Lionheart) of England.
  • Challenges and Setbacks: Frederick Barbarossa drowned en route, and Philip II soon returned home after quarreling with Richard.
  • Richard the Lionheart's Campaign: Richard I proved to be an exceptional military commander, successfully recapturing the coastal city of Acre after a long siege and winning the Battle of Arsuf. He marched to within sight of Jerusalem but ultimately could not retake the city.

Review Questions

  1. How did the political and religious landscape of the Holy Land evolve from Arab Muslim rule in 638 to the Seljuk Turk expansion in the late eleventh century, and why was this significant for the Byzantine Empire and Pope Urban II?
  2. Analyze the multi-layered appeal of Pope Urban II's speech at Clermont in 1095. Besides religious motivations, what other factors did he tap into, and how might his offer of "remission of sins" have been interpreted by medieval knights and peasants?
  3. Compare the objectives, leadership, and outcomes of the People's Crusade and the official First Crusade. What do their differing fates reveal about the challenges and realities of campaigning in the Holy Land?
  4. Discuss Saladin's impact on the Crusades. How did his leadership and strategic victories, particularly at the Battle of Hattin, change the balance of power in the Holy Land, and how did his actions upon recapturing Jerusalem contrast with the Crusaders' actions in 1099?
  5. Beyond pure religious zeal, what were some of the other motivations—political, economic, or social—that drove individuals and leaders to participate in the Crusades? Provide specific examples from the text to support your answer.
