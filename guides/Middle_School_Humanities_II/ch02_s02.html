Literature: The Song of Roland, William of Malmesbury, & the Magna Carta - Study Guide

Overview

This section explores three foundational texts from the Norman world: The Song of Roland, William of Malmesbury's Chronicle of the Kings of England, and the Magna Carta. Each document offers a distinct perspective on the era, examining themes of honor, conquest, justice, and the limits of power. Together, they provide insight into the ideals of Christian knighthood, the cultural costs of conquest, and the nascent principles of constitutional government.

Key Concepts

  • Chivalry: The ethical and social code expected of medieval knights. It emphasized loyalty to one's lord, courage in battle, protection of the weak, and unwavering devotion to the Christian faith. This ideal, often celebrated in literature, served as a moral compass for the aristocracy.
  • Christian Knighthood (Martyrdom): The concept, vividly portrayed in The Song of Roland, that a warrior could be a saint if their violence served God and Christendom. A knight's courage was not just personal bravery but service to a higher cause, and their death could be seen as martyrdom, ensuring a place in Paradise. This ideal was closely linked to the Crusades.
  • Perspective in History: The idea that a historian's background and experiences shape their interpretation of events. William of Malmesbury, being of mixed Norman and English heritage, offered a remarkably balanced, complex, and "modern" perspective on the Norman Conquest, acknowledging both sides' experiences.
  • Preservation of Culture: The act of documenting history and tradition to prevent cultural loss and ensure the memory of past glories, especially in times of significant societal upheaval or conquest. William of Malmesbury sought to preserve English history in the face of Norman dominance.
  • Limiting Arbitrary Power: The fundamental principle, solidified by the Magna Carta, that a ruler's authority is not absolute but is constrained by law and the rights of the governed. This document laid the groundwork for constitutional government by establishing written limits on royal power.

Important Terms

  • The Song of Roland (La Chanson de Roland): The oldest surviving major work of French literature, composed around 1100 AD, detailing a heroic battle from Charlemagne's reign.
  • Roncevaux: The mountain pass where Charlemagne's rearguard, led by Roland, was ambushed in 778 AD, serving as the historical basis for The Song of Roland.
  • Roland: Charlemagne's greatest knight and commander of the rearguard in The Song of Roland, known for his extreme sense of honor and courage.
  • Ganelon: Roland's stepfather, who betrays Roland to the Muslim king Marsile in The Song of Roland.
  • Marsile: The Muslim king who conspires with Ganelon to ambush Roland's rearguard.
  • Olifant: Roland's ivory horn, which he initially refuses to blow to summon Charlemagne, viewing it as an act of dishonor.
  • Durendal: Roland's legendary sword, which he attempts to break on a stone before his death to prevent it from falling into enemy hands.
  • William of Malmesbury (c. 1095–1143): A prominent medieval English historian of mixed Norman and English descent, known for his objective and complex account of the Norman Conquest.
  • Chronicle of the Kings of England: William of Malmesbury's comprehensive historical work, written around 1125, covering English history from the Anglo-Saxons to his own time.
  • Magna Carta (1215): A landmark English legal document forced upon King John by his barons, which established specific limits on royal power and introduced fundamental principles of justice and law.
  • King John: The English monarch (reigned 1199–1216) whose arbitrary rule, military losses, and taxation policies led to the baronial rebellion and the signing of the Magna Carta.
  • Runnymede: The meadow where King John met with his rebellious barons and agreed to the terms of the Magna Carta in 1215.
  • Free Man: A legal status in medieval England, initially referring to a relatively small portion of the population (nobles, knights, merchants) who were entitled to the rights protected by clauses like Magna Carta's 39 and 40.
  • Villeins (Serfs): The majority of the medieval English population, unfree peasants legally bound to their lord's land, who were initially excluded from the protections offered by the Magna Carta.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Three Voices of the Norman World

  • Overlapping Themes: The three texts—The Song of Roland, William of Malmesbury's Chronicle, and the Magna Carta—address common themes: honor and sacrifice, the costs of conquest, and the intricate relationship between power and law.
  • Distinct Perspectives:
    • The Song of Roland: Written in French for the aristocracy, celebrates Christian knightly ideals.
    • William of Malmesbury: Wrote in Latin for an educated English audience, offering a nuanced view of the Conquest from both sides.
    • Magna Carta: A legal document whose dry clauses contain revolutionary principles about limiting state power.
  • Core Questions: These texts collectively prompt reflection on loyalty, justice, and the appropriate limitations on power.

The Song of Roland

  • Origins: Composed around 1100 AD, it is the oldest major surviving work of French literature.
  • Historical Basis vs. Poetic Transformation: It is based on a real event—the ambush of Charlemagne's rearguard at Roncevaux in 778 AD—but transforms it into an epic tale embodying Christian knighthood, self-sacrifice, and divine justice.
  • Plot Highlights:
    • Roland, Charlemagne's greatest knight, commands the rearguard.
    • Betrayed by his stepfather, Ganelon, who arranges an ambush with Muslim King Marsile.
    • The Central Crisis: As his forces are overwhelmed, Roland's companion, Oliver, urges him to blow his ivory horn (Olifant) to summon Charlemagne. Roland refuses, believing it would be dishonorable to call for help, preferring to fight and die rather than admit weakness. This choice highlights the intense chivalric value system, prioritizing honor and reputation above all else ("That shame shall never fall upon my kindred"). This decision is both heroic in its courage and tragic in its consequences.
  • Roland's Death:
    • He finally blows the Olifant, but it's too late for his men, though Charlemagne hears it from a great distance.
    • Mortally wounded, Roland dies facing the enemy, having laid his glove before God as an act of surrender to divine will. His death is depicted as a martyrdom, with angels bearing his soul to Paradise, thus sanctifying the warrior's sacrifice.
  • Christian Knighthood: The poem establishes an ideal: a knight fighting for God, king, and Christendom. Roland's bravery is a service to a greater cause, and his death is seen as martyrdom. It asks if a warrior can be a saint, answering affirmatively if their violence is against enemies of the faith and their soul is dedicated to God. This ideal directly connects to the First Crusade, called shortly before the poem's composition.

William of Malmesbury on the Conquest

  • The Historian: William of Malmesbury (c. 1095–1143) was a leading medieval English historian. His unique perspective stemmed from being half Norman and half English, allowing him to view the Conquest from both sides.
  • Key Work: His Chronicle of the Kings of England (written around 1125) is a crucial near-contemporary source for understanding the Norman Conquest.
  • Complex Perspective: William avoids simplistic glorification or condemnation of William the Conqueror, instead offering a nuanced, "startlingly modern" portrayal of the Conquest's complexities.
  • Cultural Loss: He laments the transformation of England into "the residence of foreigners," reflecting the cultural loss experienced by the English-born clergy and literate class. His writing aimed to preserve the "glorious" history of the English ancestors, much like King Alfred's efforts to preserve Anglo-Saxon culture.
  • Account of Hastings: His vivid description of the Battle of Hastings contrasts the English (drinking and singing the night before) with the Normans (confessing sins and taking communion). While potentially biased by his Norman heritage, this contrast reflects the medieval Christian ideal of a holy warrior preparing their soul before battle.

The Magna Carta (1215)

  • Nature of the Document: Unlike poetry or narrative history, the Magna Carta is a legal document with sixty-three specific clauses addressing various feudal grievances from 1215. Most clauses are highly specific to the time.
  • Background:
    • King John's reign was marked by significant losses (Normandy to the French in 1204), heavy taxes to fund wars, conflicts with the Pope, and arbitrary and cruel treatment of his barons.
    • By 1215, the exasperated barons united, seized London, and forced King John to meet them at Runnymede, where they presented their demands, which became the Magna Carta.
  • Consequential Clauses: Two clauses proved revolutionary and long-lasting:
    • Clause 39: "No free man shall be seized, imprisoned, dispossessed, outlawed, exiled, or ruined in any way, nor in any way proceeded against, except by the lawful judgment of his peers and the law of the land." This established the principle of due process and trial by one's peers.
    • Clause 40: "To no one will we sell, to no one will we deny or delay, right or justice." This affirmed the right to swift and impartial justice.
  • Initial Scope vs. Lasting Impact:
    • Initially, these protections applied only to "free men," excluding the majority of the population who were villeins (serfs).
    • However, the embedded principle—that a ruler's power has limits enforced by law—was "explosive." It provided a foundational argument for later generations seeking to constrain government power, making it a cornerstone of constitutional government. It signifies a shift from tradition-bound custom to a specific, written charter limiting a ruler's power.

Reading the Three Texts Together

  • The Ideals: The Song of Roland illuminates the chivalric and Christian ideals of the age, particularly the concept of the saintly warrior.
  • The Costs: William of Malmesbury's account reveals the human and cultural costs of conquest, depicting a displaced people and a historian's effort to preserve threatened heritage.
  • The Limits: The Magna Carta demonstrates what happens when power is unchecked and how powerful subjects can force a ruler to accept legal limitations, laying the groundwork for future struggles against arbitrary rule.
  • Enduring Message: Collectively, these texts underscore that every historical era contains ideals, tragedies, and critical moments when the oppressed or aggrieved demand justice and accountability.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the heroic ideals of Roland in The Song of Roland with those of Beowulf (from earlier studies, if applicable). How does the explicit Christian element in Roland's story differentiate his heroism and his ultimate fate from Beowulf's pagan context?
  2. How did William of Malmesbury's mixed Norman and English heritage uniquely position him to write about the Norman Conquest? What was his primary purpose in writing Chronicle of the Kings of England, and how does it reflect a "cultural loss" in England?
  3. Analyze the "central crisis" in The Song of Roland: Roland's refusal to blow the Olifant. Discuss whether his decision, driven by his understanding of honor, was ultimately a virtue or a flaw, considering its consequences for himself and his men.
  4. Explain the significance of Clauses 39 and 40 in the Magna Carta. How did the initial scope of "free men" evolve to become a foundational principle for Western constitutional government, and what makes this document a "foundational moment"?
  5. The section concludes by stating that the three texts show an age's "ideals, its tragedies, and its moments when the oppressed find a way to say: this is not right, and it must stop." Discuss how each of the three texts specifically exemplifies one of these aspects.
