Literature: Eyewitnesses to Crisis Study Guide

Overview

This section explores three monumental literary works from the fourteenth century—Jean Froissart's Chronicles, Giovanni Boccaccio's Decameron, and Geoffrey Chaucer's Canterbury Tales. These texts offer vivid, often brutal, eyewitness accounts of the Late Medieval Crisis, providing invaluable insights into a civilization grappling with the devastations of the Black Death, the Hundred Years' War, and widespread social upheaval. Through diverse literary genres, these authors capture the "sound and texture" of their turbulent era, serving as essential primary sources for understanding the period.

Key Concepts

  • Literature as Eyewitness Account: Unlike traditional romances or epics, these works provide direct observations and reflections on the harsh realities of the 14th century, making them crucial primary sources for historical study.
  • The Late Medieval Crisis: The overarching historical context, primarily defined by the devastating Black Death, the protracted Hundred Years' War, and significant social unrest like the Peasants' Revolt.
  • Genre and Perspective: The different literary forms (chronicle, frame-story, poetic collection) each allow their authors to present specific facets of the crisis—reporting events, conveying emotional and physical experiences, or sketching societal characters.
  • Social Breakdown During Plague: Both Boccaccio's Decameron and, notably, Thucydides' earlier account of the Athenian plague, reveal striking similarities in human behavior under extreme stress, highlighting the collapse of social norms and family bonds.
  • Changing Warfare and Social Order: Froissart's Chronicles illustrate the shift from traditional chivalric warfare (e.g., Battle of Crécy) to more devastating tactics, and the profound impact of the Black Death on the relationship between lords and workers, leading to revolts like the Peasants' Revolt of 1381.
  • Social Observation and Satire: Chaucer's Canterbury Tales offers a rich tapestry of medieval English society, using brilliant and often satirical observations to portray a cross-section of pilgrims, reflecting on both the ideals and the complexities of human nature.
  • Language Evolution: The presence of Middle English in Chaucer's work demonstrates the historical development of the English language, making it distinct from, yet recognizably ancestral to, modern English.

Important Terms

  • Chronicles: A historical record of events in chronological order, often detailed and narrative, as exemplified by Froissart's work.
  • Frame-story: A literary technique where an overarching narrative (the frame) sets the stage for a series of individual stories, as seen in Boccaccio's Decameron.
  • Hundred Years' War: A series of conflicts waged between the Kingdom of England and the Kingdom of France from 1337 to 1453.
  • Black Death: One of the most devastating pandemics in human history, peaking in Europe from 1347 to 1351, causing widespread death and social disruption.
  • Peasants' Revolt (1381): A major uprising across large parts of England, primarily driven by economic and social grievances exacerbated by the Black Death.
  • Chivalry: The medieval knightly system with its religious, moral, and social code, which was increasingly challenged during this period.
  • Middle English: The form of the English language spoken in England from the Norman Conquest (1066) until the late 15th century, the language in which Chaucer wrote.
  • Pilgrimage: A journey, often arduous, made to a sacred place as an act of religious devotion, a central theme in The Canterbury Tales.
  • Satire: The use of humor, irony, exaggeration, or ridicule to expose and criticize people's stupidity or vices, particularly in the context of contemporary politics and other topical issues, a technique used by Chaucer.
  • Longbowmen: English archers equipped with longbows, whose skill proved devastatingly effective against French cavalry and crossbowmen in battles like Crécy.
  • Genoese crossbowmen: Mercenary soldiers from Genoa, hired by the French army, who were outmatched by English longbowmen at Crécy.

Detailed Notes

Reading the Late Middle Ages: Literature as Eyewitnesses to Crisis

The 14th century was a period of profound crisis, marked by the Black Death, the Hundred Years' War, and widespread social and political instability. Three literary giants—Jean Froissart, Giovanni Boccaccio, and Geoffrey Chaucer—produced works that serve as unique "eyewitness accounts" of this tumultuous era. Their writings move beyond traditional heroic narratives to provide vivid, often brutal, descriptions of the world as it truly was, offering a rich, multi-faceted understanding of a civilization under immense pressure. These authors, through their distinct genres, allow us to hear the "sound and texture" of the Late Middle Ages.

  • Jean Froissart (c. 1337–1405): A French-speaking chronicler from the Low Countries, Froissart spent his career documenting the Hundred Years' War. His Chronicles, based on interviews with knights and nobles, record events from the war's inception through the late 14th century. He captured both the admired courage of knights and the growing futility of their traditional warfare. His chosen form, the chronicle, excels at reporting historical events.
  • Giovanni Boccaccio (1313–1375): A Florentine humanist, Boccaccio was a direct witness to the Black Death in Florence in 1348. His Decameron is a collection of tales told by ten young Florentines who flee the plague. The Introduction to this work is considered one of the most important eyewitness accounts of the Black Death, providing a visceral sense of the physical and emotional impact of the plague. His frame-narrative form conveys personal and societal experience.
  • Geoffrey Chaucer (c. 1343–1400): An English poet, diplomat, and courtier, Chaucer lived through the initial Black Death outbreak, the Hundred Years' War, and the Peasants' Revolt. His Canterbury Tales, written in the 1380s–90s, presents a collection of stories told by pilgrims journeying to Canterbury. The General Prologue introduces these pilgrims as a diverse cross-section of medieval English society, portrayed with brilliant observation and often gentle, sometimes sharp, satire. Chaucer's poetic sketches character and societal types.

These works, despite their different languages and forms, are all invaluable primary sources, written by individuals who experienced these crises firsthand.

Froissart: The Chronicles

Froissart's Chronicles offer powerful glimpses into the realities of medieval warfare and social upheaval.

  • The Battle of Crécy (1346): Froissart's account vividly depicts the chaos of battle and the clash between old and new forms of warfare. He describes the English archers' arrows falling "so wholly and so thick, that it seemed as if it snowed." These arrows, piercing armor and bodies, caused disarray among the French forces, particularly the Genoese crossbowmen and the French cavalry who rode them down in their retreat. This battle highlighted the superior effectiveness of the English longbow and the breakdown of discipline within the French army, marking a decline for traditional chivalric warfare.
  • The Peasants' Revolt of 1381: Froissart also documented the social unrest following the Black Death. He records the grievances of peasants in Kent, Essex, Sussex, and Bedford, who protested their bondage. They argued that "at the beginning of the world there were no slaves" and that they, "men formed in the same likeness with their lords," should not be treated "as beasts." This passage reveals how the Black Death fundamentally altered the relationship between lords and workers, empowering peasants to demand an end to serfdom and challenging the established social hierarchy.

Boccaccio: The Decameron

Boccaccio's Decameron provides a harrowing, intimate look at the Black Death.

  • Eyewitness Account of the Plague in Florence (1348): The Introduction to The Decameron is a pivotal historical document. Boccaccio, aged 35 at the time, details the plague's symptoms, the failure of all human remedies, and the complete collapse of social order. He notes various theories for its origin (heavenly bodies, divine punishment) but focuses on its devastating spread and impact.
  • Breakdown of Society: Boccaccio powerfully illustrates how the "sore affliction entered so deep into the minds of men and women" that traditional bonds disintegrated. Citizens avoided each other, kinsfolk held aloof, brother forsook brother, and even parents abandoned their own children. This depiction of societal breakdown is almost unbearable, emphasizing the extreme psychological and social trauma of the pandemic.
  • Boccaccio and Thucydides: Two Plagues, Two Worlds: A comparison with Thucydides' account of the Athenian plague (430 BC) reveals striking similarities in the human experience of epidemic. Both authors describe social breakdown, abandonment of the sick, and the collapse of normal customs. However, their perspectives differ: Thucydides, a rationalist, offers a clinical description and sees social effects as predictable human reactions. Boccaccio, more emotional and morally engaged, integrates a Christian framework, viewing the plague as potential divine judgment and emphasizing the need for penance. Despite 1,800 years separating them, the core human experience of facing an epidemic remained profoundly similar.

Chaucer: The Canterbury Tales

Geoffrey Chaucer's Canterbury Tales opens with one of the most celebrated passages in English literature, inviting readers into a world of diverse characters.

  • The General Prologue: The opening describes a vibrant spring morning when pilgrims from all walks of life gather at the Tabard Inn in Southwark to embark on a pilgrimage to Thomas Becket's shrine in Canterbury. They agree to tell stories to pass the time. Chaucer’s detailed portraits of each pilgrim in the General Prologue offer a masterwork of social observation, blending sympathy with often gentle, sometimes cutting, satire. The choice of spring (renewal, new life) and pilgrimage (spiritual renewal, giving thanks) is symbolic, suggesting a world coming back to life after winter and the recent crises.
  • Middle English: Chaucer wrote in Middle English, an earlier form of the language that, while different from modern English, can often be decoded by reading aloud and identifying familiar word patterns (e.g., "Whan" for when, "shoures" for showers). This highlights the dynamic evolution of language over time.
  • Three Pilgrims: The Knight, the Prioress, and the Pardoner: Chaucer uses these characters to represent different facets of medieval society:
    • The Knight: Portrayed as the ideal, a "verray, parfit gentil knyght" who embodies chivalry, truth, honor, generosity, and courtesy. He has fought valiantly in both Christian and "heathen" lands, always respected for his worthiness. He represents the medieval ideal of aristocratic virtue.
    • The Prioress (Madam Eglantine): A more complicated character. While a nun, her "simple and coy" smile, her minor oath ("By Saint Loy!"), and her French spoken "after the scole of Stratford atte Bowe" (a provincial accent, not Parisian high culture) suggest a blend of religious devotion with worldly affectations. She is less a figure of austere piety and more a reflection of complex social aspirations.
    • The Pardoner: (While the detailed text for the Pardoner is not provided in this extract, the heading indicates he represents the "corrupt" aspect of society, often associated with selling indulgences for personal gain, a common target of satire in the period).

Review Questions

  1. How do Froissart's Chronicles, Boccaccio's Decameron, and Chaucer's Canterbury Tales each utilize their specific literary genres (chronicle, frame-story, poetic collection) to provide unique insights into the "sound and texture of a civilization under pressure" during the Late Medieval Crisis?
  2. Compare Froissart's descriptions of the Battle of Crécy and the Peasants' Revolt of 1381. What do these two historical accounts reveal about the evolving nature of warfare and the changing social dynamics between different classes in 14th-century Europe?
  3. Analyze Boccaccio's eyewitness account of the Black Death in Florence. What specific details does he provide about the plague's effects on individuals and society, and how does his perspective compare with Thucydides' account of the Athenian plague discussed in Volume I?
  4. In the General Prologue of The Canterbury Tales, Chaucer opens with a description of spring and the theme of pilgrimage. How do these elements establish the tone and context for the tales, and what do they suggest about the possibility of renewal or reflection in a post-plague world?
  5. Consider the three pilgrims introduced (the Knight, the Prioress, and the Pardoner as generally described). How does Chaucer use these character portraits to comment on different aspects of medieval English society, including ideals, complexities, and corruption?
