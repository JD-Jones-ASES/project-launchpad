Chapter 7, Section 1: Literature: The Reformation in Its Own Words

Overview

This section emphasizes the critical role of written documents – primary sources – in understanding the Protestant Reformation. It delves into the foundational texts and prayers of key reformers like Martin Luther, John Calvin, and Thomas Cranmer, demonstrating how their words ignited debates, challenged authority, and reshaped Christian theology and worship. By engaging with these original writings, we gain insight into the intense beliefs and arguments that drove one of Western history's greatest upheavals.

Key Concepts

  • The Reformation as an Argument of Words: The Protestant Reformation was primarily a battle of ideas and arguments, spread through sermons, letters, pamphlets, treatises, and especially books. Primary sources are essential for understanding the reformers' beliefs, frustrations, and visions.
  • Martin Luther's Challenge to Indulgences: Luther's Ninety-Five Theses initiated the Reformation by challenging the theological and moral basis of selling indulgences. His arguments covered papal authority, true repentance, the nature of forgiveness, and the Church's true treasures (the Gospel).
  • Luther's Appeal to Secular Authority: Recognizing the Pope's unwillingness to debate, Luther appealed to German princes in his Address to the Christian Nobility. He argued they had a divine right and duty to reform the Church, breaking down the "three walls" that protected papal power.
  • Calvin's Systematic Theology of Predestination: John Calvin's Institutes of the Christian Religion offered a structured presentation of Protestant doctrine. His doctrine of predestination (election) asserts God's sovereign choice in salvation, emphasizing that salvation is entirely God's undeserved gift (sola gratia) and not based on human merit.
  • Cranmer's Shaping of English Worship: Thomas Cranmer's Book of Common Prayer provided the English Church with a comprehensive liturgy in English, blending Reformed theology with traditional beauty. Its prayers are renowned for their theological precision, human honesty, and profound influence on the English language.
  • Theological Precision and Personal Conviction: The reformers' writings were driven by deep theological conviction, believing the eternal fate of souls depended on understanding and practicing true Christianity. Their emotional intensity and personal sacrifices underscore the seriousness of their beliefs.
  • Impact of the Printing Press: The printing press was crucial for rapidly disseminating the reformers' ideas, especially Luther's Ninety-Five Theses, ensuring they reached a wide audience beyond academic circles.

Important Terms

  • Primary Sources: Original documents or artifacts from a historical period, like letters, speeches, books, or laws. Crucial for understanding the Reformation directly from the reformers' perspectives.
  • Theses: A statement or theory put forward as a premise to be maintained or proved. Luther's Ninety-Five Theses were propositions for academic debate.
  • Indulgences: In the Catholic Church, a remission of the temporal punishment due to sins already forgiven. In the 16th century, the sale of indulgences for money was widely criticized.
  • Repentance: A sincere turning away from sin and toward God; a change of mind and heart. Luther argued the entire Christian life should be one of repentance, not just a one-time act.
  • The Three Walls: Luther's term for the barriers the Roman Church erected to protect itself from reform: (1) spiritual power over temporal, (2) Pope as sole interpreter of Scripture, (3) Pope alone can call a council.
  • Predestination (Election): The doctrine, particularly associated with John Calvin, that God has eternally determined whom He will save (elect) and whom He will condemn to destruction. It emphasizes God's sovereign grace in salvation.
  • Sola Gratia: Latin for "by grace alone," a core Reformation doctrine asserting that salvation is entirely a gift of God's grace and not earned by human merit or works.
  • Liturgy: A fixed form of public worship used in a church, often including specific prayers, readings, and rituals.
  • Collect: A short, general prayer used in Christian worship, often specific to a particular day or occasion. Cranmer's Book of Common Prayer contains many famous collects.
  • General Confession: A prayer in a liturgical service where the entire congregation confesses its sins to God.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Why Primary Sources Matter Here

  • The Protestant Reformation was fundamentally an argument conducted through words – sermons, letters, pamphlets, treatises, and especially books.
  • To truly grasp the Reformation, one must engage with the actual writings of the Reformers. These documents are not merely historical records; they are the passionate voices of individuals deeply convinced that eternal souls depended on "getting the answer right" regarding salvation.
  • The authors of these texts, initially pastors and scholars, felt the Church was leading people astray, particularly on the question of how sinners are saved. Their emotional intensity is a key part of the historical reality.

Martin Luther: The Ninety-Five Theses (1517)

  • Context: In October 1517, Martin Luther, a German monk and professor, wrote ninety-five arguments (theses) to challenge the theological foundation of selling indulgences.
  • Initial Audience: He wrote them in Latin, intending to spark an academic debate among scholars and the Archbishop of Mainz.
  • Wider Impact: The printing press ensured the theses reached a much broader audience, spreading rapidly throughout Germany and beyond.
  • Range of Arguments: The theses covered various points:
    • Theological: Thesis 1 states that the entire Christian life should be one of repentance, implying that repentance cannot be bought. Thesis 36 asserts that true repentance grants full remission of guilt, rendering indulgences unnecessary. Thesis 62 declares the Gospel itself to be the Church's true treasure.
    • Constitutional: Thesis 5 questions the Pope's authority, stating he can only remit penalties imposed by himself or canon law, not penalties imposed by God.
    • Moral/Pastoral: Thesis 27 directly attacks the sales pitch that a soul flies out of purgatory "as soon as the money clinks in the chest," calling it "mere human folly." These criticisms expressed deep pastoral concern for misled ordinary Christians.
    • Financial/Political: Thesis 86 sarcastically questions why the Pope, being wealthier than the richest, doesn't build St. Peter's Basilica with his own money rather than that of the poor.
  • Significance: The Ninety-Five Theses challenged the established practices and authority of the Roman Catholic Church, becoming a foundational document of the Reformation.

Luther: Address to the Christian Nobility of the German Nation (1520)

  • Shift in Strategy: By 1520, Luther realized the Pope would not engage in scholarly debate. He redirected his appeal.
  • New Audience: Luther turned to the German princes, arguing they possessed the right and responsibility to reform the Church if the ecclesiastical hierarchy failed to do so.
  • The "Three Walls": To justify this appeal to secular rulers, Luther identified "three walls" that the Roman party had built to defend itself from reform:
    1. Spiritual power is above temporal power: The claim that the Pope and clergy were immune from secular jurisdiction.
    2. Pope alone interprets Scripture: The assertion that only the Pope could authoritatively interpret the Bible.
    3. Pope alone can call a council: The declaration that only the Pope had the authority to convene a Church council.
  • Biblical Imagery: Luther powerfully invoked the "walls of Jericho" (from Joshua 6), casting himself and the reformers as a new Joshua, seeking to tear down these protective walls of corruption and free "Christian rods for the punishment of sin." This use of biblical comparisons was characteristic of Reformation writing.

John Calvin: Institutes of the Christian Religion (1536, rev. 1559)

  • Calvin's Style: In contrast to Luther's fiery style, John Calvin was known for his systematic, precise, and organized thought.
  • Purpose: The Institutes became the definitive theological textbook for the Reformed churches, offering a comprehensive presentation of Protestant doctrine.
  • Doctrine of Predestination (Election): The passage focuses on this central doctrine:
    • Definition: Predestination is God's eternal decree, determining what will happen to every person. Some are preordained to eternal life, others to eternal damnation.
    • Basis: This doctrine is asserted as consistent with "the clear doctrine of the Scripture," stating that God has "once for all determined both whom He would admit to salvation and whom He would condemn to destruction."
    • Purpose: For Calvin, understanding election exalts God's grace. It emphasizes that salvation flows entirely from God's "free mercy" (sola gratia) and not from any human desert or effort, crushing human pride by showing God does not "adopt all promiscuously to the hope of salvation."
  • Reactions: While a challenging doctrine, many Reformation-era Christians found predestination comforting (assurance of salvation if chosen) or terrifying (fear of not being chosen), reflecting the era's seriousness about eternal destiny.
  • Connection to Luther: Both Calvin and Luther emphasized sola gratia (salvation by grace alone), but Calvin took this concept further by explicitly articulating the doctrine of predestination as the ultimate expression of God's sovereign and free grace.

Thomas Cranmer: The Book of Common Prayer (1549)

  • Architect of English Worship: Thomas Cranmer, Archbishop of Canterbury, was central to shaping the worship life of the English Reformation.
  • Content and Purpose: The Book of Common Prayer (BCP) provided a complete liturgy in English, including collects, orders for Morning and Evening Prayer, the Communion service, and prayers for life's significant events (baptism to burial). It aimed to give the English church a distinct voice, combining Reformed theology with traditional beauty.
  • Literary Influence: Cranmer's superb ear for the rhythms of the English language profoundly influenced English writing, second only perhaps to the King James Bible.
  • The Collect for Purity:
    • Opening: "Almighty God, unto whom all hearts be open, all desires known, and from whom no secrets are hid" is a powerful theological statement about God's omniscience, implying transparency is essential in prayer.
    • Purpose: To cleanse thoughts by the Holy Spirit for pure love and worthy magnification of God's Name.
  • The General Confession:
    • Theological Precision: It is a deeply honest prayer of confession, acknowledging human sinfulness ("We have erred, and strayed from thy ways like lost sheep... there is no health in us"). This connects to Luther's sola gratia and the idea that human beings are incapable of saving themselves.
    • Human Honesty: The rhythmic repetition of "We have..." highlights collective human failure and the need for God's mercy. It assumes a fallen human nature constantly in need of repentance and forgiveness, echoing Luther's Thesis 1.
    • Redemption: It appeals to God's mercy "in Christ Jesu our Lord" and seeks to live a "godly, righteous, and sober life" hereafter, for God's glory.
  • Cranmer's Sacrifice: Cranmer's personal history demonstrates the high stakes of the Reformation. He was martyred for his Protestant faith under Mary I, famously retracting his recantation at his execution by placing the hand that signed it into the flames first. His prayers endured beyond his life.

Discussion: Connecting the Reformation to Earlier Units

  • Luther and the Early Church: Luther's argument that papal supremacy was a later, illegitimate development, not present in the early Church (e.g., Council of Nicaea), prompts historical investigation into the relationship between early bishops of Rome and the rest of the Church.
  • The Printing Press: The rapid spread of Reformation ideas, particularly Luther's Ninety-Five Theses, was enabled by the printing press, a key technological advancement discussed in earlier units.

Review Questions

  1. Why are primary sources considered so important for understanding the Protestant Reformation, according to this section? How do they help us grasp the "emotional intensity" of the period?
  2. Identify at least three distinct types of arguments (e.g., theological, moral, political) that Martin Luther made in his Ninety-Five Theses. Which of these do you think would have resonated most with ordinary people in the 16th century, and why?
  3. Explain Luther's concept of the "three walls" that the Roman Church used to defend itself. Why was it strategically significant for him to appeal to the German nobility to overcome these walls?
  4. How does John Calvin's doctrine of predestination, as described in the Institutes, connect to Luther's emphasis on sola gratia (by grace alone)? What did Calvin believe was the primary purpose or benefit of understanding this doctrine?
  5. Discuss the significance of Thomas Cranmer's Book of Common Prayer. How do the "Collect for Purity" and the "General Confession" demonstrate both theological precision and human honesty, and how do they reflect core Reformation teachings discussed by Luther and Calvin?
