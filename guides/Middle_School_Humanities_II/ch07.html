Study Guide: The Protestant Reformation

Overview

Chapter 7 examines the Protestant Reformation from its roots in Church corruption and the precedents of Wycliffe and Hus through Luther's decisive break in 1517, the spread of the Reformation across Europe (Calvin, Knox, the English Reformation), and the Catholic Counter-Reformation. The chapter traces how the same questions raised and suppressed in 1384 and 1415 finally became unstoppable in 1517 because the printing press made ideas impossible to contain. It examines the three solas (sola fide, sola scriptura, sola gratia) as the theological heart of the Reformation, profiles key figures (Luther, Tyndale, Calvin, Erasmus, Henry VIII), and concludes with the Peace of Augsburg (1555) as the first major European acknowledgment that religious division was permanent. The chapter positions the Reformation as a culmination of the entire course — building on everything from Alcuin's interpretation of history (Chapter 1) to the Avignon Papacy crisis (Chapter 5) to the printing press (Chapter 6).

Key Concepts

  • Why 1517 Was Different from 1384 and 1415: The questions Luther raised were not new — Wycliffe and Hus had raised them. The difference was the printing press. Luther's theses circulated across Germany in weeks; Wycliffe's manuscripts spread in months or years. The same ideas that could be suppressed before became impossible to contain after Gutenberg.

  • Sola Fide as the Reformation's Core: Luther's breakthrough — that salvation is God's free gift received through faith, not a human achievement earned through works and penances — was the theological foundation of everything else. If salvation is by faith alone, then indulgences are theologically incoherent and the whole medieval penitential system is built on a false premise.

  • Luther at Worms as a Defining Moment: Luther's refusal to recant at the Diet of Worms, on the grounds that his conscience was captive to the Word of God, established the principle that individual conscience bound by Scripture stands above institutional authority. This was theologically revolutionary and had political implications that Luther himself never fully worked out.

  • The Reformation as a Family Tree, Not a Single Event: Luther was one branch of a larger tree. Zwingli worked independently in Zurich. Calvin systematized Reformed theology and built a model Christian city in Geneva. Knox brought Calvinism to Scotland. The English Reformation took its own distinctive path — beginning for political reasons (Henry VIII's divorce) and developing theologically only under Edward VI and Elizabeth I.

  • Tyndale as the Reformation's Linguistic Legacy: Tyndale's English New Testament shaped the English language nearly as profoundly as Shakespeare. His translation made Scripture directly accessible to ordinary English speakers, fulfilling the sola scriptura ideal at the popular level. His death and posthumous vindication make his story one of the most powerful individual narratives in the chapter.

  • The Counter-Reformation as Genuine Reform: The Council of Trent was not merely defensive. It addressed genuine abuses (requiring bishops to live in their dioceses, establishing seminaries) alongside theological clarification. The Catholic Church that emerged from Trent was leaner, better organized, and more theologically rigorous — and the Jesuits gave it an intellectual and missionary force it had previously lacked.

  • The Peace of Augsburg as a Historical Threshold: The Peace of Augsburg acknowledged something unprecedented: that religious unity in Western Christendom was no longer achievable and that the diversity of Christianity would have to be managed politically. This was the first major European treaty built on the premise that religious pluralism was a permanent condition.

  • The Reformation's Four Legacies: Religious (permanent split of Western Christianity), political (collapse of papal authority in Protestant countries), educational (vernacular Bible reading drove literacy), and cultural (individual conscience as a concept that would eventually flower into individual rights).

Important Terms

  • simony: Buying or selling church offices; named for Simon Magus (Acts 8). Widely practiced despite condemnation — wealthy families bought positions for relatives.
  • indulgence: A certificate claiming to reduce punishment in purgatory for already-forgiven sins. Originally for penance; sold for money by Luther's time.
  • purgatory: Catholic doctrine of a state of post-mortem purification before heaven. Protestant Reformers denied its existence.
  • sola fide: 'By faith alone.' Salvation received through faith in Christ, not earned by human effort.
  • sola scriptura: 'By Scripture alone.' The Bible is the sole supreme authority — above popes, councils, and tradition.
  • sola gratia: 'By grace alone.' Salvation is entirely God's free gift, not earned by merit.
  • Diet of Worms (1521): The Holy Roman Empire assembly before which Luther refused to recant; the Emperor declared him an outlaw.
  • predestination: Calvin's doctrine that God from eternity elected who would be saved, based solely on God's own will.
  • Act of Supremacy (1534): Henry VIII declared Supreme Head of the Church of England; ended papal authority in England.
  • Council of Trent (1545–1563): The Catholic response to the Reformation; reformed abuses and clarified doctrine.
  • Jesuits: The Society of Jesus, founded by Ignatius of Loyola (1540); the elite intellectual and missionary force of the Counter-Reformation.
  • cuius regio, eius religio: 'Whose realm, his religion.' The principle of the Peace of Augsburg (1555): each prince determined his territory's religion.
  • Peace of Augsburg (1555): Treaty acknowledging permanent religious division in the Holy Roman Empire.

Detailed Notes

The Church on the Eve of the Reformation

  • Power and Corruption
    • Church owned roughly 1/3 of all land in Germany
    • Ran hospitals, universities, courts; collected taxes; appointed bishops; crowned kings
    • Simony: bishops and abbots purchased positions; never intended real religious work
    • Parish priests sometimes barely literate; could not explain Latin services
    • Monasteries had become comfortable estates for wealthy families' younger sons
  • Unresolved Questions
    • Wycliffe (died 1384): Scripture alone; attacked transubstantiation; began English Bible; Lollards
    • Hus (burned 1415): same ideas in Bohemia; burned at Council of Constance
    • Questions never answered — only silenced. Without the printing press, silence worked.

The Spark: Indulgences and Luther

  • Johann Tetzel's Campaign (1517)
    • Dominican friar selling indulgences near Wittenberg
    • Goal: fund construction of new St. Peter's Basilica in Rome
    • Proceeds split between Archbishop of Mainz (paying his debt to the Pope) and the building
    • 'As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs'
  • Luther's Crisis and Breakthrough
    • Augustinian monk and professor of biblical theology at University of Wittenberg
    • Agonized over salvation; penances, good works, indulgences left him in despair
    • Breakthrough from Romans 1:17: 'the righteous shall live by faith'
    • Salvation not earned — it is God's free gift received through faith alone
  • The 95 Theses (October 1517)
    • Formal academic arguments in Latin challenging indulgences' theological basis
    • Sent to Archbishop of Mainz; apparently posted at Wittenberg
    • Intended as academic debate — printing press made it a revolution
    • Circulated across Germany within weeks; all of Europe within months

The Diet of Worms and Luther's Protection

  • The Pope's Response
    • 1520: Pope Leo X issued Exsurge Domine threatening excommunication
    • Luther publicly burned the papal bull in Wittenberg's town square
  • The Diet of Worms (1521)
    • Summoned before Emperor Charles V and a table piled with his books
    • Asked: did he write them, and did he recant?
    • Refused: 'My conscience is captive to the Word of God. I cannot and will not retract anything. Here I stand.'
    • Emperor declared him an outlaw: illegal to give him food or shelter
  • The Wartburg
    • Frederick the Wise, Elector of Saxony, arranged friendly 'kidnapping'
    • Luther hidden at Wartburg Castle
    • Translated entire New Testament into German in eleven weeks
    • German Bible shaped the German language as profoundly as it shaped German religion

The Three Solas

  • Sola Fide (by faith alone)
    • Salvation through faith in Christ, not earned by works, penances, or indulgences
    • Dismantled the entire medieval penitential system
  • Sola Scriptura (by Scripture alone)
    • The Bible is the sole supreme authority — above popes and councils
    • Revolutionary implication: if Scripture is supreme, every literate Christian should read it
    • Drove demand for vernacular Bible translations
  • Sola Gratia (by grace alone)
    • Salvation is entirely God's free, undeserved gift
    • Human beings cannot earn or contribute to their salvation

The Spread of the Reformation

  • Zwingli (Zurich)
    • Began independently of Luther c. 1519
    • Killed fighting at Battle of Kappel (1531)
  • Calvin (Geneva)
    • French-born lawyer who converted to Reformation; settled in Geneva
    • Built 'godly city' governed by biblical principles
    • Institutes of the Christian Religion: systematic Reformed theology
    • Predestination: God elected the saved from eternity, based solely on His will
  • Knox (Scotland)
    • Brought Calvinist Reformation to Scotland
    • Established Presbyterian church (governance by elders, not bishops)
  • The English Reformation
    • Henry VIII (1491–1547): wanted annulment from Catherine of Aragon
    • Pope refused (under pressure from Catherine's nephew, Emperor Charles V)
    • Act of Supremacy (1534): Henry = Supreme Head of Church of England
    • Theologically conservative at first; Protestant direction under Edward VI (1547–1553)
    • Archbishop Cranmer: Book of Common Prayer (1549) — English liturgy
    • Mary I (1553–1558): reversed Reformation; burned ~300 Protestants
    • Elizabeth I (1558–1603): restored and made Reformation permanent
  • William Tyndale
    • Translated New Testament into English in exile (1525)
    • Copies smuggled into England hidden in bales of cloth
    • Bishop of London bought up and burned copies — only funded next edition
    • Betrayed, arrested, strangled, and burned in Antwerp 1536
    • Dying prayer: 'Lord, open the King of England's eyes'
    • Within three years: English Bible placed in every church in England by royal order

The Counter-Reformation

  • Council of Trent (1545–1563)
    • Met in three sessions in northern Italy
    • Doctrine: salvation requires faith AND works; Scripture AND tradition; seven sacraments
    • Reformed abuses: bishops required to live in dioceses; seminaries established to train priests
    • Church from Trent: leaner, better organized, more theologically rigorous
  • The Jesuits (Society of Jesus)
    • Founded by Ignatius of Loyola (1491–1556) after spiritual conversion from battlefield wound
    • Approved 1540; trained scholars, educators, and missionaries
    • Jesuit schools educated Catholic elite of Europe
    • Jesuit missionaries: India, Japan, Americas

The Peace of Augsburg and Its Legacy

  • The Peace (1555)
    • Treaty between Emperor Charles V and Protestant princes of Holy Roman Empire
    • Cuius regio, eius religio: each prince determines his territory's religion
    • Catholic and Lutheran recognized; Calvinist territories NOT recognized
    • Subjects who disagree had right to emigrate
  • What the Reformation Changed
    • Religious: Western Christianity permanently divided (Catholic/Protestant; Protestantism itself split into dozens of denominations)
    • Political: Papal authority collapsed in Protestant countries; national churches
    • Educational: Vernacular Bible reading drove literacy (Luther's German Bible, Tyndale's English NT)
    • Cultural: Individual conscience as a claim that cannot be entirely suppressed → seeds of individual rights, freedom of conscience, democratic thought

Connections

  • The Reformation's indulgence controversy has roots at the First Crusade (Chapter 3) where Urban II first offered indulgences to Crusaders.
  • Wycliffe and Hus (Chapter 5) are explicitly identified as Luther's predecessors; the Reformation is the fruit of seeds planted in the Late Medieval Crisis.
  • The printing press (Chapter 6) is specifically identified as the reason 1517 was different from 1384 and 1415 — the technological difference that made suppression impossible.
  • Erasmus and Thomas More (Chapter 6) represent the Christian humanist alternative that Luther made impossible: reform from within.
  • Alfred the Great (Chapter 1) also translated Christian texts into the vernacular for wider access — but for the educated class; Tyndale's vision of every ploughman reading Scripture reflects a fundamentally different conception of who deserves access to Scripture.
  • The concept of individual conscience that Luther articulated at Worms would eventually connect to political developments (individual rights, religious freedom, democracy) that became central to the modern world.
  • The Thirty Years' War (1618–1648), which the Peace of Augsburg failed to prevent, would be far more destructive than anything in the Reformation era — a consequence left as a horizon for the next phase of history.
