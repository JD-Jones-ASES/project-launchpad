CHAPTER 1, SECTION 2: LITERATURE: BEOWULF - Study Guide

Overview

This section explores three foundational texts from the Viking Age: the epic poem Beowulf, the historical Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, and Asser's biography, Life of King Alfred. By examining these distinct literary forms, we gain a multifaceted internal perspective on Anglo-Saxon culture, its heroic ideals, historical realities, and the character of one of its most pivotal leaders, King Alfred.

Key Concepts

  • Multiple Perspectives on the Viking Age: The section highlights how Beowulf (heroic ideals), the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle (factual events), and Asser's Life of King Alfred (personal biography) offer diverse "voices" and insights into the same historical period, moving beyond an outsider's view.
  • Beowulf's Syncretism (Christianity and Paganism): The poem Beowulf uniquely blends pre-Christian Germanic heroic values (courage, loyalty, generosity) with Christian references (Grendel as a descendant of Cain, God as creator), prompting reflection on the relationship between heroic and Christian virtues.
  • Heroic Ideal: Beowulf embodies the Anglo-Saxon ideal of a valiant warrior and wise king who sacrifices for his people, facing monsters and ultimately a dragon, even if he operates outside a Christian framework.
  • The Nature of Historical Records: The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle demonstrates a terse, factual, year-by-year recording of events, sometimes punctuated by moments of vivid, almost apocalyptic, description, raising questions about objectivity and chronicler attitudes.
  • Biography as a Window into Character and Values: Asser's Life of King Alfred provides a warm, personal account of a medieval ruler, emphasizing Alfred's admirable qualities like love of learning, piety, generosity, and just governance, offering a portrait of an "ideal Christian king."
  • Cultural Influence and Leadership: The three texts collectively suggest that King Alfred's leadership was not accidental but a product of a culture that valued the very qualities he embodied, showing how individuals can both reflect and shape their societies.

Important Terms

  • Beowulf (Poem): The longest surviving epic poem in Old English, depicting heroic deeds in pre-Christian Scandinavia.
  • Beowulf (Character): The Geat warrior and later king, protagonist of the poem, known for his strength, courage, and loyalty.
  • Old English: The language in which Beowulf and early entries of the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle were written, spoken in Anglo-Saxon England.
  • Heorot: The great mead-hall of the Danish King Hrothgar, central to the initial conflicts in Beowulf.
  • Grendel: The monstrous descendant of Cain who terrorizes Heorot in Beowulf.
  • Grendel's Mother: The second monster Beowulf defeats, seeking revenge for her son.
  • Geatland: Beowulf's homeland, a region in Scandinavia (part of modern Sweden).
  • Anglo-Saxon Chronicle: A collection of historical records, begun during Alfred's reign, providing year-by-year accounts of events in Anglo-Saxon England, often terse and factual.
  • Asser: A Welsh monk and scholar, King Alfred's biographer, author of The Life of King Alfred.
  • Life of King Alfred: A near-contemporary biography of King Alfred, offering a personal and admiring portrait of the king.
  • Viking Age: The period from the late 8th to the mid-11th century, characterized by Viking raids and settlements across Europe.
  • Pagan Setting/Themes: Refers to the pre-Christian world and values depicted in Beowulf.
  • Christian Frame/Themes: The Christian perspective and references woven into Beowulf by its monastic scribe, and the explicit Christian values in Asser's biography.
  • Heroic Virtue: The traditional values celebrated in epic poetry, such as courage, loyalty to one's lord and people, generosity, and strength in battle.
  • Oral Traditions: Stories, poems, and histories passed down verbally through generations before being written down, likely influencing Beowulf.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Reading across the Viking Age

  • Three Distinct Voices: This unit examines three texts (Beowulf, Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, Asser's Life of King Alfred) from the same historical period but with very different styles and purposes.
  • Internal Perspective: These texts offer a unique opportunity to understand the Viking Age from the viewpoint of those who lived through it, rather than through later interpretations.

Beowulf

  • Oldest Surviving Old English Poem: Over 3,000 lines, likely composed between the 8th and 10th centuries.
  • Oral Traditions and Christian Scribe: Written down by a Christian monk, drawing on much older Germanic oral traditions.
  • Pre-Christian Scandinavian Setting: The poem is set among Germanic tribes in Denmark and Geatland (modern Sweden) before the arrival of Christianity.
  • Plot Summary:
    • Initial Conflict: Geat warrior Beowulf sails to Denmark to help King Hrothgar, whose mead-hall Heorot is attacked by the monster Grendel.
    • Grendel's Defeat: Beowulf defeats Grendel in a bare-handed struggle, tearing off his arm.
    • Grendel's Mother: Beowulf then battles and kills Grendel's vengeful mother.
    • The Dragon and Beowulf's Death: Later, as an old king, Beowulf fights and kills a dragon that was awakened by a stolen cup, but he is mortally wounded and dies. The poem ends with his funeral and lament.
  • Christian Themes in a Pagan Setting:
    • The Christian poet includes references like Grendel being a descendant of Cain and God as the creator.
    • However, the poem celebrates older Germanic values: courage, loyalty, generosity, and fighting for one's people.
    • It poses a question: How do Christian and heroic virtues relate? Can a pagan hero embody holiness through his actions? The poet treats Beowulf with sympathy, despite his pre-Christian existence.
  • Depiction of Horror (Grendel): The poem vividly describes Grendel's attacks, highlighting his monstrous nature and the fear he inspires. Beowulf's refusal to use weapons against Grendel emphasizes his extraordinary strength.
  • Hero's Death and Legacy: Beowulf's final battle against the dragon culminates in his heroic death and a lament from his people, reflecting on loyalty, loss, and an uncertain future.
  • Discussion Point: Beowulf explores universal values of heroism and sacrifice. Students can discuss how these values compare to Christian virtues and whether heroism requires religious belief. The poet's sympathetic portrayal of a pagan hero is a significant literary choice, similar to how Virgil (a pagan Roman) is treated in Dante's Inferno.

The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle

  • Origin and Purpose: Begun under King Alfred's command, continued by monks until the mid-12th century. It is a vital historical document for understanding English history.
  • Style: Terse and Factual: Entries are typically brief, recording raids, battles, deaths, and treaties in a year-by-year format (e.g., "In this year the Danes came...").
  • Occasional Detailed Accounts: While generally concise, some entries open up to reveal the human impact and dramatic reality of events.
    • AD 793 Entry: Describes "dreadful fore-warnings" (lights, whirlwinds, fiery dragons) preceding the devastating Viking raid on Holy Island, conveying an almost apocalyptic tone and seeing the events as divine judgment.
    • AD 878 Entry: Starkly recounts the Danish army's invasion and subjugation of West Saxons, reducing King Alfred to hiding in woods with a small band, portraying catastrophe with understated gravity.
  • Discussion Point: The differing tones between the 793 and 878 entries are significant. The former shows a chronicler interpreting events through a religious lens, while the latter is remarkably factual and restrained, even in the face of disaster. This "economy of language" suggests a desire for objectivity or perhaps a stoic acceptance of grim realities.

Asser's Life of King Alfred

  • Author and Context: Written by Asser, a Welsh monk recruited by Alfred around 885, completed around 893 while Alfred was still alive.
  • Significance: One of the few near-contemporary biographies of a medieval ruler, offering a rare, personal glimpse into Alfred's character.
  • Admiring and Personal Tone: Asser clearly admired Alfred, resulting in a warm, detailed, and positive portrayal of the king.
  • Alfred's Love of Learning:
    • Despite parental neglect leading to illiteracy until age twelve, Alfred "listened attentively to Saxon poems day and night" and memorized them.
    • He was passionate about hunting but even more ardent in seeking wisdom, frustrated by the lack of suitable teachers.
  • Portrait of an Ideal King: Asser describes Alfred as:
    • Loved by his family and people.
    • Zealous in almsgiving and generous to both natives and foreigners.
    • Affable, pleasant, and "curiously eager to investigate things unknown."
    • A just and loving ruler who embraced and enriched people from various cultures (Franks, Frisians, Gauls, heathens, Welsh, Scots, Bretons).
  • Discussion Point: Asser presents Alfred as an "ideal Christian king," embodying virtues such as piety, learning, justice, and generosity. It's crucial to read such biographies critically, acknowledging that biographers (even contemporary ones) often have agendas. Asser wrote for Alfred's court, likely crafting a portrait Alfred himself would approve of. This doesn't make it untrustworthy, but it prompts questions about what might have been omitted or emphasized.

Reading Across the Three Texts

  • Three Windows on One World:
    • Beowulf: Reveals the core values (courage, loyalty, good kingship) of Anglo-Saxon culture.
    • The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle: Presents the stark events and historical facts of the era.
    • Asser's Life of King Alfred: Offers a personal insight into the man, King Alfred, highlighting his curiosity, piety, and love of learning.
  • Alfred as a Cultural Product and Shaper: Together, these texts suggest that Alfred's remarkable character and leadership were not accidental but the embodiment of his culture's highest ideals. He, in turn, further shaped the culture that enabled England to survive the Viking Age.

Review Questions

  1. How do Beowulf, The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, and Asser's Life of King Alfred each offer a distinct "voice" or perspective on the Viking Age? Provide examples from each text to support your answer.
  2. Discuss the blend of Christian and pagan elements in Beowulf. What specific examples of each can you identify, and what deeper questions does this blend raise about heroism and virtue in the poem?
  3. Compare and contrast the tone and style of the two Anglo-Saxon Chronicle entries (AD 793 and AD 878) discussed in the section. What do these differences suggest about the chronicler's purpose or the perceived nature of the events being recorded?
  4. Asser's Life of King Alfred presents Alfred as an "ideal Christian king." Based on the provided excerpts, what specific characteristics and actions of Alfred contribute to this ideal portrayal? How might a modern biographer approach Alfred differently?
  5. The section concludes by stating that Alfred was "the product of a culture that valued exactly the qualities he embodied—and he, in turn, shaped the culture." Elaborate on this statement, using insights from all three texts to explain how Alfred both reflected and influenced Anglo-Saxon society.
