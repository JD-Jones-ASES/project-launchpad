Study Guide: The Late Medieval Crisis

Overview

Chapter 5 describes the catastrophic collapse of the High Medieval civilization that Chapter 4 described at its peak. Three overlapping crises — the Great Famine (1315–1322), the Black Death (1347–1353), and the Hundred Years' War (1337–1453) — shattered the medieval world's demographic, economic, political, and spiritual foundations. The chapter traces the Black Death's origins, spread, and social consequences; examines the Hundred Years' War through its key battles and the extraordinary career of Joan of Arc; analyzes the double crisis of the Avignon Papacy and the Great Western Schism; and profiles the proto-reformers Wycliffe and Hus. It closes with the fall of Constantinople in 1453, which historians sometimes use as a marker for the end of the Middle Ages. The literature section studies three great writers — Froissart, Boccaccio, and Chaucer — who witnessed this civilization under pressure.

Key Concepts

  • The Multiplicative Effect of Overlapping Crises: The Late Medieval Crisis was not three separate events happening to coincide — they interacted. The Great Famine weakened European bodies and social structures before the plague struck a weakened population. The plague devastated the labor force that the Hundred Years' War simultaneously needed. Each crisis amplified the others.

  • The Black Death as World-Historical Turning Point: Nothing in recorded Western history had killed so many people so quickly. The transformation of economic relationships (end of serfdom), the shaking of religious authority, the extremes of behavior (flagellants, scapegoating, hedonism), and the demographic collapse that took a century to recover all make the Black Death one of the most significant events in European history.

  • Scapegoating as Historical Warning: The accusation against Jewish communities during the plague — completely false, condemned by the Pope, yet resulting in horrific massacres — is a powerful historical warning about what fear and ignorance can lead people to do. This pattern has appeared repeatedly in history and demands recognition.

  • The Longbow and the End of Medieval Warfare: The success of English longbowmen at Crécy, Poitiers, and Agincourt against armored French cavalry demonstrated that a man with a longbow and training could kill a man in expensive armor on an expensive horse from 300 yards. This was the beginning of the long process by which mobile weapons technology would eventually make feudal military aristocracy obsolete.

  • Joan of Arc and the Problem of Supernatural Claims: Joan's story raises questions that students must engage seriously: How do we evaluate claims of supernatural experience? How can judicial processes be corrupted by political interests? How does the Church's later reversal of her conviction (1456) model institutional humility? The text deliberately does not ask students to resolve the first question, but to take the historical evidence seriously.

  • The Church's Institutional Crisis: The Avignon Papacy and the Great Western Schism were not just embarrassing — they were existentially threatening. If the Church's supreme institution could be captured by a national power, or split into three competing popes all excommunicating each other, then the entire medieval claim that the Church stood above politics was compromised.

  • Wycliffe and Hus as Reformers Before Their Time: Both raised the core questions of the Protestant Reformation — Scripture's authority, vernacular Bible access, Church corruption — a century before Luther. Both were suppressed. The key difference in 1517 was the printing press (Chapter 6).

  • 1453 as End of an Era: The fall of Constantinople ended the last remnant of the Roman Empire, disrupted Eastern trade routes, and sent Greek scholars fleeing to Italy with manuscripts. It connects directly to both the Renaissance (Chapter 6) and the Age of Exploration.

Important Terms

  • Black Death: The epidemic of bubonic plague (Yersinia pestis) that swept Europe 1347–1353, killing 30–50% of the population.
  • Yersinia pestis: The bacterium that caused the Black Death, living in fleas on rats and other rodents.
  • serfdom: The condition of peasants legally bound to a lord's land; it largely collapsed in Western Europe after the Black Death due to labor shortages.
  • flagellants: Religious groups who publicly whipped themselves during the Black Death, believing self-punishment would end the plague.
  • Hundred Years' War: The series of conflicts between England and France (1337–1453) over the English claim to the French throne.
  • longbow: The English and Welsh war bow; 10–12 arrows per minute, penetrating armor at 300 yards. Decisive at Crécy, Poitiers, and Agincourt.
  • Joan of Arc (c. 1412–1431): French peasant girl who claimed divine visions, lifted the siege of Orléans, and was burned as a heretic; later canonized as a saint.
  • Avignon Papacy (1309–1377): The period when popes resided in Avignon under French influence; called the 'Babylonian Captivity of the Church.'
  • Great Western Schism (1378–1417): The division in which two then three rival claimants each claimed to be pope; resolved by the Council of Constance.
  • conciliarism: The doctrine that a general council of the Church has authority over even the pope.
  • John Wycliffe (c. 1330–1384): Oxford theologian who argued for Scripture alone, attacked transubstantiation, began English Bible translation. Followers called Lollards.
  • Lollards: Wycliffe's followers who advocated Church reform, Scripture supremacy, and vernacular Bible access.
  • Jan Hus (c. 1369–1415): Czech theologian influenced by Wycliffe; burned at the Council of Constance; sparked the Hussite Wars.
  • Ottoman Empire: Turkish Muslim empire that under Mehmed II captured Constantinople in 1453, ending the Byzantine Empire.

Detailed Notes

The Black Death

  • Origin and Spread
    • Yersinia pestis: bacterium in fleas infesting rats
    • Central Asian origin; spread along Silk Road with Mongol armies and merchant caravans
    • Black Sea ports → Sicily 1347 (Genoese ships from Crimea) → France, England 1348 → Scandinavia 1349 → Eastern Europe 1350
    • Followed the same trade routes as cloth, spice, and students
  • Three Forms
    • Bubonic: swollen, blackened lymph nodes (buboes) in armpit and groin; transmitted by flea bites; kills 60–70%
    • Pneumonic: airborne, transmitted by coughing; attacks lungs; almost always fatal
    • Septicemic: blood itself infected; rarest; death within hours
  • Scale of Death
    • Europe: 75 million → 45–50 million; some regions lost 50% of population
    • Clergy died in large numbers (remained with sick); convents/monasteries especially vulnerable
    • Pope Clement VI consecrated the Rhône River for mass burial
  • Medieval Understanding
    • No germ theory: blamed miasma, planetary alignment, or divine judgment
    • Some kept cats away (to prevent 'witchcraft'), possibly worsening the epidemic
  • Social Consequences
    • Economic: surviving peasants gained bargaining power; serfdom collapsed in Western Europe
    • Religious: faith in the Church as mediator shaken; priests died alongside parishioners
    • Flagellants: extreme penance movement; swept Germany and Low Countries 1349
    • Scapegoating: Jews falsely accused of poisoning wells; massacres in France, Germany, Switzerland

The Hundred Years' War

  • Origins (1337)
    • Edward III of England claimed French throne through his mother
    • France crowned their own king; intermittent warfare followed for over a century
  • Military Transformation
    • Crécy (1346): French armored cavalry destroyed by English longbowmen
    • Poitiers (1356): similar English victory
    • Agincourt (1415): Henry V's exhausted, outnumbered army defeated French cavalry in muddy field
    • The longbow made armor and horses increasingly vulnerable to commoner soldiers
  • Joan of Arc
    • Born c. 1412, Domrémy, eastern France; peasant girl
    • Beginning c. 1425: claimed visions from Saints Michael, Catherine, and Margaret
    • Convinced the Dauphin (Charles VII) to give her command of troops
    • May 1429: lifted the siege of Orléans — seemed almost miraculous to contemporaries
    • Escorted Charles VII to coronation at Reims Cathedral
    • Captured by Burgundian soldiers 1430; sold to English
    • Trial: ecclesiastical court, charges of heresy; judges were her political enemies
    • Burned May 30, 1431, Rouen, age approximately 19
    • Verdict overturned 1456; beatified 1909; canonized 1920

Crisis in the Church

  • The Avignon Papacy (1309–1377)
    • All seven popes of this period were French
    • Critics called it 'Babylonian Captivity': papacy seemed captive to France
    • Pope Gregory XI returned to Rome in 1377; died 1378
  • The Great Western Schism (1378–1417)
    • Italian Urban VI elected pope; French cardinals declared election invalid
    • French cardinals elected rival pope at Avignon
    • Situation worsened: three simultaneous popes eventually
    • Council of Constance (1414–1418): conciliarism — council above pope
    • All claimants deposed or resigned; Martin V elected as single pope
  • The Pre-Reformers
    • John Wycliffe (died 1384): Scripture alone; attacked transubstantiation; English Bible; Lollards
    • Council of Constance: exhumed and burned Wycliffe's bones posthumously
    • Jan Hus (died 1415): same ideas in Bohemia; safe conduct violated; burned
    • Hussite Wars (1419–1434): Czech armies fought crusading armies repeatedly
    • Luther at the Council of Worms: 'I am a Hussite'

The Fall of Constantinople

  • May 29, 1453
    • Ottoman Sultan Mehmed II captured the city after a siege
    • Constantine XI died fighting on the walls
    • Ended the Byzantine Empire — thousand-year survival of Eastern Rome
  • Three Major Consequences
    • Greek scholars fled to Italy with ancient manuscripts → directly fueled the Renaissance
    • Overland Silk Road trade disrupted → drove Portuguese and Spanish to seek sea routes
    • Age of Exploration → discovery of the Americas

Connections

  • The Black Death's interpretation as divine punishment echoes Alcuin's interpretation of the Lindisfarne raid (Chapter 1) — the same theological framework applied to catastrophe.
  • The Boccaccio Decameron's Introduction connects to the Chaucer Canterbury Tales already referenced in Chapter 2 (Becket's pilgrimage destination) — both emerging from this period of crisis.
  • Wycliffe and Hus directly anticipate Luther (Chapter 7); the key difference between their failure and Luther's success was the printing press (Chapter 6).
  • The fall of Constantinople in 1453 directly causes the Renaissance (Chapter 6) by sending Greek scholars to Italy with manuscripts.
  • The disruption of Eastern trade routes by the Ottomans drives the Age of Exploration described in Chapter 6.
  • The Church's institutional crisis in this chapter (Avignon, Schism) provides the context for why Erasmus and other Christian humanists felt reform was urgent (Chapter 6–7).
