The Feudal System & The Manor: A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the profound societal and economic changes brought to England by the Norman Conquest, particularly the imposition of feudalism. It details the hierarchical structure of feudal society, from king to peasant, and examines the manor as the fundamental economic unit where most people lived and worked. The section also covers medieval agricultural practices, the significance of castles, and the vital historical record provided by the Domesday Book, concluding with a comparison of feudalism to Roman imperial administration.

Key Concepts

  • The Norman Transformation of England: William the Conqueror's victory at Hastings fundamentally reshaped English society by introducing feudalism, changing land ownership, military service, justice, and daily life.
  • The Feudal Pyramid: Medieval society was structured hierarchically, with the king at the top owning all land, granting fiefs to great lords (barons), who in turn granted land to knights, and at the bottom, villeins (peasants) worked the land. This created a web of mutual obligations and loyalties.
  • Vassalage and Homage: The core relationship in feudalism was between a lord and his vassal, sealed by a ceremony of homage and an oath of fealty, wherein land was exchanged for loyalty and military service. A person could be both a lord and a vassal.
  • The Manor as the Economic Center: The manor was a lord's estate, serving as the basic unit of medieval economic life. It included the lord's residence, a village, farmland, and essential services like a mill and church, all operated by peasants under the system of manorialism.
  • The Three-Field System: An advanced agricultural method that involved rotating crops (winter grain, summer legumes) and leaving one field fallow each year to maintain soil fertility and support a growing population.
  • Castles as Instruments of Power: Norman castles, evolving from quick-build motte-and-bailey structures to imposing stone keeps, were vital tools for conquest, control, and defense, visibly symbolizing Norman authority.
  • The Domesday Book's Insights: This comprehensive survey from 1086 provides a detailed snapshot of land ownership, population, and economic value across England, revealing the extent of the Norman transfer of power from English to Norman lords.
  • Decentralized Power of Feudalism: Unlike the centralized Roman Empire, feudalism relied on delegated authority and mutual obligations between lords and vassals, leading to a less unified but often more resilient system that functioned without strong central government.

Important Terms

  • Feudalism : The organization of society around the exchange of land for military service, prevalent in medieval Europe.
  • Fiefs : Large estates of land granted by a king to his great lords.
  • Vassal : A person who holds land from a lord in exchange for loyalty and military service, sealed by homage and an oath of fealty.
  • Homage : A ceremony in which a vassal knelt before his lord, placed his hands between the lord's, and swore an oath of fealty.
  • Oath of Fealty : An oath of faithfulness sworn by a vassal to his lord during the ceremony of homage.
  • Barons / Earls : Great lords who received large land grants (fiefs) directly from the king in exchange for military service.
  • Knights : Professional warriors who formed the backbone of medieval armies, holding land from barons in exchange for military service.
  • Villeins / Serfs / Peasants : The vast majority of the population in medieval society, legally bound to the land of a lord's manor, owing labor and dues. They were not slaves but lacked freedom of movement and marriage without lord's consent.
  • Manor : The basic unit of medieval economic life; a lord's estate including his residence, a village, farmland, and essential services.
  • Manorialism : The system by which manors were organized and operated, centered on the labor and payments owed by peasants to the lord.
  • Three-field system : An agricultural method dividing farmland into three sections, rotating winter crops, summer crops, and fallow land to maintain soil fertility.
  • Fallow : Agricultural land left unplanted for a season or year to recover fertility, often used for grazing.
  • Demesne : The lord's own fields on a manor, which villeins were required to work for a specified number of days each week.
  • Motte-and-bailey : An early type of Norman castle with a wooden tower on an earthen mound (motte) and an enclosed courtyard (bailey), quick to build and crucial for conquest.
  • Stone Keep : A massive square or rectangular stone tower that became the standard form of castle after motte-and-bailey structures, offering much stronger defense.
  • Domesday Book : A comprehensive survey commissioned by William the Conqueror in 1086, recording land ownership, resources, and value across England before and after the Conquest.
  • Angevin Empire : The collection of territories, including England and vast parts of France (Normandy, Anjou, Aquitaine, etc.), controlled by Henry II and his successors.

Detailed Notes

The World the Normans Brought

  • Beyond a New King: The Norman Conquest by William the Conqueror at Hastings did more than replace the king; it imposed an entire new social and economic system: feudalism.
  • Feudalism's Core: Society was organized around the exchange of land for military service, a highly structured system developed by the Normans.
  • Total Transformation: Within a generation, feudalism thoroughly transformed England, affecting land ownership, military obligations, judicial administration, and the daily lives of ordinary people.

The Feudal Pyramid

  • Hierarchical Structure: Medieval society was visualized as a pyramid, detailing who owed what to whom.
    • King: At the apex, theoretically owning all land in the kingdom.
    • Barons/Earls: Granted large estates (fiefs) by the king in exchange for homage and a promise of military service (e.g., providing a certain number of knights).
    • Knights: Professional warriors who received smaller land portions from barons, obligated to serve their lord for a set number of days per year (e.g., 40 days) with horse and armor.
    • Villeins (Serfs/Peasants): The vast majority, who worked the land and owed labor and dues to the lord of their manor.
  • Vassalage and Homage:
    • A vassal held land from a lord in exchange for loyalty and service.
    • This relationship was formalized by homage, where the vassal knelt and swore an oath of fealty.
    • Individuals could be both a lord to those below them and a vassal to those above.
  • Interlocking Loyalties: This system created a complex web of duties, theoretically ensuring everyone had a lord above and tenants below (if they held land).
  • Decentralization: While the king was at the top, a baron's primary loyalty was often to his immediate lord, not always the distant king. This made medieval kingdoms less unified than ancient empires.

The Manor: The Basic Unit of Life

  • Economic Reality: If feudalism was the political structure, the manor was its economic foundation.
  • Lord's Estate: A manor was a lord's estate, typically encompassing:
    • The lord's hall or castle.
    • A village with peasant cottages.
    • Farmland divided into strips.
    • A church.
    • A mill and bakehouse (owned by the lord; peasants paid fees to use them).
  • Manorialism: The system by which manors were organized and operated, defining the relationship between the lord and the peasants.
  • The Three-Field System:
    • Crop Rotation: A solution to maintain soil fertility over time.
    • System: Farmland divided into three fields:
      1. Winter crop (wheat/rye, planted in autumn).
      2. Summer crop (beans/peas/barley, planted in spring).
      3. Fallow (unplanted, to rest and recover fertility).
    • Rotation: Fields rotated annually on a three-year cycle. Legumes (beans, peas) replenished nitrogen in the soil.
    • Impact: This system supported steady population growth in medieval Europe for centuries.
  • Peasant Life:
    • Agricultural Calendar: Life revolved around planting, tending, weeding, and especially harvest (August, long hours).
    • Seasonal Tasks: Slaughtering animals in autumn, winter for repairs and crafts, surviving on stored food.
    • Obligations to the Lord:
      • Labor Service: Typically 2-3 days per week working on the lord's own fields (the demesne).
      • Fees: Payments for using the lord's mill and bakehouse.
      • Marriage Fees: Payment when a daughter married.
      • Death Duty: The lord took the best animal upon a villein's death.

Castle Architecture: Stone and Power

  • Symbol of Conquest: Castles were the most visible symbol of Norman power and instruments of both conquest and defense.
  • Early Norman Castles: Motte-and-Bailey:
    • Design: An artificial earthen mound (motte) topped with a wooden tower, connected to an enclosed courtyard (bailey) ringed by a wooden palisade and ditch.
    • Advantages: Fast (days/weeks) and effective to build using forced labor, intimidating to the local populace.
  • Evolution to Stone Keeps:
    • Over the next century, wooden structures were replaced by stone.
    • Stone Keep: A massive square or rectangular stone tower became the standard.
    • Examples: William the Conqueror initiated the Tower of London (White Tower).
    • Characteristics: Expensive and slow to build but nearly impregnable against contemporary military technology.

The Domesday Book as a Window into Medieval England

  • Comprehensive Survey (1086): Commissioned by William the Conqueror, this survey recorded details for almost every estate in England.
  • Key Information Recorded:
    • Landholders in 1066 (pre-Conquest) vs. 1086 (post-Conquest).
    • Population demographics (villagers, smallholders, slaves, free men).
    • Agricultural resources (ploughs, meadow, woodland).
    • Economic value (1066 vs. 1086).
  • Insight into Transformation: The Domesday Book vividly shows the transfer of land and power from English to Norman lords, manor by manor, illustrating the profound change in England's ruling class.

Norman England and the Angevin Empire

  • Expanded Territory: By Henry II's reign (1154), English power extended far beyond England.
  • Angevin Empire: Through inheritance and conquest, Henry II controlled significant French territories (Normandy, Anjou, Maine, Touraine, Aquitaine) — often more than the King of France himself. This vast collection of territories is known as the Angevin Empire.

The Feudal System and Roman Provincial Administration (Comparison)

  • Roman Centralization:
    • Empire organized into provinces with appointed governors (legati).
    • Power flowed directly from the emperor outward, highly centralized.
  • Feudal Decentralization:
    • A baron's primary loyalty was to his immediate lord, not always the king.
    • King could not directly command a baron's vassal; commands went through intermediaries.
    • Strengths: Could function without a powerful central government, resilient.
    • Weaknesses: Could not achieve the unified action seen in the Roman Empire.
  • Governing Large Territories: Both systems addressed the challenge of governing vast lands with limited technology.
    • Rome's Answer: Centralize, standardize, direct governors.
    • Medieval Europe's Answer: Delegate, create mutual obligations, empower local levels.
  • Enduring Question: The tension between central power and local authority remains a fundamental question, as seen even in modern federal systems like the United States.

Review Questions

  1. Beyond simply replacing the king, how did the Norman Conquest fundamentally transform English society and economy through the introduction of feudalism?
  2. Describe the structure of the feudal pyramid. Explain the roles and obligations of each level, and how the concept of a "vassal" created a complex web of loyalties.
  3. What was the manor, and why was it considered the basic unit of medieval economic life? How did the three-field system contribute to its sustainability, and what was daily life like for a villein on a manor?
  4. Trace the evolution of Norman castles from motte-and-bailey to stone keeps. How did castles serve as both instruments of conquest and symbols of power during the Norman era?
  5. How does the Domesday Book serve as a unique historical document for understanding the immediate aftermath of the Norman Conquest? What insights does it provide into the shift of power and the state of England?
