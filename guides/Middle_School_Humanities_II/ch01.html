Study Guide: Alfred the Great & the Viking Age

Overview

Chapter 1 traces the Viking Age from its opening shock — the raid on Lindisfarne in 793 — through the Danish conquest of most of Anglo-Saxon England, the remarkable career of Alfred the Great, and the eventual Norman Conquest of 1066. The chapter establishes how a small Scandinavian seafaring culture, empowered by a revolutionary ship design, shook the entire Christian world. It then examines how Alfred transformed Wessex from a kingdom on the brink of destruction into the nucleus of a unified England — not only through military genius but through an ambitious programme of law, literacy, and fortification. The literary section explores three complementary lenses on this world: the heroic poem Beowulf, the terse chronicle entries of the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, and Asser's warm biographical portrait of Alfred himself.

Key Concepts

  • The Longship as Force Multiplier: The Viking longship's combination of ocean-crossing capability and shallow draft transformed raiding. It could beach directly on a riverbank, giving Vikings access to monasteries and towns that no previous sea power could reach. This single technological advantage explains much of Viking success.

  • Divine Judgment in the Medieval Worldview: When Alcuin interpreted the Lindisfarne raid as God's punishment for England's sins, he expressed a worldview shared across medieval Christendom: that national disasters are divine responses to collective sin. This framework shaped how people understood the Black Death and other calamities for centuries.

  • Alfred's Dual Role as Warrior and Scholar-King: Alfred is remarkable because he combined military leadership with intellectual vision. Defeating the Danes was necessary, but Alfred understood that Viking raids had destroyed the educational infrastructure of England. His educational programme addressed this crisis directly, making him unique among medieval kings.

  • The Danelaw as a Cultural Boundary: The Danelaw was not simply a political arrangement — it was a genuine cultural divide. Danish settlers brought their language, customs, and place-names (visible today in words ending in -by, -thorpe, and -thwaite), creating a durable imprint on the English landscape.

  • Vernacular Translation as Political Act: Alfred's decision to translate essential texts into Old English was more than a practical measure. It reflected a conviction that knowledge belongs to a community, not just to Latin-literate elites. This same conviction drove the Protestant Reformers six centuries later.

  • The Burh Network as Strategic Innovation: Alfred's system of fortified towns placed no more than a day's march apart created the first coherent territorial defense system in English history. It was as much a political statement as a military one — the king protecting his people.

  • Beowulf's Christian-Heroic Synthesis: The Beowulf poet was a Christian celebrating a pagan hero. Rather than condemning the pre-Christian world, the poet found in its heroic values — courage, loyalty, generosity — something compatible with Christian virtue. This synthesis reveals the complexity of Anglo-Saxon Christianity.

  • 1066 as a Hinge Point: The Norman Conquest is one of the most consequential events in English history. It replaced the entire Anglo-Saxon ruling class with Norman French speakers, yet Alfred's legacy survived in English common law, the concept of royal duty to the people, and the English language itself — Anglo-Saxon grammar enriched with French vocabulary.

Important Terms

  • Viking: From Old Norse vikingr, likely meaning 'sea raider.' Scandinavian peoples (Norse, Danes, Swedes) who raided, traded, and settled across Europe from the late eighth century onward.
  • Great Heathen Army: The large Danish force that invaded England in 865 and conquered Northumbria, East Anglia, and Mercia over the next fourteen years.
  • fyrd: The Anglo-Saxon militia of free men who served when summoned by the king, representing ordinary freemen rather than professional warriors.
  • Battle of Edington (878): Alfred's decisive defeat of the Danish king Guthrum, which reversed the Viking conquest of Wessex.
  • Treaty of Wedmore (878): The agreement by which Guthrum converted to Christianity (with Alfred as godfather) and withdrew north and east of the Danelaw boundary.
  • Danelaw: The area of northern and eastern England under Danish law and custom, bounded roughly from London to Chester. Danish place names still mark this region today.
  • burh: A fortified town built or strengthened by Alfred as part of a defensive network. The word survives in English place names ending in -bury or -borough.
  • Asser: Welsh monk and bishop (died c. 909) who served Alfred's court and wrote the Life of King Alfred, our most important near-contemporary biography of any medieval English king.
  • Anglo-Saxon Chronicle: The year-by-year record of English history begun under Alfred, written in Old English, continued until the mid-twelfth century.
  • Aethelstan: Alfred's grandson who in 927 unified all of England by defeating the last Scandinavian king of York.
  • Cnut: Danish king who ruled England 1016–1035, also king of Denmark and Norway; maintained Alfred's laws and supported the Church.
  • Beowulf: The longest surviving Old English poem, written by a Christian monk drawing on older Germanic oral traditions; the hero fights the monster Grendel, Grendel's mother, and a dragon.

Detailed Notes

The Vikings and the World They Raided

  • Origins of Viking Raiding
    • Scandinavia was mountainous with limited farmland — economic pressure to seek wealth elsewhere
    • Norse culture prized courage, glory, and treasure; raiding was socially prestigious
    • The longship was the key technology: ocean-going yet able to navigate rivers and beach on banks
  • The Lindisfarne Raid (793)
    • First Viking raid on the British Isles; struck the great monastery without warning
    • Monks had spent generations copying manuscripts and educating Anglo-Saxon nobles
    • Alcuin's letter to the King of Northumbria: interpreted as God's judgment on England's sins
    • Marked the beginning of two centuries of Viking activity in Britain
  • The Great Heathen Army
    • Arrived 865; coalition of Danish forces under multiple commanders
    • By 870: three of four Anglo-Saxon kingdoms had fallen (Northumbria, East Anglia, Mercia)
    • Only Wessex, in the south, remained — ruled by Alfred from 871

Alfred the Great

  • Athelney and the Turning Point
    • January 878: Guthrum surprised and overran Wessex; Alfred fled to Somerset marshes
    • Sent messengers to call the fyrd; assembled army at Egbert's Stone
    • Battle of Edington: decisive defeat of Guthrum, spring 878
  • The Treaty of Wedmore and Its Consequences
    • Guthrum baptized as a Christian; Alfred his godfather
    • Danelaw established — Danish law north and east; Anglo-Saxon law south and west
    • Peace enabled Alfred's programme of reconstruction
  • Rebuilding England
    • Gathered scholars: Asser (Wales), Grimbald (France), John the Old Saxon (Germany)
    • Translated Pastoral Care, Consolation of Philosophy, Bede's Ecclesiastical History
    • Created Anglo-Saxon Chronicle: history in Old English, continued for centuries
    • Reorganized fyrd into rotating shifts; built burh network
    • Law code drawing on Ten Commandments and Golden Rule
    • Died 899 at approximately fifty years old

After Alfred

  • Unification Under Aethelstan
    • Alfred's son Edward and daughter Aethelflaed pushed into the Danelaw
    • Aethelstan (927): defeated last Scandinavian king of York; first ruler of all England
  • Cnut and Danish Rule (1016–1035)
    • Sweyn Forkbeard invaded 1013; Cnut became king 1016
    • Created North Sea empire (England, Denmark, Norway)
    • Ruled well: maintained Alfred's laws, supported Church, made peace with Anglo-Saxon nobility
  • The Succession Crisis and 1066
    • Edward the Confessor died childless January 1066 — three claimants
    • Harold Godwinson defeated Harald Hardrada at Stamford Bridge (September 1066)
    • October 14, 1066: Harold killed at Hastings; William of Normandy crowned Christmas Day

Literature of the Viking Age

  • Beowulf
    • 3,000+ lines of Old English verse; written by Christian monk from older oral traditions
    • Set in Scandinavia: hero Beowulf kills Grendel and Grendel's mother; dies fighting a dragon
    • Grendel described as descendant of Cain — Christian framing of a pre-Christian world
    • Celebrates Germanic heroic values: courage, loyalty, generosity
  • The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle
    • Begun under Alfred; continued at various monasteries until mid-twelfth century
    • 793 entry: apocalyptic — portents of fire, famine, and the harrowing of Lindisfarne
    • 878 entry: stark and matter-of-fact about Alfred's flight through woods and fen-fastnesses
  • Asser's Life of King Alfred (c. 893)
    • Written while Alfred was still alive; warm, personal portrait
    • Describes Alfred's love of poetry, his anguish at lacking education, his generosity
    • Must be read critically: Asser wrote for Alfred's court and would not include material Alfred found unwelcome

Connections

  • Alcuin's interpretation of the Lindisfarne raid as divine judgment anticipates how medieval people would interpret the Black Death (Chapter 5) — the framework of national sin and divine punishment persisted for centuries.
  • Alfred's vernacular translation programme directly anticipates Tyndale's English Bible translation (Chapter 7) — both believed important texts should be accessible in the language people actually speak, though Alfred's target audience was the educated class, not every ploughman.
  • The Norman Conquest (1066) introduces the feudal system and the Plantagenet dynasty, both central to Chapter 2.
  • Beowulf's question about virtuous pagans — can a brave hero who never knew Christ be honored? — recurs when Dante places Virgil and other ancient poets in Limbo (Chapter 4).
  • The concept of the king as protector and law-giver, developed under Alfred, directly informs the struggle over royal power that produces Magna Carta in 1215 (Chapter 2).
