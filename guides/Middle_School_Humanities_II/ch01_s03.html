Study Guide: Historical Summary: Alfred the Great & The Viking Age

Overview

This section chronicles the dramatic period of the Viking Age in England, from the initial devastating raids to the eventual Norman Conquest. It focuses on the relentless Viking incursions that threatened to extinguish Anglo-Saxon England and highlights the pivotal role of Alfred the Great, who not only successfully defended his kingdom but also laid the foundations for a unified English identity through military, legal, and educational reforms. The section concludes with the subsequent unification under Alfred's descendants, the return of Danish rule under Cnut, and the ultimate end of Anglo-Saxon England in 1066.

Key Concepts

  • The Viking Threat and its Impact: The Vikings, originating from Scandinavia, launched devastating raids on the British Isles starting in 793, particularly targeting undefended monastic communities. Their raids escalated into full-scale invasions by 865, severely disrupting Anglo-Saxon kingdoms and Christian learning.
  • Revolutionary Viking Longship Technology: The unique design of the Viking longship, combining ocean-going capacity with a shallow draft, allowed Vikings to cross vast seas and navigate far up inland rivers, giving them unprecedented access to targets previously unreachable by sea powers.
  • Alfred the Great as Warrior and Scholar-King: Alfred's reign (871-899) was crucial for the survival of Wessex and English culture. He not only militarily defeated the Great Heathen Army, leading to the Treaty of Wedmore and the establishment of the Danelaw, but also launched an ambitious program of educational reform, translating key Latin texts into Old English and establishing the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle.
  • The Danelaw: This was the region of northern and eastern England governed by Danish law and custom after the Treaty of Wedmore. It represented a lasting cultural and legal division of England for a significant period.
  • Alfred's Legacy and English Unification: Alfred's reforms—including the burh system, military reorganization, and legal codification—created a more resilient and organized state. His successors continued his work, leading to the unification of England under his grandson Aethelstan in 927.
  • The Significance of 1066: The Norman Conquest of 1066, marked by the Battle of Hastings, fundamentally transformed England. It ended Anglo-Saxon rule, replaced Old English with a Norman-French aristocracy and language, and reshaped English law, church, and social structure, though Alfred's legal and governmental foundations subtly endured.

Important Terms

  • Viking: (From Old Norse vikingr, likely meaning “one who comes from the fjords” or “sea raider.”) Scandinavian peoples (Norse, Danes, Swedes) who raided, traded, and settled across Europe from the late eighth century onward.
  • Great Heathen Army: A large Danish force that invaded England in 865. It spent fourteen years systematically conquering the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms of Northumbria, East Anglia, and Mercia, threatening the survival of English Christianity.
  • Asser: A Welsh monk and scholar (died c. 909) who served at Alfred's court and wrote The Life of King Alfred, the most important biographical source for Alfred's life.
  • fyrd: The Anglo-Saxon militia, composed of free men obligated to serve the king when summoned. They were ordinary freemen defending their land, distinct from professional warriors.
  • Danelaw: The area of northern and eastern England under Danish law and custom after the Treaty of Wedmore. Its boundary ran roughly from London northwest to Chester.
  • burh: A fortified settlement built or strengthened by Alfred the Great as part of a defensive network against Viking raids. Burhs were strategically placed a day's march apart to ensure rapid defense across Wessex.

Detailed Notes

The Vikings and the World They Raided

  • First Raid (793 AD): The first recorded Viking raid on the British Isles targeted the wealthy, unwalled island monastery of Lindisfarne. This event shocked Christian Europe, symbolizing a new and terrifying threat.
    • Alcuin's Reaction: The scholar Alcuin interpreted the raid as divine judgment, reflecting a common medieval belief that national disaster was God's punishment for national sins.
  • Motivations for Raiding:
    • Economics: Scandinavia had limited farmland, making raiding a means of acquiring wealth and resources.
    • Technology: The Viking longship was a revolutionary vessel, capable of both ocean crossings and shallow river navigation, allowing access to inland targets previously immune to naval attack.
    • Culture: Norse society valued courage, glory, and treasure, all of which could be gained through raiding.
  • Escalation to Invasion: Initial raids intensified through the late 8th and early 9th centuries. By 865, these raids became a full-scale invasion with the landing of the Great Heathen Army, a powerful Danish coalition that systematically conquered three of the four Anglo-Saxon kingdoms (Northumbria, East Anglia, Mercia) by 870. Only Wessex remained.

Alfred the Great: Warrior and Scholar-King

  • Early Life: Alfred (born 849) was the fifth, youngest son of King Aethelwulf of Wessex, unexpectedly becoming king. He had an early love for poetry and learning, demonstrated by his memorization of Old English verse.
  • Defeat and Revival:
    • In 878, Danish forces under King Guthrum invaded Wessex, driving Alfred into hiding in the marshes of Athelney, Somerset. The legend of Alfred burning cakes, though likely apocryphal, illustrates his desperate situation.
    • From Athelney, Alfred rallied the fyrd (Anglo-Saxon militia).
    • Battle of Edington (878): Alfred decisively defeated Guthrum's forces.
    • Treaty of Wedmore (878): This treaty followed the victory, leading to Guthrum's conversion to Christianity (with Alfred as godfather) and the establishment of the Danelaw—a division of England into Anglo-Saxon and Danish spheres of influence.
  • Educational and Cultural Reforms: Alfred recognized that Viking raids had devastated English learning. He initiated a comprehensive reform program:
    • Recruitment of Scholars: He gathered scholars like Asser (his biographer), Grimbald, and John the Old Saxon to his court.
    • Vernacular Translations: Alfred himself translated crucial Latin works (e.g., Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy, Gregory the Great's Pastoral Care, Bede's Ecclesiastical History) into Old English.
    • Alfred's Vision (from Pastoral Care preface): He articulated the necessity of translating "most needful" books into English due to the widespread collapse of Latin literacy, even among the clergy. This aimed to ensure basic understanding of Christian texts for the educated class.
    • Anglo-Saxon Chronicle: Alfred initiated this historical record, written in Old English, which provided a continuous history of England for centuries.
  • Military and Administrative Reforms:
    • Reorganized Fyrd: Divided the militia into rotating shifts to ensure constant defense readiness.
    • Network of Burhs: Built a system of fortified towns (burhs) strategically placed to defend Wessex against further Viking incursions. Many English place names ending in -bury or -borough derive from these burhs.
    • Legal Reforms: Created a law code drawing on earlier Anglo-Saxon laws, the Ten Commandments, and the Golden Rule.
  • Death and Legacy: Alfred died in 899, leaving Wessex transformed into the most powerful and well-organized kingdom in England.

After Alfred: The Unification of England

  • Alfred's Successors: His son Edward the Elder and daughter Aethelflaed (Lady of the Mercians) continued to reclaim parts of the Danelaw.
  • Aethelstan's Unification (927): Alfred's grandson Aethelstan defeated the last Scandinavian king of York, becoming the first ruler to control all of England and adopting the title "King of all Britain."

The Return of the Danes: Cnut the Great

  • New Invasions: A new wave of Danish invasions began under Sweyn Forkbeard in 1013, forcing King Aethelred "the Unready" to flee.
  • Cnut's North Sea Empire: Sweyn's son, Cnut, completed the conquest by 1016, becoming King of England. He later expanded his rule to Denmark and Norway, creating a vast North Sea Empire.
  • Good Governance: Cnut ruled England effectively for nineteen years, maintaining Alfred's laws, supporting the Church, and making peace with the Anglo-Saxon nobility. The story of him commanding the tide to demonstrate humility is a famous (though likely legendary) anecdote.

The Succession Crisis and the Norman Conquest

  • Edward the Confessor: Following Cnut's death (1035) and a period of instability, the English throne passed to Edward the Confessor, a son of Aethelred who had strong Norman ties. Edward built Westminster Abbey but died childless in January 1066.
  • Three Claimants to the Throne (1066):
    • Harold Godwinson, the most powerful English earl.
    • Harald Hardrada, King of Norway.
    • William, Duke of Normandy.
  • The Battles of 1066:
    • Stamford Bridge (September 1066): Harold Godwinson defeated and killed Harald Hardrada.
    • Hastings (October 14, 1066): Harold's exhausted army was defeated by William of Normandy, and Harold was killed.
  • Significance of 1066: The Norman Conquest fundamentally ended Anglo-Saxon England, replacing its language, aristocracy, and much of its culture with Norman-French influences. While a dramatic shift, elements of Alfred's legacy, such as common law, provided continuity.

Review Questions

  1. How did the Viking longship contribute to the success of Viking raids and invasions, allowing them to target areas previously considered safe?
  2. Beyond military victory, what specific actions did Alfred the Great take to preserve and restore learning and culture in England, and why did he consider these efforts so vital?
  3. Discuss the significance of the Danelaw. How did it geographically and culturally divide England, and what does its establishment tell us about the outcome of the Anglo-Danish conflicts?
  4. Compare and contrast Alfred the Great's and Cnut the Great's reigns in England. In what ways were they similar in their approach to governance, and how did their legacies differ?
  5. Explain why the year 1066 is considered one of the most consequential dates in English history. What were the immediate and long-term impacts of the Norman Conquest on English society, culture, and governance?
