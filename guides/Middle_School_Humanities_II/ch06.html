Study Guide: The Renaissance

Overview

Chapter 6 examines the Renaissance from its Italian origins around 1350 through its intellectual flowering in the sixteenth century. The chapter covers four major dimensions: the humanist revival of classical learning centered in the Italian city-states; the transformation of visual art through linear perspective and the great Renaissance masters; the revolutionary impact of Gutenberg's printing press and the Age of Exploration; and the new political and intellectual voices represented by Machiavelli, Erasmus, and Thomas More. The chapter positions the Renaissance as both a culmination of medieval foundations and a preparation for the Protestant Reformation, emphasizing that Renaissance humanism was not anti-Christian but rather used classical tools in the service of Christian renewal.

Key Concepts

  • The Renaissance as Building on Medieval Foundations: The idea that the Middle Ages were 'Dark Ages' is now considered misleading. The Renaissance built on the great cathedrals, universities, Dante's poetry, and Aquinas's philosophy of the High Middle Ages. Petrarch's contemptuous dismissal of the medieval centuries was itself a rhetorical move, not a fair historical assessment.

  • Humanism as Scholarly Programme, Not Secularism: Renaissance humanism was the disciplined study of ancient Greek and Roman texts as preparation for a good and virtuous life. Most humanists — Petrarch, Erasmus, More — were deeply Christian. They saw the study of classical antiquity as complementary to, not in competition with, Christian faith.

  • Patronage as the Engine of Renaissance Art: Artists in the Renaissance needed patrons to survive. The Medici model — wealthy merchants using art to express piety, civic pride, and political power simultaneously — drove the extraordinary artistic output of the period. Without patrons, the Sistine Chapel ceiling would never have been painted.

  • Linear Perspective as a Conceptual Revolution: The development of linear perspective was not merely a technical improvement in painting technique. It represented a fundamentally new way of understanding the relationship between the human observer and the physical world — a mathematical framework that implied the world could be systematically understood and represented by human intelligence.

  • The Printing Press as the Engine of Change: Gutenberg's press transformed European society as profoundly as any political or military event. It made literacy practical, spread ideas with unprecedented speed, and removed the Church's near-monopoly on the reproduction of texts. Without it, Luther's theses would have remained a local academic debate.

  • Machiavelli's Realism as a Departure from Medieval Political Thought: The medieval tradition insisted that kings ruled for their people's good under God's authority. Machiavelli analyzed politics as it actually worked, treating it as a science of power separate from theology. Whether this was cynicism or courage depends on one's perspective — but it was genuinely new.

  • Christian Humanism's Third Way: Erasmus and More represented a position between the corruption they criticized in the Church and the radicalism they feared in Luther. They wanted to use humanist scholarship to purify Christianity from within. Their failure to prevent the Reformation split did not invalidate their project; it showed how deep the crisis had become.

  • The Renaissance Prepared the Reformation: Through three specific mechanisms — textual criticism of Scripture, the printing press, and the educated critique of Church corruption — the Renaissance made the Reformation possible. Luther built on tools and an audience that the Renaissance created.

Important Terms

  • Renaissance: From French, 'rebirth.' Roughly 1350–1600; the revival of ancient Greek and Roman learning, art, and philosophy.
  • city-state: An independent self-governing state consisting of a city and surrounding territory. Florence, Venice, Milan, Naples, and Rome were the major Renaissance city-states.
  • humanism: The Renaissance scholarly programme centered on studying ancient Greek and Roman texts; studia humanitatis. Most humanists were devout Christians.
  • patron: A wealthy individual who supports artists and scholars by commissioning work and providing resources. Patronage was the economic engine of Renaissance art.
  • Medici: The Florentine banking family who became the greatest patrons of the Renaissance. Key figures: Cosimo de' Medici and Lorenzo the Magnificent.
  • linear perspective: A mathematical system of representing three-dimensional space through converging parallel lines meeting at a vanishing point. Developed by Brunelleschi c. 1420; codified by Alberti (1435).
  • movable type: Gutenberg's system of individually cast metal letters arranged into words, locked into a frame, and pressed onto paper — making printing vastly faster than hand-copying.
  • caravel: A small, maneuverable Portuguese sailing ship with lateen sails that could sail into the wind; the primary vessel of the Age of Exploration.
  • Machiavellian: Calculating and willing to use deception or ruthlessness to achieve political ends; derived from Machiavelli's The Prince.
  • Christian humanism: The Renaissance movement combining humanist scholarship with Christian devotion to reform the Church from within; exemplified by Erasmus and More.
  • Utopia: Thomas More's 1516 work describing an imaginary island society with common property and religious toleration; from Greek for both 'good place' and 'no place.'

Detailed Notes

The Italian City-States and the Origins of the Renaissance

  • Why Italy
    • Wealth from Mediterranean trade (Venice, Genoa, Florence) and banking (Medici)
    • Roman ruins literally everywhere — inspiration and material
    • Latin closest vernacular to Italian — classical texts more accessible
    • Greek scholars fled Constantinople after 1453 with manuscripts
    • Competitive city-states: each city-state funded arts to outshine rivals
  • Florence
    • Center of early Renaissance; ruled by Medici family of bankers
    • Lorenzo the Magnificent: Botticelli, young Michelangelo, Leonardo at his court
  • Venice
    • Europe's greatest trading city; rich color, elaborate ceremony, celebration of civic glory
  • Rome
    • Popes recovering from Avignon and Schism rebuilt the city on a grand scale
    • Pope Julius II commissioned Michelangelo for Sistine Chapel; began rebuilding St. Peter's

Humanism

  • What It Was
    • Study of studia humanitatis: grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, moral philosophy
    • Study in original Latin and Greek, not just translation
    • Produced better people: more eloquent, more virtuous, more capable of civic life
  • Petrarch (1304–1374)
    • Born in exile from Florence; grew up near Avignon
    • Captivated by Cicero, Virgil, Livy — spent life searching for lost manuscripts
    • Letter to Posterity: described devotion to Latin poets
    • Coined 'Dark Ages' as a dismissal of the post-Roman period
  • The Medici
    • Cosimo (1389–1464): funded complete Plato translation into Latin; Platonic Academy
    • Lorenzo the Magnificent (1449–1492): artists at court; himself a poet writing in Italian

Renaissance Art

  • The Technical Revolution
    • Medieval painting: flat, symbolic, gold backgrounds, hierarchical sizing
    • Brunelleschi c. 1420: demonstrated linear perspective mathematically
    • Alberti's On Painting (1435): codified the rules; spread across Italy
    • Within a generation: three-dimensional depth replaced flat symbolism
  • The Great Masters
    • Giotto (c. 1267–1337): first to give figures real weight and three-dimensionality
    • Botticelli (c. 1445–1510): Birth of Venus (c. 1485); Primavera; dreamy, linear grace
    • Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519): Mona Lisa, Last Supper; notebooks on science and anatomy
    • Michelangelo (1475–1564): Sistine Chapel ceiling (1508–1512); David (1504)
    • Raphael (1483–1520): School of Athens — Plato and Aristotle at the center

The Printing Press

  • Gutenberg's Innovation
    • German goldsmith developed movable type c. 1440–1455
    • First major publication: Latin Bible c. 1455
    • Scribe: 1 book/year; press: hundreds in days
  • Consequences
    • Books dramatically cheaper — affordable to merchant class within a generation
    • Literacy spread (now there was something to read)
    • Ideas outran censorship — Luther's theses circulated across Germany within weeks
    • Standardized texts rather than multiplied errors

The Age of Exploration

  • Portugal
    • Prince Henry the Navigator (1394–1460): systematic African coast exploration
    • Bartolomeu Dias: rounded Cape of Good Hope (1488)
  • Spain
    • Columbus (1492): reached Caribbean; opened Americas to Europe
  • Portugal Again
    • Vasco da Gama (1498): first sea voyage Europe to India; Malabar Coast
  • Circumnavigation
    • Magellan's expedition (1519–1521): first circumnavigation of the globe
  • Consequences
    • Broke Venice and Genoa's monopoly on Eastern trade
    • Devastating consequences for peoples of Americas and Africa
    • Challenged Europeans' assumptions about the world

Machiavelli, Erasmus, and More

  • Machiavelli (1469–1527)
    • Served as diplomat for Florentine Republic; lost position when Medici returned
    • The Prince (1513): how power is actually won and kept
    • Key departure: wrote about real world, not ideal world
    • Prince must learn how to not be good when circumstances require
    • Is it better to be feared or loved? If forced to choose: fear is more reliable
  • Erasmus (c. 1466–1536)
    • Dutch scholar; Christian humanist
    • Greek New Testament (1516): corrected centuries of errors in the Latin Vulgate
    • The Praise of Folly (1509): witty satire of Church corruption
    • Reform from within; opposed Luther's break with Rome
  • Thomas More (1478–1535)
    • English lawyer and statesman; Erasmus's closest friend
    • Utopia (1516): imaginary society with common property, no poverty, religious toleration
    • Greek pun: 'good place' (eutopia) and 'no place' (outopia)
    • Refused Oath of Supremacy when Henry VIII broke with Rome
    • Executed 1535; later declared a saint by the Catholic Church

Connections

  • The Renaissance began when Greek scholars fled Constantinople after its fall (Chapter 5) with manuscripts — directly connecting the Late Medieval Crisis to the Renaissance.
  • Petrarch's dismissal of the 'Dark Ages' is implicitly corrected by Chapters 1–4, which show the real achievements of the period he dismissed.
  • Erasmus's Greek New Testament showing that the Latin Vulgate contained errors directly created the textual credibility crisis that Luther exploited (Chapter 7).
  • The printing press that spread Luther's theses (Chapter 7) was described in this chapter as the 'engine of the Reformation.'
  • Thomas More's execution for refusing the Oath of Supremacy connects directly to Henry VIII and the English Reformation (Chapter 7).
  • Machiavelli's analysis of power as separate from moral authority anticipates the secular political thought that would develop further after the Reformation.
  • The Age of Exploration's discoveries of the Americas began the processes of colonization and globalization that would reshape the modern world, connecting to larger historical themes beyond the scope of this course.
