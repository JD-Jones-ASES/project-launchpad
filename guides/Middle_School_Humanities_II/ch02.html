Study Guide: The Norman Conquest & the Feudal Order

Overview

Chapter 2 examines the Norman Conquest of 1066 and its transforming consequences for English society, law, and culture. It traces the events from the Battle of Hastings through William's consolidation of power — including the brutal Harrying of the North and the meticulous Domesday survey — and explains the feudal system the Normans imposed. The chapter follows the Plantagenet dynasty through Henry II's creation of English common law, the tragic clash between church and crown in the Becket affair, and culminates in the Magna Carta of 1215 — one of the most consequential documents in Western political history. The literature section explores the chivalric ideal through the Song of Roland and the conquered perspective through William of Malmesbury.

Key Concepts

  • The Norman Identity: The Normans were Vikings who had settled in France, been Christianized, and adopted French culture — yet retained their energy for conquest. By 1066 they had become the most dynamic military force in Europe, combining Norse warrior tradition with French feudal organization.

  • The Feudal Pyramid: Feudalism was a system of mutual obligation: land in exchange for military service. William applied it with systematic thoroughness, treating all of England as personally conquered land and granting it to Norman followers in exchange for knight quotas. This gave him control over his nobility that few European rulers matched.

  • The Domesday Book as a Tool of Power: The Domesday survey was not a historical curiosity — it was a weapon of royal administration. By knowing exactly what every estate was worth and who held it, William could demand accurate service, tax, and military contribution. It revealed how completely the Norman Conquest had remade the English aristocracy within twenty years.

  • The Investiture Controversy and Church-State Tension: The struggle between popes and emperors over bishop appointments raised a question that would haunt medieval Europe: who has ultimate authority in Christendom — the spiritual or the temporal power? The controversy reached its dramatic peak at Canossa and was formally resolved (but not truly settled) by the Concordat of Worms.

  • Common Law as Alfred's Legacy Extended: Henry II's justices in eyre created a 'common' law consistent across all England. This was, in a sense, the legal dimension of Alfred's project — creating an England governed by shared rules rather than local custom. This inheritance would become the foundation of the legal systems of much of the English-speaking world.

  • The Becket Affair as Warning about Words and Power: Henry II's ambiguous words — 'Will no one rid me of this turbulent priest?' — became an assassination command in his knights' minds. Whether or not Henry intended it, the consequences were catastrophic. The episode illustrates the danger of careless speech by those in power, a lesson the curriculum explicitly draws out.

  • The Magna Carta's Expanding Significance: The Magna Carta began as a baronial grievance document. But clauses 39 and 40 articulated a principle — that the king cannot act arbitrarily against persons, and that law stands above the will of any individual — that proved to have consequences far beyond its immediate context.

  • The Song of Roland and Chivalric Tension: Roland's refusal to blow the horn is both heroic and tragic. The poem celebrates a value system that prizes honor above survival, and asks whether such pride is virtue or flaw. This is the literature of an aristocracy that glorified war while also trying to make it holy.

Important Terms

  • Norman: From 'Northman.' Descendants of Viking settlers in Normandy, France. By 1066, Christianized and French-speaking but still militarily formidable.
  • housecarl: Professional warrior in the household of an Anglo-Saxon king or lord. The backbone of Harold's army at Hastings; the institution disappeared after the Conquest.
  • Domesday Book: The 1086 survey of virtually every estate in England, commissioned by William. Named 'Doomsday' because its decisions were final and without appeal.
  • feudalism: The system of land (fief) granted in exchange for military service (fealty). Creates a hierarchy of lords and vassals from king to knight.
  • vassal: A person who holds land from a lord and owes military service and loyalty in return.
  • fief: The land granted by a lord to a vassal in exchange for loyalty and military service.
  • Investiture Controversy: The dispute (c. 1076–1122) between popes and Holy Roman Emperors over the right to appoint bishops and abbots.
  • scholasticism: The medieval method of using philosophical reasoning (especially Aristotelian logic) to explore theology. Founded by Anselm; perfected by Aquinas.
  • Plantagenet: The royal dynasty founded by Henry II in 1154, ruling England until 1485.
  • common law: The body of English law built from judicial decisions, created by Henry II's system of royal judges on circuit.
  • Magna Carta: 'Great Charter,' agreed to by King John at Runnymede, June 15, 1215. Established the rule of law: even the king is subject to law.
  • chivalry: The code of medieval knighthood: loyalty, courage, protection of the weak, and Christian devotion.
  • Constitutions of Clarendon (1164): Henry II's assertion that clergy accused of crimes should be tried in royal, not church, courts.

Detailed Notes

The Battle of Hastings (October 14, 1066)

  • Background
    • Harold Godwinson: exhausted after defeating Harald Hardrada at Stamford Bridge days earlier
    • William landed at Pevensey September 28, 1066; Harold marched south immediately
    • Battle lasted most of the day — extraordinary endurance on both sides
  • The Battle
    • Harold's housecarls held the ridge; Norman cavalry repeatedly failed against shield wall
    • Turning point: Norman feigned retreat drew English off the ridge
    • Harold killed (arrow and/or knights — the Bayeux Tapestry is ambiguous)
    • Christmas Day 1066: William crowned at Westminster Abbey

Consolidating the Conquest

  • The Harrying of the North (1069–1070)
    • Danish fleet + English uprising in Yorkshire threatened William's rule
    • William's response: systematic destruction — crops burned, livestock slaughtered, seed grain destroyed
    • Orderic Vitalis: over 100,000 died of famine
    • Yorkshire Domesday entries: 'Wasta est' — 'It is waste' — for years afterward
  • The Domesday Book (1086)
    • Royal commissioners questioned local witnesses under oath
    • Recorded: who held land before 1066, who holds it now, how many people, what is it worth
    • Most detailed survey of a medieval kingdom's resources surviving anywhere in Europe
    • Reveals how thoroughly Normans replaced Anglo-Saxon aristocracy within 20 years

The Feudal System

  • Structure
    • King holds all land as tenant-in-chief; grants to barons for knight quotas
    • Barons grant to knights; knights hold from barons
    • Same man is lord to those below, vassal to those above
  • William's Application
    • All English land his personal property, conquered by right of war
    • Gave him firmer noble control than almost any other European ruler
  • The Investiture Controversy
    • Bishops = enormous land, wealth, political power
    • Kings wanted to appoint loyal bishops; popes insisted on Church control
    • Henry IV at Canossa (1077): waited barefoot in snow for three days
    • Concordat of Worms (1122): compromise — bishops receive spiritual authority from Church, temporal lands from king

The Plantagenet Reforms

  • Henry II (1154–1189)
    • Angevin Empire: England plus much of France (more than the French king controlled)
    • Common law: justices in eyre on regular circuits applying consistent principles
    • Jury: twelve sworn men determining facts and judging disputes
    • Becket conflict: Constitutions of Clarendon — clergy to be tried in royal courts
    • Murder of Becket at Canterbury (1170): Henry's disastrous political consequence
  • Richard I (1189–1199)
    • Spent most of reign on Third Crusade; brilliant warrior
    • Captured by Duke of Austria; ransomed for 150,000 silver marks
  • King John (1199–1216)
    • Lost Normandy to Philip II by 1204
    • Arbitrary taxation; church interdict on England for five years
    • Magna Carta rebellion: barons took London, forced John to Runnymede

The Magna Carta

  • The Document (June 15, 1215)
    • Clause 39: no free man imprisoned except by lawful judgment of peers and law of the land
    • Clause 40: to no one will we sell, deny, or delay right or justice
  • Immediate Context vs. Long-Term Legacy
    • John repudiated it immediately; Pope annulled it as extorted
    • Later kings repeatedly confirmed it; became cornerstone of English constitutional thought
    • Ancestor of habeas corpus, trial by jury, due process (US Constitution, Fifth Amendment)
    • American colonists cited Magna Carta in justifying their rebellion in the 1770s
