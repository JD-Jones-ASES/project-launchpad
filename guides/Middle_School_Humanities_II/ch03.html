Study Guide: The Crusades & the Age of Chivalry

Overview

Chapter 3 examines the Crusades from their origins in the Seljuk threat to Byzantium through the final fall of Acre in 1291, covering all major Crusades with particular depth on the First, Third, and Fourth. The chapter analyzes the complexity of Crusade motivations — religious, military, economic, and penitential — and examines both remarkable acts of magnanimity (Saladin in 1187, the Richard-Saladin relationship) and terrible moral failures (the Jerusalem massacre of 1099, the sack of Constantinople in 1204). The literature section studies three eyewitness sources that illustrate the historian's fundamental challenge: every witness interprets through their own lens.

Key Concepts

  • Jerusalem as the Center of the Medieval World: For medieval Christians, Jerusalem was not merely an important city — it was the holiest place on earth, the site of events that determined the fate of every human soul. This explains the extraordinary emotional power of Urban II's speech and the Crusades' mass appeal across all social classes.

  • The Complexity of Crusade Motivations: The Crusades were driven by a genuinely complex mix of sincere religious devotion, political ambition, economic interest, and knightly culture. Reducing them to any single cause — pure piety or pure cynicism — distorts the historical reality. Understanding this complexity is essential for honest evaluation.

  • The Military Orders as a Medieval Innovation: The warrior-monk was a new kind of person in medieval Christendom — someone who combined the total religious commitment of monasticism with the martial vocation of knighthood. The Templars and Hospitallers represent the institutional embodiment of the Crusading ideal.

  • Saladin as a Mirror: Saladin's behavior at Jerusalem in 1187 — forbidding a massacre, ransoming captives, preserving Christian access to holy sites — contrasts sharply with the Crusaders' behavior in 1099. This contrast was noted even by medieval Christians and forces a more honest evaluation of both sides.

  • The Fourth Crusade as Moral Case Study: The diversion of the Fourth Crusade to Constantinople illustrates how good intentions can be corrupted step by step — through debt, political intrigue, and gradually escalating compromise. It also illustrates how participants can remain blind to what they are actually doing while feeling they serve God.

  • Eyewitness Accounts and Historical Interpretation: Robert the Monk, Villehardouin, and Anna Komnene all witnessed Crusade events, yet each saw completely different things. This is not because any of them is lying — it is because all human perception is shaped by the perceiver's culture, loyalties, and assumptions. Historians must use all sources together, critically.

  • Indulgences: From Crusade to Reformation: Urban II's offer of a plenary indulgence to Crusaders established a tradition that grew, over the following centuries, into the commercialized system that Martin Luther attacked in 1517. The seed of the Reformation controversy was planted at Clermont.

  • The Crusades' Legacy: The Crusades failed militarily but had enormous cultural consequences — facilitating the transfer of Greek philosophy (via Arabic) back to Europe, stimulating trade that enriched Italian city-states, and leaving a legacy of bitterness between Christianity and Islam that has shaped their relationship ever since.

Important Terms

  • pilgrimage: A journey to a holy place as an act of religious devotion. Jerusalem, Rome, and Santiago de Compostela were the most important medieval destinations.
  • Seljuk Turks: Central Asian converts to Sunni Islam who built an empire across Persia and Anatolia, defeating Byzantium at Manzikert (1071) and triggering the Crusade movement.
  • Crusade: A holy war called by the Pope; participants wore a red cross and were promised remission of sins.
  • People's Crusade: The unofficial, disorganized mass movement led by Peter the Hermit, nearly destroyed in Anatolia before the organized First Crusade set out.
  • Crusader states: The four Christian principalities established after 1099 — Edessa, Antioch, Tripoli, and Jerusalem — constantly under threat and dependent on Europe.
  • Knights Templar: Warrior-monks (founded c. 1119) defending pilgrims and Crusader territories; later became wealthy bankers; dissolved by France in 1307.
  • Knights Hospitaller: Military order that began as a hospital order caring for pilgrims; later took up arms; built Krak des Chevaliers.
  • Saladin: Kurdish general and Sultan of Egypt and Syria (1137–1193) who unified Muslim resistance, defeated the Crusaders at Hattin, and recaptured Jerusalem with remarkable mercy.
  • Battle of Hattin (1187): Saladin's decisive ambush of the combined Crusader army — surrounded and cut off from water, nearly all were captured or killed.
  • Treaty of Jaffa (1192): End of the Third Crusade: Crusaders kept coastal strip; Jerusalem remained Muslim; Christian pilgrims could visit freely.
  • indulgence: A remission of temporal punishment for sins already forgiven; Urban II offered a plenary (full) indulgence to Crusaders who died in the campaign.

Detailed Notes

The Holy Land Before the Crusades

  • Jerusalem's Significance
    • Site of Christ's crucifixion, burial, and resurrection
    • Church of the Holy Sepulchre: built over the empty tomb
    • Centuries of Christian pilgrimage under Arab Muslim rule (since 638)
    • Early Arab rulers generally tolerated Christian pilgrims
  • The Seljuk Threat
    • Seljuks: Central Asian people converted to Sunni Islam
    • Battle of Manzikert (1071): Byzantine army shattered
    • Seljuks advanced to within 100 miles of Constantinople
    • Emperor Alexios I appealed to Pope Urban II for help

Urban II and the Call to Crusade

  • The Speech at Clermont (November 27, 1095)
    • Given in an open field — too many people to fit in a church
    • Described Eastern Christian suffering under Turkish rule
    • Called knights to direct their violence toward a holy purpose
    • Promised remission of sins to those who died
    • 'Deus lo volt!' — God wills it! — response of the crowd
  • Understanding Urban's Motivations
    • Religious: free holy places, help fellow Christians
    • Military: redirect knightly violence that terrorized the poor
    • Penitential: offer of indulgence as form of penance
    • Economic: addressed younger sons with no land to inherit
    • Political: strengthen papacy; respond to Byzantine appeal

The First Crusade (1096–1099)

  • The People's Crusade
    • Peter the Hermit led disorganized mass eastward before nobles organized
    • Reached Anatolia and was nearly destroyed by Seljuks
    • Showed: Crusades stirred the entire European population
  • The Organized First Crusade
    • Godfrey of Bouillon, Raymond of Toulouse, Bohemond of Taranto
    • Siege of Antioch (1097–1098): eight months; enormous suffering
    • Holy Lance discovery inspired desperate successful breakout
    • Jerusalem captured July 15, 1099: massacre of inhabitants
  • The Crusader States
    • Four principalities: County of Edessa, Principality of Antioch, County of Tripoli, Kingdom of Jerusalem
    • Constantly under pressure; dependent on European resupply
    • Required permanent military presence — hence the military orders

Saladin and the Third Crusade

  • Saladin's Rise
    • Kurdish general who unified Egypt and Syria by the 1170s
    • Battle of Hattin (1187): Crusader army surrounded, cut off from water, destroyed
    • Jerusalem recaptured; Saladin forbade massacre (contrast with 1099)
  • The Third Crusade (1189–1192)
    • Coalition: Frederick Barbarossa (drowned), Philip II (left early), Richard I
    • Richard won at Acre and Arsuf; marched to within 12 miles of Jerusalem twice
    • Knew he could not hold a besieged Jerusalem
    • Treaty of Jaffa: Crusaders keep coastal strip; pilgrims may visit Jerusalem

The Fourth Crusade (1202–1204)

  • The Plan
    • Pope Innocent III called it; Venetians contracted to transport army to Egypt
    • Crusaders could not pay Venice for ships
  • The Diversion
    • Venice persuaded Crusaders to attack Zara (Christian city) as payment
    • Pope excommunicated them — they went anyway
    • Byzantine prince promised rewards for helping him reclaim throne
    • Scheme went wrong; April 1204: Crusaders sacked Constantinople
  • Consequences
    • Greatest Christian city looted; Hagia Sophia despoiled
    • Orthodox-Catholic wound that has never fully healed
    • Byzantine Empire weakened; ultimately fell to Ottomans in 1453 partly because of this damage

The End of the Crusades

  • Later Crusades: Diminishing Returns
    • Fifth Crusade (1217–1221): attacked Egypt; failed
    • Seventh (1248–1254) and Eighth (1270): Louis IX of France; both failed
    • Louis IX died of disease on the Eighth Crusade
  • The Final Chapter
    • Acre fell to Mamluk Sultan al-Ashraf Khalil in 1291
    • Surviving Crusaders evacuated by ship
    • Two centuries of Crusader presence in the Holy Land ended

Connections

  • Urban II's offer of indulgences connects directly to Martin Luther's 95 Theses (Chapter 7) — the Crusade indulgence was the seed of the Reformation controversy.
  • The chivalric ideal explored through the Song of Roland (Chapter 2) and the Crusader military orders share the same culture and assumptions.
  • The massacre of Jerusalem (1099) and the scapegoating of Jews during the Black Death (Chapter 5) both illustrate how religious fervor can lead to terrible violence against civilians.
  • The sack of Constantinople (1204) weakened the Byzantine Empire enough that it fell to the Ottomans in 1453 (Chapter 5) — the event that sent Greek scholars fleeing to Italy and helped spark the Renaissance (Chapter 6).
  • The introduction of Aristotle's works to Western Europe through Arabic translations (a consequence of Crusade contact) is directly relevant to Thomas Aquinas's synthesis in Chapter 4.
