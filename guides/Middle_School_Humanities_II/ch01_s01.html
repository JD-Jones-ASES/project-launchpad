Geography & The Sea Roads: A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores how the unique geography of Scandinavia shaped the Viking people, pushing them to become masterful sailors and outward-bound explorers. It highlights the revolutionary technology of the Viking longship, which enabled unprecedented access to distant lands and the interiors of continents via "sea roads." Finally, it details the significant impact of Viking expansion on England, particularly the establishment of the Danelaw, and contrasts this with Alfred the Great's administrative reorganization of Anglo-Saxon territories.

Key Concepts

  • Scandinavian Geography and Outward Expansion: The rugged, resource-poor landscapes of Norway, Sweden, and Denmark (mountains, fjords, marshes, limited fertile soil) led to overpopulation relative to food supply. This environment naturally fostered a seafaring culture, making Vikings skilled sailors long before they became raiders and settlers.
  • The Longship as a Technological Revolution: A masterpiece of engineering, the Viking longship was crucial to their success. Its shallow draft, flexibility (rowed or sailed), symmetric design, and lightweight construction allowed it to navigate both open oceans and shallow rivers, beach directly on shores, and even be carried overland (portaged).
  • The Viking Sea Roads: These were the extensive routes of Viking expansion:
    • West: To the Faroe Islands, Iceland, Greenland, and ultimately Vinland (North America), marking the earliest confirmed European presence in the Americas.
    • East: Swedish Vikings (Varangians) followed Russian rivers, founding trading cities like Kiev and Novgorod, reaching the Black and Caspian Seas, and even serving the Byzantine Emperor in Constantinople as the Varangian Guard.
    • South and West: Danish Vikings raided and settled in England, Scotland, Ireland, France (establishing Normandy), and the Mediterranean.
  • The Danelaw: A specific geographical and cultural division of England established by the Treaty of Wedmore (878). It separated Alfred's Anglo-Saxon territories from areas under Danish law and settlement, with its influence still traceable today through place names.
  • Anglo-Saxon Administrative Geography: Alfred the Great's lasting achievement was strengthening the administrative divisions of his kingdom into shires (counties, each with an ealdorman) and further subdividing them into hundreds (sufficient land for 100 households), forming the basis of the English county system.

Important Terms

  • Shallow Draft: The minimal depth of water a ship requires to float without touching the bottom. Viking longships had an extremely shallow draft, enabling river navigation and beach landings.
  • Fjord: A long, narrow, deep inlet of the sea between high, steep cliffs, common in Norway, which fostered seafaring.
  • Portage: The act of carrying boats or goods overland between two navigable bodies of water. The lightweight longship could be portaged across narrow land bridges.
  • Vinland: The name given by the Norse to a part of North America, where Leif Eriksson established a short-lived settlement (L'Anse aux Meadows, Newfoundland).
  • Varangians: Scandinavian (primarily Swedish) traders and mercenaries who traveled along the river routes of Eastern Europe, establishing trade networks and settlements in modern Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus.
  • Varangian Guard: An elite corps of Norse (and later Anglo-Saxon) mercenaries who served as the personal bodyguards of the Byzantine Emperor in Constantinople.
  • Constantinople: The capital of the Byzantine Empire, a major destination for the Varangians via the Black Sea.
  • Normandy: A region in northern France settled by Norse (Northmen) Vikings, from which William the Conqueror later launched his invasion of England in 1066.
  • Danelaw: The historical region in England, primarily in the north and east, where Danish law, customs, and culture predominated following the Viking invasions and subsequent peace treaties, notably the Treaty of Wedmore (878).
  • Shire: An administrative division of Anglo-Saxon England, roughly equivalent to a county, administered by a royal officer (ealdorman/earl). Many modern English counties retain their shire names.
  • Hundred: A subdivision of an Anglo-Saxon shire, typically comprising enough land to support approximately one hundred households.

Detailed Notes

The World the Vikings Came From

To comprehend the Vikings, one must first grasp the geographical realities of their homeland, Scandinavia (modern Norway, Sweden, and Denmark).

  • Norway: Characterized by rugged mountains and deep, narrow fjords that cut into the coast, leaving very little flat, arable land.
  • Denmark: Largely low-lying and marshy, offering limited fertile ground.
  • Sweden: Dominated by vast forests and extensive coastlines, but with restricted areas of fertile soil suitable for agriculture.
  • Consequences of Geography: This challenging terrain meant that Scandinavia often faced a surplus population that its land could not adequately feed. Crucially, the natural layout of these lands—with numerous islands, archipelagos, and fjords—made travel by water far more practical than by land. This environment inherently cultivated a people already skilled in seafaring, meaning the Vikings were expert sailors long before they turned to raiding and expansion.

The Longship: A Technological Revolution

The cornerstone of Viking success and expansion was their innovative longship, an engineering marvel for its time.

  • Shallow Draft: Requiring less than a meter of water, the longship could navigate extremely shallow rivers and could be beached directly onto sandy shores or riverbanks without the need for a harbor.
  • Flexibility: Capable of being both rowed and sailed, it allowed Vikings to maintain speed and direction even against unfavorable winds.
  • Symmetric Design: Its identical bow and stern meant the ship did not need to be turned around in narrow waterways; it could simply be rowed in the opposite direction.
  • Lightweight Construction: The longship's relatively light build allowed it to be portaged—carried overland—across narrow strips of land between different river systems, effectively connecting previously disparate waterways.
  • Impact: This combination of oceanic capability and unparalleled river navigation granted the Vikings access to the interior of Europe, a feat unmatched by any previous sea power. Rivers in England, France, and Russia became critical "Viking highways."

The Sea Roads: Where the Vikings Went

The longship allowed the Vikings to explore and exploit vast regions, establishing distinct "sea roads."

  • Westward Expansion:
    • Around 800 CE, the Norse sailed to the Faroe Islands.
    • By 874 CE, they reached Iceland, establishing a permanent settlement.
    • Greenland was settled around 985 CE by Erik the Red.
    • Around 1000 CE, Leif Eriksson reached the coast of North America, which the Norse named Vinland. His settlement at L'Anse aux Meadows in modern Newfoundland is the first confirmed European presence in the Americas.
  • Eastward Expansion (The Varangians):
    • Swedish Vikings, known as Varangians by the Slavs, traveled south along the major rivers of modern Russia.
    • They founded important trading cities like Kiev and Novgorod.
    • Their trade routes extended from the Baltic Sea all the way to the Black Sea and the Caspian Sea, often requiring extensive portaging.
    • From the Black Sea, they could reach Constantinople, the opulent capital of the Byzantine Empire. Here, Norse mercenaries formed the renowned Varangian Guard, serving as the Emperor's elite personal bodyguards.
  • South and Westward Expansion (Danish Vikings):
    • Danish Vikings focused their attention on raiding the coasts of England, Scotland, Ireland, France, and eventually reaching the Mediterranean.
    • Significantly, Norse settlers established the Duchy of Normandy in France (named after the "Northmen"), from which William the Conqueror, a descendant of these Vikings, would launch his successful invasion of England in 1066.

The Danelaw as Geography

The impact of Viking settlement in England was so profound that it created a distinct geographical and cultural division.

  • Treaty of Wedmore (878): This treaty, signed between Alfred the Great and the Viking leader Guthrum, formally divided England. The Anglo-Saxon south and west remained under Alfred's rule, while the north and east became known as the Danelaw, a region under Danish law and custom.
  • Lasting Geographical and Cultural Traces: The Danelaw was more than just a political boundary; it left an indelible mark on the landscape and culture. This is evident in English place names:
    • Danelaw areas: Names ending in -by (e.g., Whitby, Derby), -thwaite (e.g., Slaithwaite), and -thorpe (e.g., Scunthorpe) are common.
    • Anglo-Saxon areas: Names ending in -ton (e.g., Southampton), -ham (e.g., Birmingham), and -bury (e.g., Canterbury) are prevalent.
    • This linguistic legacy allows for a literal mapping of the historical Danish and Anglo-Saxon zones.

Anglo-Saxon England: Shires and Hundreds

While Vikings were shaping new geographies, Alfred the Great was busy reorganizing his own kingdom.

  • Strengthening Administration: Alfred fortified the system of shires, which were administrative divisions roughly corresponding to modern counties. Each shire was managed by a royal officer called an ealdorman (later known as an earl), responsible for maintaining law and order and leading local military service.
  • Subdivisions: Below the shires were hundreds, smaller administrative units each containing enough land to support approximately a hundred households.
  • Lasting Legacy: This administrative geography proved to be one of Alfred's most enduring achievements. The English county system, in various forms, persisted from his reign well into the 20th century, demonstrating the profound and long-lasting impact of these reforms.

Then and Now: The Viking Sea Roads and Earlier Networks

The section draws a parallel between Viking expansion and earlier civilizations.

  • Shared "Logic of the Sea": Both the ancient Greeks (as discussed in Volume I, Unit 1) and the Vikings utilized mastery of water transport to connect distant places and establish networks of trade and power.
  • Key Difference - Initial Intent: While the Greeks often established planned colonies with defined citizens and laws, the Vikings initially focused on raiding parties.
  • Evolutionary Pattern: Over time, however, the Norse also transitioned from raiders to settlers in various regions like Ireland, England, France, Iceland, and Russia. This pattern—raid evolving into settlement, and settlement eventually laying foundations for civilization—is a recurring theme in Western history, mirroring, for example, the Germanic tribes settling in former Roman territories after 476 CE.

Review Questions

  1. How did the challenging geographical features of Scandinavia compel its people to become experienced sailors and explorers, even before the major Viking Age began?
  2. Identify and describe the four key technological innovations of the Viking longship. Explain how each feature enabled the Vikings to achieve unprecedented reach and success in both maritime and riverine environments.
  3. Trace the three main "Sea Roads" of Viking expansion (West, East, South/West). For each direction, name at least two significant places or achievements associated with Viking activity.
  4. Explain what the "Danelaw" was and how it came into existence. Provide examples of how its historical presence can still be observed in the geography and culture of England today.
  5. Based on the "Then and Now" section, compare the motivations and methods of Viking expansion with those of ancient Greek civilization. What fundamental difference in their initial approach is highlighted, and how did both eventually contribute to the development of new societies?
