The Late Medieval Crisis: A Study Guide

Overview

The 14th century marked a profound turning point in European history, transitioning from the height of medieval civilization to a period of intense upheaval. This era, known as the Late Medieval Crisis, was defined by three overlapping catastrophes: the Great Famine, the devastating Black Death, and the prolonged Hundred Years' War. These interconnected events fundamentally reshaped European society, its economy, religious life, and systems of warfare, leaving a lasting impact on the trajectory of Western civilization.

Key Concepts

  • The Late Medieval Crisis: A term historians use to describe the cluster of disasters that struck Europe between roughly 1315 and 1453. It encompasses the Great Famine, the Black Death, and the Hundred Years' War, which collectively challenged medieval institutions and beliefs.
  • The Great Famine (1315–1322): An early 14th-century crisis caused by widespread crop failures and starvation, particularly severe in northern Europe, which weakened populations even before the arrival of the plague.
  • The Black Death (1347–1353): The most devastating epidemic in recorded European history, caused by bubonic plague. It killed an estimated 30–50% of Europe's population, leading to immense social, economic, and religious disruption.
  • The Hundred Years' War (1337–1453): A series of intermittent conflicts between England and France, primarily over the English claim to the French throne. This long war devastated the French countryside and brought significant changes to medieval military tactics.
  • Serfdom: The condition of peasants legally bound to a lord's land, owing labor and payments in exchange for land use. The labor shortages caused by the Black Death dramatically weakened and largely ended serfdom in Western Europe.
  • Flagellants: Religious groups who emerged during the Black Death, publicly whipping themselves as acts of penance to appease God's wrath, believing the plague was divine punishment. The movement was eventually condemned by the Church.
  • Longbow: A powerful English and Welsh war bow, approximately six feet tall, capable of firing arrows with enough force to penetrate armor at long distances. Its effective use by English armies at battles like Crècy and Agincourt revolutionized medieval warfare, diminishing the dominance of heavy cavalry.
  • Joan of Arc (c. 1412–1431): A French peasant girl who claimed divine visions commanded her to lead French forces against the English during the Hundred Years' War. Her leadership at Orléans and role in Charles VII's coronation were pivotal moments, though she was later executed for heresy.

Important Terms

  • Black Death: The name given to the epidemic of bubonic plague (caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis) that swept through Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East between 1347 and 1353. It killed an estimated 30–50% of Europe's population—the worst demographic catastrophe in recorded Western history.
  • Serfdom: The condition of peasants who were legally bound to a lord's land and could not leave without permission. Serfs owed labor service and payments to their lord in exchange for the use of land. Serfdom was the economic foundation of the feudal system in Western Europe; it largely collapsed in England and France after the Black Death due to labor shortages.
  • Flagellants: Religious groups who publicly whipped themselves (flagellare = to whip) as an act of penance during the Black Death. The flagellant movement swept through Germany and the Low Countries in 1349. They believed that extreme self-punishment would appease God's wrath and end the plague. Pope Clement VI condemned the movement in 1349.
  • Hundred Years' War: The series of conflicts between England and France from 1337 to 1453, fought over the English claim to the French throne. Despite several major English victories (Crècy, Poitiers, Agincourt), France ultimately expelled the English from all their French territories except Calais by 1453.
  • Longbow: The English and Welsh war bow, typically about six feet tall and made from yew wood. Trained archers could fire ten to twelve arrows per minute with enough power to penetrate armor at distances up to 300 yards. The longbow gave English armies a decisive advantage over French heavy cavalry at Crècy (1346), Poitiers (1356), and Agincourt (1415).
  • Joan of Arc: (c. 1412–1431) A French peasant girl who claimed to have received visions from Saints Michael, Catherine, and Margaret commanding her to drive the English from France. She was given command of French troops, lifted the siege of Orléans (1429), and led Charles VII to his coronation at Reims. Captured by Burgundian allies of England in 1430, she was tried for heresy and burned at Rouen in 1431. She was canonized as a saint by Pope Benedict XV in 1920.

Detailed Notes

A World on the Edge: The 14th Century's Sudden Collapse

  • Pre-Crisis Europe: The early 14th century saw medieval civilization at its peak, characterized by soaring cathedrals, thriving universities (Oxford, Paris, Bologna), extensive trade routes (England to China), and intellectual achievements like Thomas Aquinas's synthesis of faith and reason.
  • Rapid Decline: Within a single generation, this flourishing world was shattered by a series of catastrophic events.

The Late Medieval Crisis: Three Overlapping Shocks (1315-1453)

This term encapsulates the interconnected disasters that fundamentally altered European society:

  1. The Great Famine (1315–1322): Caused by widespread crop failures, particularly in northern Europe, leading to starvation and significantly weakening the population's resilience.
  2. The Black Death (1347–1353): The most devastating epidemic in recorded European history.
  3. The Hundred Years' War (1337–1453): A prolonged conflict between England and France that ravaged vast areas of the French countryside.
  • Overall Impact: These crises collectively shook the foundations of the Church, the feudal system, the economy, and the intellectual confidence that characterized medieval civilization.

The Black Death: Origin, Spread, and Forms

  • Origin and Path:
    • Originated in Central Asia, likely near the Caspian Sea, where the Yersinia pestis bacterium resided in fleas infesting rodents.
    • Spread westward along the Silk Road by Mongol armies and merchant caravans.
    • Reached Black Sea ports by the late 1340s.
    • In 1347, Genoese trading ships brought the disease from Crimea to Sicily.
    • Rapidly followed established European trade routes, reaching northern France and England by 1348, Scandinavia by 1349, and Eastern Europe by 1350.
  • Forms of the Disease:
    • Bubonic Plague: Most common form, transmitted by flea bites. Caused swollen, blackened lymph nodes (buboes) in the armpit and groin. Killed 60–70% of those infected.
    • Pneumonic Plague: An airborne form that attacked the lungs, spread by coughing and breathing. Almost always fatal.
    • Septicemic Plague: The rarest and most lethal, infecting the blood directly, leading to death within hours.
  • Medieval Understanding and Treatments:
    • Lack of germ theory led to theories of poisoned air (miasma), bad planetary alignment, or God's judgment.
    • Treatments like bleeding, herbal remedies, and burning aromatic herbs were ineffective.
    • Misunderstanding the link between rats and disease, some communities harmed cats (believing them linked to "witchcraft"), potentially exacerbating the spread.

The Human Cost

  • Demographic Catastrophe: Europe's population plummeted from approximately 75 million to 45–50 million by 1400.
  • Mortality Rates: In regions like England, France, and parts of Italy, death rates reached as high as 50%. Entire villages were abandoned.
  • Universal Impact: No social class was immune. Peasants died in close quarters; the wealthy died in their manors. Clergy suffered heavy losses due to their care for the sick and communal living in convents and monasteries. Pope Clement VI even consecrated the Rhône River for mass burials in Avignon due to overwhelming death tolls.
  • Eyewitness Accounts: Contemporary writers like Giovanni Boccaccio (in his Decameron) vividly described the sudden onset of symptoms (buboes) and the powerlessness of medicine.

The Social Aftermath: A World Turned Upside Down

  • Labor and Economics:
    • Massive population decline created severe labor shortages.
    • Surviving peasants gained unprecedented economic power, demanding higher wages and freedom from manorial obligations.
    • Landlords initially resisted with wage-fixing laws but ultimately could not prevent the shift.
    • End of Serfdom: In Western Europe, serfdom largely collapsed within a generation as landlords desperately needed free labor and could no longer enforce old rules.
  • Religion and the Church:
    • Faith was severely shaken, not necessarily in God, but in the Church's ability to mediate between God and humanity.
    • The ineffectiveness of prayers and sacraments, coupled with the high death toll among clergy, led to widespread questioning.
    • Responses ranged from extreme piety (like the Flagellants, condemned by Pope Clement VI for their excesses) to profound cynicism and indulgence.
    • Literary works like Boccaccio's Decameron and Chaucer's Canterbury Tales reflect the period's gallows humor and social critique.
  • Scapegoating:
    • In their terror and ignorance, some communities falsely accused Jewish populations of poisoning wells to cause the plague.
    • These baseless accusations led to horrific massacres across France, Germany, and Switzerland, despite Pope Clement VI's condemnation, who pointed out that Jewish communities suffered from the plague at the same rate as Christians. This illustrates a dark pattern of blaming vulnerable groups during crises.

The Hundred Years' War (1337–1453)

  • Causes: Began as a dynastic dispute when England's King Edward III claimed the French throne through his mother.
  • Impact: A century of intermittent warfare devastated the French countryside and dramatically transformed military practices.
  • Crècy (1346): A pivotal battle where a smaller English army decisively defeated a much larger French cavalry force.
    • The English utilized the longbow effectively, firing arrows at a rapid rate that decimated French armored knights before they could reach the English lines.
    • This battle marked the beginning of the end for the armored knight as the decisive unit in battle.
  • Agincourt (1415): Another major English victory under Henry V.
    • Outnumbered English longbowmen again overwhelmed French knights, who charged across a muddy field, suffering catastrophic losses.
    • This victory allowed Henry V to capture much of France, including Paris, and secure a marriage to the French king's daughter.

Joan of Arc (c. 1412–1431)

  • Divine Mission: A teenage peasant girl from Domrémy, France, who claimed to receive visions from Saints Michael, Catherine, and Margaret instructing her to expel the English from France.
  • Turning Point for France:
    • Convinced the Dauphin (later Charles VII) of her divine mission.
    • Given command of French troops, she famously lifted the Siege of Orléans in 1429.
    • Led Charles VII to his coronation at Reims, boosting French morale and legitimacy.
  • Capture and Execution:
    • Captured by Burgundian allies of England in 1430.
    • Tried by an English-backed ecclesiastical court for heresy and witchcraft.
    • Burned at the stake in Rouen in 1431.
    • Later exonerated and canonized as a saint by Pope Benedict XV in 1920.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the initial state of European civilization at the beginning of the 14th century. What specific examples illustrate its apparent "height," and how quickly did this change?
  2. Identify and explain the three major, overlapping catastrophes that constitute the "Late Medieval Crisis." How did these events collectively challenge the established institutions of medieval Europe?
  3. Discuss the origins and spread of the Black Death across Europe. What were the different forms of the disease, and why were medieval attempts to understand and treat it largely unsuccessful?
  4. Analyze the significant social and economic consequences of the Black Death. How did the plague lead to the decline of serfdom in Western Europe, and what other societal shifts (e.g., religious, psychological) emerged in its aftermath?
  5. Explain how the Hundred Years' War transformed medieval warfare, focusing on the role of new weaponry and tactics. What was Joan of Arc's significance in the latter stages of the war, and how did her actions impact the conflict?
