Literature of the Renaissance: A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the influential literature of the Renaissance, focusing on four key writers: Niccolò Machiavelli, Erasmus, Thomas More, and Petrarch. These authors engaged deeply with ancient texts, explored human nature and politics, and dared to challenge conventional wisdom, often through groundbreaking literary forms like political treatises, satire, and philosophical dialogues about ideal societies.

Key Concepts

  • Renaissance Humanism: An intellectual movement that emphasized human potential and achievements, focusing on classical studies (Greek and Roman texts) and critical thinking. Petrarch is considered the "father" of this movement.
  • Realpolitik: A practical approach to politics, exemplified by Machiavelli, that prioritizes power, stability, and effectiveness over moral or ethical considerations. It suggests that a ruler must be willing to act unethically if it serves the state's interests.
  • Satire: A literary genre that uses humor, irony, exaggeration, or ridicule to expose and criticize people's stupidity or vices, particularly in the context of contemporary politics and other topical issues. Erasmus's The Praise of Folly is a prime example.
  • Irony/Satirical Persona: A literary device where the intended meaning is different from the literal meaning. A satirical persona (like Erasmus's "Folly") is a fictional character used by an author to voice criticisms or observations while providing a layer of detachment.
  • Ideal Societies and Social Critique: The practice of imagining perfect or utopian societies to highlight and critique the flaws of existing societies. Thomas More's Utopia is the defining work in this tradition.
  • Separation of Politics and Morality: Machiavelli's radical idea that a ruler's actions in the pursuit of power and stability should not always be judged by traditional moral or religious standards.

Important Terms

  • Humanism: A philosophical and ethical stance that emphasizes the value and agency of human beings, preferring critical thinking and evidence over dogma or superstition. Renaissance humanists revived classical learning.
  • Prince: In Machiavelli's context, the ruler or leader of a state, particularly a principality.
  • Utopia: An imagined community or society that possesses highly desirable or nearly perfect qualities for its citizens. The word, coined by Thomas More, means "no place" or "good place" in Greek.
  • Satire: A literary technique used to expose and criticize foolishness and corruption of an individual or a society by using humor, irony, exaggeration, or ridicule.
  • Irony: A literary device in which a statement, situation, or event seems to mean one thing but actually means the opposite.
  • Satirical Persona: A fictional character or voice adopted by an author to deliver satirical commentary, often to provide a layer of distance between the author and potentially controversial criticisms.
  • Sonnets: A poem of fourteen lines using any of a number of formal rhyme schemes, typically having ten syllables per line. Petrarch popularized the sonnet form.
  • Canzoniere: Petrarch's collection of 366 poems, mostly sonnets, written in Italian, primarily addressed to a woman named Laura.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Writing in a New Key

The Renaissance was a period of profound literary innovation, producing works that continue to influence Western thought. Writers of this era shared several key characteristics:

  • Engagement with Antiquity: They meticulously studied the works of ancient Greek and Roman authors, drawing inspiration and knowledge.
  • Focus on Human Nature and Politics: Their writings often explored the complexities of human behavior, morality, and the practicalities of political leadership and societal structure.
  • Challenge to Received Wisdom: They were willing to question and critique the established ideas and institutions of their time, sometimes subtly and sometimes with direct force. This section highlights four pivotal figures: Machiavelli, Erasmus, Thomas More, and Petrarch.

Machiavelli: The Prince (1513)

Niccolò Machiavelli wrote The Prince in 1513, seeking to regain favor with the Medici family after losing his government position in Florence. This short but impactful book offers an unflinching analysis of how rulers acquire and maintain power.

  • Focus on Reality over Ideals: Machiavelli departs from earlier political thinkers by emphasizing "the real truth of a matter than the imagination of it." He argues that focusing on how things ought to be, rather than how they are, leads to ruin for a prince. He critiques idealized "republics and principalities which in fact have never been known or seen" (like Plato's Republic or More's Utopia), suggesting they are impractical for real-world governance. This contrasts sharply with medieval thinkers like Thomas Aquinas, who believed rulers should follow a moral framework based on natural law.
  • Feared vs. Loved: Machiavelli famously poses the question of whether it is "better to be loved than feared or feared than loved?" He concludes that while a prince should ideally be both, it is "much safer to be feared than loved." This is because men are inherently "ungrateful, fickle, false, cowardly, covetous," and their loyalty is unreliable when danger approaches. Love is based on obligation, which can be broken, while fear is maintained by punishment, which is constant.
  • The Fox and the Lion: A prince must adopt the qualities of both a "fox" and a "lion." The lion represents strength and the ability to "terrify the wolves," while the fox represents cunning and the ability "to discover the snares." Relying solely on brute force (the lion) is insufficient, as a prince must also be shrewd enough to avoid traps and deception.
  • Appearance vs. Reality of Virtue: Machiavelli argues that a prince does not need to possess all traditionally "good qualities" but it is "very necessary to appear to have them." Furthermore, he shockingly suggests that "to have them and always to observe them is injurious, and that to appear to have them is useful." This means a prince must be willing to act against virtue (e.g., be cruel, dishonest) when necessary to secure and maintain power, while still presenting a virtuous public image.

Machiavelli's Break with Medieval Tradition: Medieval political thought consistently linked good kingship with Christian virtue and morality. Machiavelli shattered this tradition by asserting that a prince must be willing to separate politics from personal ethics, prioritizing the needs of the state and the preservation of power above all else. This controversial stance has been debated ever since.

Erasmus: The Praise of Folly (1509)

Desiderius Erasmus, a Dutch humanist, wrote The Praise of Folly as a playful gift for his friend Thomas More. Despite its humorous origins, the book became a highly influential satire, using the fictional character of "Folly" to critique various aspects of society and the Church.

  • Satirical Persona: Erasmus employs the technique of irony by having Folly herself speak, praising her own importance to human life. This allows Erasmus to deliver sharp criticisms through Folly's voice, giving him "cover" for potentially dangerous statements about powerful institutions like the Church. This is similar to Chaucer's use of different narrators in The Canterbury Tales.
  • Critique of Scholars: Folly first skewers grammarians, portraying them as miserable, overworked individuals trapped in noisy "dungeons" (schools), yet believing themselves to be "most excellent of all men" thanks to Folly's influence.
  • Critique of Theologians: She mocks theologians for their arrogance and their elaborate, overly specific pronouncements on abstract religious matters, such as "the dimensions of purgatory, the height, breadth, and length of hell, the exact shape and fashion of the Devil." Folly suggests they speak with confidence "as if they had spent many years in the very bowels of those regions."
  • Critique of Church Leadership: Most daringly, Folly turns her attention to monks, popes, cardinals, and bishops. She suggests that without her (Folly's) influence, their lives would be "dismal and troublesome." She implies that those who "hunt after preferments with such eagerness" would shy away if they truly understood the heavy burden of their offices. This implicitly criticizes the ambition and worldliness of many churchmen of the time.

Thomas More: Utopia (1516)

Thomas More, an English humanist and royal counselor, published Utopia in Latin for a scholarly European audience. The book is structured as a dialogue in which a traveler, Raphael Hythloday (whose name means "purveyor of nonsense" in Greek), describes an ideal island society called Utopia.

  • The Utopian Ideal: In Utopia, there is no private property; "every man has a right to everything." This communal ownership eliminates poverty and anxiety, as citizens are secure in the knowledge that "no private man can want anything." Everyone lives a "serene and cheerful life," free from worries about their own needs or those of their families for generations. This system stands in stark contrast to the economic realities of More's time.
  • Critique of Existing Governments: Hythloday delivers a scathing indictment of real-world governments, labeling them a "conspiracy of the rich." He argues that governments, under the pretense of public service, "only pursue their private ends." Laws are not for justice but are "only a pretense of justice" designed to "preserve all that they have so ill acquired" and "engage the poor to toil and labour for them at as low rates as possible, and oppress them as much as they please."
  • Purpose of Utopia: More, despite being a royal counselor, used Utopia ("no place") as a powerful mirror to reflect and critique the injustices and flaws of European society, particularly English society. The debate continues among scholars whether More truly advocated for the Utopian system of common property or merely used it as a thought experiment to expose societal problems.
  • Comparison to Plato's Republic: Like Plato's Republic (which also imagined a perfectly just society), More's Utopia explores a society without private property and social competition, albeit in different ways. Plato's ideal society featured philosopher-kings, while More's Utopians are governed by elected officials.

Petrarch: The Humanist Ideal

Francesco Petrarch (1304–1374), an Italian poet and scholar, is widely regarded as the "father of Renaissance humanism."

  • Poetry: His Canzoniere, a collection of 366 sonnets written in Italian and mostly dedicated to a woman named Laura, became the foundational model for love poetry across Europe for two centuries.
  • Humanist Vision: Petrarch's humanist ideals are most evident in his letters and Latin writings. He passionately advocated for the study of classical texts, believing that understanding ancient Greek and Roman thought was crucial for human intellectual and moral development. He saw history as a "praise of Rome" and found immense delight and instruction in reading classical authors like Virgil, Cicero, and Livy. His work marked a significant shift towards valuing human achievements and classical learning as a path to virtue and wisdom.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast Machiavelli's view on princely virtue with the traditional Roman virtue exemplified by figures like Cincinnatus (from Volume I, Unit 3). How does Machiavelli's approach differ from earlier ideas about ideal leadership?
  2. How did Renaissance humanism, as championed by Petrarch, connect with the philosophy of ancient Greece (from Volume I, Unit 2)? What aspects of classical learning did humanists particularly value, and why?
  3. Discuss the role of irony and satirical persona in Erasmus's The Praise of Folly. How does Folly's voice allow Erasmus to criticize religious and scholarly figures without directly facing the same dangers he might have otherwise?
  4. Explain the core ideas of Thomas More's Utopia. In what ways does the Utopian system of common property serve as a critique of European societies of his time, particularly regarding wealth inequality and the nature of law?
  5. Machiavelli famously stated it is "safer to be feared than loved." Do you agree with this statement in the context of leadership, either historically or in modern times? Justify your answer using examples from the text or your own reasoning.
