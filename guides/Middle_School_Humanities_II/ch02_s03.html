Study Guide: The Norman Conquest & The Feudal Order

Overview

This section provides a historical summary of the pivotal Norman Conquest of England in 1066, detailing the events leading up to, during, and immediately after William the Conqueror's victory at the Battle of Hastings. It explores how the new Norman ruling class transformed Anglo-Saxon England by introducing continental feudalism, consolidating royal power through brutal means like the Harrying of the North, and implementing administrative innovations such as the Domesday Book. The section also touches upon broader European conflicts like the Investiture Controversy and introduces key intellectual figures like Anselm of Canterbury.

Key Concepts

  • The Norman Conquest (1066): The invasion and subsequent take-over of England by William, Duke of Normandy, after defeating Harold Godwinson at the Battle of Hastings. This event fundamentally altered England's language, legal system, social structure, and ruling class.
  • Feudalism: The socio-political and economic system established by the Normans, based on the exchange of land (fiefs) for loyalty and military service between lords and vassals. William used this to centralize power and create a strong, royal-dependent army.
  • The Battle of Hastings: The decisive battle on October 14, 1066, where William's Norman forces defeated Harold Godwinson's Anglo-Saxon army, leading to Harold's death and paving the way for William's coronation as King of England.
  • The Bayeux Tapestry: An extraordinary 70-meter-long embroidery (not a true tapestry) created in the 1070s that visually depicts the events of the Norman Conquest from a Norman perspective, serving as a vital historical source for the period.
  • The Harrying of the North (1069-1070): William the Conqueror's brutal campaign of deliberate destruction and depopulation in Yorkshire and surrounding counties to suppress English rebellions, leading to widespread famine and ensuring Norman control.
  • The Domesday Book (1086): A comprehensive survey commissioned by William the Conqueror to record the ownership, value, and resources of virtually every estate in England, providing invaluable information for taxation, military planning, and historical insight.
  • The Investiture Controversy: A significant conflict in 11th and 12th-century Europe between popes and kings (like Pope Gregory VII and Emperor Henry IV) over the right to appoint bishops and abbots, highlighting the struggle between religious and political authority.
  • Scholasticism: An influential medieval intellectual method, pioneered by figures like Anselm of Canterbury, which used philosophical reasoning, particularly Aristotelian logic, to explore and defend theological questions, asserting that faith and reason are complementary.

Important Terms

  • Norman: Descendants of Viking settlers who received the Duchy of Normandy in northern France in 911. By 1066, they were Christianized and French-speaking but retained a strong martial spirit, leading to the Conquest of England.
  • Housecarl: A professional, full-time warrior in the personal household of an Anglo-Saxon king or great lord. They were well-armed and paid, forming the elite backbone of Harold's army at Hastings but disappeared after the Conquest.
  • Bayeux Tapestry: A 70-meter-long embroidery, probably made in England in the 1070s, which depicts the events leading up to and including the Norman Conquest from William's perspective, offering a unique visual record of 11th-century life and warfare.
  • Domesday Book: The great survey of England commissioned by William the Conqueror in 1086, recording land ownership, value, and population for administrative and taxation purposes. Its name reflects its final, unappealable judgments, like the Last Judgment.
  • Feudalism: The dominant system of political, social, and military organization in medieval Europe, based on a hierarchical exchange where a lord granted land (a fief) to a follower (a vassal) in return for an oath of loyalty (fealty) and military service.
  • Investiture Controversy: A prolonged dispute (c. 1076–1122) between the Papacy and secular rulers, primarily the Holy Roman Emperors, concerning who had the authority to appoint bishops and other church officials. It raised fundamental questions about the balance of spiritual and temporal power.
  • Scholasticism: An intellectual and educational tradition of the Middle Ages that emphasized the use of rational thought and philosophical inquiry (especially Aristotelian logic) to understand and systematize Christian theology and knowledge. Anselm of Canterbury is considered a founder.
  • Plantagenet: The royal dynasty founded by Henry II in 1154, which ruled England until 1485. The name derives from the planta genista (broom plant), a heraldic emblem of the family.

Detailed Notes

England Before the Conquest

Before 1066, Anglo-Saxon England was a relatively sophisticated kingdom with a rich heritage. It had been shaped by the legacy of Alfred the Great, featuring established administrative divisions (shires and courts), a literate clergy, a tradition of royal law, and a vibrant literary language. This strong Anglo-Saxon identity would soon be dramatically challenged and overlaid by a new ruling class.

William the Conqueror and the Battle of Hastings

  • William's Claim: William, Duke of Normandy, asserted his right to the English throne, claiming that King Edward the Confessor had promised it to him and that Harold Godwinson had sworn to support this claim on holy relics.
  • Invasion Preparation: When Harold accepted the crown, William secured the blessing of Pope Alexander II and assembled a formidable army from across northern France.
  • Landing and Battle: William landed at Pevensey, Sussex, on September 28, 1066. Harold's forces, exhausted from a rapid march south after defeating Vikings at Stamford Bridge, met William's army near Hastings on October 14, 1066.
  • The Conflict: The battle was an arduous, day-long struggle. Harold's elite housecarls, armed with battle-axes, maintained a strong shield wall on a ridge. Norman cavalry struggled to break their lines.
  • Turning Point and Harold's Death: The Normans eventually broke the English resistance by feigning retreats, which lured some English soldiers off the ridge, breaking their disciplined formation. Harold's death, whether by arrow or sword, led to the collapse of English resistance.
  • Coronation: On Christmas Day, 1066, William was crowned King of England in Westminster Abbey, symbolizing the formal beginning of Norman rule.

Consolidating Power: The Harrying of the North

  • Continued Resistance: William's coronation did not immediately end resistance. English nobles frequently rebelled, sometimes supported by Danish raiders.
  • The 1069-1070 Uprising: The most significant challenge came from a combined English and Danish uprising in Yorkshire and the North.
  • William's Brutal Response: William responded with the "Harrying of the North," a systematic campaign of destruction. He devastated villages, farms, and food supplies, burning houses, slaughtering livestock, and destroying seed grain.
  • Devastating Impact: This act caused a terrible famine, killing an estimated 100,000 people and ensuring that no further major rebellions emerged from the north. The Domesday Book later recorded vast swathes of the region as "Wasta est" ("It is waste").
  • Historical Evaluation: The Harrying presents a complex ethical challenge for historians, prompting discussions on how to evaluate figures whose significant achievements are intertwined with extreme cruelty.

The Domesday Book

  • Purpose and Commission: In 1085–1086, William ordered an unprecedented survey of all landholdings in England. Its primary purpose was to provide the king with accurate information for taxation and military planning.
  • Methodology: Royal commissioners traveled across the country, questioning local witnesses under oath about land ownership (before and after 1066), population, and value.
  • Name and Significance: Originally called the "Winchester Book," it became known as the "Domesday Book" because its judgments on land tenure and value were final and unappealable, like the Last Judgment (dom in Old English meaning judgment). It remains the most detailed record of a medieval kingdom's resources.
  • Historical Insight: Beyond its administrative function, the Domesday Book offers an unparalleled window into 11th-century English society, illustrating the dramatic shift in land ownership from English to Norman aristocracy within two decades of the Conquest.

Feudalism: The New Order

  • Introduction to England: The Normans brought with them a sophisticated system of land tenure and military organization known as feudalism, which was widespread on the European continent.
  • Core Exchange: At its heart, feudalism was a reciprocal arrangement: a lord granted a fief (land) to a vassal (follower), who in return swore an oath of fealty (loyalty) and promised military service. This created a complex web of interlocking obligations.
  • William's Implementation: William applied this system rigorously to England, declaring all land his property by right of conquest. He then granted portions of this land to his Norman followers, typically obligating them to provide a specific number of armed knights when called upon.
  • Centralized Control: This centralized application of feudalism gave William exceptionally firm control over his nobility and military resources, making him one of the most powerful rulers in Europe.

Church and State: The Investiture Controversy

  • The Conflict: A major struggle in 11th and 12th-century Europe concerned who held the right to appoint bishops and abbots—the Pope or secular rulers. This was crucial because bishops controlled vast land and wealth, making them powerful allies in both church and state.
  • Key Figures: The conflict reached a dramatic peak with Pope Gregory VII's excommunication of German Emperor Henry IV in 1076, leading to Henry's famous penitent vigil at Canossa. The underlying issues were largely resolved by the Concordat of Worms in 1122.
  • Anselm of Canterbury: In England, the controversy involved figures like Anselm of Canterbury, Archbishop from 1093. Anselm, a towering intellectual figure, championed the Church's rights. He is renowned for his theological motto, "fides quaerens intellectum" (faith seeking understanding), advocating for the harmonious relationship between faith and reason.
  • Father of Scholasticism: Anselm is often considered the founder of scholasticism, an intellectual method that used philosophical logic, particularly Aristotelian thought, to explore and defend theological questions, paving the way for later great thinkers like Thomas Aquinas.

The Rise of Common Law (Henry II)

  • "The Anarchy": England experienced a period of civil war known as "the Anarchy" (1135–1154) following William the Conqueror's reign, as King Stephen and Empress Matilda contended for the throne.
  • Henry II and the Plantagenets: The anarchy concluded with the ascension of Henry II in 1154, who founded the powerful Plantagenet dynasty. Henry's "Angevin Empire" was vast, stretching from Scotland to the Pyrenees, giving him more territory in France than the French king himself.

Review Questions

  1. How did Anglo-Saxon England, as described before 1066, differ from the kingdom that emerged within a generation of the Norman Conquest? What were the key elements of continuity and change?
  2. Beyond simply winning the Battle of Hastings, what strategies and actions did William the Conqueror employ to consolidate his rule and prevent further rebellions in England? Discuss the role of the Harrying of the North and the Domesday Book.
  3. Explain the system of feudalism introduced by the Normans. How did William apply it to England, and what impact did this have on royal power compared to other European rulers?
  4. What was the Investiture Controversy, and why was the appointment of bishops such a significant point of contention between popes and kings? How does Anselm of Canterbury's intellectual approach relate to this broader conflict?
  5. The text introduces the Bayeux Tapestry as a crucial historical source. Discuss its value as a primary source for understanding the Norman Conquest, while also considering any potential biases or limitations it might have.
