Literature of the High Middle Ages: Dante, Aquinas, Marco Polo, and Francis of Assisi

Overview

This section explores the rich and diverse literature of the High Middle Ages, focusing on four pivotal figures from the 13th and early 14th centuries: Dante Alighieri, Thomas Aquinas, Marco Polo, and Francis of Assisi. These authors demonstrate the era's intellectual and spiritual ambition, employing distinct approaches—from epic poetry and systematic theology to travel narratives and hymns—to interpret the world and humanity's place within it. Their works collectively showcase the extraordinary range of thought and expression during this period.

Key Concepts

  • Literary Ambition: The High Middle Ages produced literature, like Dante's Divine Comedy and Aquinas's Summa Theologica, that was as grand in scope and intellectual ambition as the period's magnificent cathedrals, attempting to map the spiritual universe or answer fundamental questions about existence.
  • Diversity of Approaches: Writers of this era utilized a wide spectrum of styles and purposes, including philosophical argument (Aquinas), poetic imagery (Dante), careful observation (Marco Polo), and simple devotional language (Francis of Assisi).
  • Vernacular Literature: Dante's revolutionary decision to write The Divine Comedy in the Tuscan dialect of Italian, rather than Latin, elevated everyday language for serious literature and laid the groundwork for modern standard Italian.
  • Allegory and Symbolism: Dante's Divine Comedy uses an allegorical journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Heaven to explore moral, spiritual, and theological truths, with characters and settings representing deeper meanings.
  • Reason and Faith (Scholasticism): Thomas Aquinas's work exemplifies scholasticism, demonstrating that human reason, through careful logical argument, can arrive at conclusions consistent with Christian faith, and that there is no fundamental contradiction between natural philosophy (science) and theology.
  • Justice and Consequences of Sin: Dante's Hell is structured on a precise logical principle where punishments perfectly correspond to the nature of the sins committed, reflecting the belief that sin is a disorder of the soul with logical, eternal consequences.
  • The Nature of Evil: Dante portrays Satan not as a magnificent rebel, but as a grotesque, impotent, and weeping figure, symbolizing that evil is ultimately the absence of good and leads to nothingness and helplessness rather than power.
  • Expanding Worldview: Marco Polo's Travels provided medieval Europeans with unprecedented, detailed accounts of the vast and diverse world to the east, significantly broadening their geographical and cultural understanding.

Important Terms

  • Dante Alighieri: Florentine poet (1265-1321), author of The Divine Comedy.
  • The Divine Comedy: Dante's epic poem describing a journey through Hell (Inferno), Purgatory (Purgatorio), and Heaven (Paradiso).
  • Tuscan Dialect: The everyday language of central Italy that Dante chose for The Divine Comedy, which became the basis for standard Italian.
  • Virgil: The ancient Roman poet who guides Dante through Hell and Purgatory in The Divine Comedy, representing classical reason.
  • Limbo: A region of Hell in Dante's Inferno where virtuous pagans, like Virgil and Homer, who lived before Christ and could not be saved, reside without torment but also without hope of Heaven.
  • Thomas Aquinas: Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian (1225-1274), author of Summa Theologica.
  • Summa Theologica: Aquinas's comprehensive theological treatise that systematically addresses major philosophical and theological questions using logical argumentation.
  • The Five Ways (quinque viae): Aquinas's five logical arguments for the existence of God, derived from observable facts about the natural world (motion, causation, contingency, etc.).
  • Unmoved Mover: A concept from Aristotle, adopted by Aquinas in the First Way, positing a first cause of motion that is itself unmoved, which Aquinas identifies with God.
  • Marco Polo: Venetian merchant and traveler (1254-1324), author of The Travels of Marco Polo.
  • The Travels of Marco Polo: A detailed account of Polo's journey and experiences in the Mongol Empire and across Asia, dictated in 1298.
  • Kublai Khan: The Emperor of the Mongol Empire and founder of the Yuan dynasty in China, whom Marco Polo served for many years.
  • Khanbaliq: The capital city of Kublai Khan's empire (modern-day Beijing), vividly described by Marco Polo.
  • Francis of Assisi: Italian Catholic friar and mystic (1181/1182-1226), known for his love of nature and God's creation, and his "hymn to creation."

Detailed Notes

Introduction: The Literature of a World Reaching Upward

  • The literature of the High Middle Ages (13th and early 14th centuries) mirrored the ambition of its cathedrals.
  • Key figures: Dante Alighieri, Thomas Aquinas, Marco Polo, and Francis of Assisi.
  • These authors, while contemporaries, approached their subjects with vastly different styles:
    • Aquinas: Precise philosophical argument.
    • Dante: Soaring poetic imagery.
    • Marco Polo: Careful observation of a merchant and traveler.
    • Francis: Simple, grateful language.
  • Their collective works demonstrate the extraordinary range of the era's literary output.

Dante Alighieri: The Divine Comedy

  • Biography:
    • Born in Florence in 1265, died in Ravenna in 1321.
    • Poet, philosopher, politician.
    • Banished from Florence in 1301 due to political conflicts; never returned.
    • Wrote The Divine Comedy in exile during the last 20 years of his life, influenced by his longing for Florence and anger at his exile.
  • The Divine Comedy:
    • A poem that maps the entire moral and spiritual universe.
    • Follows Dante as a character, guided by the Roman poet Virgil, on a journey through:
      • Hell (Inferno)
      • Purgatory (Purgatorio)
      • Heaven (Paradiso)
    • The journey begins on Good Friday of the year 1300, with Dante lost in a dark forest.
  • Revolutionary Use of Vernacular:
    • Written in the Tuscan dialect of Italian, not Latin (the standard for serious literature).
    • Dante believed Christian faith's greatest truths deserved expression in common language.
    • His Italian became the model for the standard Italian language.
  • Inferno, Canto I: The Dark Wood:
    • Opens with Dante lost in a forest, his path blocked by three beasts (leopard, lion, she-wolf) symbolizing sins.
    • Virgil appears and offers to guide Dante.
    • Virgil's Significance:
      • A soul in Limbo (for virtuous pagans who lived before Christ).
      • Not evil, but "incomplete" as he cannot enter Heaven.
      • Represents classical reason, which can guide through sin (Hell) and repentance (Purgatory).
      • Cannot lead to Heaven; Christian faith (symbolized later by Beatrice) is required for the final vision of God.
      • Dante revered Virgil (and Homer, Horace, Ovid, Lucan) as great poets, honoring them while acknowledging their exclusion from Heaven.
  • Inferno, Canto III: The Gate of Hell:
    • Dante and Virgil read the famous inscription above Hell's gate: "All hope abandon, ye who enter in!"
    • Inside, they encounter the souls of the indifferent—those who committed to neither good nor evil. They are punished by having no hope of death and are envious of every other lot, disdained by both mercy and justice.
  • The Logic of Dante's Hell:
    • Hell is organized on a strict principle: punishments fit the crimes.
    • Sins of weakness (lust, gluttony, anger) are punished higher up.
    • Sins of deliberate choice and cunning (fraud, treachery) are punished lower, closer to Satan.
    • Dante viewed sin as a disorder of the soul, a turning away from God. Punishments are the logical, eternal consequences of these choices.
  • Inferno, Canto XXXIV: Satan at the Center of the Earth:
    • At the very bottom of Hell, frozen in a lake of ice, is Satan.
    • Depicted as grotesque, weeping, and mindless, eternally chewing the greatest traitors (Judas Iscariot, Brutus, Cassius) with his three mouths.
    • Dante's Portrayal of Satan:
      • Deliberately the opposite of a magnificent rebel.
      • Symbolizes that the rejection of God leads not to power but to nothingness, helplessness, and an absence of good. Evil lacks intrinsic power.
      • Judas Iscariot is in the worst position for betrayal, reflecting theology that makes treachery the gravest sin.

Thomas Aquinas: The Summa Theologica

  • Summa Theologica:
    • A monumental work of Western intellectual history.
    • A systematic attempt to answer every important theological and philosophical question.
    • Written in a specific logical format: question posed, objections stated, a reply given, and then objections answered one by one.
    • Nothing is dismissed; every argument is considered seriously.
  • The Five Ways (quinque viae):
    • Aquinas's five famous arguments for the existence of God.
    • Each argument reasons from an observable fact about the world to the conclusion that God must exist.
    • Arguments from Reason: These are not arguments from Scripture or Church authority but from reason alone, starting from universal observations.
    • Purpose: To demonstrate that reason, properly used, leads to conclusions consistent with faith, showing no contradiction between natural philosophy (science) and Christian theology.
    • Key Examples (from the text):
      1. Argument from Motion: Everything in motion is moved by another; this chain cannot go to infinity, so there must be a first mover, unmoved by another (God). (Influenced by Aristotle's "Unmoved Mover").
      2. Argument from Efficient Cause: Nothing can be its own efficient cause; the chain of causes cannot go to infinity, so there must be a first efficient cause (God).
      3. Argument from Possibility and Necessity: Things in nature are possible to be or not to be; if everything were merely possible, there could have been a time when nothing existed. Thus, there must be a being with its own necessity (God).

Marco Polo: The Travels

  • The Travels of Marco Polo:
    • Dictated to Rustichello of Pisa in 1298 while Polo was a prisoner of war.
    • One of the most remarkable travel books ever written.
    • Provided medieval Europeans with their first detailed accounts of the world east of Persia.
    • Descriptions covered the Mongol Empire, China, Southeast Asia, Japan, India, and East Africa.
  • Kublai Khan's Court:
    • Polo spent many years in the service of Kublai Khan, whom he greatly admired.
    • His descriptions of the Khan's court at Khanbaliq (modern Beijing) are vivid.
    • He noted the immense size of the court, with over a hundred thousand people residing there for three months of the year.

Francis of Assisi

  • Mentioned in the introduction for singing a hymn to creation that is still sung today, demonstrating his simple, grateful language and love for God's creation.

Review Questions

  1. Dante Alighieri made the revolutionary decision to write The Divine Comedy in the Tuscan dialect rather than Latin. What was the significance of this choice, both for literature and for the development of language?
  2. In The Divine Comedy, Virgil guides Dante through Hell and Purgatory but cannot enter Heaven. What does this symbolize about the relationship between classical reason and Christian faith, according to Dante?
  3. Explain the purpose and methodology of Thomas Aquinas's Summa Theologica. How do his "Five Ways" for the existence of God exemplify his overall approach to integrating reason and faith?
  4. Dante's depiction of Satan at the bottom of Hell is notably different from many later portrayals. Describe Dante's Satan and discuss what this specific imagery conveys about his theological understanding of evil.
  5. What was the impact of Marco Polo's The Travels on medieval European society? How did his descriptions, particularly of Kublai Khan's court, challenge or expand their worldview?
