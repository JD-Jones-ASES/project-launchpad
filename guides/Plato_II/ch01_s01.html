Study Guide: The Story So Far — Returning to Socrates

Overview

This section serves as a crucial recap, bringing readers up to speed on the dramatic events leading to Socrates' execution, as chronicled in Plato's dialogues Euthyphro, Apology, and Crito. It highlights Socrates' philosophical journey from his accusation of impiety, through his powerful defense at trial, and finally to his principled decision to accept his death, setting the stage for the profound discussions on the afterlife in the Phaedo.

Key Concepts

  • The Socratic Method (Elenchus): Socrates' unique technique of rigorous questioning, where he seeks a definition, tests it for contradictions, and refines the inquiry through persistent follow-up. This method aims to expose false claims of knowledge and advance understanding.
  • The Euthyphro Dilemma: A foundational problem in the philosophy of religion posed by Socrates: "Are pious acts loved by the gods because they are pious, or are acts pious because the gods love them?" It questions whether goodness is independent of divine will or merely a product of it.
  • Socratic Ignorance: The recognition that true wisdom begins with acknowledging how little one actually knows. Socrates' realization that his "wisdom" lay in knowing that he did not possess special knowledge, unlike those who falsely claimed it.
  • The Unexamined Life: Socrates' famous declaration that "the unexamined life is not worth living," emphasizing the vital importance of self-reflection, critical inquiry, and the relentless pursuit of truth and virtue.
  • Priority of the Soul: Socrates' belief that caring for one's soul (implying one's character, virtue, and moral integrity) is paramount, far more important than external goods like wealth, reputation, or physical comfort. He consistently prioritized what was right over what was safe.
  • The Rule of Reason: The principle that one must always follow the best argument, even if it leads to unpopular or personally costly conclusions, rather than succumbing to popular opinion or emotional pressures.
  • Justice Over Survival (Social Contract): Socrates' refusal to escape prison, driven by his conviction that a good person must uphold justice and principles, even unto death. His imagined dialogue with "The Laws" foreshadows the concept of the social contract, suggesting citizens tacitly agree to obey the state in exchange for its benefits.

Important Terms

  • Socratic Method (Elenchus): Socrates' conversational technique for philosophical inquiry, involving asking for definitions, exposing contradictions, and refining understanding through persistent questions.
  • Euthyphro Dilemma: A philosophical question from Plato's Euthyphro concerning the relationship between divine command and morality: "Is X good because God commands it, or does God command X because it is good?"
  • Apology (Greek apologia): In ancient Greek, a formal speech delivered in one's own defense in court, distinct from the modern sense of expressing regret.
  • Oracle at Delphi: The most renowned prophetic sanctuary in ancient Greece, where the priestess Pythia delivered prophecies from the god Apollo.
  • Socratic Ignorance: The wisdom of knowing that one does not know; the understanding that one's knowledge is limited and often superficial.
  • Social Contract: A philosophical concept (later developed by thinkers like Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau) suggesting that individuals implicitly consent to surrender some freedoms and obey the state in exchange for protection of their rights and the benefits of an organized society.

Detailed Notes

The Story So Far: An Introduction

This section sets the stage for the next volume by reviewing the final weeks of Socrates' life, as depicted in three key dialogues by his student Plato. These dialogues — Euthyphro, Apology, and Crito — trace Socrates' journey from his initial accusation, through his trial, and into imprisonment, culminating in his impending execution. This recap serves to highlight the philosophical journey and principles that define Socrates before his final discussions in the Phaedo.

The Euthyphro: What Is Piety?

  • The Encounter: Socrates, on his way to court to face charges of impiety, meets Euthyphro, who claims expertise in religious matters. Euthyphro is prosecuting his own father for a death, believing his actions are perfectly pious.
  • The Socratic Method in Action: Socrates seizes the opportunity to learn about piety, applying his famous method of relentless questioning. He asks Euthyphro for a definition of piety and proceeds to dismantle each proposed answer by revealing its contradictions or insufficiencies.
    • Example: Euthyphro's definition that "piety is what the gods love" is challenged by Socrates, who points out that the gods often disagree amongst themselves.
  • The Euthyphro Dilemma: This pivotal philosophical puzzle emerges from the discussion: "Do the gods love pious acts because they are pious, or are acts pious because the gods love them?" This dilemma forces a fundamental question about the nature of goodness: Is goodness an independent standard that even divine beings recognize, or is it merely defined by divine command?
  • Conclusion: The dialogue ends inconclusively, with Euthyphro departing without providing a satisfactory definition. Socrates' conversation established that true knowledge of virtue is exceedingly difficult to achieve, and those who confidently claim such knowledge often lack it most.

The Apology: The Examined Life

  • Socrates' Defense: At his trial, Socrates delivers a formal defense (apologia in Greek, meaning a speech in one's defense, not an expression of regret). He faces charges of corrupting the youth and introducing new gods.
  • Refusal to Beg: Socrates rejects the common practice of appealing to the jury's emotions, choosing instead to explain his life's philosophical mission.
  • The Oracle at Delphi: His mission began when the Oracle declared that no one was wiser than Socrates. Puzzled, Socrates embarked on a quest to find someone wiser, questioning politicians, poets, and craftsmen.
  • Socratic Ignorance: He discovered that while these supposed experts believed they knew much, they actually knew very little. Socrates concluded that his unique wisdom lay in his awareness of his own ignorance – he knew that he did not know. This recognition is the starting point of genuine wisdom.
  • The Unexamined Life: Socrates powerfully declares that he cannot abandon philosophy, even to save his life, famously stating, "The unexamined life is not worth living." He would rather die than cease his search for truth and encourage others to examine their lives.
  • Verdict and Sentence: Despite his defense, the jury convicts Socrates by a narrow margin and sentences him to death by drinking hemlock.

The Crito: The Laws and the Good Man

  • The Escape Offer: While in prison awaiting execution, Socrates is visited by his friend Crito, who has arranged an escape. Crito argues that Socrates has obligations to his friends and children that compel him to flee.
  • Socrates' Refusal: Socrates rejects the escape, engaging in a profound discussion about justice and obligation.
  • The Argument of The Laws: Socrates imagines a personified "Laws of Athens" addressing him. They argue:
    • Socrates has lived his entire life under their protection, benefiting from the city's order.
    • By choosing to remain in Athens, he has implicitly agreed to obey their authority (an anticipation of the social contract theory).
    • To break the laws now, especially after accepting their benefits for so long, would be to fundamentally undermine the very fabric of justice and the state.
  • Upholding Principles: Socrates concludes that a truly good man must follow reason and justice, even when it demands the ultimate sacrifice. He chooses to accept the verdict and die, demonstrating the philosopher's commitment to principles above personal survival.

The Arc of Volume I

The three dialogues together form a cohesive narrative:

  • Euthyphro: Establishes the profound difficulty of answering fundamental questions about virtue and justice, emphasizing the need for rigorous thought.
  • Apology: Articulates the philosophical life – a life devoted to questioning and self-examination – as the only life truly worth living.
  • Crito: Demonstrates the philosopher's unwavering commitment to their principles, even when faced with death, showcasing the priority of justice and reason.

These dialogues reinforce the core Socratic principles: the Socratic method as a path to knowledge, the wisdom of Socratic ignorance, the supreme importance of caring for the soul, the necessity of following reason, and the ultimate priority of justice over personal gain or survival.

The conclusion of the Crito brings us to the moment of Socrates' death, setting the stage for the Phaedo, where Socrates will confront the ultimate philosophical question: What happens to the soul after death?

Review Questions

  1. Explain the Socratic method and how it was employed in the Euthyphro. What was the ultimate outcome of this application?
  2. What is the Euthyphro Dilemma, and why is it considered a significant question in the philosophy of religion even today?
  3. How did Socrates interpret the Oracle at Delphi's declaration that he was the wisest man, and what did "Socratic ignorance" mean in the context of his life's mission?
  4. In the Apology, Socrates declared, "The unexamined life is not worth living." What did he mean by this, and how did this belief shape his defense and his willingness to face death?
  5. Summarize the arguments made by "The Laws of Athens" to Socrates in the Crito as to why he should not escape. How do these arguments relate to the later concept of the social contract?
