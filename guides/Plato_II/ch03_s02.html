Objections and Replies: Ancient and Modern Perspectives on the Soul

Overview

This section explores how ancient philosophical inquiries into the nature of the soul, particularly objections raised in Plato's Phaedo, continue to resonate in modern thought. It traces the development of the mind-body problem from Simmias's "soul-as-harmony" concept to modern functionalism and the "hard problem of consciousness." Additionally, it examines Plato's more developed theory of the "tripartite soul" from the Republic, which addresses internal psychological conflict.

Key Concepts

  • Mind-Body Problem: The fundamental philosophical question concerning the relationship between mental phenomena (consciousness, thoughts, emotions) and physical phenomena (the brain, body).
  • Functionalism: A theory in the philosophy of mind proposing that mental states are defined by their functional roles—what they do (inputs, outputs, relations to other mental states)—rather than by their physical composition. The mind is akin to software, and the brain to hardware.
  • Hard Problem of Consciousness: Coined by David Chalmers, this is the challenge of explaining why and how physical processes in the brain give rise to subjective experience, or "what it is like" to be conscious, beyond merely explaining behavior or functional operations.
  • Tripartite Soul: Plato's theory, primarily presented in the Republic, that the human soul is composed of three distinct parts: Reason, Spirit (Thumos), and Appetite. These parts often engage in internal conflict.
  • Principle of Non-Contradiction (Platonic Application): The idea that the same thing cannot simultaneously desire and refuse the same object in the same respect, which Plato uses to argue for distinct parts within the soul when internal conflict occurs.

Important Terms

  • Functionalism: The philosophical view that mental states are defined by their functional roles within a system, rather than by the physical material that realizes them. Developed by Hilary Putnam.
  • Hard Problem of Consciousness: The problem of explaining how physical processes give rise to subjective, qualitative experience (qualia), as distinguished from the "easy problems" of explaining cognitive functions. Formulated by David Chalmers.
  • Thumos (Spirit): In Plato's Republic, this is the spirited, emotional, or passionate part of the soul, associated with anger, indignation, ambition, courage, and the drive for honor. It naturally tends to ally with reason against appetite.
  • Appetite (Epithumia): The part of the soul associated with basic bodily desires and urges, such as hunger, thirst, and sexual desire.
  • Reason (Logistikon): The rational, calculating, and truth-seeking part of the soul, responsible for thought, judgment, and seeking wisdom.

Detailed Notes

Tracing Ancient Objections into Modern Inquiry

The objections raised by Simmias and Cebes in Plato's Phaedo are not just historical footnotes; they initiated lines of inquiry that continue to shape philosophy, theology, and science. This section focuses on two primary areas: the mind-body problem in modern philosophy and Plato's further development of his psychology in the Republic.

The Soul-as-Harmony and Modern Materialism

Simmias's objection that the soul is a harmony of the body, and thus ceases when the body is destroyed, finds a striking parallel in modern materialist views of the mind.

  • Hilary Putnam and Functionalism:
    • In the 1960s, Hilary Putnam proposed functionalism. This theory suggests the mind is not the physical "stuff" of the brain, but its functional organization.
    • Think of it like software (mind) running on hardware (brain). A mental state like pain is defined by its role: it's caused by damage, causes distress, and leads to avoidance behavior, regardless of the specific neurons firing.
  • The Parallel to Simmias:
    • Simmias viewed the soul as the arrangement or harmony of the body's elements, not the elements themselves. Functionalism sees the mind as the organization of the brain, not its raw matter.
    • Both views imply that if the physical basis (body/lyre for Simmias; brain/hardware for functionalism) is destroyed, the mind/soul ceases to exist. The core idea—that the mind is a pattern or organization rather than a substance—is remarkably similar across millennia.
  • David Chalmers and the Hard Problem of Consciousness:
    • Despite functionalism's explanatory power for behavior, David Chalmers identified a crucial limitation: the hard problem of consciousness.
    • Even if we could fully explain how the brain processes information (inputs, outputs, functional steps), we still wouldn't have explained why there is subjective experience—what it feels like to be conscious. A complete functional map might explain actions, but not inner experience (e.g., why seeing red feels like anything).
  • Socrates' Ancient Argument Resonates:
    • Socrates argued against Simmias by pointing out that the soul rules the body, resisting physical impulses. A harmony, being a product of the strings, cannot rule them.
    • This echoes Chalmers' hard problem: if consciousness is merely a functional byproduct of brain activity, it's unclear how it could then govern or "resist" the very physical processes that produce it. The fundamental challenge of explaining the mind's apparent independence from the body has persisted from ancient Greece to today.
    • Think Deeper: While a smartphone's parental control app can "block" certain actions, this is a pre-programmed resistance based on algorithms. Socrates' point about the soul resisting the body implies a conscious, deliberative choice that can override immediate physical impulses (like denying thirst for a greater good), which is different from a purely mechanistic, pre-programmed response. The smartphone doesn't "feel" the impulse it resists or consciously decide to oppose it in the way a human might.

The Tripartite Soul in the Republic

While the Phaedo often presents the soul as a single entity in conflict with the body, Plato's Republic offers a more nuanced view, proposing that the soul itself has distinct parts.

  • Internal Conflict as Evidence:
    • In Republic, Book IV, Socrates argues that the soul must have at least three parts because humans frequently experience internal conflict.
    • The core argument uses the Principle of Non-Contradiction: the same thing cannot simultaneously desire and refuse the same object in the same respect. Therefore, if we find such contradictions within ourselves, different parts of the soul must be at play.
  • The Example of the Thirsty Man:
    • A man is thirsty (his appetite desires drink), but he knows the water is contaminated (his reason forbids him to drink).
    • Since the same part cannot both desire and forbid the same thing, appetite and reason must be distinct.
    • A third element, spirit (thumos), often sides with reason, providing the emotional drive and courage needed to resist the appetite's temptation. This spirited part gives the energy to overcome desires when reason dictates it.
  • The Three Parts of the Soul:
    1. Reason (Logistikon): The rational, calculating, and governing part, seeking truth and wisdom.
    2. Spirit (Thumos): The emotional, spirited part, the seat of anger, courage, and the drive for honor. It can be a powerful ally to reason.
    3. Appetite (Epithumia): The desires for bodily pleasures and necessities (food, drink, sex, wealth).

Plato's account in the Republic thus moves beyond a simple soul-versus-body dichotomy to explore the complex internal dynamics of the human psyche, providing a foundational model for understanding psychological conflict and moral choice.

Review Questions

  1. How does Simmias's ancient "soul-as-harmony" objection find a modern echo in Hilary Putnam's functionalism? What are the key similarities between these two views regarding the nature of the mind/soul?
  2. Explain David Chalmers' "hard problem of consciousness." How does it pose a significant challenge to theories like functionalism, and in what way does Socrates' ancient argument against Simmias foreshadow this modern philosophical difficulty?
  3. According to Plato in the Republic, what primary evidence or argument leads him to conclude that the soul is not a simple, unified entity but has distinct parts? Provide a specific example from the text to illustrate this point.
  4. Describe each of the three parts of the soul as presented in Plato's Republic (Reason, Spirit, and Appetite). How do these parts interact, particularly in situations of internal conflict?
  5. Consider the "thinkdeeper" question regarding the smartphone. In what essential ways is a smartphone's programmed "resistance" to certain inputs similar to, and different from, what Socrates means when he discusses the soul's ability to "oppose" the body's impulses?
