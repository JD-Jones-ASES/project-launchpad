Study Guide: Socrates' Final Argument and Death

Overview

This section of the Phaedo presents Socrates' intellectual autobiography, detailing his philosophical journey from an early interest in natural science to his unique method of inquiry through reason. It lays the groundwork for his most powerful argument for the immortality of the soul, built upon the Theory of Forms, and concludes with the anticipation of his famous death scene and a mythological account of the afterlife.

Key Concepts

  • Socrates' Intellectual Autobiography: Socrates recounts his shift from studying natural philosophy (ancient science) to examining reality through arguments and definitions (logoi), prompted by his frustration with purely physical explanations.
  • Cause vs. Necessary Condition: A pivotal distinction made by Socrates, where a "true cause" explains the purpose or reason for something, while a "necessary condition" describes the physical prerequisites without which an action or state could not occur.
  • The "Second Sailing" (deuteros plous): A nautical metaphor describing Socrates' decisive turn from direct sensory observation of the world to philosophical inquiry through abstract reasoning, hypotheses, and arguments. This method became the foundation of Plato's philosophy.
  • Method of Hypothesis: Socrates' approach to philosophical inquiry, which involves starting with a "safest" principle (like the existence of Forms), deriving consequences, and testing their consistency. If challenged, one "ascends" to a higher, more fundamental hypothesis.
  • The Theory of Forms: Plato's central philosophical idea, positing that perfect, eternal, and unchanging realities (Forms like Beauty itself, Life itself) exist beyond the physical world. Physical objects are merely imperfect copies or participants in these Forms.
  • The Final Argument for Immortality: Socrates' comprehensive argument, which asserts the soul's immortality by demonstrating that the soul, as the bringer of life, cannot admit its opposite (death) and therefore must be indestructible.

Important Terms

  • Natural Philosophy: The ancient equivalent of science, involving investigations into the physical causes of natural phenomena, material composition, and the structure of the cosmos.
  • Anaxagoras (c. 500–428 BC): A pre-Socratic philosopher who proposed that nous (Mind) was responsible for ordering the cosmos but disappointed Socrates by using purely physical explanations for specific phenomena.
  • Nous (Mind): An intelligent principle proposed by Anaxagoras as the cause and ordering principle of all things.
  • Second Sailing (deuteros plous): A metaphorical turn from studying reality directly through the senses to studying it indirectly through arguments, definitions, and rational inquiry (logoi).
  • Logoi: Greek word for "words," "arguments," "reasons," or "accounts." In Socrates' "second sailing," it refers to the method of examining reality through rational discourse.
  • Hypothesis: A proposed explanation or principle used as a starting point for reasoning and investigation, which is then tested for consistency.
  • Aitia: Greek for "cause" or "explanation." In Plato's usage, it refers to the formal reason why a thing is what it is, often linked to its participation in a Form.
  • Forms: Perfect, eternal, unchanging, non-physical blueprints or essences that exist independently of the physical world (e.g., the Form of Beauty, the Form of Life). Physical things "participate" in Forms.
  • Athanatos: Greek for "deathless" or "immortal," referring to something not subject to death.
  • Anolethros: Greek for "indestructible" or "imperishable," meaning something that cannot be destroyed or cease to exist.
  • Psyche: The Greek word for "soul" or "life principle." For Plato, it is the animating force that makes a living being alive.

Detailed Notes

The Final Argument and Death: An Overview

The final section of Plato's Phaedo is rich, encompassing Socrates' personal philosophical history, his most robust argument for the soul's immortality, and a mythological depiction of the afterlife, leading to his renowned death scene.

Socrates' Intellectual Autobiography

  1. Cebes' Objection: The section begins with Cebes' challenge: the soul, like a weaver's cloak, might outlast many bodies but eventually wear out and perish itself.
  2. Early Interest in Natural Philosophy: As a young man, Socrates was intensely drawn to "natural philosophy" (early science). He sought the physical causes for growth, thought processes, and the structure of the cosmos.
  3. Growing Confusion: Despite eager study, Socrates found himself more confused than enlightened by natural philosophy. Simple observations, when closely examined, became puzzling.
  4. Encounter with Anaxagoras and Disappointment: Socrates was initially thrilled by Anaxagoras' idea that Nous (Mind) causes and orders all things. He hoped this meant everything was arranged for the best, and understanding a thing's purpose would explain its nature. However, Anaxagoras disappointed him by explaining particular phenomena through purely physical, mechanical causes (air, ether, water) after initially invoking Mind for cosmic creation.
  5. Cause vs. Necessary Condition: This disappointment led Socrates to a crucial philosophical distinction. He used the analogy of his imprisonment: the true cause is his choice to accept the verdict, not the physical mechanisms of his bones and muscles (which are merely necessary conditions for sitting). A true cause, for Socrates, explains the why (purpose/reason), not just the how (mechanism).
  6. The "Second Sailing": Unable to find a teacher or method that explained things by their purposes, Socrates embarked on a "second sailing." This metaphor signifies a turn away from direct sensory investigation of the physical world (like staring at the sun) to examining reality through "logoi" – arguments, definitions, and hypotheses. This shift laid the groundwork for Plato's philosophical method of investigating reality through the mind rather than the senses, which ultimately led to the Theory of Forms.

The Method of Hypothesis

  1. Starting with Forms: The "second sailing" culminates in Socrates' method of hypothesis. He begins by laying down the "safest" possible principle: the existence of Forms (e.g., absolute Beauty, Goodness, Greatness).
  2. Forms as True Causes (Aitia): For Socrates, the Forms are the true aitia (causes/explanations). A flower is beautiful not because of its color or shape (physical conditions), but because it participates in the Form of Beauty itself.
  3. Testing and Ascending Hypotheses:
    • One derives consequences from a hypothesis and checks for consistency.
    • If the hypothesis is challenged, one does not defend it directly but "goes up" to a higher, more fundamental hypothesis.
    • This process continues until a sufficiently fundamental and self-justifying principle is reached.
  4. Purpose: This method establishes the Forms as true causes and provides the rational framework for Socrates' final argument concerning the soul's immortality.

The Final Argument for Immortality

This argument is complex and builds on the method of hypothesis and the Theory of Forms.

  1. Step 1: Forms Exclude Their Opposites: Essential Forms cannot admit their opposites. The Form of Greatness can never be small; the Form of Oddness can never be even.
  2. Step 2: Concrete Things Carrying a Form Also Exclude the Opposite: This is the key extension. It's not just the Form of Odd that excludes Evenness, but any concrete thing that always carries oddness (like the number three) also cannot be even. Similarly, fire (which always brings heat) cannot be cold; snow (which always brings cold) cannot be hot. When the opposite approaches, the thing either retreats or is destroyed (e.g., snow melts when heat approaches).
  3. Step 3: The Soul Always Brings Life: The psyche (soul) is the principle that makes a body alive. Wherever the soul is present, life is present. The soul is to life as fire is to heat, or three is to oddness; it always carries life with it.
  4. Step 4: The Soul Cannot Admit Death: Since the soul always brings life (its essential characteristic), it cannot admit its opposite, death. Therefore, the soul is athanatos (deathless, immortal).
  5. Step 5: The Soul is Indestructible: When death approaches the body, the soul does not perish or cease to exist. Instead, because it is immortal, it departs, intact and unharmed, to another realm. Socrates argues that what is immortal must, by its very nature, be anolethros (imperishable or indestructible).

Critique of the Final Argument

  • Strengths: The first four steps possess logical elegance; if the soul's essence is life, then a "dead soul" is a contradiction.
  • Weaknesses (Step 5): The leap from "immortal" (not admitting death) to "indestructible" (cannot cease to exist) is the most debated point. Critics question if "not admitting death" necessarily means survival. For example, snow doesn't become hot but is destroyed by heat (it melts). Could the soul be "destroyed" by death, ceasing to exist, rather than departing? Socrates' argument that the immortal must be imperishable is where the argument requires the most assent.

The Myth of the Afterlife

Following this rigorous philosophical argument, Socrates shifts to a mythological narrative about the afterlife, providing a rich, imaginative picture of what awaits the soul.

Review Questions

  1. Describe Socrates' philosophical journey from his early interest in natural philosophy to his "second sailing." What prompted this significant shift, and what was its core methodology?
  2. Explain the crucial distinction Socrates makes between a "cause" and a "necessary condition." Provide an example from the text and one of your own to illustrate this difference.
  3. How does Socrates' Method of Hypothesis work? What role do the Forms play in this method, and how is a hypothesis tested or refined according to Socrates?
  4. Outline the five steps of Socrates' Final Argument for the Immortality of the Soul. Focus on how each step builds upon the previous one, particularly the connection between Forms, concrete things, and the nature of the soul.
  5. What is considered the weakest point of Socrates' Final Argument for Immortality? Explain the objection using the analogy of snow and heat, and discuss how Socrates attempts to address it. Do you find his counter-argument convincing?
