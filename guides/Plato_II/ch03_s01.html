Study Guide: Objections and Replies in Plato's Phaedo, Part II

Overview

This section of the Phaedo introduces Socrates' third argument for the soul's immortality, the Argument from Affinity, which suggests the soul's nature aligns with the divine and unchanging. Following this, Socrates presents an imaginative "ghost passage" illustrating the soul's fate based on its earthly life. The core of this section then shifts to two powerful objections: Simmias' Harmony Objection, which likens the soul to a bodily arrangement, and Cebes' Weaver Objection, which questions the soul's eternal durability even if it is a distinct substance. These objections lead to a dramatic crisis of confidence and a warning from Socrates against misology.

Key Concepts

  • Argument from Affinity: Socrates' argument that there are two kinds of reality—visible, changing, and mortal (like the body) and invisible, unchanging, and divine (like the Forms). The soul, being invisible and contemplating the Forms, has an "affinity" with the divine, suggesting it is not easily destroyed.
  • Two Sorts of Existence (Plato's Dualism): A fundamental philosophical distinction between the material, sensory world (visible, changing, perishable) and the immaterial, intellectual world of the Forms (invisible, unchanging, eternal). This distinction underpins the Argument from Affinity.
  • The Ghost Passage: An imaginative and mythic description by Socrates of what happens to souls after death. Philosophically pure souls ascend to the divine, while souls polluted by bodily pleasures become "heavy" ghosts bound to the physical world and are reborn into animal forms reflecting their character. This passage dramatizes the philosophical principle that how one lives shapes the soul's post-mortem destiny.
  • Simmias' Harmony Objection: This powerful objection likens the soul to the harmony produced by a lyre. Just as a lyre's harmony is invisible and beautiful but ceases when the lyre is destroyed, the soul might be a mere arrangement or byproduct of the body's physical elements, perishing with the body.
  • Cebes' Weaver Objection: This objection grants that the soul might be a distinct substance stronger than the body, but argues it's not necessarily immortal. Using the analogy of a weaver who outlasts many coats but eventually dies, Cebes suggests the soul could pass through many bodies (reincarnation) but still eventually wear out and be destroyed.
  • Crisis of Confidence: The emotional and intellectual state of the listeners (and narrator Echecrates) after hearing Simmias' and Cebes' objections, where their previous faith in Socrates' arguments for immortality is shaken.
  • Misology: The hatred of argument or reason. Socrates warns against this, explaining it arises from disillusionment with arguments, similar to how misanthropy (hatred of people) arises from disillusionment with friends.

Important Terms

  • Affinity (suggeneia): Kinship or natural likeness. In this context, the soul's resemblance to the divine and unchanging realm.
  • Forms: Plato's term for the eternal, perfect, invisible realities (e.g., Beauty Itself, Justice Itself) that particular physical things only approximate. They are grasped by the mind, not the senses.
  • Sepulchres: Burial vaults or tombs. Mentioned in the ghost passage as places where impure souls/ghosts might prowl.
  • Lyre: A stringed musical instrument, central to ancient Greek music. Used by Simmias in his analogy to represent the body, with its harmony representing the soul.
  • Misology (misologia): From Greek misos (hatred) + logos (word, argument, reason). The hatred of argument or reason, which Socrates warns against.
  • Misanthropy (misanthropia): Hatred of people. Socrates uses this as an analogy to explain the origins of misology.

Detailed Notes

Introduction to Part II: New Arguments and Strong Objections

  • Part I of the Phaedo established the soul's pre-existence (Arguments from Opposites and Recollection).
  • Simmias and Cebes correctly noted that pre-existence does not guarantee post-mortem survival.
  • Part II focuses on proving the soul's survival after death and addressing significant challenges.

The Argument from Affinity

  • Socrates' Core Question: What kinds of things can be scattered and destroyed, and what kinds cannot?
  • Two Sorts of Existence (Platonic Dualism Revisited):
    1. Visible, Changing, Mortal: The physical world, perceived by senses (e.g., bodies, flowers, stones). Nothing in this realm is permanent.
    2. Invisible, Unchanging, Divine: The world of the Forms (e.g., Beauty itself, Equality itself). These are grasped by the mind alone and are eternal.
  • Categorizing Body and Soul:
    • The body is clearly visible, physical, and subject to change, belonging to the first category.
    • The soul is invisible, reasons, reflects, and contemplates the Forms, activities associated with the invisible, unchanging realm.
  • Conclusion: Therefore, the soul has a natural affinity (kinship or likeness) with the divine and unchanging. It resembles the kind of thing that cannot be scattered or destroyed.
  • Limitation of the Argument: It's an argument from analogy ("resembles the unchanging"), not a strict proof ("is identical with the unchanging"). Resemblance does not guarantee identity or immortality. Socrates seems to acknowledge this limitation by transitioning to a more imaginative mode of discourse.

The Ghost Passage: Soul's Post-Mortem Destiny

  • Socrates shifts from strict argument to a vivid, imaginative description of the afterlife.
  • Fate of Purified Souls:
    • Souls purified by philosophy, seeking truth and avoiding bodily distractions, depart to the invisible, divine world, dwelling in blessedness with the gods.
  • Fate of Polluted Souls:
    • Souls that loved physical pleasures and sensory experience become "heavy and weighty and earthy."
    • They are dragged back towards the visible world, becoming ghosts that "prowl about tombs and sepulchres," still "cloyed with sight" (attached to the physical).
  • Rebirth/Transmigration:
    • Socrates suggests these impure souls are reborn into bodies matching their character:
      • Gluttonous/intemperate become donkeys.
      • Unjust/tyrannical become wolves or hawks.
      • Moderately virtuous (civil/social virtues from habit, not philosophy) return as social creatures like bees or ants.
    • Philosophical Interpretation: This passage is not meant as literal evidence for ghosts, but as a myth or moral teaching. It dramatizes a core philosophical principle: a life dedicated to the body binds the soul to the body; a life dedicated to truth prepares the soul for the realm of truth. Philosophy is portrayed as the soul's preparation for eternity, emphasizing a sharp distinction between genuine wisdom and mere conventional virtue.

Simmias' Harmony Objection: The Soul as a Bodily Byproduct

  • The Analogy: Simmias compares the soul to the harmony of a lyre.
    • The harmony is invisible, beautiful, and "divine" (like the soul).
    • The lyre and its strings are physical, composite, and mortal (like the body).
    • When the lyre is smashed, the harmony ceases; it doesn't float away.
  • The Objection: The soul might not be an independent substance but a "harmony" or specific arrangement of the body's physical elements (e.g., "hot and cold, wet and dry"). If the body is destroyed, the soul, being dependent on it, would simply cease to exist.
  • Significance: This is considered the most powerful objection in the Phaedo, raising a problem that resonates with modern thinking (e.g., how brain damage affects consciousness, how consciousness seems to end with brain death). It directly challenges the idea that the soul can survive the body's death.

Cebes' Weaver Objection: Independent but Still Mortal

  • Cebes' Concession: He grants that the soul is a real, powerful, and independent substance, possibly even "stronger and more lasting" than the body.
  • The Analogy: A weaver makes many coats over a lifetime, outlasting each one. But the weaver eventually dies, and the last coat may still exist after the weaver's death.
  • The Objection: Even if the soul outlasts many bodies (perhaps through reincarnation), this doesn't prove it's immortal. The soul could "wear out" after many cycles, or its entrance into a body could be a "disease" leading to eventual dissolution. The soul's power doesn't imply its permanence.
  • Distinction from Simmias:
    • Simmias: Denies the soul is an independent substance; it's a product of the body.
    • Cebes: Grants the soul is an independent substance but questions its indestructibility over infinite time.

The Crisis of Confidence

  • The listeners, including the narrator Phaedo, feel an "unpleasant feeling" as the previous arguments seem ruined.
  • Echecrates, Phaedo's audience, interrupts the story, echoing the doubt: "What argument can I ever trust again?"
  • Plato uses this dramatic moment to show the genuine philosophical challenge and to allow the objections their full force, creating a crisis of faith in reason.

The Misology Digression

  • Before replying, Socrates warns against the danger of misology (hatred of argument/reason).
  • Analogy to Misanthropy: Just as repeated disappointment in friends can lead to believing no one can be trusted (misanthropy), repeated disappointment with arguments (or encountering strong objections) can lead to distrusting all arguments and reason itself.
  • Socrates' Purpose: To prepare his friends (and the reader) to avoid cynicism and maintain faith in the philosophical pursuit of truth, even when facing difficult objections.

Review Questions

  1. Explain Socrates' Argument from Affinity. What distinction about reality does it rely upon, and what is its primary limitation as a proof for immortality?
  2. Describe the "Ghost Passage." What are the fates of purified versus polluted souls, and what philosophical principle is Socrates dramatizing through this mythic description?
  3. Articulate Simmias' Harmony Objection. What analogy does he use, and why does this pose a strong challenge to the soul's immortality? How does this objection relate to modern discussions about consciousness?
  4. Explain Cebes' Weaver Objection. How does it differ from Simmias' challenge, and what specific aspect of the soul's immortality does it question, even if the soul is an independent substance?
  5. What is "misology," and why does Socrates introduce this warning immediately after Simmias and Cebes present their objections? How does misology relate to misanthropy in Socrates' explanation?
