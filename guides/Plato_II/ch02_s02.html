Chapter 2, Section 2: Recollection and the Body-Soul Problem

Overview

This section explores Plato's theory of recollection (anamnesis) as presented in the Meno dialogue, arguing that all learning is essentially remembering innate knowledge. It then traces the influence of this idea through later philosophers like Descartes and Kant. Finally, the section delves into Plato's body-soul dualism, contrasting it with the Christian understanding of the body and its ultimate hope in the resurrection.

Key Concepts

  • Recollection (Anamnesis): Plato's theory that all learning is a process of remembering knowledge that the soul already possessed before birth, acquired when it directly knew the Forms. The Meno's slave boy demonstration is a vivid example.
  • Innate Knowledge/Ideas: The concept that the mind is not a blank slate but is born with certain fundamental ideas or truths. Plato attributed this to pre-birth knowledge of Forms. Descartes argued these were implanted by God.
  • A Priori Knowledge: Kant's refinement of innate knowledge, suggesting that certain frameworks (like space, time, and causation) are built into the mind and are necessary conditions for experiencing the world, rather than being derived from experience itself.
  • Dualism (Platonic): The philosophical view that the soul and body are two fundamentally different kinds of entities. Plato's version posits the body as an obstacle, a source of distraction and error, from which the soul seeks to escape to attain truth.
  • Resurrection of the Body: The Christian doctrine that, unlike Plato's idea of the soul escaping the body, human salvation involves the eventual reuniting of the soul with a transformed, glorified physical body at the end of time. This contrasts with Platonic escapism.

Important Terms

  • Meno: A dialogue by Plato (c. 385 BC) named after Meno of Thessaly, known for the slave boy demonstration and the theory of recollection.
  • Phaedo: Another Platonic dialogue, which provides the metaphysical explanation for the theory of recollection, linking it to the soul's prior knowledge of the Forms.
  • René Descartes (1596–1650): French philosopher, often called the father of modern philosophy, who argued for the existence of innate ideas (e.g., God, infinity, mathematical truths) implanted by God.
  • Immanuel Kant (1724–1804): German philosopher who, in Critique of Pure Reason, argued for a priori knowledge, asserting that fundamental categories like space and time are conditions for experience, not derived from it.
  • Tabula Rasa: Latin for "blank slate," a concept promoted by British empiricists like John Locke, which argues that the mind begins devoid of innate knowledge and all understanding comes from sensory experience.
  • Apostle Paul: An influential early Christian writer who described a tension between "flesh" (sarx) and "spirit" (pneuma), often seen as analogous to Plato's body-soul conflict but with different underlying theological meanings.
  • Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD): Influential early Christian theologian who, while acknowledging the soul's importance, rejected Plato's view of the body as inherently evil, instead focusing on the corruption of the whole person (body and soul) by original sin.
  • Incarnation: The Christian doctrine that God the Son took on human flesh in the person of Jesus Christ, affirming the goodness of matter and challenging philosophies that denigrate the body.

Detailed Notes

Recollection and Innate Knowledge

Plato's Argument in the Meno

  • Plato first presented the argument from Recollection (anamnesis) in the Meno.
  • The dialogue features Meno of Thessaly asking Socrates if virtue can be taught.
  • Socrates claims all learning is actually recollection.
  • The Slave Boy Demonstration:
    • To prove his claim, Socrates calls upon one of Meno's uneducated slave boys.
    • Through a series of carefully crafted questions, Socrates guides the boy to independently discover how to construct a square with double the area of a given square.
    • The boy makes initial mistakes but is never given the answer directly; Socrates only asks questions.
    • The boy eventually realizes the solution: the double square is built on the diagonal of the original square.
    • Socrates' Conclusion: Since the boy was never taught geometry, the knowledge must have been already present in his soul, placed there before birth when the soul knew the Forms directly. The questions merely "stirred up" this latent knowledge.

Connection to the Phaedo

  • While the Meno dramatizes the process of recollection, the Phaedo offers the metaphysical explanation.
  • In the Phaedo, the idea of recognizing "equal sticks" as falling short of perfect Equality implies remembering the Form of Equality known before birth.

Influence on Western Philosophy

  • René Descartes and Innate Ideas:

    • In the 17th century, Descartes revived a version of Plato's doctrine in his Meditations.
    • He argued that certain ideas (e.g., God, infinity, basic mathematical truths) are "innate"—they could not have come from sensory experience but were implanted in the mind by God.
    • These ideas are like a craftsman's trademark, built into our minds.

  • Immanuel Kant and A Priori Knowledge:

    • A century later, Kant developed the concept further in Critique of Pure Reason.
    • He distinguished a priori knowledge (knowledge prior to and independent of experience) from a posteriori knowledge (derived from experience).
    • Kant argued that concepts like space, time, and causation are not learned from observation but are fundamental categories, or frameworks, that the mind brings to experience, making experience possible.
    • While not believing in pre-birth recollection like Plato, Kant shared the core idea that the mind is not a blank slate but actively structures experience.

  • Contrast with Empiricism:

    • These views contrast sharply with the British empiricists (e.g., John Locke), who championed the idea of the mind as a "tabula rasa" (blank slate), asserting that all knowledge originates from sensory experience.

The Body-Soul Problem

Plato's Dualism

  • Socrates (and Plato) drew a clear distinction between the body and the soul.
  • Body: Seen as a source of confusion, distraction, and error; an obstacle to truth.
  • Soul: When it acts independently of the body, it can access truth and reason.
  • Philosopher's Life: A progressive withdrawal from bodily influences. Death is the ultimate, complete separation, allowing the soul to fully attain truth.
  • Platonic Dualism: The strong doctrine that soul and body are fundamentally different kinds of things, with the body being an active impediment to the soul's proper function.

Christian Perspectives on Body and Soul

  • Apostle Paul and the Flesh vs. Spirit:

    • Paul described a similar internal conflict, a tension between "flesh" (sarx) and "spirit" (pneuma), where bodily desires can pull against spiritual aspirations (Romans 7:19).
    • However, Paul's "flesh" refers to the whole person in rebellion against God, not just the physical body as inherently evil.

  • Crucial Departure from Plato:

    • While agreeing on the existence of internal conflict and the soul's importance, Christianity fundamentally differs from Plato's view of the body.
    • Plato: Body is inherently inferior, a prison to be escaped.
    • Christianity: The body is created by God and declared "very good" (Genesis 1:31).
    • Christian Hope: Not escape from the body, but the resurrection of the body—a transformed, glorified body reunited with the soul at the end of time (as affirmed in the Nicene Creed). This directly contradicts Platonic escapism.

  • Augustine of Hippo:

    • Agreed with Plato that the soul is more important than the body.
    • Rejected the idea that the body is merely a prison.
    • Argued that the problem is not embodiment itself, but the disordering of the entire person (body and soul together) due to original sin.

  • The Incarnation:

    • The Christian doctrine of the Incarnation (God the Son taking on human flesh in Jesus Christ) is the ultimate challenge to any philosophy that denigrates the body.
    • If God himself chose to inhabit a human body, then matter and the body cannot be inherently evil; they are good enough for God.

  • Summary of Differences:

    • Both Plato and Christianity acknowledge the soul's importance and the struggle against bodily desires.
    • However, Plato views the body as inherently negative and seeks escape.
    • Christianity affirms the body's goodness as God's creation and looks forward to its resurrection and transformation.

Review Questions

  1. Explain Socrates' slave boy demonstration from the Meno. What was the boy asked to do, and what conclusion did Socrates draw from the boy's ability to solve the problem?
  2. How does Plato's theory of recollection (anamnesis) connect to his broader philosophical concept of the Forms?
  3. Compare and contrast the ideas of innate knowledge/a priori knowledge as presented by Plato, Descartes, and Kant. What common thread runs through their arguments, and how do they differ in their explanations?
  4. Describe Plato's concept of dualism and his view of the body-soul relationship. Why did he consider the body an obstacle to philosophical truth?
  5. Discuss the key differences between Plato's view of the body and the Christian understanding, particularly concerning the ultimate destiny of the body. If Socrates had heard about the resurrection of the body, would he have considered it good news or bad news? Why?
