Two Philosophers, One Academy: A Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the foundational relationship between two of history's most influential philosophers, Plato and Aristotle, who studied and taught together at Plato's Academy. It details Aristotle's life, his background, his role as a tutor to Alexander the Great, and the establishment of his own school, the Lyceum. Crucially, it highlights the profound philosophical and temperamental differences between master and student, encapsulated by their contrasting views on the nature of reality and how knowledge is acquired, while acknowledging their shared commitment to the pursuit of wisdom.

Key Concepts

  • Plato's Academy: Founded by Plato around 387 BC, it was a prominent intellectual center in Athens, lasting nearly nine centuries. It served as the training ground for many thinkers, including Aristotle.
  • Aristotle's Intellectual Journey: Arrived at the Academy at 17, stayed for 20 years as a student, teacher, and researcher, demonstrating exceptional brilliance and eventually developing philosophies that challenged Plato's core ideas.
  • The Lyceum (Peripatetic School): Aristotle's own school, founded in Athens around 335 BC, after his time tutoring Alexander the Great. Its students were known as Peripatetics because they discussed philosophy while walking in the covered walkways (peripatoi).
  • Divergent Views on Reality: Plato believed true reality lay in eternal, unchanging "Forms" accessible only by the mind (represented by his pointing upward in Raphael's fresco). Aristotle believed reality was found in the observable, particular things of the physical world, understood through careful observation and classification (represented by his hand gesturing outward).
  • Contrasting Philosophical Temperaments: Plato is described as a "poet-philosopher" who used dialogues, myths, and allegories to prompt intellectual vision. Aristotle is characterized as a "biologist-philosopher" focused on systematic classification, precise analysis, and empirical observation across various sciences.
  • Respectful Disagreement: Despite their significant philosophical differences, Aristotle maintained deep respect for Plato, famously stating, "Plato is dear to me, but dearer still is truth," signifying his independent pursuit of knowledge while valuing his teacher.
  • Shared Philosophical Ground: Despite their divergences, Plato and Aristotle agreed on fundamental principles, such as the importance of philosophy, virtue, the possibility of knowledge, and the significance of the soul.

Important Terms

  • Plato's Academy: The school of philosophy founded by Plato in Athens around 387 BC, where Aristotle studied and taught.
  • Aristotle (384–322 BC): Greek philosopher, student of Plato, tutor of Alexander the Great, and founder of the Lyceum. Known for his empirical approach and vast contributions across many fields of study.
  • Stagira: A small Greek colony in northern Greece, Aristotle's birthplace.
  • Nicomachus: Aristotle's father, who was the personal physician to King Amyntas III of Macedon.
  • King Philip II of Macedon: The king who invited Aristotle to tutor his son, Alexander, in the Macedonian court.
  • Alexander the Great (356–323 BC): King of Macedon, Aristotle's most famous pupil, who went on to conquer a vast empire and spread Greek culture.
  • Lyceum: The school founded by Aristotle in Athens around 335 BC, an intellectual rival to Plato's Academy.
  • Peripatetics: The name given to Aristotle's students at the Lyceum, derived from their practice of discussing philosophy while walking in the peripatoi (covered walkways).
  • The School of Athens: A fresco painted by Raphael (1509–1511) that famously depicts Plato pointing upwards and Aristotle gesturing outwards, symbolizing their differing philosophies.
  • Timaeus: A dialogue by Plato, held by him in Raphael's fresco, which discusses cosmology, the natural world, and the Forms.
  • Nicomachean Ethics: A treatise by Aristotle on ethics, held by him in Raphael's fresco, focusing on character, virtue, and the good life.
  • Amicus Plato, sed magis amica veritas: A Latin phrase meaning "Plato is dear to me, but dearer still is truth," attributed to Aristotle, reflecting his intellectual independence.

Detailed Notes

The Genesis of a Philosophical Legacy

  • Aristotle's Arrival: In 367 BC, at the age of seventeen, Aristotle arrived in Athens from Stagira, a small Greek colony in northern Greece. He enrolled in Plato's Academy, a prestigious school founded by Plato around 387 BC in a grove sacred to the hero Akademos.
  • The Academy's Endurance: Plato's Academy was an institution of immense longevity, continuing in various forms for nearly nine centuries until its closure by Roman Emperor Justinian in 529 AD.
  • Aristotle's Tenure: Aristotle remained at the Academy for two decades, evolving from a student into a revered teacher and researcher. He was recognized as Plato's most brilliant pupil, yet he was also the one who would most profoundly challenge his master's core beliefs.
  • A Unique Relationship: The bond between Plato and Aristotle is legendary—a master and student who shared mutual respect and learning, yet fundamentally diverged on matters of philosophy.

Aristotle's Life and Career

  • Early Life and Influences: Born in Stagira in 384 BC, Aristotle's early life was shaped by his father, Nicomachus, who was the personal physician to King Amyntas III of Macedon. This connection likely ignited Aristotle's lifelong passion for biology and established his ties to the Macedonian court.
  • Departure from Athens: After Plato's death in 347 BC, Aristotle left Athens, possibly because the leadership of the Academy passed to Plato's nephew, Speusippus, rather than to him.
  • Years of Travel and Research: He spent several years traveling, notably conducting extensive biological research on the island of Lesbos, deepening his empirical approach to knowledge.
  • Tutor to a Prince: Around 343 BC, King Philip II of Macedon invited Aristotle to tutor his thirteen-year-old son, Prince Alexander (later Alexander the Great). While the full extent of Aristotle's influence on Alexander is debated, ancient accounts suggest Alexander valued his teacher greatly, even carrying an annotated copy of Homer's Iliad by Aristotle on his campaigns.
  • Founding the Lyceum: In 335 BC, Aristotle returned to Athens and established his own school, the Lyceum, located in a gymnasium and grove sacred to Apollo Lyceus. His students became known as the Peripatetics, referring to their custom of discussing philosophy while walking in the covered walkways (peripatoi).
  • Prolific Scholarship: For twelve years, Aristotle taught at the Lyceum, producing an astonishing and comprehensive body of work spanning logic, physics, biology, ethics, politics, rhetoric, and poetics. His works are characterized by their systematic organization, precision, and analytical rigor.
  • Flight from Athens: Following Alexander the Great's death in 323 BC, a wave of anti-Macedonian sentiment swept through Athens. As a resident alien with strong Macedonian connections, Aristotle was charged with impiety. To avoid a fate similar to Socrates, he chose to flee to the island of Euboea, famously stating he would not allow Athens "to sin twice against philosophy." He died the following year in 322 BC.

Plato Points Up; Aristotle Gestures Out

  • Raphael's Symbolic Depiction: The profound philosophical difference between Plato and Aristotle was visually captured by Renaissance painter Raphael in his fresco The School of Athens (1509–1511).
    • Plato's Idealism: Plato, depicted as the older figure holding his dialogue Timaeus, points one finger straight up, symbolizing his belief that true reality resides in the invisible, eternal, and unchanging world of the Forms, grasped by the mind alone.
    • Aristotle's Empiricism: Aristotle, younger and holding his Nicomachean Ethics, extends his hand outward, palm down, representing his conviction that reality is found in the visible, particular things of the earth, understood through careful observation and classification.

The Poet and the Biologist: Temperamental Differences

  • Plato, the Poet-Philosopher: Plato's philosophical style was literary and dramatic. He wrote in dialogues, weaving myths and allegories (like the Allegory of the Cave, Chariot, and Divided Line) into his works. He believed ultimate truths were approached through intellectual vision rather than precise articulation, often leaving his prose deliberately ambiguous to encourage readers' critical thought.
  • Aristotle, the Biologist-Philosopher: Aristotle's approach was systematic and analytical. He meticulously classified, categorized, and analyzed data, whether dissecting sea creatures, cataloging political constitutions, or organizing human knowledge into distinct sciences. His surviving works are precise, often dense, and structured like lecture notes, reflecting his empirical and methodological focus.

A Relationship of Respectful Disagreement

  • "Dearer Still is Truth": Despite their intellectual divergence, Aristotle never lost respect for Plato. The famous adage, "Plato is dear to me, but dearer still is truth" (Amicus Plato, sed magis amica veritas), accurately reflects his commitment to rigorous intellectual inquiry, even when it led him away from his teacher's conclusions.
  • Shared Foundations: It's crucial not to overstate their opposition. Both philosophers shared fundamental commitments: they believed philosophy was humanity's highest pursuit, that virtue was essential for a good life, that knowledge was attainable and worthwhile, and that the soul held greater importance than the body. Their disagreements occurred within a common framework of dedication to the philosophical life.

Review Questions

  1. Describe Aristotle's journey to and within Plato's Academy. How long did he stay, and what was his role there?
  2. Explain the key differences in Plato's and Aristotle's philosophical approaches to understanding reality, using Raphael's The School of Athens as a metaphor.
  3. Compare and contrast the temperamental styles of Plato as a "poet-philosopher" and Aristotle as a "biologist-philosopher." Provide examples of how these temperaments manifested in their writing and methods.
  4. Beyond his time at the Academy, what were the major events and achievements in Aristotle's life, including his connection to Alexander the Great and the founding of his own school?
  5. Despite their significant disagreements, what fundamental philosophical ideas did Plato and Aristotle share? How does the quote "Plato is dear to me, but dearer still is truth" encapsulate their complex relationship?
