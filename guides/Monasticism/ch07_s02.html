The Carthusian Path: Guigo II's Ladder of Monks and St. Bruno's Solitary Ideal

Overview

This section explores two foundational texts of the Carthusian tradition: Guigo II's Ladder of Monks and St. Bruno's Letter to Raoul le Verd. Guigo's treatise outlines lectio divina, a four-rung spiritual practice—reading, meditation, prayer, and contemplation—designed to lead the soul from Scripture to God's presence. St. Bruno's letter offers a personal account of the Carthusian commitment to solitude, silence, and manual labor, describing its beauty and the profound spiritual peace it brings.

Key Concepts

  • Lectio Divina: A traditional monastic practice of sacred reading, aiming for spiritual transformation rather than mere information. Guigo II's Ladder of Monks provides the classic articulation of this practice.
  • The Four Rungs of Lectio Divina:
    • Lectio (Reading): Slow, reverent, and attentive engagement with Sacred Scripture, like placing food in the mouth.
    • Meditatio (Meditation): Active, intellectual engagement with the text, chewing on its meaning, seeking understanding, and sparking desire, like teeth chewing food.
    • Oratio (Prayer): Spontaneous, heartfelt speaking to God, fueled by the desires kindled in meditation, like the savour rising from food.
    • Contemplatio (Contemplation): A divinely given grace of direct, experiential knowledge of God's presence, surpassing intellect, like the nourishment of digested food.
  • Interdependence of the Rungs: Guigo emphasizes that the four stages are inextricably linked; one cannot truly progress without engaging all of them.
  • Carthusian Solitude: A core tenet of the Carthusian order, involving monks living largely apart in individual cells (small houses) for prayer, reading, writing, and manual labor, with strict silence.
  • The "Desert" Ideal: In monastic terms, a remote wilderness or secluded place, offering an environment conducive to spiritual focus, far from worldly distractions.
  • Silence as a Spiritual Vessel: Carthusian silence is not empty but a deliberate practice to create space for profound prayer and inner listening.
  • Self-Sufficiency: Monastic communities like the Carthusians often maintained gardens, orchards, and pastures to support themselves, integrating manual labor into spiritual life.

Important Terms

  • Scala Claustralium: Latin for "The Ladder of Cloistered Monks," the title of Guigo II's influential treatise. It evokes Jacob's ladder from Genesis.
  • Lectio Divina: Latin for "sacred reading," a slow, prayerful engagement with Scripture aimed at spiritual transformation.
  • Lectio: Latin for "reading," specifically the slow, attentive reading of sacred texts in a monastic context.
  • Meditatio: Latin for "meditation," involving the active intellectual engagement with and internal pondering of what has been read.
  • Oratio: Latin for "prayer," referring to heartfelt, spontaneous conversation with God arising from meditation.
  • Contemplatio: Latin for "contemplation," a grace-filled, direct, and experiential knowledge of God's presence that transcends intellectual effort.
  • Cell (Carthusian usage): Not merely a small room, but a monk's private dwelling, typically a small house with its own garden, workshop, and covered walkway.
  • Sacred Offices / Divine Office: The canonical hours of daily liturgical prayer (e.g., Matins, Lauds, Vespers), chanted in common by Carthusians only on Sundays and great feasts.
  • Desert (monastic meaning): A remote, wild, or secluded place chosen for spiritual retreat and solitude, not necessarily a sandy wasteland.
  • Bridegroom: A spiritual metaphor for Christ, often used in monastic theology (e.g., from the Song of Songs) to describe the soul's intimate relationship with God.

Detailed Notes

Guigo II, The Ladder of Monks (Scala Claustralium)

Guigo II, the ninth prior of the Grande Chartreuse around 1150, authored The Ladder of Monks, a concise yet profoundly influential guide to prayer in the Western tradition. His work outlines a four-step spiritual ascent, collectively known as lectio divina, which helps the soul move from engaging with the written word of Scripture to experiencing the living presence of God. Guigo employs the metaphor of a ladder, recalling Jacob's ladder, to illustrate this spiritual journey from earth to heaven.

The Four Rungs of the Ladder:

  1. Lectio (Reading): This is the first step, characterized by slow, careful, and attentive reading of Sacred Scripture. It's not a hurried scan but a reverent encounter, likened to placing a sweet grape or food in the mouth. The monk dwells on each word, taking time to savor it. An example given is pausing on "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God," recognizing it as something profound to consider. This initial engagement prepares the soul for deeper understanding.

  2. Meditatio (Meditation): Following reading, meditation involves actively "chewing" on what has been read. It's an intellectual engagement where the monk turns the text over in their mind, breaks it open, and searches for its deeper meaning. Like teeth chewing food, meditation aims to extract the essence. Questions arise: "What does it mean to be pure in heart?" "How is the heart purified?" Through this mental exploration, considering examples of saints and one's own conscience, a strong desire for the purity and divine vision described in the text is kindled. This desire is crucial, acting as "the hinge upon which the whole ladder turns," propelling the soul upwards.

  3. Oratio (Prayer): The intense desire sparked by meditation naturally leads to prayer. The soul moves from intellectual reflection about God to direct, heartfelt speaking to God. This is not formal liturgical prayer but spontaneous cries from the heart, expressing longing and need: "Lord, when shall I find this purity?" Guigo compares prayer to the savor or taste that rises from food being chewed—the broken-open text now releases its fragrance to God. Prayer is an act of sincere longing, begging God to grant what reading and meditation have revealed as necessary. Sometimes, by God's free gift, this prayer is answered, and the soul is prepared for the highest rung.

  4. Contemplatio (Contemplation): This final rung is described as God's own gift, a grace rather than something achieved by human effort. Contemplation is a direct, experiential knowledge of God's presence, an illumination of the mind and a setting aflame of the heart that transcends intellectual understanding. It's like the nourishment the body receives from fully digested food, the sweetness passing into the soul's very substance. While profound, this state is brief due to human weakness; the soul eventually descends, returning to the earlier rungs, enriched by the experience and hungering for more.

Guigo concludes by emphasizing the interconnectedness of these four rungs. Separating them renders them less effective: reading without meditation is dry, meditation without prayer is fruitless, and prayer without meditation lacks substance. True progress involves ascending the ladder step by step, recognizing that "the last can never—or hardly ever—be reached without the first."

St. Bruno, Letter to Raoul le Verd

St. Bruno of Cologne, the founder of the Carthusian order, left very few writings. His Letter to Raoul le Verd, written late in his life from Calabria (c. 1100), offers a rare and personal glimpse into the Carthusian way of life and its spiritual benefits. Bruno wrote to his friend, a fellow canon, who was considering monastic life, hoping to inspire him by describing the joys of solitude.

The Carthusian Environment:

Bruno describes the "desert" (meaning a remote wilderness, like the steep Alpine valley of the Grande Chartreuse) where he and his brethren dwell. He paints a vivid picture of its natural beauty: healthful air, pleasant plains, green meadows, mountains, shaded valleys, abundant rivers, streams, and springs. He also notes the presence of gardens, orchards, and fruit-bearing trees, highlighting the self-sustaining nature of monastic communities. While acknowledging the refreshing quality of these natural goods for the "weary spirit," Bruno quickly shifts to emphasize that the true "pleasures of the man of God" are far greater and more worthy, found in spiritual discipline rather than material beauty.

The Carthusian Manner of Life:

The core of the Carthusian life, as described by Bruno, is radical solitude and disciplined living:

  • Individual Cells: Each monk lives in his own "cell," which is more accurately a small house equipped with a garden, workshop, and covered walkway. Monks spend the vast majority of their lives within this private enclosure.
  • Daily Activities: Within their cells, monks engage in prayer, reading, writing, and manual labor (each according to his ability). This private work is central to their spiritual discipline.
  • Communal Gatherings: Community life is limited. Monks come together in the church only on Sundays and feast days for the "sacred offices" (the canonical hours of the Divine Office). On these occasions, they also eat together and enjoy brief conversation, "rejoicing in one another's company."
  • Strict Silence: A defining characteristic of Carthusian life is profound silence. Outside of the designated communal times, "silence reigneth among us." This silence is not emptiness but a conscious choice to create a profound space for prayer and spiritual concentration.

The Benefits of Solitude:

Bruno powerfully articulates the spiritual fruits of this solitary existence:

  • Peace and Joy: He states that only those who have "tasted it can know" the immense peace and joy this solitude brings.
  • Unhindered Spiritual Work: In solitude, a man is free to grieve for his sins, weep for the sins of the world, and dedicate himself wholly to prayer and the study of holy things, without any "tumult of worldly cares to distract him."
  • Intimate Connection with God: The solitude allows the "soul watcheth with the Bridegroom" (Christ), leading to an "inward gladness which no speech can utter." The "eye of the heart is purified," enabling the soul to perceive God more directly.

Bruno's letter serves as a testament to the Carthusian conviction that profound solitude and disciplined interior life are direct paths to an intimate and transformative experience of God.

Review Questions

  1. Guigo II compares the four rungs of lectio divina to the process of eating and digesting food. Explain each comparison and how it illustrates the spiritual purpose of that rung.
  2. What is the significance of "desire" in Guigo's Ladder of Monks? How does it serve as a "hinge" between different stages of the spiritual journey?
  3. Compare and contrast the meaning of "prayer" (oratio) as described by Guigo with more formal, liturgical prayer. How does Guigo's understanding contribute to the personal spiritual growth of the monk?
  4. Based on St. Bruno's letter, what are the key elements of the Carthusian daily life and environment? How do these elements contribute to the overall spiritual goals of the order?
  5. How do the teachings of Guigo II and St. Bruno complement each other? Discuss how Guigo's structured approach to lectio divina might be practiced within the solitary and silent lifestyle described by Bruno.
