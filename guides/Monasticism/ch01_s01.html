Why Monasticism? A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the profound motivation behind monasticism, a way of life chosen by individuals who interpret Christ's call to "sell what thou hast" literally. It defines monasticism as a radical dedication to God through prayer, work, and community, built upon the three Evangelical Counsels of poverty, chastity, and obedience. The text highlights how this rigorous spiritual discipline, though distinct, complements the universal call to holiness for all Christians.

Key Concepts

  • Radical Following of Christ: Monasticism stems from a literal and absolute interpretation of Gospel passages (e.g., Matthew 19:21, Luke 14:33) that call for renouncing all possessions and comforts to follow Christ entirely. Monks and nuns perceive this as an unrefusable invitation from God.
  • Universal Call to Holiness vs. Consecrated Life: While the Catholic Church teaches that all Christians are called to holiness in various vocations (marriage, family, work), some receive a specific, radical calling to "leave everything behind." This is the consecrated life, with monasticism being its oldest and most enduring form.
  • The Evangelical Counsels: These are three core promises—poverty, chastity, and obedience—freely chosen by monks and nuns. They are recommendations from Christ in the Gospels, distinct from commandments binding on all Christians, and serve as the foundation of monastic commitment.
  • Purpose of the Counsels: Each counsel directly counters a fundamental human desire: poverty renounces possessions, chastity renounces pleasure, and obedience renounces power/autonomy. By surrendering these worldly values, the monk creates space for God alone to be their ultimate value and security.
  • Monastic Way of Life: Monasticism is a comprehensive daily rhythm of prayer, manual work, sacred study, and silence. This structured routine is seen as a form of "spiritual training" or "athletic discipline of the soul," aimed at achieving freedom from disordered desires and self-tyranny, ultimately leading to freedom for God.
  • Contemptus Mundi: This Latin phrase, meaning "contempt of the world," describes the monk's attitude. It is not hatred of creation, but rather a deliberate refusal to allow worldly things to become idols or distractions that displace God from the central position in one's life.

Important Terms

  • Monk: From the Greek monachos, meaning "one who lives alone" or "solitary." A man who withdraws from ordinary society to devote himself entirely to God through prayer, work, and community in a monastic setting.
  • Nun: The feminine equivalent of a monk; a woman who dedicates her life to God in a monastic or religious community. The term "sister" is sometimes used for women in active (non-cloistered) religious communities.
  • Monasticism: From the Greek monazein, "to live alone." In practice, it refers to any form of consecrated religious life where men or women withdraw from ordinary society to devote themselves entirely to God through prayer, work, and community.
  • Consecrated Life: A state of life in the Catholic Church where a person makes public vows (or similar commitments) of poverty, chastity, and obedience, dedicating their entire life to God. Monasticism is its oldest form.
  • Evangelical Counsels: From evangelium ("gospel"). These are counsels (recommendations) given by Christ in the Gospels, freely chosen by those called to a more radical following of Christ, distinct from commandments binding on all Christians. The three counsels are poverty, chastity, and obedience.
  • Poverty (Evangelical Counsel): The promise to give up personal property and possessions, holding all things in common with the community, and depending on God rather than wealth for security.
  • Chastity (Evangelical Counsel): The promise to renounce marriage and family life, dedicating one's heart entirely to God with an undivided love.
  • Obedience (Evangelical Counsel): The promise to submit one's own will to the authority of a superior (abbot or abbess) and to the rule of the community. It is often considered the most difficult counsel.
  • Abbot: From the Aramaic abba, meaning "father." The abbot is the elected head of a monastery, who governs the community according to its monastic rule.
  • Cell: The monk's private room, typically small and sparsely furnished. From the Latin cella, meaning "a small room or storeroom."
  • Habit: The distinctive clothing worn by members of a religious order, serving as a visible sign of their consecration to God. From the Latin habitus, meaning "condition" or "dress."
  • Tonsure: The ritual shaving of part or all of the head as a sign of religious dedication, symbolizing the crown of thorns in the Western tradition and marking a departure from the world.
  • Cloister: From the Latin claustrum, "an enclosed place." It refers both to the covered walkway surrounding a monastery's central courtyard and, more broadly, to the entire enclosed world of the monastery, separated from secular life.
  • Contemptus Mundi: A Latin phrase meaning "contempt of the world." In monasticism, it signifies a refusal to let worldly things (possessions, pleasures, power) become idols that displace God from the center of one's life, rather than a hatred of creation itself.

Detailed Notes

The Radical Call to Follow Christ

The section begins by highlighting a profound challenge found in Matthew 19:21: "If thou wilt be perfect, go sell what thou hast, and give to the poor... and come follow me." While most people admire such words from a distance, monks and nuns have historically taken this passage literally. They commit to radical poverty, intense prayer, and profound silence, leaving behind possessions, families, careers, and worldly comforts.

  • The term monk comes from the Greek monachos ("one who lives alone"), and nun is its feminine equivalent.
  • Monasticism, from the Greek monazein ("to live alone"), describes a form of consecrated religious life where individuals withdraw from ordinary society to devote themselves entirely to God through prayer, work, and community.
  • The fundamental "why" for this choice is simple: "because God asked." This reflects a literal interpretation of Christ's demanding invitation, as seen in Luke 14:33: "Every one of you that doth not renounce all that he possesseth, cannot be my disciple."

Monasticism Within the Broader Christian Context

It is crucial to understand that monks do not believe ordinary Christians are less faithful. The Catholic Church affirms the universal call to holiness, teaching that there are many paths to sanctity, including marriage, family life, honest work, and service to others. This was affirmed by the Second Vatican Council's document Lumen Gentium (1964).

However, the Church also recognizes that some Christians receive a specific, more radical calling to leave everything behind. This is known as the consecrated life, and monasticism represents its oldest and most enduring expression.

The Evangelical Counsels: Pillars of Monastic Life

At the core of monastic life are three promises known as the evangelical counsels, derived from Christ's recommendations in the Gospels:

  1. Poverty:

    • Practice: Monks give up personal property and possessions, holding everything in common with their community. They rely on God for their security, mirroring the early Church in Acts 2:44 ("All that believed were together, and had all things common").
    • Purpose: This counsel renounces the fundamental human desire for possessions and wealth, fostering dependence on God.

  2. Chastity:

    • Practice: Monks renounce marriage and family life, dedicating their entire heart to God. This is a choice to love God with an undivided heart (1 Cor. 7:32), not a rejection of marriage itself as inherently bad.
    • Purpose: This counsel renounces the fundamental human desire for pleasure and intimate human relationships, redirecting all love towards God.

  3. Obedience:

    • Practice: Monks submit their own will and judgment to the authority of a superior (an abbot or abbess) and to the established rule of their community.
    • Purpose: Often considered the most difficult counsel, it requires surrendering one's desire for power and autonomy. By giving up self-will, the monk makes space for God's will to be paramount in their life.

These three counsels are not arbitrary but are intentionally designed to address fundamental human desires for possessions, pleasure, and power/autonomy. By freely relinquishing what the world often values most, the monk makes room to value God alone above all else.

A Distinctive Way of Life

Monasticism is more than an institution; it's a comprehensive way of life characterized by a daily rhythm of prayer, work, study, and silence.

  • Daily Rhythm: Monks typically rise before dawn for prayer, chant psalms, engage in slow and prayerful Scripture reading, and perform manual labor (such as farming, baking, copying manuscripts, or building). Meals are simple, speech is limited, and sleep occurs in a small, sparsely furnished room called a cell.
  • Spiritual Training: This rigorous life is a form of "spiritual training" or "athletic discipline of the soul." Through daily effort, sacrifice, prayer, fasting, and self-denial, monks train their souls to achieve inner freedom.
  • Goal of Freedom: The ultimate goal is not suffering for its own sake, but freedom: freedom from disordered desires, freedom from the "tyranny of the self," and ultimately, freedom for God.
  • Contemptus Mundi: This Latin phrase, literally "contempt of the world," describes the monk's perspective. It does not mean hating creation but rather refusing to allow worldly things to become idols that displace God from the center of one's existence.
  • Visible Signs: Monks wear a distinctive garment called a habit, a visible sign of their consecration. In some traditions, they receive a tonsure (ritual shaving of the head) to symbolize their leaving the world behind. They live within an enclosed space known as a cloister, which physically and symbolically separates them from the noise and distractions of ordinary life.

Ultimately, monks embrace this challenging life not out of a grim asceticism but out of a profound love for God, believing that this love is "worth everything" and joyfully running towards the God who first loved them.

Review Questions

  1. What is the fundamental motivation for choosing a monastic life, according to the text? How does this spiritual motivation differ from how "most people" interpret the relevant Gospel passages about radical discipleship?
  2. Explain the Catholic Church's teaching on the "universal call to holiness." How does monasticism, as a specific form of the consecrated life, fit within this broader understanding of Christian vocations without diminishing other paths to sanctity?
  3. Describe each of the three Evangelical Counsels (Poverty, Chastity, and Obedience). For each counsel, explain what it entails in practice and which fundamental human desire it addresses. Why is Obedience often considered the most difficult of the three?
  4. How does the daily rhythm of monastic life, encompassing prayer, work, study, and silence, contribute to the monk's "spiritual training"? What is the ultimate goal or "freedom" that this discipline aims to achieve?
  5. The text introduces the Latin phrase contemptus mundi. What does this phrase literally mean, and what is its true, nuanced meaning and purpose within the context of monasticism? How does it relate to the monk's overall orientation towards God?
