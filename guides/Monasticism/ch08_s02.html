Study Guide: Bernard of Clairvaux - On Loving God

Overview

This section introduces Bernard of Clairvaux's profound treatise On Loving God, which explores the fundamental questions of why and how humanity should love God. Bernard argues that God is the sole reason and immeasurable measure of our love, citing His prior love for us and the countless benefits, both physical and spiritual, He bestows. The text then begins to outline Bernard's influential framework of the "Four Degrees of Love," starting with the most basic, self-centered form of love and charting its gradual transformation toward divine love.

Key Concepts

  • God as the Reason and Measure of Love: Bernard asserts that God Himself is the ultimate reason for loving Him, and that the only fitting measure of this love is "immeasurable love." This is rooted in both God's inherent loveliness and the benefits derived from loving Him.
  • God's Prior Love as the Chief Claim: The primary justification for our love of God is that "He first loved us" (1 John 4:19). This love is demonstrated by His self-sacrifice, His love for us even while we were enemies, and the giving of His only-begotten Son.
  • Innumerable Benefits and Gifts: God's claim on our love is also evident in the endless physical blessings (food, light, air) and higher spiritual endowments (dignity, wisdom, virtue) He provides to all humanity.
  • The Three Highest Gifts: Humanity is distinguished by three superior gifts:
    • Dignity (Free-will): Man's unique capacity for dominion and self-determination.
    • Wisdom: The ability to recognize this dignity and understand that it, like all good qualities, originates from God, not from oneself.
    • Virtue: The impulse to seek, find, and cling to God, the Author of all good.
  • Vain-Glory vs. True Glory: Bernard emphasizes that true glory comes from recognizing God as the source of all gifts and giving Him the credit ("Glory in the Lord"), as opposed to "vain-glory," which is taking credit for what one has received.
  • The Four Degrees of Love: This is Bernard's original contribution to mystical theology, outlining a progressive spiritual journey. These are not distinct types of love but stages of growth in the soul's relationship with God.
  • First Degree: Carnal Love (Self-Love): The initial, natural, and necessary form of love where man loves himself for his own sake. While natural, it must be guided and curbed by the commandment to "love thy neighbor as thyself" to prevent it from becoming excessive or purely self-indulgent.

Important Terms

  • Bernard of Clairvaux: A prominent French abbot and a major leader in the Cistercian monastic order, known for his writings on spiritual life and theology.
  • On Loving God (De Diligendo Deo): Bernard's most systematic theological presentation of divine love, addressed to Cardinal Haimeric, explaining why and how God should be loved.
  • Cardinal Haimeric: The Cardinal Chancellor of Rome, to whom Bernard's treatise On Loving God was addressed, having requested Bernard's explanation of divine love.
  • Dignity (as a gift): Refers to humanity's free-will, which elevates man above other earthly creatures and grants him dominion.
  • Wisdom (as a gift): The capacity to perceive one's inherent dignity and understand that it, along with all other good qualities, is a gift from God, not self-acquired.
  • Virtue (as a gift): The spiritual drive that leads man to eagerly seek and steadfastly cling to God, the ultimate Source of his being and blessings.
  • Vain-glory: The false pride of taking credit for gifts or accomplishments that one has received from another (God), rather than acknowledging their true source.
  • Carnal Love: The first of Bernard's four degrees of love; it is natural self-love, where an individual loves himself primarily for his own needs and desires.
  • Four Degrees of Love: Bernard's influential mystical framework that describes the soul's progressive journey from self-love to a transformative, selfless love of God.

Detailed Notes

I. Introduction to Bernard of Clairvaux and On Loving God

  • Bernard of Clairvaux was an exceptionally influential writer and spiritual figure during the Middle Ages.
  • His treatise, On Loving God, written between 1126 and 1141, is considered his most beautiful and systematic exploration of the theology of divine love.
  • It was composed in response to Cardinal Haimeric, who sought an explanation for why and how God should be loved.
  • Bernard's approach is experiential rather than purely philosophical, focusing on God's goodness as the foundation for love.

II. Why We Should Love God and the Measure of That Love

  • The Core Thesis: God is the reason for loving God, and the measure of love due to Him is "immeasurable love."
  • Twofold Reason for Loving God:
    1. What is lovely in God? His inherent nature and actions.
    2. What shall I gain by loving God? The ultimate benefit is God Himself.
  • God's Primary Claim: His Pre-existing Love for Humanity:
    • He first loved us (1 John 4:19): This is God's chiefest title to our love.
    • Who loved: The holy, supreme, omnipotent God, whose love "seeketh not her own."
    • Whom He loved: Unworthy wretches and enemies (Romans 5:10).
    • How much He loved: He gave Himself, His only-begotten Son for our everlasting life (John 3:16); He spared not His own Son (Romans 8:32); the Son laid down His life for His friends (John 15:13).
    • Application to Angels: God preserved angels from the need for redemption, demonstrating His love for them in a different manner than for fallen humanity.
  • God's Innumerable Benefits (General Providence):
    • Even for unbelievers, God's benevolence is evident in the physical world.
    • Essential bodily gifts: Food, sunlight, air – necessary for life.
    • Man's "Higher Nature" - The Highest Gifts:
      1. Dignity (Free-will):
        • Man excels all other earthly creatures and has dominion over them.
        • Reflects man being made in God's image (Genesis 1:26).
      2. Wisdom:
        • The power to recognize this dignity and understand that it is not an accomplishment of one's own, but a gift.
        • God "teacheth man knowledge" (Psalm 94:10).
      3. Virtue:
        • Impels man to seek eagerly for God, his Source, and to cling to Him once found.
        • God is the "Lord of virtue."
  • The Interdependence of the Highest Gifts and the Avoidance of Vain-Glory:
    • Dignity without Wisdom is useless: There's no glory in having a gift without knowing it.
    • Wisdom without Virtue is harmful: Knowing you have a gift but believing it's of yourself leads to vain-glory, not true glory in God (1 Cor. 4:7).
      • True glory is "glorying in the Lord" (1 Cor. 1:31), for "our Lord is Truth" (John 14:6).
      • Man, ignorant of his true internal glory, becomes captive to curiosity and sensual external things, resembling irrational beasts.
    • Virtue as the Proper Fruit: It leads one to call upon and laud God, confessing "Not unto us, O Lord, not unto us, but unto Thy name give glory" (Psalm 115:1).
      • Failing to put knowledge to good effect (knowing what is right but not doing it) leads to punishment (Luke 12:47).
      • When one possesses virtue, wisdom and dignity are blessed, not dangerous.
  • Conclusion: The Universal Obligation to Love God:
    • Even those who do not know Christ are sufficiently instructed by natural law and their innate endowments of soul and body.
    • Every infidel knows they receive light, air, food, humanity's eminence, and knowledge from God.
    • Therefore, anyone who knows himself is bound to love God for God's own sake and is "unpardonable" if he fails to love the Lord his God with all his heart, soul, and mind.

III. The Four Degrees of Love (Introduction and First Degree)

  • Bernard's Original Contribution: This framework is a cornerstone of Catholic mystical theology, detailing the soul's gradual journey toward God.
  • Not Separate Types, but Stages: Love transforms and matures, rather than being replaced.
  • Love as a Natural Affection: It is natural, and thus it is right to love the Author of nature first, as per the first great commandment.
  • The First Degree: Carnal Love (Man loves himself for his own sake):
    • Natural Necessity: Due to human frailty, nature compels us to love ourselves first (1 Cor. 15:46, Eph. 5:29).
    • Potential for Excess: This self-love can become immoderate, overflowing into "voluptuousness" or selfish pleasure.
    • The Curb of Temperance: The commandment "Thou shalt love thy neighbor as thyself" acts as a "dike" to restrain excessive self-love.
    • Implication: If one finds it burdensome to minister to a neighbor's needs or pleasures, they should mortify those same affections in themselves. One can cherish oneself, but only if they extend the same indulgence to their neighbor. This prevents self-destruction through uncontrolled passions.

Review Questions

  1. How does Bernard argue that God is both the "reason" and the "measure" of our love, and what specific examples does he use to support this claim?
  2. Explain the "three best gifts" (dignity, wisdom, and virtue) that Bernard identifies in humanity. How does he illustrate their interdependence, and what happens when they are not properly aligned?
  3. What is the distinction Bernard makes between "vain-glory" and "true glory"? Why is it crucial to understand the source of our gifts, according to his argument?
  4. According to Bernard, why are even those who do not know Christ still "unpardonable" if they fail to love God? What evidence does he present for this assertion?
  5. Describe Bernard's First Degree of Love, "carnal love" or self-love. How does he view its natural necessity, its potential pitfalls, and the role of the commandment "Thou shalt love thy neighbor as thyself" in regulating it?
