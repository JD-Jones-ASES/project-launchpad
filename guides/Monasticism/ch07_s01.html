The Carthusians: St. Bruno and the Ladder of Monks Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the emergence of the Carthusian order in the late eleventh century, a period marked by significant monastic and ecclesiastical reform. It details the life and motivations of St. Bruno of Cologne, the founding of the remote Grande Chartreuse hermitage, and the unique, austere lifestyle adopted by the Carthusian monks. The section also covers the codification of their customs by Guigo I and the influential spiritual treatise on lectio divina by Guigo II, highlighting the order's remarkable and unbroken tradition of stability and fidelity to its original ideals.

Key Concepts

  • Monastic Reform in the 11th Century: The success and splendor of the Cluniac order led some monks to seek a more austere, simple, and silent life, reminiscent of the Desert Fathers. This impulse aligned with broader Church reforms, such as the Gregorian Reform, aimed at freeing the Church from secular control and restoring clerical discipline.
  • St. Bruno's Call to Simplicity: Troubled by corruption within the institutional Church, particularly simony, St. Bruno renounced a prestigious academic career to pursue a life of profound prayer, solitude, and austerity in a remote hermitage.
  • The Carthusian Way of Life: The Carthusians pioneered a unique synthesis of eremitical (hermit-like) solitude and cenobitic (communal) stability. Monks largely lived alone in their cells, dedicated to prayer, reading, and manual labor, but gathered for specific communal prayers, fostering deep personal spirituality within a structured community.
  • Austerity and Silence: Carthusian life was characterized by extreme austerity, including minimal meals, strict fasting, perpetual abstinence from meat, and almost constant silence. This demanding lifestyle was intentionally chosen to foster spiritual focus and detachment from worldly distractions.
  • Oral Tradition and Codification: St. Bruno did not write a formal rule; the Carthusian way was initially transmitted through the living example of the first community. Later, Guigo I formalized these customs in the Consuetudines Cartusiae, providing a detailed portrait of their established life.
  • The Ladder of Monks (Scala Claustralium): Guigo II's influential treatise laid out the four stages of lectio divina (reading, meditation, prayer, contemplation), providing a foundational framework for spiritual growth through engagement with Scripture, which remains central to Catholic spirituality.
  • Enduring Stability: The Carthusian order is remarkable for its continuous fidelity to its original form, famously expressed by the motto "Never reformed, because never deformed." This stability is attributed to its extreme austerity, which attracts only the most dedicated, and its deliberate remoteness, which minimizes external influences.

Important Terms

  • Cluniac Reform: A movement originating from the Abbey of Cluny in the 10th and 11th centuries, which reformed monasticism by emphasizing elaborate liturgy, centralized authority, and independence from secular lords. Its success, however, eventually led some to seek greater simplicity.
  • Desert Fathers: Early Christian hermits, ascetics, and monks who lived in the deserts of Egypt and Syria, renowned for their pursuit of spiritual purity through solitude, fasting, and prayer. They served as an inspiration for later monastic reforms.
  • Pope Gregory VII (c. 1015–1085): A pivotal pope whose Gregorian Reform sought to assert papal authority, free the Church from secular control (e.g., lay investiture), and restore clerical discipline.
  • St. Bruno of Cologne (c. 1030–1101): Founder of the Carthusian order, a renowned scholar and teacher who abandoned a prestigious career to seek a life of eremitical austerity.
  • Reims Cathedral School: One of the most important cathedral schools in 11th-century Europe, where Bruno was educated and later served as headmaster. These schools were precursors to medieval universities.
  • Manasses de Gournay: The corrupt and simoniacal Archbishop of Reims who Bruno opposed, leading to his disillusionment with the institutional Church.
  • Simony: The buying or selling of spiritual things, especially Church offices or sacraments, considered a grave sin. Named after Simon Magus (Acts 8:18–20).
  • St. Hugh of Grenoble (1053–1132): Bishop of Grenoble, a close friend and protector of Bruno who led him and his companions to the Chartreuse valley.
  • Cartusia (Chartreuse): The Latin name for the remote, desolate valley in the French Alps where St. Bruno founded the first Carthusian hermitage. The order takes its name from this location.
  • Grande Chartreuse: The original and mother house of the Carthusian order, located in the French Alps.
  • Carthusian Cell: A small, self-contained dwelling for a Carthusian monk, typically including a workroom, bedroom, small garden, and covered walkway. Monks spend most of their lives here in solitude.
  • Matins: The first and longest of the canonical hours, traditionally prayed in the middle of the night (around 2:00 a.m.) by Carthusian monks communally in church.
  • Prior: The head of a Carthusian monastery (charterhouse). Carthusian superiors are called priors, not abbots, reflecting their emphasis on humility and service rather than grandeur.
  • Pope Urban II (c. 1042–1099): A former student of St. Bruno at Reims, he later summoned Bruno to Rome as an advisor and is famous for calling the First Crusade.
  • Guigo I (1083–1136): The fifth prior of the Grande Chartreuse, who, around 1127, composed the Consuetudines Cartusiae, formalizing the Carthusian customs and way of life.
  • Consuetudines Cartusiae ("Customs of the Chartreuse"): A detailed description of the daily routine, liturgical practices, and way of life of the early Carthusian monks, serving as the foundation for their future legislation.
  • Spatiamentum: The weekly communal walk taken by Carthusian monks, during which they are permitted to converse freely. It is one of the few breaks in their habitual silence.
  • Guigo II (d. c. 1188): A later prior of the Grande Chartreuse who wrote Scala Claustralium (The Ladder of Monks), an influential treatise on lectio divina.
  • Scala Claustralium ("The Ladder of Cloistered Monks"): Also known as The Ladder of Monks or The Ladder of Four Rungs, this short treatise by Guigo II describes the four stages of prayerful engagement with Scripture: lectio, meditatio, oratio, and contemplatio.
  • Lectio Divina ("Sacred Reading"): A slow, prayerful method of reading Scripture, central to monastic tradition, involving the four stages described by Guigo II.
  • Cartusia numquam reformata quia numquam deformata: The unofficial motto of the Carthusian order: "The Carthusian order was never reformed because it was never deformed," signifying its remarkable fidelity to its original way of life.
  • Stat crux dum volvitur orbis: The official motto of the Carthusian order: "The cross stands while the world turns," expressing their conviction in the unchanging nature of Christ's cross amidst a changing world.

Detailed Notes

The Monastic Landscape in the Late 11th Century

  • Cluniac Dominance and Discontent: By the late 11th century, the Cluniac reform had profoundly transformed European monasticism, leading to hundreds of flourishing monasteries with magnificent liturgies and substantial wealth. However, this success also generated concern among some monks who felt the splendor and elaborate rituals had drawn them away from the simplicity and silence valued by the Desert Fathers.
  • An Age of Reform: The 11th century was broadly an era of reform within the Western Church.
    • Gregorian Reform: Pope Gregory VII championed reforms to free the Church from secular control and restore clerical discipline.
    • New Religious Movements: Across Europe, new movements emerged, all seeking a return to Gospel simplicity and early monastic austerity. The Carthusians were among the most radical and enduring of these.

St. Bruno of Cologne: Scholar to Hermit

  • Early Life and Education: Bruno was born around 1030 in Cologne, Germany. He received a top-tier education at the cathedral school of Reims, one of Europe's finest, and quickly became a celebrated teacher of theology and philosophy. By the 1050s, he was head of the Reims cathedral school, a position of significant influence.
  • Disillusionment and Call: Despite a promising career, Bruno was deeply troubled by the corruption prevalent in the Church.
    • He opposed Manasses de Gournay, the Archbishop of Reims, who had obtained his office through simony (bribery). This opposition forced Bruno and others to flee Reims.
    • In this context of disillusionment, Bruno heard a profound call to a different life.
  • Founding the Grande Chartreuse (1084):
    • Around 1084, Bruno and six companions sought out St. Hugh of Grenoble, the local bishop, requesting a remote place for hermitage, devoted to prayer and silence.
    • Hugh led them to the desolate Cartusia (Chartreuse) valley, high in the French Alps (nearly 4,000 feet altitude).
    • Here, amidst towering peaks and dense forests, they built their first hermitage.

The Grande Chartreuse: A Radical Experiment

  • Remote and Austere Location: Bruno deliberately chose an inhospitable, remote site in the mountains north of Grenoble, accessible only by a narrow, winding road. The harsh climate, poor land, and immense solitude were precisely what he desired for a life of spiritual focus.
  • The First Carthusian Community:
    • The community was small: Bruno and six companions.
    • Solitude and Communal Life: A new, yet ancient, form of religious life emerged. Each monk lived in his own small, self-contained cell (a dwelling with a workroom, bedroom, garden, and walkway), spending most of his time alone in prayer, reading, and manual labor.
    • Limited Communal Gatherings: Monks gathered in the church only for certain communal prayers, notably Matins (prayed around 2:00 a.m.) and Vespers. For the rest of the day and night, they were alone with God.
    • Prior vs. Abbot: The community was governed by a prior, whose authority was less intrusive than an abbot's in a Benedictine house, reflecting the Carthusian emphasis on profound personal solitude within a framework of communal obedience.

Bruno in Calabria and the Unwritten Rule

  • Summoned by Pope Urban II: In 1090, Bruno's former student, Pope Urban II, called him to Rome as an advisor. Bruno obeyed, leaving the Grande Chartreuse in the care of his companions.
  • Declining Honors: Bruno declined the Pope's offer of the archbishopric of Reggio Calabria, demonstrating his continued commitment to a simple, contemplative life.
  • Second Hermitage: He never returned to the Alps but founded a second hermitage at La Torre in Calabria, Southern Italy, where he died in 1101.
  • Tradition, Not Rule: Bruno wrote very little (only two letters and a profession of faith survive). He left no formal written rule for his order. The Carthusian way of life was transmitted through the living example of the first community – a tradition of silence, solitude, and prayer passed down from monk to monk.

Guigo I and the Consuetudines Cartusiae

  • Formalizing the Customs: It was the fifth prior of the Grande Chartreuse, Guigo I (1109-1136), who gave the order its first formal structure. Around 1127, he composed the Consuetudines Cartusiae ("Customs of the Chartreuse").
  • A Detailed Portrait: This document was not a new rule, but a careful description of the life Bruno and his companions had already lived for forty years, detailing their established practices.
  • Extraordinary Austerity: The Consuetudines describe a life of intense austerity:
    • Diet: One meal a day (two on Sundays/feast days), frequent fasting, and perpetual abstinence from meat (a practice maintained to this day).
    • Clothing: Coarse wool habits, barefoot within the cloister.
    • Silence: Almost perpetual silence, speaking only when absolutely necessary.
    • Spatiamentum: The only significant social contact was the weekly communal walk (spatiamentum), during which monks were permitted to talk freely.

Guigo II and The Ladder of Monks

  • Influential Spiritual Work: A later prior, Guigo II (d. c. 1188), authored one of Christianity's most important short spiritual treatises: Scala Claustralium ("The Ladder of Cloistered Monks"), also known as The Ladder of Monks.
  • Lectio Divina: This brief work (approx. 3,000 words) describes the four stages by which a monk ascends from reading Scripture to contemplation. These four "rungs" – lectio (reading), meditatio (meditation), oratio (prayer), and contemplatio (contemplation) – became the classic framework for lectio divina ("sacred reading"), a practice central to Catholic spiritual life.

"Never Reformed, Because Never Deformed"

  • Unique Stability: The Carthusians hold a unique position in monastic history: they have never undergone a major reform. Unlike other orders that eventually required renewal (e.g., Benedictines needing Cluny, Cluny needing Cistercians), the Carthusians have maintained their original way of life with remarkable fidelity for over nine centuries.
  • The Unofficial Motto: This astonishing consistency is encapsulated in their unofficial motto: Cartusia numquam reformata quia numquam deformata – "The Carthusian order was never reformed because it was never deformed."
  • The Official Motto: Their official motto, Stat crux dum volvitur orbis – "The cross stands while the world turns" – reflects their conviction that amidst a changing world, their devotion to the cross of Christ remains fixed.
  • Factors for Consistency:
    • Extreme Austerity: The demanding nature of Carthusian life naturally attracts only the most serious and dedicated candidates, ensuring a consistent commitment to their ideals.
    • Remote Location: Their deliberate isolation minimizes external influences and temptations, helping to preserve their unique charism.

Review Questions

  1. How did the perceived "dangers" of Cluniac success contribute to the emergence of new monastic movements like the Carthusians in the late 11th century?
  2. Describe the personal and ecclesiastical context that led St. Bruno of Cologne to abandon a prestigious career and found the Carthusian order. What specific issues troubled him?
  3. Explain the unique synthesis of eremitical and cenobitic life found in the early Carthusian community at the Grande Chartreuse. How did their daily routine balance solitude with communal aspects?
  4. Compare and contrast the role of a "rule" in other monastic orders (like the Rule of Saint Benedict) with the way the Carthusian way of life was initially transmitted and later formalized by Guigo I.
  5. What factors are cited as reasons for the Carthusian order's extraordinary stability and fidelity to its original ideals, as expressed by the motto "Never reformed, because never deformed"?
