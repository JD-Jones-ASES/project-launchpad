Chapter 9, Section 2: A Living Tradition – Monasticism in the Modern World

Overview

This section highlights that monasticism is a vibrant, ongoing tradition, not a relic of the past. It showcases Our Lady of Clear Creek Abbey in Oklahoma as a prime example of Benedictine life thriving today, building a monastery stone by stone to embody ancient ideals. The section also surveys other diverse modern monastic communities around the world, emphasizing their continued devotion to prayer, manual labor, and traditional practices.

Key Concepts

  • Monasticism as a Living Tradition: The monastic way of life, from its ancient origins with the Desert Fathers to figures like Saint Benedict, continues to be practiced by thousands of men and women globally in active communities.
  • Clear Creek Abbey as a Modern Example: Our Lady of Clear Creek Abbey in Oklahoma exemplifies the Benedictine ideal in the 21st century through its full observance of the Rule of Saint Benedict, commitment to traditional Romanesque architecture, and a long-term vision.
  • The Solesmes Congregation and Gregorian Chant: Many Benedictine monasteries, including Clear Creek's mother house, belong to the Solesmes congregation, known for its deep devotion to Gregorian chant and the traditional Latin liturgy.
  • The Opus Dei (Work of God): Communal chanting of the Divine Office, central to Benedictine life, is performed multiple times a day and night, reflecting Saint Benedict's priority for liturgical prayer.
  • Benedictine Vow of Stabilitas Embodied: The act of building a monastery in stone, designed to last for centuries, physically manifests the Benedictine vow of stability and permanence in a transient world.
  • Diversity of Modern Monastic Communities: Beyond Clear Creek, monasteries like Solesmes, Norcia, Pluscarden, the Trappists, and numerous women's orders demonstrate the varied and widespread nature of contemporary monasticism across different traditions and continents.
  • Integration of Prayer and Manual Labor: Modern monastic communities continue the ancient practice of balancing intense prayer with physical work, whether farming, brewing, building, or producing goods to support themselves.
  • Contemplative Life's Value: Enclosed women's communities, despite their "hiddenness," are recognized by the Church as profoundly sustaining the entire Body of Christ through their prayer.

Important Terms

  • Divine Office: The public prayer of the Church, recited at various canonical hours throughout the day and night by religious communities and clergy. Also known as the Liturgy of the Hours.
  • Gregorian Chant: A body of monophonic (single-line melody), unaccompanied sacred Latin song of the Roman Catholic Church, historically used in the Divine Office and Mass.
  • Solesmes Congregation: A network of Benedictine monasteries established by Dom Guéranger in 1833, known for its revival and devotion to Gregorian chant and traditional Latin liturgy.
  • Opus Dei: Latin for "the work of God," Saint Benedict's term for the communal chanting of the Divine Office, which he considered the monk's highest priority.
  • Romanesque: An architectural style prevalent in Western Europe from roughly the 10th to the 12th century, characterized by rounded arches, thick walls, massive towers, and a sense of permanence.
  • Stabilitas: The Benedictine vow of stability, committing a monk or nun to a particular monastic community for life, symbolizing spiritual steadfastness and rootedness.
  • Trappists (Order of Cistercians of the Strict Observance): A contemplative Roman Catholic religious order of monks and nuns, forming a branch of the Cistercian order, known for rigorous silence, manual labor, and asceticism.
  • Discalced Carmelites: A contemplative Roman Catholic religious order of friars and nuns, founded by Saint Teresa of Ávila, known for strict enclosure, prayer, and devotion to Marian spirituality.
  • Poor Clares: A contemplative Roman Catholic religious order for women in the Franciscan tradition, founded by Saint Clare of Assisi, characterized by evangelical poverty, prayer, and enclosure.
  • Valliscaulian Monks: A small, short-lived French monastic order founded in the 12th century, related to the Carthusians, which followed a rigorous contemplative life.

Detailed Notes

Monasticism: A Continuous Journey

Monasticism is not merely a historical subject; it is a vibrant, evolving tradition. Today, thousands of men and women worldwide embrace the monastic life, dedicating themselves to prayer, manual labor, and the pursuit of God within the cloister. This continuity connects them directly to the earliest monastic figures like the Desert Fathers, Cassian, Benedict, and later movements such as the Cistercians and Carthusians. Monastic communities actively welcome new vocations, ensuring the tradition's ongoing vitality.

Our Lady of Clear Creek Abbey: A Modern Benedictine Ideal

Located in the Ozark hills of eastern Oklahoma, Our Lady of Clear Creek Abbey stands as a powerful testament to living monastic tradition.

  • Founding and Heritage: Established in 1999 by a group of monks from the Abbey of Notre-Dame de Fontgombault in France, Clear Creek draws its spiritual lineage from the Solesmes congregation. This tradition emphasizes liturgical beauty, the full observance of the Rule of Saint Benedict, and the preservation of Gregorian chant. The founding monks settled in Oklahoma at the invitation of the local bishop.
  • Daily Life and Opus Dei: The monks adhere strictly to the Rule of Saint Benedict. Their day is meticulously structured around the Opus Dei ("the work of God"), which involves the communal chanting of the Divine Office in Latin with traditional Gregorian melodies. This sacred prayer occurs seven times throughout the day and once during the night, signifying its utmost priority in their lives.
  • Manual Labor and Self-Sufficiency: Between their hours of prayer, the monks engage in various forms of manual labor. This includes farming the land, tending to cattle, constructing their monastery buildings, and producing goods necessary for the community's sustenance. Their motto, "Building Something Beautiful For God — To Last A Thousand Years," encapsulates their long-term vision and commitment.
  • Architecture as an Expression of Faith: A particularly striking aspect of Clear Creek is its physical monastery. The monks are meticulously constructing a grand stone church in the Romanesque architectural style, utilizing traditional methods and local stone. This choice of architecture, known for its thick walls, rounded arches, and sense of permanence, directly reflects the Benedictine vow of stabilitas (stability). In an era of disposable goods and quick results, their multi-century building project is a tangible symbol of enduring faith and commitment.
  • Embodiment of Benedictine Ideals: Clear Creek Abbey embodies key Benedictine ideals: rootedness in one place, devotion to God's praise through beautiful liturgy, self-sufficiency through manual labor, and patient, faithful building for future generations. It serves as compelling evidence that the Rule of Saint Benedict, formulated fifteen centuries ago, remains a relevant and fruitful path for modern spiritual life.

Other Thriving Modern Communities

Clear Creek is one of many thriving monastic centers worldwide:

  • Solesmes Abbey (France): As the mother house of the Solesmes congregation, this monastery remains the global authority on Gregorian chant. Its monks are responsible for producing the official editions of chant used by the Catholic Church and have popularized its beauty through their recordings.
  • Norcia (Italy): In 2000, American Benedictine monks founded a monastery in Saint Benedict's birthplace. The Monks of Norcia gained international recognition for their traditional Benedictine observance, their craft brewing, and their resilience in rebuilding after severe earthquake damage in 2016.
  • Pluscarden Abbey (Scotland): Founded in 1230 by Valliscaulian monks and later restored to Benedictine use in 1948, Pluscarden is the United Kingdom's most northerly monastery. It stands as one of the rare medieval British monasteries that continues to serve its original purpose.
  • The Trappists (Order of Cistercians of the Strict Observance): This branch of the Cistercian order maintains numerous monasteries globally, including notable houses like Gethsemani (Kentucky) and Spencer (Massachusetts). Trappist communities are characterized by rigorous silence, profound commitment to manual labor, and the production of high-quality goods such as beer, cheese, and preserves, often bearing the "Authentic Trappist Product" label.
  • Women's Communities: Contemplative women's orders also flourish across the continents. Examples include the Discalced Carmelites (founded by Saint Teresa of Ávila), the Poor Clares (founded by Saint Clare of Assisi), and various Benedictine nuns. These communities live lives of deep enclosure and profound hiddenness, yet the Church teaches that their continuous prayer sustains the entire Body of Christ (as per Perfectae Caritatis 7).

Experiencing Monastic Life

For those curious about monastic life, many monasteries offer guest facilities for short retreats. Spending time in a monastery—sharing in the monks' or nuns' prayer, silence, and simple meals—provides an invaluable firsthand understanding of the traditions studied in this book. Most Benedictine and Trappist communities maintain guest houses and welcome visitors of all ages and backgrounds.
