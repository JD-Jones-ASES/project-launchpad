Chapter 8, Section 3 Study Guide: Bernard's Theology of Love and Mysticism

Overview

This section delves into the core of Bernard of Clairvaux's spiritual theology, focusing on his "Four Degrees of Love" as a path for the soul's growth toward God. It also explores his unique development of "bridal mysticism," which describes the intimate relationship between the soul and God using the imagery of the Song of Songs. Finally, the section highlights Bernard's emphasis on experiential knowledge of God over mere intellect, and how this conviction shaped Cistercian spirituality and contrasted with the more ornate Cluniac tradition.

Key Concepts

  • The Four Degrees of Love: This is Bernard's foundational framework describing the soul's progressive journey towards God. It begins with natural self-love and moves through loving God for what He provides, to loving God purely for who He is, and finally to loving oneself entirely for God's sake. Bernard emphasizes that this is a dynamic, continuous process rather than a linear ladder, reflecting constant spiritual striving.
  • Bridal Mysticism: Bernard's distinctive theological contribution, which uses the imagery of marriage and romantic love, drawn primarily from the Old Testament's Song of Songs, to depict the profound, passionate, and intimate union between the individual soul and God (or Christ). It suggests that God desires not just obedience, but the heart of humanity.
  • Amor ipse intellectus est ("Love itself is a form of knowing"): This Latin phrase encapsulates Bernard's conviction that true and deep knowledge of God is primarily gained through direct, lived experience of love, rather than through intellectual reasoning alone. While not rejecting intellect, Bernard insisted that love leads to a more profound understanding of divine truths.
  • Cistercian Simplicity: A theological principle manifested in the architectural and daily life practices of the Cistercian order, especially articulated in Bernard's Apologia to Abbot William. It advocates for austerity, unadorned beauty, and a lack of excessive ornamentation in monasteries and churches, believing that such simplicity helps focus the soul inward towards God, free from worldly distractions.

Important Terms

  • The Four Degrees of Love: Saint Bernard of Clairvaux's influential model outlining the spiritual progression of human love, from natural self-love to a selfless, God-centered love.
  • Bridal Mysticism: A tradition within Christian theology and spirituality that employs the metaphor of marriage and romantic love (particularly inspired by the Song of Songs) to symbolize the soul's intense and intimate relationship with God.
  • Song of Songs (Canticle of Canticles): An Old Testament book interpreted allegorically by Bernard and many within the Catholic tradition as representing the loving dialogue between God (the Bridegroom) and the soul or the Church (the Bride).
  • Amor ipse intellectus est: A Latin phrase meaning "Love itself is a form of knowing," coined by Bernard of Clairvaux to express his belief that the deepest understanding of God comes through the experience of love rather than solely through intellectual reasoning.
  • Apologia to Abbot William (Apologia ad Guillelmum): A powerful treatise written by Bernard of Clairvaux around 1125, in which he criticizes the elaborate and often opulent practices and architectural ornamentation of Cluniac monasteries, advocating instead for the Cistercian ideal of simplicity.
  • Doctor of the Church: A prestigious title conferred by the Catholic Church on certain saints whose theological writings and teachings are recognized as having made an exceptional and enduring contribution to Christian doctrine and spiritual life. Bernard was declared a Doctor of the Church in 1830.
  • Order of Cistercians of the Strict Observance (Trappists): A reformed branch of the Cistercian order, established in the 17th century, known for its rigorous adherence to the Rule of Saint Benedict, emphasizing a life of prayer, manual labor, and silence, thus continuing Bernard's foundational Cistercian spiritual legacy.

Detailed Notes

The Four Degrees of Love: A Path to God

Bernard of Clairvaux’s spiritual theology centers on the soul’s journey towards God, articulated through his "Four Degrees of Love." This framework describes a natural, yet transformative, progression:

  1. Love of self for self's sake: This is the starting point for all humanity. It's a natural, God-given inclination to seek basic needs like food, comfort, and safety. Bernard views this not as sinful, but as a primal, self-centered form of love not yet consciously directed towards God.
  2. Love of God for self's sake: As humans encounter suffering or need, they begin to turn to God for assistance. This stage represents an initial engagement with God, where love is still primarily motivated by what God can provide or do for the individual. It's a genuine beginning of faith, but still centered on the self's needs.
  3. Love of God for God's sake: Through continued experience of God’s goodness and grace, the soul gradually realizes that God is inherently worthy of love, not just for His gifts, but for His very being – His goodness, beauty, and truth. This marks a profound shift from self-centeredness to God-centeredness, where the soul loves God simply because He is God.
  4. Love of self for God's sake: This is the highest and rarest degree, rarely fully attained in earthly life. Here, the soul loves itself, and indeed everything, only within God and for God's glory. The self is not annihilated but completely absorbed and transformed by divine love, much like a drop of water dissolving into wine, taking on its character. In this state, the human will becomes perfectly united with God's will.

Bernard understood this journey to be challenging and dynamic. It is not a fixed ladder to be climbed once, but a continuous process of advancing, falling back, and pressing forward, with the ultimate direction of the soul's desire towards God being paramount.

The Soul as Bride: Bernard's Mystical Theology

Bernard's most distinctive theological contribution is his bridal mysticism, which draws heavily on the imagery of the Song of Songs. He preached eighty-six sermons on this Old Testament love poem, interpreting every verse as an allegory for the intimate and passionate relationship between God and the soul.

For Bernard, the bride's longing cry ("My beloved to me, and I to him") embodies the soul's desire for Christ, and the bridegroom's praise ("Thou art all fair, O my love") represents Christ's transforming grace bestowed upon the soul. This imagery, though perhaps startling to modern sensibilities, conveys a profound truth: the relationship between God and humanity transcends mere obedience or even friendship; it is a deep, personal, and transforming love. Bernard, like other mystics, emphasized that God desires not just our adherence to rules, but our very hearts.

Experience and Theology: "Amor Ipse Intellectus Est"

A defining characteristic of Bernard's theology is his insistence on its roots in experience. For him, knowing God was not merely an intellectual exercise but a direct encounter, a "tasting and seeing that the Lord is good."

This conviction is captured in the famous Cistercian phrase: Amor ipse intellectus est – "Love itself is a form of knowing." Bernard believed that the deepest truths about God are revealed through the experience of love, which can go beyond what pure reason can grasp. While a highly learned man himself, he argued that the goal of theological study was not simply to know about God, but to know God intimately. He maintained that a monk praying with a fervent heart gains an insight into God that an intellectual philosopher, relying solely on logic, cannot achieve. This emphasis on lived spiritual experience, often described using sensory language (tasting, feeling, touching God's presence), became a hallmark of the entire Cistercian tradition.

Bernard Versus Cluny: Simplicity and Splendor

Bernard's commitment to simplicity, central to Cistercian spirituality, found its most famous expression in his Apologia to Abbot William (c. 1125). In this treatise, Bernard sharply criticized the opulent and elaborate decorations prevalent in Cluniac monasteries, which contrasted with the Cistercian vow of strict poverty.

Bernard's argument was not just aesthetic; it was profoundly theological. He questioned why monks, who had left the world for simplicity, would surround themselves with excessive gold, intricate sculptures of fantastical beasts, and other visual distractions. Such ornamentation, he argued, diverted the soul's focus from inward prayer and God. Cistercian monasteries, in stark contrast, embraced radical architectural simplicity. Their beauty derived from pure proportion, light, and unadorned stone, creating spaces that spoke of silence, eternity, and a direct path to God unburdened by material splendor. It's crucial to note that Bernard did not condemn Cluniac practices for all, but deemed them inappropriate for monks committed to the strictest poverty, representing different, yet legitimate, monastic approaches.

The Catholic Context

Bernard of Clairvaux's profound theology of love, his bridal mysticism, and his emphasis on experiential knowledge have had an immense and lasting impact on the Catholic mystical tradition. His ideas have influenced countless mystics and theologians, including Bonaventure, Teresa of Ávila, John of the Cross, and Thérèse of Lisieux.

Recognized as a Doctor of the Church in 1830, Bernard's writings continue to be studied and prayed with today as living sources of spiritual wisdom. His legacy also lives on through the Cistercian order, particularly the Order of Cistercians of the Strict Observance (Trappists), who maintain the core Cistercian traditions of prayer, work, and silence in monasteries around the globe, striving to live the essential character of the monastic life Bernard championed.

Review Questions

  1. Describe each of Bernard's Four Degrees of Love, outlining how the focus of love shifts through each stage. Why does Bernard consider this journey to be dynamic rather than a fixed, once-and-for-all ascent?
  2. What is "bridal mysticism," and how did Bernard use the imagery of the Song of Songs to convey his understanding of the soul's relationship with God? What "deep truth" does this intense imagery reveal about God's desire for humanity?
  3. Explain the meaning and significance of Bernard's phrase "Amor ipse intellectus est." How does this concept challenge or complement purely intellectual approaches to knowing God, and how did it influence Cistercian spirituality?
  4. In his Apologia to Abbot William, Bernard criticized the Cluniac monasteries. What specific aspects did he criticize, and what theological principles underpinned the Cistercian commitment to architectural simplicity as a response?
  5. Beyond his specific theological contributions, how has Bernard of Clairvaux's legacy continued to shape the Catholic Church and monastic life, even centuries after his death? Give examples of his ongoing influence.
