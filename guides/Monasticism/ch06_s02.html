The Benedictine Centuries: Cluny and Reform Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces the foundational aspects of the Abbey of Cluny, a pivotal institution in medieval monasticism. It examines the 910 AD Foundation Charter issued by Duke William of Aquitaine, highlighting his personal motivations and the revolutionary freedoms granted to the monks, particularly their independence from secular and episcopal control. The section also delves into the distinct and elaborate Cluniac liturgy, known for its extensive communal prayer and emphasis on intercession for the dead.

Key Concepts

  • Cluniac Reform: A significant movement in the 10th and 11th centuries aimed at reforming monasticism by establishing monasteries free from lay and episcopal interference, allowing them to adhere strictly to the Rule of Saint Benedict and foster spiritual renewal.
  • Foundation Charter of Cluny (910 AD): The legal document issued by Duke William I of Aquitaine that established the Abbey of Cluny, crucially granting it unprecedented freedoms, most notably the right of the monks to elect their own abbot.
  • Monastic Independence: The core principle of Cluny, allowing the monastery to operate autonomously, free from the political and financial control of local lords or bishops, which was a common problem for many monasteries of the era.
  • Canonical Election of Abbot: The right of the monks to freely elect their spiritual leader according to monastic rules and the will of God, without external pressure. This was a radical departure from the common practice of lay or episcopal appointments.
  • Direct Papal Protection: Cluny's allegiance to and protection by the Roman papacy, symbolized by a token annual payment. This direct link bypassed local hierarchies and further secured Cluny's independence.
  • Cluniac Liturgy (Opus Dei): The distinctive and highly elaborate form of communal worship practiced at Cluny. It significantly expanded upon the basic prescriptions of the Rule of Saint Benedict, emphasizing splendor, duration, and extensive ritual.
  • Prayer for the Dead: A central feature of Cluniac spirituality and liturgy, involving extensive commemorations, suffrages, and the daily chanting of psalms for deceased benefactors and all souls. This practice contributed to the institution of the Feast of All Souls.
  • Cluny III: The third and largest abbey church built at Cluny, begun in 1088. Its immense size and architectural grandeur reflected the wealth, influence, and liturgical emphasis of the Cluniac order.
  • Cistercian Critique: Later criticisms from monastic reformers like Saint Bernard of Clairvaux and the Cistercians, who argued that Cluny's elaborate liturgy and wealthy ornamentation deviated from Benedict's original vision of simplicity, manual labor, and austerity.

Important Terms

  • Cenobitic Community: A monastic community where monks live together under a common rule and an abbot, as prescribed by Saint Benedict.
  • Regular Monastery: A monastery governed by a regula (rule), such as the Rule of Saint Benedict, which dictates the daily life and spiritual practices of the monks.
  • Canonical Election: The process by which a religious community (like monks electing an abbot) chooses its leader in accordance with canon law and without undue external interference.
  • Opus Dei: Latin for "the work of God," referring to the communal prayer of the Divine Office, which Saint Benedict deemed the most important activity of a monk's day.
  • Canonical Hours: The eight prayer offices (Matins, Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, Compline) that structure the monastic day around communal prayer, derived from ancient Jewish and early Christian prayer practices.
  • Psalter: The Book of Psalms, comprising 150 psalms. In monastic life, the psalter is chanted as part of the Divine Office, often over a weekly or even daily cycle.
  • Suffrages: Intercessory prayers offered on behalf of the dead, rooted in the Catholic belief that the prayers of the living can assist souls in purgatory.
  • Ordo Cluniacensis: The Cluniac customary, or book of customs, which meticulously described how each liturgical celebration was to be performed within the Cluniac order, including specific rubrics and gestures.
  • Lay Interference: The control or influence exerted by secular lords, nobles, or non-clerical individuals over church appointments, property, or affairs.
  • Episcopal Interference: The control or influence exerted by local bishops over monasteries, often regarding the appointment of abbots or the administration of monastic property.

Detailed Notes

The Foundation of Cluny and Its Charter (910 AD)

The Abbey of Cluny was founded in 910 AD by William I, Duke of Aquitaine, through a pivotal document known as the Foundation Charter of Cluny. This charter is exceptional not only for its legal provisions but also for the profound personal motivations articulated by William.

William I's Motivations and the Donation

  • Personal Salvation: William expresses a deep concern for his own salvation and a desire to use his "transitory possessions" to "merit everlasting rewards." He hopes that by supporting a "congregation of monks" (a cenobitic community), he might, through their righteousness, receive a share of the reward of the righteous, even if he himself cannot fully "despise all things." This reflects a medieval understanding of merit and the intercessory power of monastic prayer.
  • Lasting Purpose: William intends his wealth to serve a purpose that extends beyond his death, ensuring a continuous stream of prayers for his soul.
  • The Gift: He hands over his rule to the holy apostles Peter and Paul, specifically donating the town of Cluny, along with its court, demesne manor, the Church of St. Mary and St. Peter, and all associated lands, vills, chapels, serfs, vines, fields, meadows, woods, waters, mills, incomes, and revenues. This comprehensive donation aimed to make the monastery self-sufficient.

Revolutionary Freedoms and Provisions

The charter outlines critical freedoms that distinguished Cluny and became the bedrock of its influence:

  • Adherence to the Rule of Saint Benedict: The monastery was to be "regular," meaning it would be governed strictly by the Rule of Saint Benedict, focusing on communal living and spiritual discipline.
  • Right to Elect Their Own Abbot: This was the most revolutionary provision. After the death of the first abbot, Berno, the monks were granted "power and permission to elect any one of their order whom they please as abbot and rector," ensuring a "purely canonical election" free from any "intervention of our own nor of any other power." This removed the monastery from the common practice of lay lords or bishops appointing abbots for political or personal gain.
  • Freedom from External Interference: The charter explicitly protected Cluny from interference by local lords (lay interference) and bishops (episcopal interference), granting it a unique degree of autonomy.
  • Direct Papal Allegiance and Protection: Every five years, the monks were to pay a token sum of ten shillings to the church of the apostles at Rome for lights. This symbolic payment placed Cluny directly "under the protection of those same apostles and the defence of the Roman pontiff." This direct allegiance to the papacy, rather than local secular or ecclesiastical authorities, was crucial for Cluny's independence and its ability to spearhead broader monastic reform.
  • Sanctions Against Violators: The charter includes a severe curse against anyone—"whether he be a neighbour or a stranger, no matter what his condition or power"—who attempts to violate the terms of the gift, calling for divine wrath and eternal damnation.

Significance of the Charter

The Foundation Charter of Cluny was a landmark document. It created a monastery that was exceptionally independent, spiritually focused, and directly linked to Rome. This independence allowed Cluny to attract the most devout monks, grow immensely, and eventually become the head of a vast network of reformed monasteries, playing a central role in the spiritual and political life of the Middle Ages.

Think Deeper: Motivation and Salvation

The question of whether William's motivation was selfish ("salvation of my soul") or genuinely generous is complex. In the medieval Catholic understanding, acts of charity and piety (like founding a monastery) were seen as means to accumulate "merit" and facilitate one's salvation through God's grace. This perspective views personal salvation not as a purely selfish act, but as participation in a spiritual economy where good works, prayer, and intercession (especially by monks) could genuinely benefit the giver. Thus, an act motivated by concern for one's soul could simultaneously be a profound act of generosity that served God and the community.

The Cluniac Liturgy

The monks of Cluny were renowned for their liturgical worship, which was far more elaborate and extensive than what was originally prescribed by the Rule of Saint Benedict. This focus on the "Work of God" (Opus Dei) became a defining characteristic of Cluniac life.

Expansion of the Opus Dei

  • Beyond Benedict's Rule: While Saint Benedict outlined eight canonical hours of communal prayer, the Cluniacs significantly expanded these services. They added numerous supplementary psalms, litanies, processions, and ceremonies.
  • Time Commitment: By the 11th century, Cluniac monks spent an astonishing six to eight hours daily in choir, dedicated to singing psalms, hymns, and prayers.
  • Daily Psalter Chanting: Unlike Benedict's arrangement of chanting the entire psalter over a week, the Cluniacs introduced the practice of chanting many psalms multiple times each day, sometimes even the entire psalter daily.

Centrality of Prayer for the Dead

  • Extensive Commemorations: Special prayers for the dead became a hallmark of Cluniac worship. The monks maintained lengthy lists of deceased benefactors and others, offering daily elaborate commemorations and suffrages on their behalf. This practice underscored the belief that the prayers of the living could aid souls in purgatory.
  • Feast of All Souls: Abbot Odilo of Cluny is credited with instituting the Feast of All Souls (November 2) around 998 AD, a feast still celebrated by the universal Church today, further highlighting Cluny's influence on liturgical practice.

The Ordo Cluniacensis and Liturgical Grandeur

  • Detailed Customary: The Ordo Cluniacensis, or Cluniac customary, was a detailed book of customs that prescribed the exact performance of every liturgical celebration, down to the minutiae of monks' postures and gestures. This ensured uniformity and solemnity across the vast Cluniac network.
  • Physical Setting: The architectural grandeur of Cluny's churches, especially Cluny III, matched the splendor of its liturgy. Cluny III, completed in the early 12th century, was the largest church in Christendom for centuries (187 meters long), with five aisles, double transepts, and soaring barrel vaults. This majestic space was designed to elevate the spiritual experience of worship.

Criticism and Lasting Impact

  • Cistercian Critique: Not all admired Cluny's liturgical expansion. Reformers like Saint Bernard of Clairvaux and the Cistercians (to be discussed in Chapter 8) criticized the Cluniacs for what they perceived as excessive ornamentation, wealth, and the inordinate length of their services. They argued that these practices diverted monks from Benedict's original emphasis on simplicity, manual labor, and private contemplation.
  • Legacy: Despite criticisms, Cluny's liturgical tradition profoundly influenced medieval spiritual life, emphasizing the beauty and power of communal prayer and shaping the development of Catholic liturgy and devotional practices for centuries.

Review Questions

  1. What were Duke William of Aquitaine's primary motivations for founding the Abbey of Cluny, as expressed in the Foundation Charter? How do these motivations reflect medieval beliefs about wealth, salvation, and the role of monasticism?
  2. Explain the significance of the specific freedoms granted to the monks in the Foundation Charter, particularly the right to elect their own abbot and the direct allegiance to the papacy. How did these provisions contribute to Cluny's unique position and influence?
  3. Describe the key characteristics of the Cluniac liturgy. In what ways did it expand upon or differ from the liturgical practices prescribed in the original Rule of Saint Benedict?
  4. Discuss the importance of "prayer for the dead" in Cluniac worship. How did this practice become a central feature of Cluniac spirituality, and what lasting impact did it have on the wider Catholic Church?
  5. The section mentions criticisms leveled against Cluny by later monastic reformers like the Cistercians. What were these criticisms, and what do they reveal about the ongoing tensions and different ideals within Western monasticism regarding simplicity, labor, and elaborate worship?
