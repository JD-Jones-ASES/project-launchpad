Thomas Merton: A Modern Monk Study Guide

Overview

This section introduces Thomas Merton, one of the most significant figures in bringing monasticism into modern consciousness. It traces his extraordinary life from a restless youth and profound conversion to his impactful life as a Trappist monk, influential writer, advocate for peace, and pioneer in interfaith dialogue. Merton's work emphasized the universal human need for contemplation and the pursuit of one's "true self" in a noisy modern world.

Key Concepts

  • Thomas Merton's Influence: Merton (1915–1968) was instrumental in making monastic spirituality accessible and relevant to the modern world through his prolific writing and personal journey.
  • Twentieth-Century Conversion: His autobiography, The Seven Storey Mountain, details a profound spiritual journey from a secular, restless youth to a committed Catholic monk, mirroring ancient conversion narratives.
  • Contemplation as a Human Need: Merton argued that contemplation is not just for monks but a fundamental human necessity, crucial for navigating the spiritual crisis caused by modern life's constant distraction and noise.
  • Monasticism for Clarity: He believed the monastic life offers a path to strip away societal illusions, discover one's "true self," and see the world more clearly, rather than serving as an escape from it.
  • Social Justice and Interfaith Dialogue: As he matured, Merton expanded his focus to advocate for peace, nonviolence, and racial justice, believing a contemplative stance fueled solidarity. He also engaged in deep dialogue with Eastern spiritual traditions like Zen Buddhism, Taoism, and Sufism.
  • Enduring Legacy: Merton demonstrated that monastic practices like silence, solitude, lectio divina, and the Divine Office are vital for addressing urgent human needs, impacting not just the Church but a global audience of seekers.

Important Terms

  • Thomas Merton (1915–1968): An American Trappist monk, priest, writer, mystic, and social activist, whose works bridged monasticism with the modern world.
  • Trappists (Order of Cistercians of the Strict Observance, O.C.S.O.): A reform branch of the Cistercian order known for its emphasis on silence, manual labor, and strict adherence to the Rule of Saint Benedict. Merton belonged to this order.
  • Abbey of Gethsemani: A Trappist monastery in Kentucky, United States, where Thomas Merton lived from 1941 until his death in 1968.
  • The Seven Storey Mountain: Merton's acclaimed autobiography (1948), detailing his conversion to Catholicism and entry into monastic life, which became a spiritual bestseller. Its title alludes to Dante's Purgatory, symbolizing his journey of purgation.
  • Divine Office: The public prayer of the Church, consisting of psalms, readings, and prayers, chanted or recited at various times throughout the day and night in monastic communities.
  • Novitiate: The period of training and probation, typically one to two years, that a person undergoes before making religious vows in a monastic order. The novice lives by the Rule and discerns their calling.
  • Contemplation: A deep, prolonged, prayerful consideration or meditation on spiritual truths or God, often involving silence and interiority. Merton saw it as a fundamental human need.
  • True Self: A concept central to Merton's spirituality, referring to the authentic person God created each individual to be, often obscured by societal expectations and false identities.
  • Dorothy Day (1897–1980): American Catholic social activist and co-founder of the Catholic Worker movement, who corresponded with Merton and shared his commitment to peace and social justice.
  • Thich Nhat Hanh (1926–2022): Vietnamese Buddhist monk, Zen master, and peace activist, known for his teachings on mindfulness and engaged Buddhism, with whom Merton dialogued.
  • Lectio Divina: A traditional monastic practice of prayerful reading of Scripture, involving reading, meditation, prayer, and contemplation.

Detailed Notes

Thomas Merton: A Modern Monk

  • Unparalleled Influence: Thomas Merton stands as the most influential figure in introducing monasticism to the modern world.
  • Multifaceted Identity: He was a convert to Catholicism, a prolific writer, a deep contemplative, and a prophetic voice in his time.
  • A Modern Conversion Story: His life echoes the great conversion narratives of Christian history, such as those of Saint Anthony and Saint Bernard.

Biographical Sketch: Thomas Merton (1915–1968)

  • Early Life and Orphanhood: Born in France to artist parents (American mother, New Zealand father). Orphaned by age sixteen, leading to a restless youth across France, England, and the U.S.
  • Education and Conversion: Studied at Columbia University in New York. Converted to Catholicism in 1938.
  • Monastic Vocation: Entered the Abbey of Gethsemani (a Trappist monastery in Kentucky) in December 1941 at age twenty-six. He remained there until his death.
  • Priesthood and Writings: Ordained a priest in 1949, taking the religious name Father Louis. He authored over sixty books on spirituality, contemplation, social justice, and interfaith dialogue.

A Twentieth-Century Conversion

  • The Seven Storey Mountain: His 1948 autobiography became one of the most widely read spiritual books of the 20th century, describing his journey of purgation.
  • Candid Self-Reflection: Merton honestly depicted his early life: a childhood marked by loss, a youth of aimless pleasure and intellectual pride, and an increasing spiritual hunger.
  • Path to Catholicism: His conversion was gradual, influenced by medieval philosophers, mystics, and a pivotal visit to Gethsemani during Holy Week 1941.
  • "Coming Home": He described feeling profoundly at home upon hearing the monks chant the Divine Office at Gethsemani.
  • Entering Monastic Life: Within months, he renounced possessions and entered the novitiate.

The Contemplative Life in the Modern World

  • Universal Need for Contemplation: Merton's key insight was that contemplation is essential for all humans, not just monastics.
  • Modern Spiritual Crisis: He identified the modern world's noise, speed, and distractions as causes of a spiritual crisis, diminishing people's capacity for silence and interiority.
  • Monasticism as Clarity, Not Escape: He argued that monastic life isn't an escape but a means to perceive the world with greater clarity.
  • Discovering the "True Self": Through solitude and silence, monks (and all people) can shed societal illusions and discover their authentic self, created by God.
  • Broad Appeal: Merton's message resonated widely with monks, laypeople, and non-Christians alike who sought a deeper life.
  • "Think Deeper" Insight: Merton believed being "fully awake to reality" is the essence of the contemplative life, suggesting that contemplation is possible in everyday activities by bringing deep attention to them.

Merton's Wider Influence

  • Advocacy for Peace and Nonviolence: In the 1960s, Merton became a significant voice for peace, corresponding with figures like Dorothy Day, Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., and Thich Nhat Hanh.
  • Contemplation and Solidarity: He passionately opposed nuclear weapons and racial injustice, asserting that monastic silence was a profound form of solidarity with global suffering, not indifference.
  • Interfaith Dialogue: He extensively explored Eastern contemplative traditions (Zen Buddhism, Taoism, Sufism) without compromising his Catholic faith, believing such dialogue deepened one's own spiritual understanding.
  • Tragic End in Asia: He died accidentally by electrocution in Bangkok, Thailand, in December 1968, while attending a monastic conference that brought together Christian and Buddhist monastics. This happened exactly 27 years after he entered Gethsemani.

Merton's Legacy for the Church

  • Modern Relevance of Monasticism: Merton effectively demonstrated that monastic life is not an outdated practice but remains relevant for modern spiritual needs.
  • Timeless Practices: He showed that ancient monastic practices like silence, solitude, lectio divina, and the Divine Office address fundamental human desires for meaning and connection, which are only amplified by modern life's busyness.
  • Papal Recognition: Pope Francis, in 2015, praised Merton to the U.S. Congress, alongside figures like Abraham Lincoln and Martin Luther King Jr., as "a man of dialogue, a promoter of peace between peoples and religions."
  • Global Impact of the Contemplative Life: This tribute underscores the remarkable influence of a cloistered monk on the world, illustrating the transformative power of contemplative life.

Review Questions

  1. How did Thomas Merton's personal conversion story, as described in The Seven Storey Mountain, set the stage for his later spiritual teachings?
  2. Merton argued that "contemplation is not a luxury for specialists but a fundamental human need." What did he mean by this, and why did he believe it was particularly crucial for the "modern world"?
  3. Explain Merton's perspective that monastic life is "not an escape from the world but a way of seeing the world more clearly." How does this idea connect to his concept of the "true self"?
  4. Discuss how Merton reconciled his commitment to a contemplative, cloistered life with his vocal advocacy for peace, nonviolence, and interfaith dialogue in the 1960s. How did he see these two aspects of his life as interconnected?
  5. What is Thomas Merton's lasting legacy for both the Church and broader society, particularly concerning the perception and practice of monasticism in the modern era?
