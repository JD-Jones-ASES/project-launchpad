Chapter 8, Section 1: The Cistercians: St. Bernard and the White Monks Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the origins and early development of the Cistercian order, a significant monastic reform movement of the 11th and 12th centuries. It details how the Cistercians emerged as a reaction to the perceived wealth and liturgical excesses of the powerful Cluniac order, seeking to return to a stricter, more literal interpretation of Saint Benedict's Rule emphasizing simplicity, manual labor, and balanced spiritual life. Key figures like Robert of Molesme, Stephen Harding, and especially St. Bernard of Clairvaux are highlighted for their roles in shaping the order's unique governance and facilitating its explosive growth across Europe.

Key Concepts

  • The Problem with Cluniac Success: By the late 11th century, the Benedictine abbey of Cluny had become immensely wealthy and influential, with an elaborate liturgy that consumed much of the monks' day. This success, however, led some to question if it truly aligned with Saint Benedict's call for simplicity, manual labor, and a balanced life of prayer and work.
  • Cistercian Reform Movement: Founded at Cîteaux in 1098 by Robert of Molesme, the Cistercians sought to restore the strict observance of the Rule of Saint Benedict, rejecting the traditions and elaborations that had accumulated over centuries, especially in the Cluniac tradition.
  • Cistercian Governance (Charter of Charity): Stephen Harding authored the Carta Caritatis, a revolutionary constitutional document for the order. Unlike Cluny's centralized authority, it established a decentralized system where each abbey was self-governing but accountable through annual visitations by abbots of founding houses and a yearly General Chapter at Cîteaux.
  • The Influence of St. Bernard of Clairvaux: Bernard's arrival at Cîteaux in 1112 with thirty companions sparked the order's explosive growth. He became abbot of Clairvaux and an enormously influential figure in 12th-century Europe, known for his charisma, theological writings, and Marian piety.
  • Cistercian Way of Life: Characterized by stark simplicity, austerity in architecture and art (stripped to bare stone, no ornamentation), a balanced daily routine of prayer, lectio divina, and significant manual labor, and economic self-sufficiency through their own work. They adopted a white habit to signify their commitment to simplicity and distinguish themselves from "Black Monks" (Benedictines like Cluny).
  • Lay Brothers (Conversi): The Cistercians introduced a new class of monks who took vows but focused primarily on physical labor, particularly agriculture. This allowed the choir monks to maintain their rigorous schedule of prayer and study while ensuring the monastery's economic viability and self-sufficiency.
  • Explosive Growth: From a struggling community, the Cistercians grew to hundreds of monasteries across Europe by the mid-12th century, driven by religious fervor, Bernard's charisma, their willingness to settle and cultivate remote lands, and the appeal of their strict, simple lifestyle.

Important Terms

  • Opus Dei: Latin for "work of God"; refers to the communal prayer of the Divine Office, the central liturgical activity in monastic life. The Cluniacs emphasized its splendor and length.
  • Lectio divina: Latin for "sacred reading"; the slow, prayerful reading of Scripture, prescribed by Saint Benedict as a daily practice. Cistercians restored its prominence.
  • Cîteaux (Cistercium): The marshy, desolate site in Burgundy where Robert of Molesme and his monks founded the "new monastery" in 1098, giving the Cistercians their name.
  • White Monks: The nickname for Cistercian monks, derived from their distinctive undyed white or grey wool habits, adopted as a sign of simplicity and to differentiate themselves from the "Black Monks" (traditional Benedictines).
  • Charter of Charity (Carta Caritatis): The constitutional document of the Cistercian order, written by Stephen Harding around 1119. It established the order's unique decentralized governance structure based on mutual visitation and an annual General Chapter.
  • Clairvaux (Clara Vallis): St. Bernard's abbey in the Champagne region of France, founded in 1115. It became the most famous Cistercian monastery and a major center of expansion.
  • Doctor of the Church: A title conferred by the Catholic Church on saints recognized for their outstanding contributions to theology and doctrine. St. Bernard of Clairvaux was declared one in 1830.
  • Vigils (Matins): The first liturgical hour of the Divine Office, typically prayed in the deep hours of the night (e.g., around 2:00 a.m.).
  • Lay brothers (conversi): A class of Cistercian monks who took monastic vows but were not ordained priests and focused primarily on manual labor, especially agricultural work, to support the monastery.

Detailed Notes

The Problem with Success: Cluny's Magnificence

  • Cluniac Achievement: By the late 11th century, Cluny had grown from a single monastery (founded 910) to a network of over a thousand houses across Europe.
  • Wealth and Liturgy: Cluniac abbeys were renowned for their splendor, glittering churches, and an elaborate Opus Dei (Divine Office) that occupied most of the monks' waking hours.
  • Spiritual Crisis: Reformers questioned if this wealth and expanded liturgy aligned with Saint Benedict's Rule, which called for simplicity, manual labor, and a balanced life of prayer and work (including lectio divina).
  • Deviation from the Rule: At Cluny, the extensive liturgy left little time for manual labor or sacred reading, and monastic life became comfortable, adorned with elaborate decoration. This was seen as a "softening of the monastic spirit," trading Benedict's simplicity for "Cluniac magnificence."
  • Renunciation Challenged: The core monastic ideal of renouncing worldly comforts to seek God alone seemed compromised when monasteries became some of the richest and most splendid institutions.

A New Monastery: The Founding of Cîteaux

  • Foundation (1098): Robert of Molesme, abbot of Molesme, led about twenty monks to Cîteaux, a desolate, marshy site in Burgundy.
  • Purpose: To found a novum monasterium (new monastery) dedicated to following the Rule of Saint Benedict exactly as written, without the traditional additions and elaborations common in other Benedictine houses, especially Cluny.
  • Early Leadership:
    • Robert of Molesme: Recalled to Molesme by papal order shortly after the foundation.
    • Alberic (d. 1109): Second abbot, organized the community, obtained papal approval, and introduced the distinctive white habit, leading to the nickname "White Monks."
    • Stephen Harding (d. 1134): Third abbot, an Englishman with exceptional organizational skills. He authored the Charter of Charity.

The Charter of Charity: Revolutionary Governance

  • Constitutional Document: Written around 1119, the Carta Caritatis became the foundational constitution of the Cistercian order.
  • Decentralization vs. Centralization: In contrast to Cluny's model where the abbot of the mother house held centralized authority over all daughter houses, the Charter established a network of independent but mutually accountable monasteries.
  • System of Governance:
    • Each abbey was self-governing.
    • Each abbey was subject to annual visitation by the abbot of its founding (mother) house.
    • All abbots gathered annually at Cîteaux for a General Chapter to address common concerns and maintain discipline.
  • Balance: This system balanced autonomy with accountability, and freedom with order, a truly revolutionary form of governance for its time.

St. Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153)

  • Background: Born into a noble Burgundian family, Bernard was brilliant, ambitious, and deeply devout.
  • Entry to Cîteaux (1112): At age twenty-two, Bernard entered Cîteaux with approximately thirty companions, including four of his brothers. This influx saved the struggling community.
  • Founding Clairvaux (1115): Within three years, Bernard was sent to found a new monastery at Clairvaux ("Bright Valley") in the Champagne region, where he served as abbot for 38 years.
  • Immense Influence: From Clairvaux, Bernard became one of the most influential churchmen of his age: an adviser to popes, a preacher of crusades, a theological controversialist, and a mystic whose writings on the love of God are renowned.
  • Doctor of the Church: Declared a Doctor of the Church in 1830 by Pope Pius VIII, known as the "Doctor Mellifluus" ("honey-sweet Doctor") for his beautiful and warm theological prose. His Marian piety was a hallmark of Cistercian spirituality.

Explosive Growth

  • Turning Point: Bernard's arrival in 1112 marked the beginning of rapid expansion.
  • Scale: From 1112 to Bernard's death in 1153, the Cistercian order grew to 339 monasteries across Europe. By 1200, it exceeded 500.
  • Factors Contributing to Growth:
    • Religious Fervor and Reform: The 12th century was a time of spiritual renewal, and the Cistercian vision of returning to Benedict's original simplicity resonated deeply.
    • Bernard's Charisma: His personal fame and persuasive power attracted hundreds of men.
    • Settlement in Remote Areas: Cistercians willingly settled in uncultivated, wild areas, allowing for continuous expansion.
    • Agricultural Innovation: They drained swamps, cleared forests, and transformed wilderness into productive farmland, becoming leading agricultural innovators.

The Cistercian Way of Life

  • Rejection of Ornamentation: In stark contrast to Cluniac magnificence, Cistercian churches were stripped to bare stone, beautiful in their simplicity but devoid of sculpture, painting, or precious metals. Early Cistercian manuscripts also avoided elaborate illumination.
  • Balanced Routine: Restored Benedict's prescribed balance of prayer, work, and reading.
    • Rose before dawn for Vigils (night office).
    • Chanted seven daytime hours of the Divine Office.
    • Engaged in lectio divina.
    • Devoted substantial hours to physical work in fields, workshops, and the scriptorium.
  • Self-Sufficiency: Insisted on supporting themselves through their own manual labor, rather than relying on income from estates and tithes.
  • Austerity: Simple diet (no meat, plain bread, vegetables, water/thin beer). Strict silence observed most of the day, with hand signals for communication.
  • Lay Brothers (Conversi): Introduced a class of monks who took vows but focused primarily on physical labor (farming, craftsmanship). This division of labor allowed choir monks to maintain their prayer/study schedule while ensuring the monastery's economic self-sufficiency.

Other Key Figures

  • Aelred of Rievaulx (1110–1167): Abbot of the English Cistercian abbey of Rievaulx. Wrote On Spiritual Friendship, adapting classical ideas of friendship to the Christian monastic context, arguing that true friendship rooted in Christ enhances monastic life.
  • Hildegard of Bingen (1098–1179): A contemporary Benedictine abbess, mystic, composer, scientist, and theologian. Though not Cistercian, she represents the broader spirit of 12th-century monastic renewal and was declared a Doctor of the Church in 2012.

Review Questions

  1. What spiritual and practical concerns did some monks have regarding the success and splendor of the Cluniac order by the end of the 11th century? How did these concerns motivate the founding of Cîteaux?
  2. Describe the key innovations in monastic governance introduced by Stephen Harding's Charter of Charity. How did this system differ fundamentally from the centralized authority model of Cluny?
  3. Discuss the multiple factors that contributed to the "explosive growth" of the Cistercian order in the 12th century. What role did St. Bernard of Clairvaux play in this expansion?
  4. Compare and contrast the typical Cistercian way of life with that of the Cluniacs, focusing on aspects like church decoration, daily schedule, economic support, and dietary practices.
  5. Explain the purpose and significance of the "lay brothers" (conversi) within the Cistercian order. How did their introduction impact the daily life and economic model of Cistercian monasteries?
