The Carthusians: St. Bruno and the Ladder of Monks - Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the spiritual foundations of the Carthusian order, focusing on Guigo II's formalized practice of lectio divina and the distinctive Carthusian balance between solitude and community. It delves into the four rungs of prayerful Scripture reading, the unique semi-eremitic lifestyle, the profound discipline of silence, and the theological understanding of the contemplative life exemplified by the Carthusians.

Key Concepts

  • The Four Rungs of Lectio Divina: Guigo II's enduring framework for prayerful Scripture reading, progressing from attentive reading (Lectio) to pondering (Meditatio), spontaneous prayer (Oratio), and ultimately resting in God's presence (Contemplatio).
  • Contemplation as a Divine Gift: While the first three rungs of lectio divina are within human effort, contemplation is a gift of divine grace from God, not something that can be achieved through human will or technique.
  • Semi-Eremitic Life: The unique Carthusian monastic structure that blends elements of both solitary (eremitic) and communal (cenobitic) life, allowing for deep solitude within the protection and support of a community.
  • The Carthusian Cell: A self-contained hermitage within the monastery walls where the monk lives, prays, eats, and works alone, fostering profound silence and detachment.
  • Discipline of Silence: For Carthusians, silence is not merely the absence of noise but a positive spiritual reality—a space for listening to God and emptying oneself of worldly distractions.
  • Contemplative vs. Active Life: Interpreted through the story of Mary and Martha, the contemplative life (prayer, silence, attention to God) is considered "the best part," representing a path to holiness distinct from the active life of service, though both are valid.
  • Intercessory Prayer as Carthusian Work: Carthusians embrace the contemplative life in its purest form, seeing their hidden life of prayer and intercession as a vital service to the Church and the world.
  • Guigo II's Legacy: His framework for lectio divina has profoundly influenced Catholic spiritual tradition, extending beyond the cloister to be recommended for all believers.

Important Terms

  • Lectio Divina: A traditional monastic practice of prayerful Scripture reading, developed into a four-step framework by Guigo II.
  • Lectio: The first rung of lectio divina, involving slow, attentive reading of a Scripture passage.
  • Meditatio: The second rung, where one ponders and "chews" (ruminates) on the words of Scripture, extracting their meaning and asking what God is saying.
  • Ruminatio: The ancient monastic metaphor of "chewing the cud," referring to the slow, reflective pondering of Scripture.
  • Oratio: The third rung, a spontaneous heart-felt prayer rising in response to what has been read and meditated upon.
  • Contemplatio: The fourth and highest rung, a gift from God where the soul rests in God's presence, overwhelmed by divine love and knowledge beyond words.
  • Ascetical effort: Human actions and disciplines undertaken for spiritual growth, such as reading, meditation, and prayer.
  • Mystical grace: A supernatural gift from God, such as contemplation, that transcends human effort.
  • Semi-eremitic: A monastic lifestyle combining elements of both solitary (hermitic) and communal (cenobitic) life, notably practiced by the Carthusians.
  • Eremitic: Pertaining to the life of a hermit, characterized by solitude.
  • Cenobitic: Pertaining to monastic life lived in community.
  • Charterhouse: A Carthusian monastery.
  • Divine Office: The public prayer of the Church, consisting of psalms, readings, and prayers, recited at various times throughout the day.
  • Spatiamentum: A weekly communal walk taken by Carthusian monks outside their cells.
  • Contemplative life: A life dedicated primarily to prayer, silence, and direct attention to God, often within a monastic setting.
  • Active life: A life dedicated primarily to service, good works, and charity in the world.

Detailed Notes

The Four Rungs of Lectio Divina

Guigo II's Ladder of Monks provided the most influential framework for lectio divina, a practice of prayerful Scripture reading that existed since the Desert Fathers. This framework describes a natural progression:

  1. Lectio (Reading): This is attentive, slow reading of a Scripture passage, akin to savoring food. The purpose is not to read quickly but to encounter the "living Word of God." Guigo compares it to placing food in the mouth.
  2. Meditatio (Meditation): The monk deeply ponders the words, "chewing" on them (known as ruminatio), digesting their meaning. This ancient monastic metaphor emphasizes extracting all spiritual nourishment. The monk asks, "What does this mean? What is God saying to me?" Guigo likens this to chewing and breaking food apart.
  3. Oratio (Prayer): Spontaneous prayer arises from the heart, a direct response to the truths encountered in reading and meditation. It's not formulaic but a heartfelt cry to God, compared by Guigo to the sweetness released by chewing.
  4. Contemplatio (Contemplation): This highest rung is a pure gift from God. The monk transcends words and reasoning, resting in God's presence, experiencing a love and knowledge beyond expression. Guigo compares this to the nourishment and joy that food brings to the whole body.

It is crucial to understand that while lectio, meditatio, and oratio are within the monk's power (ascetical effort), contemplatio is entirely a gift of divine grace. One can prepare for it through faithful practice, but it cannot be forced. This distinction is central to Catholic spiritual theology, emphasizing that the spiritual life is a relationship with God, not merely a technique to be mastered.

The Carthusian Balance: Solitude and Community

The Carthusians are renowned for their distinctive "semi-eremitic" life, a unique synthesis of eremitic (hermitic, solitary) and cenobitic (communal) traditions. This approach was a solution to the challenges faced by both extremes:

  • Pure solitude, as seen in some Desert Fathers, could lead to spiritual dangers like delusion or pride without communal support or guidance.
  • A purely cenobitic life, typical of many active orders, might lack the profound silence necessary for deep contemplative prayer.

The Carthusians resolved this by having each monk live primarily as a hermit in his own cell, while still belonging to a community. This community provides:

  • A Prior for spiritual guidance and correction.
  • Sacraments and communal liturgical prayer (Matins, Vespers, Mass).
  • Fraternal support and stability under a common rule.

The Carthusian Cell: This is not just a room but a self-contained hermitage, equipped with a workshop, sleeping area, private garden, and a covered walkway. A small hatch allows food delivery, minimizing contact. Most of the monk's day—praying the Divine Office, eating, working (manual labor or intellectual tasks like copying manuscripts)—is spent alone in this cell. Monks only leave their cells for communal liturgy and a weekly communal walk (spatiamentum), which provides essential human companionship. This structure ensures both the solitude needed for deep prayer and the protection and support of monastic life.

The Discipline of Silence

For Carthusians, silence is a profound spiritual discipline. It is not merely the absence of noise but a positive space where God's voice can be heard. Inspired by the Desert Fathers' teaching to "sit in your cell... and your cell will teach you everything," Carthusians embrace silence as a way of life, going further than most Western orders.

  • They choose silence as a form of spiritual poverty, emptying themselves of superfluous words.
  • This allows them to listen more intently for the Word of God.
  • Silence is intimately connected to lectio divina, forming the necessary environment for reading, meditating, praying, and ultimately, contemplation. It is in the deepest silence that contemplation becomes possible.

The Contemplative Life and the Active Life

The Carthusians epitomize the contemplative life, a concept rooted in Christian theology often illustrated by the Gospel story of Mary and Martha (Luke 10:38–42).

  • Martha is traditionally seen as a figure of the active life: dedicated to good works, service, and charity.
  • Mary represents the contemplative life: focused on prayer, silence, and attentive listening to God's word.

Jesus' statement that Mary chose "the best part" (optimam partem) has been interpreted throughout Christian history to mean that the contemplative life, while not the only good life, holds a special, elevated place.

Important Caution: This does not imply that contemplatives are inherently holier than those in active service. Holiness is measured by charity (love of God and neighbor). The Church teaches that both paths lead to holiness, and most Christians are called to integrate elements of both: active service nourished by regular prayer and interior silence.

Carthusians, however, choose the contemplative life in its most radical form. They do not engage in external apostolates like schools or hospitals. Their sole "work" is prayer for the entire Church and world. This hidden life of intercession is understood as a real and powerful service, a "remarkable sacrifice of praise" and a source of sanctity for God's people, even if invisible to the world. Their prayer is not private but an act of communion, joining the entire Body of Christ.

The Carthusian Legacy

Despite their deliberate obscurity, the Carthusians have significantly impacted Catholic spiritual tradition. Guigo II's framework for lectio divina has spread far beyond the cloister, now widely recommended to all Catholics as a method for prayerful Scripture reading. Their commitment to contemplative prayer and silence continues to serve as an inspiring witness to the enduring value of seeking God above all else.

Review Questions

  1. Describe the four rungs of lectio divina as outlined by Guigo II. How does each step build upon the previous one, and what is the ultimate goal of the practice?
  2. Explain the crucial distinction between "ascetical effort" and "mystical grace" in the context of lectio divina. Why is contemplation considered a gift rather than something achievable by human will?
  3. How do the Carthusians reconcile the potential dangers of extreme solitude with the need for deep contemplative silence? Describe their "semi-eremitic" lifestyle and the role of the Carthusian cell.
  4. Beyond the mere absence of noise, what is the positive spiritual significance of silence for the Carthusians? How does their understanding of silence connect with the practice of lectio divina?
  5. Discuss the theological interpretation of the Mary and Martha story in relation to the contemplative and active lives. What is the "work" of the Carthusians, and how is it understood as a powerful service to the Church and the world?
