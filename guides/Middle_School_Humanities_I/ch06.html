Study Guide: The Fall of the Roman Empire

Overview

In AD 410, the Visigoth king Alaric sacked Rome — the first time the Eternal City had fallen to an enemy in 800 years. The event sent shockwaves across the Mediterranean world and prompted Augustine to write The City of God. But the fall of the Western Roman Empire was not really a single event — it was the final act of a slow decline building for more than two centuries, from the Crisis of the Third Century through Diocletian's desperate reforms, the catastrophic Battle of Adrianople, the sequential sacks of Rome, and finally the deposition of the boy-emperor Romulus Augustulus in AD 476. This chapter examines why Rome fell, what Justinian's Eastern Empire preserved and attempted to recover, and how Augustine and Boethius gave the Christian West a framework for understanding civilizational collapse — and what survives it.

Key Concepts

  • The East-West Division: Diocletian's division of the Empire reflected a real difference between the wealthier, more urbanized East (with impregnable Constantinople) and the more exposed, less urban West (with longer, more vulnerable frontiers along the Rhine and Danube). When the crises of the late fourth and fifth centuries arrived, the West could not sustain them; the East survived for another thousand years as the Byzantine Empire.

  • The 'Barbarian' Complexity: By Rome's fall, the Visigoths, Franks, Ostrogoths, and other Germanic peoples were not primitive savages. Many had been in contact with Rome for centuries, served in Roman armies, and converted to Christianity (the Arian form). The Visigoths even had a Gothic-language Bible from bishop Wulfila. The 'barbarian invasions' were as much migration, negotiation, and cultural exchange as civilization-savage collision.

  • Why Did Rome Fall? Multiple Causes: Modern historians cite many interacting causes: military overstretching, economic decline, currency debasement, the third-century crisis, plague, administrative corruption, and loss of political legitimacy. The Eastern Empire's survival for another thousand years shows collapse was not inevitable — the Western Empire's specific vulnerabilities made the difference.

  • The Two Cities: Augustine's response to Rome's fall is the most intellectually powerful: two cities are built on two fundamentally different loves. The City of Man (amor sui — self-love) includes Rome — a great civilization, but temporal. The City of God (amor Dei — love of God) cannot be destroyed by any barbarian army. Rome's fall should only shock Christians who had confused Rome with the City of God.

  • Fortune's Wheel: Boethius, writing while awaiting execution, articulated the lesson of his own fall and Rome's: Fortune by nature cannot be stable. Expecting wealth, power, and fame to remain is like expecting the sea to always be calm. The real goods — intelligence, learning, virtue — cannot be taken by any court or executioner. This message spoke to millions in the collapsing West.

  • What Survived: The fall of the Western Empire was catastrophic for trade, urban life, and population. But several things survived and formed the foundation of medieval civilization: the Church (with its institutional stability), Latin (the language of scholarship), Roman law (preserved in Justinian's Corpus Juris Civilis), manuscripts (copied by monks), and the idea of Rome (which inspired Charlemagne and the medieval papacy).

  • Justinian's Legal Legacy: Justinian I (r. AD 527–565) briefly reconquered much of the West, but his military gains could not be held after the catastrophic Plague of Justinian. His lasting achievement was legal: the Corpus Juris Civilis, which became the foundation of legal systems across most of Europe from medieval Italy to modern France, Germany, and Spain.

Important Terms

  • Crisis of the Third Century (AD 235–284): Fifty years of extreme instability: 50+ emperors (most murdered), civil war, frontier collapse, trade breakdown, and currency debasement. Set in motion the long decline ending in 476.
  • Currency Debasement: Reducing the precious metal content of coins while keeping their face value. Roman emperors did this when tax revenues fell short, causing inflation as merchants raised prices. By mid-third century, Roman silver coins contained almost no silver.
  • Diocletian (r. AD 284–305): Emperor who stabilized the Empire through drastic reforms: doubled the army, reorganized provinces, divided the Empire between East and West (the Tetrarchy). Also launched the Great Persecution of Christians (303–311).
  • Tetrarchy: "Rule of four" — Diocletian's system of two senior emperors (Augusti) and two junior emperors (Caesars) ruling the two halves of the Empire simultaneously.
  • Constantine I (r. AD 306–337): Eventually reunited the Empire after the Tetrarchy's civil war. Founded Constantinople (330). Issued the Edict of Milan (313). Called the Council of Nicaea (325). Reviewed in Chapter 5.
  • Huns: Horse-warriors from Central Asia who swept westward across the Eurasian steppes in the 370s AD, driving Germanic peoples into Roman territory in panic and setting in motion the invasions that cracked the Western Empire.
  • Visigoths: Germanic people who had been Rome's neighbors for generations. Fleeing the Huns, they crossed the Danube into Roman territory in AD 376 as refugees. After Roman mistreatment provoked their revolt, they defeated and killed Emperor Valens at Adrianople (378) and eventually sacked Rome under Alaric (410).
  • Battle of Adrianople (AD 378): Catastrophic Roman defeat: Emperor Valens killed, two-thirds of the Roman field army destroyed. Worst military defeat since Cannae (216 BC). The Danube frontier was cracked open and never fully repaired.
  • Foederati: Latin for "allies" (from foedus, treaty). Germanic groups settled inside the Roman Empire under treaty, providing soldiers in exchange for land and payment. Their first loyalty was to their chieftains, not to Rome — a structural weakness embedded in the late Roman army.
  • Alaric: Visigothic leader who had served as a Roman military officer. After the Western government repeatedly refused or broke promises regarding his demands, he sacked Rome in AD 410 — three days that shook the entire Mediterranean world. He died shortly after.
  • Attila the Hun (r. AD 434–453): United the Hunnic tribes into the most feared military force of his age. Called "the Scourge of God." Defeated at the Catalaunian Plains (451) by a combined Roman-Visigothic force. Invaded Italy (452) but turned back after meeting Pope Leo I. Died on his wedding night, 453.
  • Battle of the Catalaunian Plains (AD 451): Last great Roman military victory in the West — a combined Roman-Visigothic force under general Aetius fought Attila to a standstill in modern France.
  • Pope Leo I (the Great): Pope who met Attila at the Mincio River (452) and persuaded him to withdraw from Italy. Described by the text as "alone among the great figures of the late West with genuine moral authority." Exemplifies how the Church filled the institutional vacuum left by imperial decline.
  • Vandals: Germanic warriors who crossed through Spain into North Africa, seized Carthage, and in AD 455 sailed up the Tiber to sack Rome a second time — more thoroughly than Alaric, stripping the gilded roof tiles from the Capitoline temples.
  • Romulus Augustulus: The last emperor of the Western Roman Empire, deposed by Odoacer in August AD 476. About fourteen years old; held power only months. His name — Romulus (legendary founder) + Augustulus (diminutive of Augustus, "little Augustus") — carries almost unbearable historical irony.
  • Odoacer (Flavius Odoacer): Germanic chieftain who deposed Romulus Augustulus in August AD 476, sent the imperial regalia to Constantinople, and did not bother naming a new emperor. Traditional date for the fall of the Western Roman Empire.
  • Justinian I (r. AD 527–565): Eastern Roman Emperor; most ambitious Byzantine ruler. Through general Belisarius, briefly reconquered North Africa, Italy, and southern Spain. Produced the Corpus Juris Civilis. Built the Hagia Sophia. His empire was devastated by the Plague of Justinian.
  • Corpus Juris Civilis: Justinian's systematic compilation of all Roman law (AD 529–534): the Institutes (student textbook), Digest (legal opinions), Codex (imperial decrees), and Novels (Justinian's new laws). Foundation of most European legal systems.
  • Plague of Justinian (AD 541–549): First major recorded outbreak of bubonic plague. Spread from Egypt along trade routes; killed 25–50 million people — perhaps a quarter to a third of the Byzantine Empire. Prevented Justinian from consolidating his western reconquests.
  • Hagia Sophia: "Holy Wisdom." Justinian's great church in Constantinople, completed AD 537, still standing today. When Justinian first entered it, he reportedly exclaimed: "Solomon, I have surpassed thee!"
  • Byzantine Empire: The Eastern Roman Empire after AD 476. Byzantines called themselves Romaioi ("Romans") until Constantinople fell to the Ottoman Turks in 1453. Contributions: preserved Greek learning, Justinian's law code, converted Slavic peoples (Saints Cyril and Methodius), Eastern Church art.
  • Boethius (c. AD 480–524): Roman aristocrat and philosopher; chief minister to Theodoric. Translated Aristotle into Latin — preserving Greek thought for the Latin West. Falsely accused of treason; wrote the Consolation of Philosophy while awaiting execution.
  • Consolation of Philosophy: Boethius' masterpiece written in prison: a dialogue between Boethius and Lady Philosophy. Central argument: Fortune's wheel by nature cannot be stable; the only real goods (virtue, wisdom, intelligence) cannot be taken from you. Most read book outside the Bible in the Middle Ages.
  • Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430): Bishop of Hippo in North Africa; one of the greatest theologians in Christian history. Wrote the Confessions (first great Western autobiography, addressed to God) and The City of God (response to Rome's fall). Died as the Vandals besieged his city.
  • City of God (De Civitate Dei): Augustine's 22-book response to Rome's fall (AD 413–426). Central argument: two cities, two loves — the City of Man (self-love, temporal) and the City of God (love of God, eternal). Rome was a great City of Man but always temporal. The City of God cannot be destroyed by any barbarian army.
  • Amor sui / Amor Dei: Augustine's Latin terms for the two foundational loves: amor sui (self-love, even to contempt of God — the City of Man's foundation) and amor Dei (love of God, even to contempt of self — the City of God's foundation).

Detailed Notes

The Long Decline (Before AD 376)

  • Third Century Crisis (235–284)
    • 50+ emperors in 50 years, most murdered by their own soldiers
    • Civil war, frontier collapse, trade breakdown
    • Currency debasement → inflation → collapse of monetary confidence
    • Cities shrank; the carefully maintained Roman infrastructure stopped being maintained
  • Diocletian's Reforms (284–305)
    • Doubled army size; reorganized provinces; created the Tetrarchy
    • East-West division reflected real geographic and economic differences
    • Eastern provinces: wealthy, urban, well-defended; Constantinople nearly impregnable
    • Western provinces: exposed Rhine/Danube frontier, less urban, more vulnerable
  • Third-Century Plagues
    • Antonine Plague (AD 165): killed millions at height of the Pax Romana
    • Plague of Cyprian (AD 249): another devastating outbreak
    • Each plague reduced the army, disrupted trade, and weakened the fiscal base

The Invasions

  • The Hunnic Pressure and AD 376
    • Huns: cavalry warriors from Central Asia; swept westward from the Eurasian steppes
    • Visigoths fled Huns; begged Valens to admit them; he agreed (AD 376)
    • Roman administrators exploited refugees: denied grain, demanded children as slaves
    • Visigoths revolted
  • Battle of Adrianople (AD 378)
    • Visigoths meet Roman field army under Emperor Valens himself
    • Catastrophe: Valens killed, two-thirds of Roman field army destroyed
    • Worst Roman defeat since Cannae (216 BC, Unit 3)
    • Danube frontier cracked — never fully repaired
    • Visigoths settled as foederati: pragmatic solution but embedded structural weakness
  • Alaric and the Sack of Rome (AD 410)
    • Alaric: former Roman military officer; repeatedly demanded land and official recognition
    • Western government refused or broke promises; Alaric invaded Italy
    • AD 410: Rome sacked for three days — first time in 800 years
    • Psychological devastation; Jerome's lament; pagan blame of Christianity
    • Augustine begins The City of God in direct response
    • Alaric died shortly after; buried in the bed of the Busento River
  • Attila the Hun
    • United Hunnic tribes; extorted tribute from Eastern Empire
    • Catalaunian Plains (451): last great Roman military victory in West
    • Italy (452): sacked Aquileia; refugees found Venice
    • Pope Leo I meets Attila at Mincio River; persuades withdrawal
    • Attila died on wedding night, AD 453; empire collapsed immediately
  • AD 455 and AD 476
    • Vandals (455): sacked Rome more thoroughly than Alaric
    • Late Western emperors: puppets of Germanic magistri militum
    • Orestes made his son Romulus Augustulus emperor — age ~14
    • August AD 476: Odoacer leads mutiny, kills Orestes, deposes Romulus Augustulus
    • Sends imperial regalia to Constantinople; does not name a new emperor
    • Eastern emperor Zeno received them without much comment

Justinian and the Byzantine Empire

  • Military Reconquests (527–565)
    • General Belisarius reconquers North Africa (533), Italy (535–554), southern Spain
    • Briefly reunites Roman Empire under one emperor
    • But the Plague of Justinian prevents consolidation
  • Plague of Justinian (541–549)
    • First major bubonic plague outbreak; spread from Egypt along trade routes
    • 25–50 million dead — perhaps a quarter to a third of Byzantine population
    • Armies decimated; revenues collapsed; western territories could not be held
  • Corpus Juris Civilis (529–534)
    • Systematic compilation of all Roman law: Institutes, Digest, Codex, Novels
    • Foundation of European legal systems to the present day
  • Hagia Sophia (537)
    • "Holy Wisdom" — built in Constantinople; still standing
    • Engineering marvel: massive dome using lightweight hollow bricks
    • Justinian: "Solomon, I have surpassed thee!"

Augustine, Boethius, and Christian Response to Collapse

  • Augustine's Life
    • Born AD 354 in North Africa; brilliant, ambitious, morally restless
    • Manichaean phase; career in rhetoric; concubine; son Adeodatus
    • Heard Bishop Ambrose in Milan; conversion in garden (AD 386): "Tolle, lege" / Romans 13
    • Bishop of Hippo from AD 396; died as Vandals besieged the city (430)
  • The Confessions (397–400)
    • First great autobiography in Western literature — addressed directly to God
    • "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee"
    • Structure: longing → searching → finding; speaks to universal human experience
    • Mother Monica: thirty years of prayer for his conversion; died in Ostia after his conversion
  • The City of God (413–426)
    • Written in response to pagans blaming Christianity for Rome's fall
    • Two cities, two loves: City of Man (amor sui) vs. City of God (amor Dei)
    • Rome was a great but temporal City of Man — achieved tremendous things; always going to end
    • The City of God cannot be destroyed; its citizens are intertwined with the City of Man throughout history
    • Earthly peace is a real good worth working for — Augustine does not advocate political withdrawal
  • Boethius (c. 480–524)
    • Most learned Latin-speaker of his age; translated Aristotle's logical works
    • Medieval universities used his translations for 700 years — Anselm, Abelard, Aquinas built on him
    • Falsely accused of treason by political enemies; Senate did nothing; imprisoned at Pavia
    • Executed AD 524 — a gruesome death (cord tightened around head until skull cracked)
    • Consolation of Philosophy: Fortune's Wheel; the only real goods cannot be taken
    • Most read book outside the Bible throughout the Middle Ages; translated by King Alfred, Chaucer, Queen Elizabeth I

What Survived the Fall of Rome

  • The Church: bishops maintained poor relief, dispute resolution, record-keeping
  • Latin: remained the language of scholarship, law, and theology in the fragmented West
  • Roman law: Justinian's Corpus Juris Civilis preserved for re-emergence in 12th century
  • Manuscripts: monks in scriptoria copied Virgil, Cicero, and Greek authors — without this, classical learning would have been lost
  • The idea of Rome: the memory of a unified Christian Roman Empire inspired Charlemagne (800), the medieval papacy, and the Holy Roman Empire for another thousand years

Connections

  • To Chapter 3 (Roman Republic): The institutions that took centuries to build — the Senate, the consulship, Roman law — were what collapsed in 476. The Corpus Juris Civilis (Justinian) preserved the legal principles of the Republic for medieval and modern Europe.
  • To Chapter 5 (Christianity): Augustine's conversion to Christianity through reading Paul (Romans 13) in a Milan garden connects directly to the letters Paul wrote in Unit 5. The same legal framework that protected Paul now failed to protect Boethius.
  • To Chapter 7 (Dark Ages): What survived Rome — the Church, Latin, manuscripts, Roman law — becomes the foundation of early medieval civilization. The monasteries that preserved manuscripts are the subject of Chapter 7. Charlemagne's coronation (800) is explicitly the reassertion of "the idea of Rome."
  • Broader Theme — Providence and Collapse: Augustine's two cities framework provides a theological answer to the question every civilization must eventually face: what do you do when the world falls apart? His answer — the things Fortune can take were never truly yours; the City of God cannot fall — gave medieval Christianity the framework to survive and rebuild Western civilization from the ruins of Rome.
