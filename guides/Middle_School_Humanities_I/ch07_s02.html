Chapter 7, Section 2 Study Guide: Literature: Bede, Einhard, and the Survival of the Word

Overview

This section challenges the notion of the "Dark Ages" by highlighting three literary masterpieces from the period: Bede's Ecclesiastical History of the English People, Einhard's Life of Charlemagne, and Gregory the Great's Pastoral Care. These works demonstrate sophisticated scholarship, vivid storytelling, and profound theological insights, consciously producing literate and thoughtful content during times when such qualities were rare. A central theme is the medieval understanding of history as providential, guided by God's purposes, contrasting with the classical focus on human nature.

Key Concepts

  • Literary Masterpieces of the Period: Contrary to the "Dark Ages" stereotype, the early medieval period produced significant works like Bede's Ecclesiastical History, Einhard's Life of Charlemagne, and Gregory the Great's Pastoral Care, showcasing advanced scholarship, biographical skill, and practical theology.
  • Bede's Historical Methodology: The Venerable Bede was a pioneering historian who identified sources, distinguished between eyewitness testimony and hearsay, and acknowledged uncertainty, a method comparable to classical historians like Thucydides. He also standardized the AD (Anno Domini) dating system.
  • Providential History vs. Human Nature: A key difference between medieval historians (like Bede) and classical ones (like Thucydides) lies in their understanding of historical drivers. Thucydides saw history driven by human nature (power, fear), while Bede viewed it as providential, guided by God's divine plan.
  • The "Sparrow in the Mead-Hall" Passage: From Bede's Ecclesiastical History, this poignant passage uses the metaphor of a sparrow's flight through a warm hall to express universal human longing for meaning in the face of life's brevity and the unknown.
  • Caedmon and Divine Inspiration: Bede's account of Caedmon, the first named English poet, frames poetic talent as a direct gift from God, a Christian interpretation of artistic inspiration akin to the Greek concept of the Muse but emphasizing divine grace for the undeserving.
  • Einhard's Classical Imitation: Einhard's Life of Charlemagne consciously emulated classical biography, specifically Suetonius' Lives of the Twelve Caesars, reflecting the Carolingian Renaissance's effort to revive classical forms and learning.
  • Christian Leadership and Service (Gregory the Great): Gregory I's Pastoral Care provided a foundational guide for bishops, emphasizing that authority is best exercised as service and that leaders must set an example through their own moral conduct.

Important Terms

  • The Venerable Bede (c. AD 673–735): An English monk and scholar, author of the Ecclesiastical History of the English People.
  • Ecclesiastical History of the English People (AD 731): Bede's masterpiece, a primary historical source for early English Christianity and a model of historical scholarship.
  • AD System (Anno Domini): Bede's dating system, counting years from the Incarnation of Christ, which became standard throughout the West.
  • Thucydides (c. 460–400 BC): Classical Greek historian known for his rigorous historical methodology and focus on human nature as the driver of events.
  • Providential History: The belief, common in the medieval period, that God is actively guiding and directing human events towards His ultimate purposes.
  • Sparrow in the Mead-Hall: A famous passage from Bede's Ecclesiastical History illustrating the human condition and the appeal of Christianity.
  • Caedmon: The first named English poet, whose divinely inspired hymn is the oldest surviving poem in Old English.
  • Old English: The earliest form of the English language, spoken in England from the 5th to the 11th century.
  • Einhard (c. AD 770–840): A Frankish scholar and courtier of Charlemagne, author of the Vita Karoli.
  • Vita Karoli (Life of Charlemagne, c. AD 830): Einhard's biography of Emperor Charlemagne, modeled on classical Roman biographies.
  • Suetonius' Lives of the Twelve Caesars: A classical Roman biographical work that Einhard consciously imitated.
  • Carolingian Renaissance: A period of intellectual and cultural revival during the reign of Charlemagne, characterized by renewed interest in classical learning.
  • Gregory the Great (c. 540–604): Pope Gregory I, author of Regula Pastoralis.
  • Regula Pastoralis (Pastoral Care, c. AD 591): Gregory the Great's handbook for bishops on Christian leadership, spiritual guidance, and ethical conduct.
  • King Alfred the Great (r. 871–899): English king who translated Gregory's Pastoral Care into Old English and distributed it to bishops.

Detailed Notes

Introduction: Literature Beyond the "Dark Ages"

The section begins by asserting that the literature of the early medieval period, often inaccurately labeled the "Dark Ages," was not dark at all. Instead, it produced three masterpieces: Bede's Ecclesiastical History, Einhard's Life of Charlemagne, and Gregory the Great's Pastoral Care. These authors consciously worked against the prevailing conditions, creating literate and thoughtful works when such qualities were rare.

Bede: Ecclesiastical History of the English People (AD 731)

  • The Venerable Bede: Spent almost his entire life in the monastery of Wearmouth-Jarrow in Northumbria.
  • His Work: Ecclesiastical History of the English People, completed in AD 731, is the most important historical source for early English Christianity and a top work of medieval historical writing.
  • Bede's Method:
    • Source Criticism: He meticulously identifies his sources, which included written documents, letters from popes, and oral testimony.
    • Distinction: He differentiates between eyewitness accounts and hearsay.
    • Acknowledgement of Uncertainty: He admits when his knowledge is limited.
    • Comparison to Thucydides: Bede's rigorous approach to historical inquiry is closer to that of the classical Greek historian Thucydides than to most medieval chroniclers.
    • AD Dating System: Bede's innovation of counting years from the Incarnation of Christ (Anno Domini) became the standard dating system in the West.
  • Bede and Thucydides: Understanding History's Drivers:
    • While both historians took their craft seriously and sought to explain why events happened, they differed fundamentally on what drives history.
    • Thucydides: Believed history is driven by human nature: the desire for power, fear, and self-interest.
    • Bede: Believed history is providential, guided by God's purposes. For Bede, the conversion of the English was not just political but a chapter in salvation history.
    • Synthesis: A complete historical understanding benefits from both perspectives – Thucydides' skeptical eye on human motives and Bede's sense of overarching meaning.
  • The "Sparrow Passage":
    • This is a famous excerpt from Bede's Ecclesiastical History (II.13), spoken by a pagan counselor to King Edwin of Northumbria (c. AD 627) while debating conversion to Christianity.
    • Metaphor: It compares human life to a sparrow's swift flight through a warm mead-hall from winter to winter, symbolizing life's brief moment of warmth and light amidst the vast unknown before and after.
    • Significance: It is considered one of the most beautiful and moving passages in early medieval literature, expressing a universal human longing for meaning and permanence, which Christianity offered as an answer.
  • Caedmon: The First English Poet:
    • Bede preserves the story of Caedmon, an illiterate cowherd who miraculously received the gift of poetry in a dream from a divine visitor.
    • Divine Inspiration: Bede's account frames poetry as a divine gift, a distinctly Christian parallel to the Greek concept of the Muse.
    • Oldest English Poem: Caedmon's hymn, preserved by Bede, is the oldest surviving poem in Old English.
    • Contrast with Greek Muse: While both traditions attribute poetry to external divine force, Caedmon's story emphasizes divine grace given to the undeserving and reluctant, contrasting with Homer's professional invocation of the Muse. The content of Caedmon's poetry is also explicitly Christian (creation).

Einhard: Life of Charlemagne (c. AD 830)

  • Einhard's Background: A scholar at Charlemagne's court, he wrote his Vita Karoli (Life of Charlemagne) after the emperor's death, drawing on personal observations.
  • Classical Model: Einhard deliberately modeled his biography on Suetonius' Lives of the Twelve Caesars, showcasing a conscious effort of the Carolingian Renaissance to imitate classical literary forms.
  • Portrait of Charlemagne:
    • Physical Description: Einhard provides vivid details of Charlemagne's appearance (tall, strong, large piercing eyes, white hair, dignified).
    • Character Traits: He describes Charlemagne's temperament (temperate in eating and drinking, especially hating drunkenness).
    • Intellectual Pursuits: Charlemagne learned Latin fluently and understood Greek. He even attempted to learn to write late in life but had little success, a detail Einhard includes despite his admiration, highlighting his biographical integrity.
  • Comparison to Plutarch: Like Plutarch, Einhard uses physical description, anecdotes, and moral evaluation. However, Einhard emphasizes Charlemagne's personal piety and love of the church, while Plutarch focuses more on the moral exemplar for citizens.

Gregory the Great: Pastoral Care (c. AD 591)

  • Author and Purpose: Pope Gregory I's Regula Pastoralis was a crucial handbook for bishops, guiding them on how to teach, lead, and care for their diverse congregations.
  • Influence: King Alfred the Great translated it into Old English in the 9th century and distributed copies to all bishops in his kingdom, demonstrating its lasting impact.
  • Christian Leadership: The work presents a distinctly Christian vision of leadership, emphasizing that authority should be exercised as service.
  • Excerpt Significance: Gregory highlights the immense harm caused by religious leaders who act evilly while holding a title of holiness. He stresses that such a leader's sin is more damnable because their position often protects them from correction, underscoring the necessity for pastors to lead by example.

How These Writers Understood History

  • Providential View: All three authors (Bede, Einhard, Gregory) shared a view of history distinct from classical historians like Thucydides and Livy. They understood history as providential, believing that God guides human events towards a predetermined end.
  • Divine Significance:
    • For Bede, the conversion of the English was not merely political but a chapter in the story of salvation.
    • For Einhard, events like Charlemagne's coronation on Christmas Day were imbued with divine significance.
  • Dominant Paradigm: This providential view of history, so different from the rational analysis of the classical world, became the dominant approach to historical writing in the West until the Renaissance.
  • Comparison with Augustine: The section also notes a comparison between Bede's sparrow passage and Augustine's "restless heart" (Confessions), both expressing a longing for something beyond this life answered by Christianity, though through different literary styles (narrative vs. introspective philosophy).

Review Questions

  1. How do the literary works of Bede, Einhard, and Gregory the Great challenge the common perception of the early medieval period as the "Dark Ages"? Provide specific examples from their works.
  2. Compare and contrast Bede's historical methodology and his understanding of what drives history with that of a classical historian like Thucydides. What are the key similarities and differences?
  3. Discuss the literary and theological significance of Bede's "sparrow in the mead-hall" passage. How does it encapsulate a universal human experience and frame the appeal of Christianity?
  4. Einhard deliberately modeled his Life of Charlemagne on classical biographies. What elements of classical biography does he adopt, and what unique aspects related to medieval Christian culture does he emphasize that a classical biographer might not?
  5. Explain the concept of "providential history" as understood by Bede, Einhard, and Gregory the Great. How did this view differ from classical historical writing, and what impact did it have on medieval historical interpretation?
