Reading the New Testament and Early Christian Writers

Overview

This section explores key primary sources from early Christianity, offering a multi-faceted view of its foundational beliefs, ethical demands, and external perception. Through selections from the Gospels, the Book of Acts, and early Christian writings, students gain insight into the revolutionary teachings of Jesus, the theological arguments for his divinity, and the diverse missionary strategies employed by the early Church. It also touches on how early Christians navigated a Greco-Roman world that often misunderstood or opposed them, culminating in the example of early martyrdom.

Key Concepts

  • Inversion of Greco-Roman Values: Jesus's teachings, particularly the Beatitudes, challenged and redefined what was considered "blessed" or "honorable" in the ancient world, prioritizing humility, suffering, and righteousness over wealth, power, and status.
  • Theological Synthesis (Logos and Incarnation): The Gospel of John's Prologue bridges Greek philosophical thought (the Logos as a divine rational principle) with Christian theology, asserting that this cosmic principle became a human being in Jesus Christ. This "Word made flesh" (Incarnation) is a central doctrine.
  • Contextualized Evangelism: The Apostle Paul's missionary approach, as seen in his sermon at Athens, demonstrates the strategy of adapting the presentation of the Gospel to the specific cultural and intellectual background of the audience without compromising its core message.
  • Personal Testimony and Historical Argument: Paul's defense before King Agrippa combines his personal conversion story with an appeal to verifiable historical facts and prophetic fulfillment, illustrating how early Christians presented their faith.
  • Embracing Martyrdom: The example of Ignatius of Antioch highlights the early Christian readiness to suffer and die for their faith, a defining characteristic that revealed the depth of their commitment in the face of Roman persecution.

Important Terms

  • Sermon on the Mount: A collection of ethical teachings attributed to Jesus, found in Matthew chapters 5–7, considered the most concentrated body of ethical teaching in Western literature.
  • Beatitudes: The opening section of the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5:3–12), where Jesus declares certain qualities and states of being "blessed." The word comes from the Latin beatus ("blessed") and Greek makarios (happy, fortunate, divinely favored).
  • Eudaimonia: A Greek term often translated as "human flourishing," "well-being," or "happiness," which Aristotle taught required wealth and good fortune.
  • Logos: A Greek philosophical term meaning "Word," "Reason," or "Divine Principle," used by Stoics to describe the rational principle ordering the universe. The Gospel of John uses this term to identify Jesus Christ.
  • Ho Logos sarx egeneto: Greek for "the Word was made flesh," a concise theological statement from John's Prologue asserting the Incarnation of Jesus.
  • Incarnation: The Christian doctrine that the divine Son of God (the Logos) became human, taking on flesh in the person of Jesus Christ.
  • Contextualization: A missionary approach that seeks to find points of connection between an unfamiliar message (like the Gospel) and an audience's existing cultural, religious, or philosophical knowledge, using that as a bridge for communication.
  • Patristic Sources: Writings by the Church Fathers (early and influential theologians and writers of the Christian Church).
  • Martyrdom: The suffering of death on account of one's adherence to a faith or cause, a significant aspect of early Christian identity and witness.

Detailed Notes

Part I: The New Testament

The Sermon on the Mount: Matthew 5

  • Significance: This sermon (Matthew 5–7) is recognized as the most profound body of ethical teaching in Western literature, delivered by Jesus to a crowd in Galilee.
  • The Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3–12):
    • Core Message: These opening declarations fundamentally reversed the prevailing values of the Greco-Roman world, which esteemed military glory, wealth, power, and high status.
    • Value Inversion: Jesus declared "blessed" (from Latin beatus and Greek makarios) those who mourned, the poor in spirit, the meek, and the persecuted.
    • Revolutionary Claim: In the ancient context, applying "blessed" (a term often reserved for the Olympian gods, signifying freedom from suffering) to those experiencing hardship was a radical assertion about the moral order of the universe. It implied that God honors those whom the world might consider "losers."
    • Contrast with Aristotle: This teaching directly contradicted philosophical ideas like Aristotle's eudaimonia, which linked human flourishing to external good fortune and wealth. For Jesus, true fortune lay in spiritual qualities often associated with suffering or humility.
    • Impact: This inversion was simultaneously electrifying to the poor and marginalized, and deeply threatening or strangely compelling to the powerful, highlighting how genuinely radical Jesus's message was.

The Prologue of John: The Word Made Flesh

  • Unique Opening: Unlike other Gospels, John's Gospel begins not with a genealogy or birth narrative, but with a profound theological statement.
  • The Concept of the Logos: John intentionally employs the Greek philosophical term Logos (meaning "Word," "Reason," or "Divine Principle"). For Stoic philosophers, the Logos was the rational principle that ordered and permeated all of reality.
  • Incarnation Doctrine: John makes the astonishing claim that this eternal, rational principle – the very Logos that Greek philosophers had abstractly sought – "was made flesh" (ho Logos sarx egeneto) in a specific person, Jesus Christ. This is the doctrine of the Incarnation, meaning the divine became human.
  • Historical Reality: This claim moved the Logos from an impersonal philosophical concept to a concrete historical event, stating that the eternal God entered human history in Bethlehem during the reigns of Augustus and Herod.
  • Engagement with Greco-Roman Thought: John's deliberate use of Greek philosophical vocabulary shows early Christianity's intention to engage the intellectual world of its time on its own terms, presenting Jesus as the fulfillment (and radical redefinition) of philosophical longing. While Stoics saw the Logos as impersonal, John declared it a Person who could be touched, followed, and loved.

Part II: Acts of the Apostles

Paul at Athens: Acts 17

  • Setting: Paul's sermon on the Areopagus (Mars' Hill) in Athens, the intellectual heart of the ancient world, was a significant missionary encounter. He debated with Epicurean and Stoic philosophers.
  • Contextualized Approach: Recognizing his audience's background, Paul chose not to quote Hebrew Scriptures, which they wouldn't know. Instead, he started with their existing religious practices and philosophical ideas.
  • Points of Connection:
    • He referenced their altar "TO THE UNKNOWN GOD," using it as a starting point to declare the God who made the world and all things.
    • He quoted their own poets, such as the Stoic poet Aratus ("for we are also his offspring"), demonstrating common ground.
  • Definition of Contextualization: This method is called contextualization—finding the most effective point of connection between an unfamiliar message and the audience's existing knowledge to build a bridge, without compromising the Gospel's truth.
  • Results: The response was mixed: some mocked, others expressed interest in hearing more, and a few believed (like Dionysius the Areopagite and Damaris). While not producing mass conversions, it planted seeds for future growth.

Paul Before Agrippa: Acts 26

  • Setting and Purpose: Paul stood trial before King Agrippa II and Roman governor Festus, accused of inciting trouble among the Jews. His defense was a powerful personal testimony.
  • Autobiographical Defense: Paul presented his life story, recounting his past as a zealous Pharisee, his dramatic encounter with the risen Christ on the Damascus road, and his divine call to preach to the Gentiles.
  • Appeal to Scripture and History: He appealed directly to Agrippa's knowledge of Jewish Scripture, framing the Resurrection of Christ not as a novel idea, but as the fulfillment of prophetic promises. He also emphasized the public, verifiable nature of his claims ("this thing was not done in a corner").
  • Diverse Responses:
    • Agrippa: "Almost thou persuadest me to be a Christian"—representing the "nearly-persuaded" who are intellectually moved but hesitate to commit.
    • Festus: "Paul, thou art beside thyself; much learning doth make thee mad"—representing the "Roman rationalist" who dismisses faith as madness or irrationality.
    • Paul: The fully committed believer, willing to stand trial and suffer for his faith.
  • Strategic Adaptation: This account shows Paul's ability to adapt his message for different audiences: using natural theology and Greek philosophy in Athens, but Hebrew Scripture and personal testimony before Agrippa, while always proclaiming the same Gospel.

Part III: Patristic Sources

Ignatius of Antioch: Embracing Martyrdom

  • Identity and Role: Ignatius was the third bishop of Antioch, a significant early Christian center from which Paul had launched his missionary journeys.
  • Context of Persecution: He was arrested under Emperor Trajan and transported under Roman military guard to Rome, where he was destined for execution in the arena.
  • Significance: Though the text provided cuts off here, his journey and eventual martyrdom symbolize the early Church's profound commitment to Christ, even unto death, highlighting the concept of martyrdom as a powerful witness of faith.

Review Questions

  1. Explain how the Beatitudes, as presented in Matthew 5, represent a radical "inversion of the world's value system" when compared to the dominant ideals of the Greco-Roman world. Provide specific examples of what was valued in each system.
  2. How does the Gospel of John's use of the term Logos in its prologue connect with and diverge from the philosophical understanding of the Logos by Stoic philosophers? What is the theological significance of "the Word was made flesh"?
  3. Compare and contrast Paul's missionary approach in Acts 17 (Athens) with his defense before King Agrippa in Acts 26. What principles of effective communication for diverse audiences can be learned from these two accounts?
  4. Describe the three distinct responses to Paul's message observed in Acts 26 (Agrippa, Festus, and Paul himself). What does each response reveal about common reactions to the Gospel throughout history?
  5. Based on the information provided, what was the historical context and significance of Ignatius of Antioch's journey and impending martyrdom? How does his experience contribute to our understanding of early Christianity?
