Study Guide: The Rise of the Roman Republic

Overview

The Greeks, as we have seen, built a civilization on the sea — fragmented, brilliant, and ultimately unable to unite. Rome's answer to the Greek problem — how do you hold a civilization together? — was not philosophy but institutions. From the expulsion of the last king in 509 BC to the eve of the great Punic Wars in 264 BC, Rome built the Senate, the consulship, the Twelve Tables of law, and a culture of duty and sacrifice that shaped Western civilization as profoundly as Greek philosophy. This chapter examines Rome's founding legends, its Republican institutions, the moral exemplars Romans admired, and the slow process by which a city on the Tiber became master of the Italian peninsula — preparing the stage for world conquest.

Key Concepts

  • Origin Stories as Value Statements: Every great civilization tells stories about where it came from — Greece had Homer, Israel had the Exodus, Rome had Romulus and Aeneas. These stories are not merely entertainment; they answer the question "Who are we, and what do we stand for?" Even educated Romans preserved their founding legends because those legends revealed what Rome valued: pietas, endurance, and the willingness to carry one's obligations even at personal cost.

  • Institutional Design Against Tyranny: The Roman Republic was explicitly designed to prevent the return of monarchy. Two consuls with mutual veto power, a Senate with moral (not legal) authority, tribunes who could protect ordinary citizens — these interlocking institutions distributed power while keeping government effective. The mixed constitution was later identified by Polybius and Cicero as the reason Rome lasted while other city-states collapsed.

  • Written Law as a Foundation of Liberty: The Twelve Tables (451–450 BC) established the principle that law must be public, knowable, and consistent — applying equally to rich and poor. Before them, patrician judges interpreted unwritten law as they pleased. This principle of public written law is foundational to Western legal tradition, reappearing in Magna Carta, the US Constitution, and modern human rights law.

  • Roman Virtue (Virtus, Pietas, Fides): Rome believed its greatness rested on a cluster of moral qualities: virtus (active courage in service of the community), pietas (dutiful devotion to gods, family, and state), and fides (faithfulness — keeping one's word regardless of cost). These virtues were demonstrated publicly, under pressure, and in service of the res publica — not for personal glory.

  • The Moral Historian's Method: Livy wrote Roman history openly as a moral project, using exemplary heroes and cautionary villains to form readers. His preface says he cares about Rome's "life and manners," not just battles. Plutarch similarly wrote "ethical history" — moral portraits of great men. Both authors shaped Western civilization by giving it vocabulary for republican virtue that the American Founders used directly.

  • Roman vs. Christian Virtue: Roman virtue is genuinely admirable — the text calls it real courage, faithfulness, and self-sacrifice. But its horizon of moral concern is Rome: Regulus died to preserve Rome's honor; Horatius held the bridge so Rome could survive. Christian virtue, by contrast, aims at a different city entirely, one "whose architect and builder is God." The virtues overlap; the destinations differ.

  • Incorporation Over Enslavement: Rome's policy of offering Latin rights or full citizenship to defeated Italian peoples — rather than enslaving them — gave conquered peoples a stake in Roman success. They contributed soldiers and taxes and gained the protection of Roman law. This strategic generosity created the loyal manpower base that fueled Rome's later conquests.

Important Terms

  • Res publica: Literally "the public thing" or "the public affair" — the Roman name for their system of shared government. The direct ancestor of the English word "republic."
  • Romulus: Legendary founder of Rome (753 BC by Roman reckoning); twin brother of Remus; traditional first king. His story embeds the founding of Rome's institutions in a divine narrative.
  • Aeneas: Trojan hero who escaped burning Troy carrying his father and household gods; ancestor of the Roman people in legend. His story, told by Virgil in the Aeneid, embodies pietas in its fullest form.
  • Pietas: Dutiful devotion to gods, family, and state. The most fundamental of Roman virtues — embodied in Aeneas carrying his father from Troy. Close to the Christian virtue of honor to parents and obedience.
  • Virtus: From vir (man). Active courage, self-discipline, and excellence in the service of the community. Directed toward the res publica, not toward personal glory. Demonstrated in battle, in the Senate, in court — wherever the public needed it.
  • Gravitas: The quality of seriousness, weight, and dignity appropriate to a man of public responsibility. Its opposite, levitas (frivolity), was considered deeply un-Roman.
  • Fides: Faithfulness; trustworthiness; keeping one's word regardless of cost. Romans considered it the foundation of all social life: without fides, no agreement holds, no court can function, civilization unravels.
  • Tarquinius Superbus (Tarquin the Proud): Last king of Rome; overthrown in 509 BC after his son violated the noblewoman Lucretia. His tyranny — executing citizens without trial, ignoring the Senate — became the permanent warning against monarchy.
  • Intercessio: The right of each consul (and later tribunes) to block or veto the action of another magistrate. The constitutional mechanism that prevented any single person from holding unchecked power.
  • Imperium: The right to command — held by consuls and military commanders. Full authority to lead armies, execute criminals, and govern provinces.
  • Consul: One of two annually elected chief magistrates of the Roman Republic. Held full imperium but each could veto the other. The highest regular office in the cursus honorum.
  • Tribune of the Plebs: One of ten annually elected officials protecting plebeian citizens. Their persons were legally sacrosanct (inviolable); they could veto any magistrate's action affecting a plebeian. The 'common citizen's shield.'
  • Cursus Honorum: The "path of honors" — the required sequence of offices (quaestor, aedile, praetor, consul, censor) that Roman nobles had to hold in order, with minimum ages and waiting periods between each post.
  • Senate: The advisory body of experienced former magistrates. Controlled state finances, foreign policy, and military commands. Its decrees were not formally laws but carried overwhelming moral authority.
  • Patricians: The hereditary noble families of Rome who originally monopolized the Senate, major priesthoods, and the consulship.
  • Plebeians: The ordinary free citizens of Rome — the majority of the population — who struggled for two centuries to gain political rights equal to the patricians.
  • Conflict of the Orders: The long struggle (c. 494–287 BC) by which plebeians gained equal political rights: creation of the tribune (494 BC), Twelve Tables (451–450 BC), opening of consulship (367 BC), Lex Hortensia (287 BC).
  • Twelve Tables: Rome's first written law code (451–450 BC), inscribed on twelve bronze tablets displayed publicly in the Forum. Made law knowable and consistent for all citizens.
  • Livy (Titus Livius, c. 64 BC – AD 17): Roman historian who wrote 142 books (35 survive) on Rome from its founding to his day. Openly a moral historian using exemplary stories to form readers.
  • Plutarch (c. AD 46–120): Greek biographer under the Roman Empire. Wrote the Parallel Lives, pairing 23 Greeks and 23 Romans in moral portraits. One of Shakespeare's most important sources.
  • Horatius Cocles: Roman hero who held the Sublician Bridge alone against an Etruscan army while his comrades demolished it, then swam the Tiber in full armor. Embodies virtus — courage for the community, indifferent to personal glory.
  • Cincinnatus: Called from his four-acre farm to serve as dictator; rescued a surrounded army; resigned the dictatorship after fifteen days and returned to farming. Rome's supreme example of republican virtue: power as burden, not prize.
  • Regulus: Roman consul captured by Carthage; argued against favorable peace terms before the Roman Senate, then kept his oath and returned to Carthage to be tortured to death. Rome's supreme example of fides.

Detailed Notes

Roman Founding Legends

  • Romulus and Remus
    • Twin sons of the war god Mars and a Vestal Virgin; nursed by a she-wolf; raised by a shepherd
    • Romulus killed Remus in a quarrel and founded Rome in 753 BC
    • Romans counted years from this date (ab urbe condita — "from the founding of the city")
  • The Legend of Aeneas
    • Trojan prince who escaped burning Troy carrying his aged father and household gods
    • After years of wandering, reached Italy and became ancestor of the Roman people
    • Virgil's Aeneid (written under Augustus) made this legend the basis of Rome's claim to divine destiny
    • Story reveals Roman values: pietas, endurance, and the willingness to carry one's obligations

Republican Institutions

  • The Expulsion of the Tarquins (509 BC)
    • Brutus (who had feigned stupidity to survive Tarquin's reign) rallied the Romans after the violation of Lucretia
    • Romans expelled the Tarquins and swore never again to be ruled by a king
    • Established the consulship: two annually elected magistrates with mutual veto
  • The Three Pillars
    • Senate: experience and continuity; controls finances and foreign policy; life tenure
    • Consuls: executive energy; full imperium; each can veto the other; one dictator possible in emergencies (max. 6 months)
    • Tribunes: protection of ordinary citizens; sacrosanct persons; veto power over magistrates
  • The Mixed Constitution
    • Senate = aristocratic element; consuls = monarchical element; tribunes/assemblies = democratic element
    • Polybius and Cicero: this balance explains Rome's durability compared to other city-states

The Conflict of the Orders

  • The Problem
    • Patricians monopolized the Senate, priesthoods, consulship, and legal knowledge
    • Unwritten law could be applied selectively by patrician judges
    • Plebeians, who made up the army, had no protection against arbitrary treatment
  • Key Milestones
    • First secession (494 BC): plebeian soldiers walk out of Rome — creation of the tribune
    • Twelve Tables (451–450 BC): written, public law displayed in the Forum
    • Licinian-Sextian Laws (367 BC): consulship opened to plebeians
    • Lex Hortensia (287 BC): plebeian assembly resolutions binding on all Romans
  • Achievement
    • By 287 BC Rome had a genuinely mixed constitution in practice, not just theory

Roman Moral Exemplars

  • Horatius at the Bridge
    • Lars Porsena, Etruscan king, advancing on Rome to restore Tarquin; Roman troops panicking
    • Horatius holds the Sublician Bridge alone while comrades demolish it behind him
    • Wounded, leaps into the Tiber in full armor and swims to safety
    • Livy's lesson: sometimes one brave man, at exactly the right moment, can save a republic
    • Key contrast: Achilles fought for personal glory; Horatius offered himself so others could survive
  • Cincinnatus
    • Called from his four-acre farm when senators arrived in official robes
    • Led the Roman army, rescued surrounded troops, forced enemy surrender
    • Resigned the dictatorship after only fifteen days; returned to farming
    • Power as burden, not prize — George Washington later called "the American Cincinnatus"
  • Regulus
    • Captured by Carthage in the First Punic War; sent to Rome on parole to negotiate peace
    • Argued before the Senate against the Carthaginian terms (favorable to Carthage)
    • Kept his oath: sailed back to Carthage knowing he would be tortured to death
    • Cicero's supreme example of the obligation to keep oaths — demonstrates fides

Livy and Plutarch as Sources

  • Livy's Method and Purpose
    • 142 books (35 survive); written over 400 years after many events he describes
    • Freely admits the earliest accounts are legendary — honest about uncertainty
    • Wrote as a moral historian: heroes are exemplars, villains are warnings
    • Opening preface: cares about "their life and manners" — a moral history, not a chronicle
  • Plutarch's Parallel Lives
    • Greek philosopher writing under Roman Empire (c. AD 46–120)
    • 23 pairs of Greeks and Romans compared in terms of character
    • Wrote "ethike historia" (ethical history) — moral portraits, not comprehensive biography
    • Working from earlier (now lost) sources; guide to Roman values more than Roman facts
    • One of Shakespeare's most important sources; used by the American Founders

Connections

  • To Chapter 2 (Greece): Roman virtus differs from Greek arete: Achilles sought personal glory (kleos), while Horatius fought for the community. The Greek hero's horizon was personal fame; the Roman hero's horizon was the res publica. Greece asked "What is the good life?"; Rome asked "What does the community need?"
  • To Chapter 4 (Roman Empire): The Republic's mixed constitution eventually broke down under the pressures of conquest, wealth, and civil war — creating the conditions for Augustus' principate. The cursus honorum became the pathway for figures like Cicero, Caesar, and Augustus himself.
  • To Chapter 6 (Augustine): Augustine will argue in The City of God that Roman virtue, as glorious as it was, was ultimately self-serving — aimed at earthly glory rather than God. Romans "received their reward" but sought nothing beyond it.
  • Broader Theme — Liberty and Institutions: The Roman Republic's institutional design — shared power, mutual veto, written public law, term limits — directly influenced the American Constitution. The Founders read Polybius and Cicero on the mixed constitution. The Senate, consulship, and the very word "republic" are Rome's gifts to American political vocabulary.
