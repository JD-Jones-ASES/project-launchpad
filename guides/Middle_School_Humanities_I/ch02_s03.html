The Golden Age: War, Democracy, and Empire

Overview

This section chronicles the transformative period in Greek history following the Persian Wars, which propelled Athens into its "Golden Age" of democratic innovation and imperial expansion under Pericles. However, Athens' growing power led to the devastating Peloponnesian War against Sparta, ultimately weakening the Greek city-states and creating a power vacuum filled by the rising kingdom of Macedonia under Philip II and his son, Alexander the Great, whose conquests spread Greek culture across a vast new Hellenistic world.

Key Concepts

  • The Legacy of the Persian Wars: The Greek victories against the Persian Empire (Marathon, Thermopylae, Salamis) instilled Athens with immense confidence and provided the resources (Laurium silver mines) to fund a powerful navy, solidify its democracy, and initiate ambitious building programs like the Parthenon. This period laid the groundwork for Athens' cultural and intellectual flourishing.
  • Athenian Empire and the Delian League: Formed originally as a mutual defense alliance after the Persian Wars, the Delian League rapidly transformed into an Athenian empire. Athens extracted tribute from member states, which it then used for its own infrastructure and cultural projects, often against the will of its allies.
  • Pericles and Athenian Democracy: Under the leadership of Pericles, Athenian democracy deepened, extending participation to poorer citizens through paid jury and public office service. His famous Funeral Oration celebrated Athens as a meritocracy that served as "an education to Greece," though this democracy still excluded the majority of the population (women, slaves, foreigners).
  • The Peloponnesian War: This protracted and devastating conflict between the Athenian (maritime, democratic) and Spartan (land-based, oligarchic) alliances marked the end of Athens' political dominance and significantly weakened the entire Greek city-state system, leaving it vulnerable to external powers.
  • Macedonian Military Innovation: Philip II of Macedon revolutionized warfare by developing the sarissa (an 18-foot pike) and organizing his soldiers into the formidable Macedonian phalanx, allowing him to conquer Greece and lay the foundation for his son's vast empire.
  • Alexander's Conquests and Hellenism: Alexander the Great, tutored by Aristotle, conquered a massive empire stretching from Greece to India in just thirteen years. While his political empire fragmented after his death, his conquests profoundly shaped the ancient world by spreading Greek language, culture, and ideas across this vast territory—a process known as Hellenism. This cultural unity had long-lasting impacts, including providing the linguistic and urban infrastructure for the later spread of Christianity.

Important Terms

  • Delian League: An alliance of Greek city-states formed in 478 BC after the Persian Wars, initially for mutual defense against Persia. Under Athenian leadership, it gradually evolved into an Athenian empire, with Athens using member states' tribute for its own benefit.
  • Pericles (c. 495–429 BC): A prominent and influential statesman, orator, and general during Athens' Golden Age. He significantly advanced Athenian democracy and spearheaded the city's massive building program.
  • Pericles' Funeral Oration: A famous speech delivered by Pericles (recorded by Thucydides) during the Peloponnesian War, extolling the virtues of Athenian democracy, its citizens, and its unique cultural and political values.
  • Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC): A major ancient Greek war fought between the Delian League (led by Athens) and the Peloponnesian League (led by Sparta). It resulted in Athens' defeat, the end of its hegemony, and a lasting weakening of the Greek city-state system.
  • Philip II of Macedon (382–336 BC): King of Macedon and a brilliant military strategist. He developed the sarissa and Macedonian phalanx, conquering most of Greece and uniting it before his assassination.
  • Sarissa: An exceptionally long (up to 18 feet) pike used by the Macedonian army, invented by Philip II, which gave the Macedonian phalanx a significant advantage in battle.
  • Macedonian Phalanx: A highly effective and fearsome military formation developed by Philip II, consisting of tightly packed infantry wielding sarissas, which proved instrumental in his conquests.
  • Alexander the Great (356–323 BC): Son of Philip II, he created one of the largest empires of the ancient world, stretching from Greece to India. His conquests were crucial in initiating the Hellenistic era.
  • Hellenism: The historical period and cultural process following Alexander the Great's conquests, characterized by the widespread dissemination of Greek language, culture, and ideas throughout the Middle East and Central Asia. It led to a blending of Greek and local traditions.
  • Koine Greek: The common form of the Greek language that became the lingua franca (common language) of the Hellenistic world, used for trade, administration, and scholarship. The New Testament was written in Koine Greek.
  • Septuagint: The Koine Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures, created by Jewish scholars in Alexandria during the Hellenistic period.

Detailed Notes

The Persian Wars: Crisis and Triumph

  • First Invasion (490 BC): A Persian army under King Darius I landed at Marathon, north of Athens. The Athenians, with assistance from Plataea and without Spartan aid, decisively defeated the Persian forces. A runner, Pheidippides, carried the news of victory to Athens.
  • Second Invasion (480 BC): Ten years later, Darius's son Xerxes launched a massive invasion, overwhelming Greece.
    • A small Spartan force led by King Leonidas famously held the mountain pass of Thermopylae for three days, buying crucial time for other Greek forces to prepare.
    • Athens was evacuated and burned after Thermopylae fell.
    • The Athenian navy, guided by General Themistocles, lured the larger Persian fleet into the narrow straits of Salamis and destroyed it, effectively saving Greece.
  • Legacy: The Persian Wars cemented Athens' status as the most powerful Greek city-state. The wealth from silver mines at Laurium, combined with a newfound confidence, funded a powerful navy, bolstered Athenian democracy, and laid the foundation for the ambitious building programs and cultural flourishing of the Golden Age.

Pericles and the Height of Athenian Democracy

  • Delian League Formation: After the Persian Wars, Athens organized many Greek city-states into the Delian League (478 BC) for mutual defense against future Persian threats.
  • Transformation into Athenian Empire: Over time, Athens increasingly dominated the league, moving its treasury to Athens and using tribute money from allied states to fund its own lavish projects, effectively turning the alliance into an empire. Member states attempting to secede were forcibly kept in the league.
  • Pericles' Leadership: Under the influential statesman Pericles (c. 495–429 BC), Athens embarked on its most ambitious building program, constructing iconic monuments like the Parthenon, the Erechtheion, and the Propylaea on the Acropolis, largely financed by Delian League tribute.
  • Deepening Democracy: Pericles further strengthened Athenian democracy by introducing pay for jurors and public officeholders. This reform allowed poorer citizens to actively participate in government without financial hardship.
  • Pericles' Funeral Oration: In his famous oration (recorded by Thucydides), Pericles articulated the ideals of Athenian democracy, describing Athens as "an education to Greece" where merit trumped birth, laws protected the weak, and citizens balanced private excellence with public duty.
  • Limitations of Athenian Democracy: Despite its brilliance, Athenian democracy was not universal. It excluded women, slaves, and foreign residents (metics), who together constituted the majority of Athens' population.
  • Historical Context of the Oration: It's important to remember that Pericles' speech was delivered during the first year of the Peloponnesian War, a conflict that Athens would ultimately lose. Thucydides recorded it not as an endorsement but as a reflection of Athens' self-perception at its zenith, offering a poignant contrast to its eventual decline.

The Peloponnesian War and the Decline of Athens

  • Causes: The growing power and imperial ambitions of Athens alarmed Sparta and its allies, leading to the outbreak of war in 431 BC.
  • Conflict: The war pitted the maritime, democratic Athenian alliance against the land-based, oligarchic Spartan-led Peloponnesian League. The conflict dragged on for twenty-seven years, as neither side could deliver a decisive knockout blow.
  • Key Events:
    • A devastating plague struck Athens in 430 BC, killing a quarter of the population, including Pericles himself.
    • Athens suffered a catastrophic defeat in a failed invasion of Sicily (415–413 BC), losing much of its navy and army.
  • Outcome: Athens finally surrendered to Sparta in 404 BC. Its city walls were torn down, and its political dominance ended. The war severely weakened the entire Greek city-state system, leaving it exhausted and vulnerable.

Macedonia and Alexander the Great

  • Rise of Philip II: The weakened Greek city-states created a power vacuum. Philip II of Macedon (382–336 BC) seized this opportunity.
    • He was a military genius who transformed the Macedonian army, introducing the sarissa (an 18-foot pike) and organizing his forces into the highly effective Macedonian phalanx.
    • By 338 BC, after the Battle of Chaeronea, Philip had conquered most of Greece, effectively ending the independence of the city-states.
  • Alexander's Conquests: Philip's son, Alexander the Great (356–323 BC), tutored by Aristotle, inherited his father's powerful army and embarked on one of history's most ambitious military campaigns.
    • In just thirteen years, Alexander conquered the vast Persian Empire, Egypt, Mesopotamia, and pushed into modern India before his troops refused to go further.
    • He died in Babylon in 323 BC at the age of thirty-two, his empire fragmenting among his generals shortly thereafter.
  • The Age of Hellenism: While Alexander's political empire quickly dissolved, his conquests had an immense and lasting cultural impact, initiating the period of Hellenism.
    • Hellenism refers to the widespread dissemination of Greek language, culture, art, and philosophy across the vast territories he conquered.
    • Koine Greek became the common language of educated people and trade from Egypt to Persia, facilitating communication across diverse regions.
    • Alexander founded many Greek-style cities, which served as centers for Greek culture and became crucial trade and communication hubs.
    • This cultural unity proved far more durable than Alexander's political empire, creating a "single political space" where Greek influence was pervasive.
    • The legacy of Hellenism is profound: the New Testament was written in Koine Greek, the great Library of Alexandria was a Hellenistic institution, and the Stoic philosophy that influenced early Christianity was a product of this era. The infrastructure of Greek-speaking cities and roads laid by Alexander's era would later be used by missionaries like Paul to spread the Gospel.

Review Questions

  1. How did the Persian Wars, particularly the battles of Marathon and Salamis, fundamentally alter the trajectory of Athens and contribute to the flourishing of its Golden Age? What specific resources and shifts in confidence did these victories bring?
  2. Discuss the key features of Athenian democracy under Pericles, including its achievements and its inherent limitations. In what ways did Pericles' Funeral Oration capture the ideal of Athenian citizenship, and how does historical context challenge this ideal?
  3. Analyze the principal causes and consequences of the Peloponnesian War. How did this conflict signify the decline of Athenian political power and permanently weaken the independent Greek city-state system?
  4. Describe the military innovations of Philip II of Macedon and how they enabled him to conquer Greece. How did his son, Alexander the Great, then build upon this foundation to create his vast empire, and what was the immediate fate of that empire after his death?
  5. Explain the concept of Hellenism and its lasting impact beyond Alexander's political empire. How did the spread of Greek language, culture, and urban centers during the Hellenistic period influence subsequent historical developments, particularly in areas like religion and intellectual life?
