Study Guide: Reading the Greeks: Drama and Philosophy

Overview

This section explores the profound impact of Greek drama and philosophy, highlighting how theater served as a civic and religious forum for Athenians to collectively grapple with deep moral questions. It introduces key tragedians like Aeschylus and Sophocles, whose plays probed themes of justice, fate, and law, as well as the foundational philosophy of Socrates, as recorded by Plato, which championed the "examined life." Ultimately, the section emphasizes the enduring relevance of these ancient texts and their unexpected alignment with certain Christian ethical principles.

Key Concepts

  • Greek Drama as Civic Religion: In Athens, attending the theater was a public, state-funded event integral to civic and religious life, serving as a communal space for citizens to reflect on moral, ethical, and theological questions.
  • The Purpose of Tragedy: Greek tragedies, particularly those by Aeschylus and Sophocles, explored fundamental human dilemmas such as justice, vengeance, fate, and the complex relationship between human beings and the gods.
  • Human Law vs. Divine/Natural Law: Exemplified in Sophocles' Antigone, this concept highlights the conflict between the laws of the state and higher, unwritten moral laws believed to be eternal and universal, transcending human decree.
  • The Examined Life: A core principle of Socrates, as presented by Plato, stating that a life without critical self-reflection, philosophical inquiry, and a pursuit of truth is not worth living.
  • Natural Law (as intuited by Greeks): The idea, discernible in Greek thought even without special revelation, that certain moral truths are inherent, eternal, and not invented by human societies, aligning with principles later articulated by Christian thinkers.
  • The Priority of Conscience/God over State: Both Antigone and Socrates demonstrate a willingness to defy human authority when it conflicts with what they perceive as higher, divine commands or moral imperatives.
  • Enduring Relevance of Greek Thought: The ethical and philosophical questions raised by Greek drama and philosophy continue to be pertinent across cultures and time, influencing Western thought and sparking ongoing moral debates.
  • Intersection of Greek Thought and Christianity: The recognition that some moral intuitions of the ancient Greeks (e.g., the destructiveness of pride, the existence of higher laws, the importance of the soul) resonate with Christian teachings, leading early Church Fathers to engage with and "baptize the best of" Greek philosophy.

Important Terms

  • Dionysus: The Greek god of wine, fertility, ritual madness, and theatre, in whose honor dramatic festivals were held.
  • Tragedians: Playwrights who wrote tragedies; the three great tragedians mentioned are Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides.
  • Aeschylus (c. 525–456 BC): One of the earliest Greek tragedians, author of the Oresteia trilogy, which includes Agamemnon.
  • Agamemnon: The first play in Aeschylus's Oresteia, depicting King Agamemnon's return from Troy and his murder by his wife, Clytemnestra.
  • Clytemnestra: Agamemnon's wife, who murders him as revenge for his sacrifice of their daughter, Iphigenia.
  • Sophocles (c. 497–406 BC): A highly regarded Greek tragedian, author of Antigone.
  • Antigone: The protagonist of Sophocles' play, who defies King Creon's decree to bury her brother, arguing for the supremacy of divine law.
  • Creon: The king of Thebes in Antigone, who insists on the absolute authority of state law.
  • Polynices: Antigone's brother, whom King Creon declares a traitor and forbids burial.
  • Natural Law: The philosophical concept that there are universal moral principles inherent in nature, discoverable by human reason, that are not dependent on human legislation.
  • Socrates (469–399 BC): An Athenian philosopher, known for his method of questioning (Socratic method) and his belief in the "examined life." He wrote nothing himself.
  • Plato: Socrates' most famous student, whose dialogues (e.g., The Apology) are our primary source for Socrates' teachings and life.
  • The Apology: Plato's account of Socrates' defense speech during his trial before an Athenian jury.
  • Examined Life: Socrates' dictum that "the unexamined life is not worth living," emphasizing critical self-reflection and philosophical inquiry.
  • Logos: A Greek term meaning "word," "reason," or "divine principle," used by Justin Martyr to connect Socrates' philosophical guidance to the divine Word.
  • Arete: A Greek concept of excellence or virtue. The section notes its evolution from Homeric martial prowess to Sophoclean moral integrity and adherence to higher laws.

Detailed Notes

Greek Drama: Theater as Civic Religion

  • Central Role in Athenian Life: In Athens, theatre was not mere entertainment but a fundamental civic and religious duty. Plays were performed at the festival of Dionysus, a religious event, and were funded publicly, drawing thousands of citizens.
  • Athens Thinking Out Loud: Drama served as a vital forum for Athenians to publicly debate, explore, and come to terms with complex societal, moral, and existential questions.
  • The Great Tragedians:
    • Aeschylus (c. 525–456 BC): A veteran of the Battle of Marathon, Aeschylus is known for his profound exploration of justice and vengeance. His Oresteia is the only complete tragic trilogy to survive.
      • Agamemnon: The first play of the Oresteia, it opens with a watchman awaiting news of Troy's fall. King Agamemnon's triumphant return leads to his murder by his wife Clytemnestra, driven by revenge for their daughter Iphigenia's sacrifice. The play raises deep questions about justice and cycles of vengeance. Aeschylus often gives "the clearest moral vision to ordinary characters rather than heroes," as seen with the Watchman's soliloquy.
    • Sophocles (c. 497–406 BC): Often considered the greatest tragedian, Sophocles explored the conflicts between individual conscience and state authority.
      • Antigone: Presents one of literature's most powerful conflicts: human law (King Creon's decree) versus divine, unwritten law (Antigone's belief). Antigone defies Creon by burying her traitor brother, Polynices, insisting that divine laws are eternal and superior to any king's edict. The play ends tragically, with both Antigone and Creon dying, neither fully winning.
      • Relevance to Christian Ethics: Antigone directly poses questions about allegiance to the state versus God/conscience, echoing dilemmas faced by early Christian martyrs and figures like Thomas More. Sophocles' articulation of "immutable unwritten laws of Heaven" aligns with the concept of natural law, demonstrating an intuition of universal moral truths even without special revelation.
  • Comedy: Playwrights like Aristophanes used comedy to satirize public figures, politicians, and philosophers, providing another avenue for civic critique.

Plato: The Apology of Socrates

  • Socrates (469–399 BC): A pivotal Athenian philosopher who wrote nothing himself. Our understanding of him comes primarily through the writings of his students (Plato, Xenophon) and his critics (Aristophanes).
    • The "Plato's Socrates" Puzzle: It's crucial to remember that what we read as "Socrates said" is filtered through Plato's interpretation, memory, and philosophical agenda, making it more literature illuminating a historical figure than a direct transcript.
  • The Apology: Plato's detailed account of Socrates' defense at his trial before an Athenian jury of 501 citizens. Socrates was charged with impiety and corrupting the youth.
    • Socrates' Extraordinary Defense: Instead of seeking mercy, Socrates famously argued for the profound value of philosophical inquiry and self-examination.
    • The "Examined Life": Socrates asserted that "the unexamined life is not worth living," considering daily discourse about virtue and examining oneself and others to be "the greatest good of man."
    • Obeying God over Men: Socrates declared his unwavering commitment to philosophy as a "command of God," stating, "I shall obey God rather than you." He preferred death to ceasing his philosophical mission.
    • Outcome: Despite his compelling defense, Socrates was convicted by a vote of 280–221 and subsequently executed. This event is a sobering historical reminder that democracy does not inherently guarantee wisdom or protect intellectual freedom.

Why These Texts Still Matter

  • Profound Historical Influence: Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Plato profoundly shaped Western thought for millennia. Their works were read by Roman poets, studied by Church Fathers, taught in medieval universities, debated by Reformers, and known by American founders like Thomas Jefferson.
  • Continuing Urgency and Relevance:
    • Antigone's Dilemma: The conflict between state commands and individual conscience remains a perpetually urgent question, reflected in the choices of figures from Thomas More (1535) to Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1945) and conscientious objectors in every era.
    • Socrates' "Examined Life": The conviction that we must rigorously think about what is true, good, and beautiful is as necessary today as it was in ancient Athens for personal and societal flourishing.
  • Greek Thought and Christian Ethics ("Then and Now"):
    • Shared Moral Intuitions: Remarkably, Greek tragedy and philosophy, produced in a society without special revelation (church or Bible), often arrived at moral insights that Christians recognize as true. Examples include:
      • Pride's destructive nature (Agamemnon).
      • The existence of moral laws superior to human legislation (Antigone).
      • The greater importance of the soul over the body (Socrates).
    • Romans 2:15 Connection: This phenomenon aligns with Apostle Paul's observation that Gentiles "show the work of the law written in their hearts."
    • Early Church Engagement: The early Church did not reject Greek philosophy outright but "baptized the best of it."
      • Augustine drew on Platonic ideas regarding the soul's journey toward transcendent truth.
      • Thomas Aquinas integrated Aristotle's logic into Christian theology.
      • Justin Martyr (2nd century AD) even considered Socrates "in a measure a Christian" because he was guided by the divine Logos (Word) before the Incarnation.
    • Conclusion: This allows Christians to approach Greek philosophy with gratitude and discernment, recognizing genuine glimpses of truth within it, rather than with suspicion.
  • Evolution of Arete: The Greek concept of arete (excellence or virtue) evolved significantly. While Homer's heroes like Achilles valued arete as personal glory and military courage, Sophocles' Antigone embodies a different kind of arete – moral integrity and adherence to higher, unwritten laws, even at the cost of her life.

Review Questions

  1. How did Greek drama function as a "civic religion" in Athens, and what kind of questions did tragedies like Agamemnon and Antigone prompt Athenians to consider collectively?
  2. Explain the central conflict in Sophocles' Antigone. How does Antigone's argument for "unwritten laws of Heaven" relate to the concept of natural law, and why is this conflict still profoundly relevant today?
  3. What do we primarily learn about Socrates through Plato's Apology? Discuss Socrates' core beliefs, particularly the "examined life" and his stance on obeying God over men, and why these ideas were revolutionary.
  4. The textbook notes that Athens, the birthplace of democracy, executed its greatest philosopher for asking too many questions. What historical lesson can be drawn from this event regarding the potential limitations or dangers of democracy and its relationship with wisdom?
  5. How does the "Then and Now" section bridge Greek thought with Christian ethics? Provide specific examples of how early Church Fathers engaged with Greek philosophy and discuss the concept of arete evolving from Homer to Sophocles.
