Study Guide: From the Incarnation to the Nicene Council

Overview

This section explores the historical context into which Jesus was born, detailing the powerful Roman Empire and the unique conditions of the Pax Romana that inadvertently facilitated the rapid spread of Christianity. It covers the life, revolutionary ministry, and resurrection of Jesus, the foundational events of the early Church from Pentecost through the missionary journeys of Paul, and the periods of Roman persecution that tested but ultimately strengthened the Christian movement up to the early 4th century.

Key Concepts

  • Pax Romana and its Role: The "Roman peace" (27 BC – AD 180) established by Augustus Caesar, which unified the Mediterranean world under one government, a shared road system, and a common language (Koine Greek), creating ideal conditions for the Gospel's swift dissemination.
  • The Fullness of Time (Providence): The theological idea, articulated by figures like Paul and Justin Martyr, that God providentially prepared the world through both Jewish Scripture and Greek philosophy, alongside Roman political and infrastructural developments, for the Incarnation and the spread of Christianity.
  • Historical Reality of Jesus and the Resurrection: Supported by both hostile/neutral secular sources (Josephus, Tacitus) and early Christian accounts (Paul's letters), emphasizing Jesus's historical existence, crucifixion, and the physically attested resurrection as the central, transformative event of Christian faith.
  • Revolutionary Teaching of Jesus: Jesus's teachings, particularly the Sermon on the Mount, challenged conventional wisdom, asserted his divine authority over the Torah, and included claims (forgiving sins, accepting worship) that led to his crucifixion.
  • The Birth and Growth of the Early Church: Initiated by the outpouring of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, the Church quickly grew in Jerusalem, led by figures like Peter and James, before expanding outwards through missionaries like Paul.
  • Paul's Missionary Strategy and Roman Citizenship: Paul leveraged Roman roads and his citizenship to travel extensively, preach first in synagogues, establish churches across Asia Minor and Greece, and use legal protections to his advantage. His letters form the earliest New Testament writings.
  • Persecution and Martyrdom: The Roman state, beginning with Nero, periodically persecuted Christians, viewing them as a "mischievous superstition." While brutal, these persecutions, including the severe Diocletianic Persecution, paradoxically strengthened the Church, with martyrs' courage drawing new converts ("the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church").

Important Terms

  • Pax Romana: Latin for "Roman peace." A period of relative political stability and absence of major wars throughout the Roman Empire, from Augustus (27 BC) to Marcus Aurelius (AD 180). Its conditions (safe roads, shared language, single legal system) were crucial for Christianity's spread.
  • Messiah: Hebrew for "anointed one," a promised deliverer expected by the Jewish people, traditionally a conquering king.
  • Koine Greek: The common spoken dialect of Greek, spread by Alexander's conquests, that served as the lingua franca (common language) of the eastern Roman Empire, enabling widespread communication of the Gospel.
  • Incarnation: The Christian doctrine that God became flesh in the person of Jesus of Nazareth.
  • Apostles: The twelve chief disciples of Jesus, chosen to be the inner circle of his new covenant community and primary witnesses of his life, ministry, and resurrection.
  • Pentecost: A major Jewish festival, fifty days after Passover. For Christians, it marks the descent of the Holy Spirit upon the apostles, signifying the birth of the Church.
  • Martyr: A person who willingly suffers death rather than renounce their religion or cause. The courage of early Christian martyrs was a powerful witness.
  • Pharisee: A member of an ancient Jewish sect, distinguished by strict observance of the traditional and written law. Paul was a Pharisee.

Detailed Notes

The World Into Which Jesus Was Born

  • Roman Hegemony: Jesus was born at the height of the Roman Empire's power, under Augustus Caesar (27 BC–AD 14), who initiated the Pax Romana.
    • Pax Romana Benefits: Two centuries of relative peace, unified government, extensive road system, and a shared language (Koine Greek) across the Mediterranean world. These conditions significantly aided the spread of Christianity.
  • Judea's Context: The Roman province of Judea was governed by a client king, Herod the Great (d. 4 BC), known for rebuilding the Jerusalem Temple and his cruelty.
    • Double Authority: Jews lived under Roman taxation/occupation and the authority of the Torah/Temple priesthood.
    • Messianic Expectations: Many Jews awaited a "Messiah," typically envisioned as a conquering king.
  • "The Fullness of Time": Early Christians saw God's providential hand in these historical circumstances (Galatians 4:4).
    • Justin Martyr (c. AD 150): Argued God prepared the world through Jewish Scripture and Greek philosophy.
    • Historical Observation: The Gospel spread faster in the 1st century due to political unity, over 50,000 miles of Roman roads, and Koine Greek as a common language, which protected missionaries (like Paul) and facilitated communication.

The Life, Ministry, and Resurrection of Jesus

  • Early Life and Ministry:
    • Born in Bethlehem (c. 4 BC), grew up in Galilee.
    • Baptized by John the Baptist (c. AD 27–30), marking the start of his three-year public ministry.
    • Traveled Galilee and Judea, teaching, healing, and calling twelve apostles.
  • Revolutionary Teachings:
    • Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7): Challenged conventional wisdom (e.g., blessing the meek, loving enemies).
    • Claims of Authority: Asserted personal authority over the Torah ("You have heard...but I say"), forgave sins, and accepted worship, leading Jerusalem religious authorities to deem him a blasphemer.
  • Crucifixion and Resurrection:
    • Entered Jerusalem on Palm Sunday (c. AD 30 or 33) to popular acclaim.
    • Arrested at the instigation of chief priests, tried by Pontius Pilate, and crucified (Roman execution for slaves/enemies of the state). His followers fled.
    • Resurrection: Three days later, multiple eyewitnesses (Mary Magdalene, women at the tomb, Peter and John, the twelve apostles, Paul) reported encountering him alive.
      • Central Christian Claim: Regarded as a bodily, physical event, not a vision. Paul's earliest account (1 Cor. 15:3–8, c. AD 54–55) names living witnesses.
      • Historical Impact: Transformed fearful disciples into a movement willing to die for their testimony; the pivotal fact for the entire New Testament.
  • Secular Corroboration:
    • Josephus (c. AD 93): Described Jesus as a "wise man" who "performed surprising feats" and whose followers reported his post-crucifixion appearances.
    • Tacitus (c. AD 116): Confirmed "Christus suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus." These non-Christian accounts attest to Jesus's historical reality and execution.

The Early Church: Pentecost to Paul

  • Pentecost (c. AD 30 or 33): Fifty days after the Resurrection, the Holy Spirit descended on the disciples in Jerusalem. They spoke in other languages, allowing Jews from across the Diaspora to hear the Gospel. Peter preached, leading to 3,000 baptisms and the birth of the Church.
  • Jerusalem Church: Led by Peter and James (Jesus's brother). Rapid growth despite opposition.
    • First Martyr: Stephen, one of the first deacons, was stoned to death (c. AD 35), witnessed by Saul of Tarsus.
  • Paul (Saul) of Tarsus:
    • Background: Roman citizen, Pharisee trained under Gamaliel, energetic persecutor of Christians.
    • Conversion: Encounter with the risen Christ on the road to Damascus (c. AD 33–35) blinded and permanently transformed him.
    • Missionary Journeys (c. AD 46–57): Carried the Gospel from Antioch through Asia Minor and Greece to Rome.
      • Utilized Roman Infrastructure: Traveled Roman roads, leveraged Roman citizenship for legal protection (e.g., appeal to Caesar, Acts 25:11).
      • Strategy: Preached first in synagogues to those familiar with Hebrew Scriptures, weathered opposition, planted churches, and then moved on.
      • Epistles: His letters to these young churches are the earliest written documents of the New Testament.
    • Arrest and Martyrdom: Arrested in Jerusalem (c. AD 57), appealed to Caesar, spent two years under house arrest in Rome (c. AD 60–62), and was likely martyred under Nero (beheaded as a citizen).

Persecution and the Martyrs

  • Nero's Persecution (AD 64): The first organized state persecution. Nero blamed Christians for the Great Fire of Rome.
    • Brutal Tactics: Tacitus described Christians sewn into animal skins, exposed to dogs, or burned as human torches.
    • Martyrdoms: Tradition holds Peter was crucified upside down, and Paul was beheaded in Rome during this time.
  • Varying Intensity: Persecution was not constant terror but permanent legal vulnerability.
    • "Good Emperors" (Nerva, Trajan, Hadrian, Antoninus Pius, Marcus Aurelius): Policies varied. Trajan was moderate ("do not hunt them down"), Hadrian discouraged false accusations, but Marcus Aurelius was more hostile in practice.
  • Later Persecutions:
    • Domitian (AD 81–96)
    • Trajan (AD 98–117)
    • Decius (AD 249–251)
    • Diocletian (AD 284–305): Launched the "Great" Persecution (AD 303–311), the most severe. Churches destroyed, Scriptures confiscated, clergy imprisoned, forced sacrifices to Roman gods on pain of death.
  • Impact of Persecution:
    • "The blood of the martyrs was the seed of the Church" (Tertullian): Persecutions, far from stamping out Christianity, produced public witnesses whose courage and steadfastness drew converts.
    • Ignatius of Antioch (d. AD 107/108): Bishop martyred under Trajan; his letters remain powerful documents of early Christianity.

Review Questions

  1. How did the political, infrastructural, and linguistic conditions of the Pax Romana directly contribute to the rapid spread of the Christian Gospel in the first century?
  2. Describe the key elements of Jesus's public ministry and teachings that were considered revolutionary or controversial by the religious authorities of his time.
  3. Based on the text, what evidence, both secular and Christian, supports the historical reality of Jesus and the Resurrection, and why is the Resurrection considered the central claim of Christianity?
  4. Trace the trajectory of Paul's life from his background as a Pharisee to his role as a missionary, highlighting how his Roman citizenship and strategic approach impacted his evangelistic efforts.
  5. Discuss the nature and severity of Roman persecutions against Christians, citing specific emperors or events. How did these persecutions, despite their brutality, ultimately contribute to the growth and resilience of the Church, as suggested by Tertullian?
