Shakespeare, Plutarch, and Cicero: Two Ways of Telling the Truth

Overview

This section explores how William Shakespeare adapted historical accounts, primarily Plutarch's Parallel Lives, to create his play The Tragedy of Julius Caesar. It highlights the crucial distinction between a historian's obligation to factual accuracy and a dramatist's goal to illuminate human nature and universal truths, even if it means inventing or rearranging events. By comparing Shakespeare's portrayal of key characters and events with historical records, the section reveals how both forms of "truth" are valuable.

Key Concepts

  • Historical Truth vs. Dramatic Truth: A historian seeks to recount what actually happened (historical accuracy), while a dramatist aims to reveal something fundamental about human nature or a situation (literary truth and dramatic plausibility), often by altering historical facts. Both types of truth are important.
  • Adaptation and Interpretation: Shakespeare did not simply copy history; he interpreted and reshaped it to serve his dramatic purposes, making characters and events more impactful for a theatrical audience.
  • Rhetoric and Persuasion: The section showcases the powerful and often dangerous nature of rhetoric through Antony's funeral speech, demonstrating how language can be used to manipulate public opinion and incite action.
  • Character Portrayal for Dramatic Effect: Shakespeare altered the historical personalities of figures like Cassius, Caesar, Brutus, and Antony to create clearer moral contrasts, heighten dramatic tension, and enhance the tragic elements of his play.
  • The Nuance of "Truth": Different contexts (historical, dramatic, psychological) require different understandings of what constitutes "truth."

Important Terms

  • Plutarch's Parallel Lives: A collection of biographies of famous Greeks and Romans, written by the Greek historian Plutarch, which served as Shakespeare's primary source for Julius Caesar.
  • Thomas North's Translation: The 1579 English translation of Plutarch's Parallel Lives that Shakespeare used.
  • The Tragedy of Julius Caesar: William Shakespeare's play, written around 1599, depicting the conspiracy against Julius Caesar, his assassination, and the aftermath.
  • Historical Accuracy: The extent to which an account faithfully reflects events as they actually occurred, based on available evidence.
  • Dramatic Plausibility: The believability of events and character actions within the context of a play or story, regardless of strict historical fact.
  • Literary Truth: The deeper insights into human nature, universal themes, or psychological realities conveyed by a work of literature, even if fictionalized.
  • Rhetoric: The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing, especially the use of figures of speech and other compositional techniques.
  • Colossus: A statue of gigantic size; used metaphorically by Cassius to describe Caesar's overwhelming power.
  • Republican Ideal: The political philosophy supporting a republic, where power is held by the people and their elected representatives, rather than a monarch.
  • Ides of March: March 15th, the date on which Julius Caesar was assassinated in 44 BC.
  • Stoic: A follower of Stoicism, an ancient Greek philosophy that emphasizes virtue, reason, and emotional fortitude in the face of adversity.
  • Philippi: The site of two major battles in 42 BC, where the forces of Mark Antony and Octavian defeated Brutus and Cassius, leading to their suicides.

Detailed Notes

Two Ways of Telling the Truth

  • Shakespeare's Source: In 1599, William Shakespeare drew inspiration from Plutarch's Parallel Lives of the Noble Greeks and Romans, specifically Thomas North's 1579 English translation, to write The Tragedy of Julius Caesar.
  • Distinction between History and Drama:
    • Historian's Obligation: To accurately recount "what actually happened" (historical accuracy).
    • Dramatist's Obligation: To illuminate "something true about human nature" (literary truth), even if it involves compressing, inventing, or rearranging events.
  • Types of Truth:
    • Historical Accuracy: What the historical record states occurred.
    • Dramatic Plausibility: What could believably have happened within the story's context.
    • Literary Truth: What the scene or story reveals about human nature, psychological states, or universal themes, irrespective of exact facts.

Act I: Cassius Tempts Brutus

  • Cassius's Strategy: In Act I, Scene ii, Cassius attempts to recruit Brutus into the conspiracy by appealing to Brutus's sense of Roman honor and his fear that Caesar is becoming a king.
  • Rhetorical Appeal: Cassius's argument plays on Brutus's pride, suggesting they are "petty men" beneath Caesar's "Colossus"-like stature, and invoking the republican ideal of self-rule with the line, "The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars, / But in ourselves, that we are underlings." This demonstrates how dangerous arguments often cloak self-interest in noble principles.
  • Gaius Cassius Longinus:
    • In Shakespeare: Portrayed as a "lean and hungry," jealous, and scheming villain driven by envy of Caesar. Caesar perceives him as dangerous. He is manipulative (forges letters) and politically ruthless (suggests killing Antony).
    • In Plutarch / History: A competent military officer and experienced politician with genuine republican convictions, serving as the chief organizer of the conspiracy. Plutarch presents him as more capable and less villainous.
    • Shakespeare's Change and Purpose: By making Cassius a darker figure, Shakespeare creates a moral contrast with Brutus, whose motives appear purer, thereby sharpening the tragic nature of the play.

Act III: The Assassination and Its Aftermath

  • "Et tu, Brute?": This famous Latin line ("Even you, Brutus?") from Act III, Scene i, is Shakespeare's invention.

    • Plutarch's Account: Plutarch states that Caesar, upon seeing Brutus's sword, "covered his face with his robe and submitted."
    • Shakespeare's Purpose: While not historically accurate, the invented line captures the deep emotional and psychological truth of Caesar's betrayal and shock, suggesting that Brutus's involvement was the ultimate blow.

  • Julius Caesar:

    • In Shakespeare: Depicted as proud, superstitious (but dismissive of warnings), and arrogant. He ignores soothsayers and his wife Calphurnia's prophetic dream, speaks of himself in the third person, and is easily flattered, making him seem oblivious to danger.
    • In Plutarch / History: A more complex figure: genuinely beloved by his troops, personally brave, and politically astute. Plutarch records numerous warnings he received and his epilepsy (which Shakespeare omits). Caesar's decision to go to the Senate despite warnings is attributed partly to a conspirator convincing him it would be cowardly not to.
    • Shakespeare's Change and Purpose: Shakespeare heightens Caesar's arrogance to make the conspirators' fears seem more justified, while still making him human enough for his death to be genuinely tragic. The historical Caesar's complexity would have made for a less clear-cut dramatic antagonist.

"Friends, Romans, Countrymen": The Rhetorical Masterpiece

  • Brutus's Speech: After the assassination, Brutus addresses the Roman crowd with plain, logical prose, appealing to reason and principle. His speech is initially successful in justifying the killing.

  • Antony's Speech: Mark Antony then delivers a masterful rhetorical performance, systematically undoing Brutus's arguments.

    • Strategy: Antony begins with false humility ("I come to bury Caesar, not to praise him") but then uses irony, evidence (Caesar's filled coffers, refusal of the crown), and emotional manipulation (repeatedly calling Brutus "an honourable man" until the phrase becomes ironic, reading Caesar's will, showing Caesar's wounds) to incite the crowd to rage against the conspirators.
    • Impact: This speech is a prime example of how powerful and dangerous rhetoric can be in swaying public opinion and moving crowds towards violence.

  • Mark Antony:

    • In Shakespeare: A politically brilliant, calculating, and masterful orator who single-handedly turns the mob against the conspirators with his funeral speech.
    • In Plutarch / History: A skilled politician who delivered a funeral oration, which indeed led to public anger against the conspirators. However, Plutarch does not record the specific content of the speech.
    • Shakespeare's Change and Purpose: Shakespeare invents the specific, iconic content of Antony's speech, creating a pivotal turning point in the play and one of the most famous examples of manipulative rhetoric in literature, while preserving its historical effect.

  • Marcus Junius Brutus:

    • In Shakespeare: Portrayed as "the noblest Roman of them all," motivated purely by a genuine (though ultimately mistaken) conviction that killing Caesar would save the Republic. His tragic flaw is idealism that blinds him to political realities (e.g., refusing to kill Antony, allowing Antony to speak, tactical errors at Philippi).
    • In Plutarch / History: A more complicated figure with mixed motives: genuine republican conviction, but also indebtedness to Caesar, peer pressure from Cassius, and the burden of his family name. Plutarch notes he was beloved even by enemies.
    • Shakespeare's Change and Purpose: Shakespeare simplifies Brutus's motives to make him purely principled, creating a clearer tragic structure of a good man performing a terrible act for what he believes are the right reasons, only to be destroyed by the consequences.

  • Portia:

    • In Shakespeare: Brutus's devoted, courageous wife, daughter of a Stoic senator. She wounds herself to prove her loyalty and ability to keep secrets, making a powerful impression despite limited appearances.
    • In Plutarch / History: The historical Portia did wound herself to prove constancy, as recorded by Plutarch. Her death occurred before the Battle of Philippi, with some accounts suggesting she swallowed burning coals.
    • Shakespeare's Change and Purpose: Shakespeare romanticizes the marriage and compresses the timeline, highlighting her extraordinary courage and loyalty within the dramatic framework.

Review Questions

  1. Explain the fundamental difference between a historian's primary obligation and a dramatist's primary obligation, using examples from the text regarding Shakespeare and Plutarch.
  2. How did Shakespeare alter the character of Cassius from Plutarch's historical account, and what dramatic purpose did this change serve in Julius Caesar?
  3. Discuss the significance of Shakespeare's invented line, "Et tu, Brute?" in the context of the assassination scene. What kind of "truth" does it convey, even if it's not historically accurate?
  4. Analyze Mark Antony's rhetorical strategies in his "Friends, Romans, countrymen" speech. How does he systematically turn the crowd against the conspirators without directly accusing Brutus of lying?
  5. Consider the portrayals of Julius Caesar and Marcus Brutus in Shakespeare's play compared to Plutarch's accounts. How did Shakespeare's changes to these characters contribute to the tragic elements and themes of his play?
