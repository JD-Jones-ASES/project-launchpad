Augustine, Boethius, and the Christian Response to Collapse: A Study Guide

Overview

This section explores how two pivotal Christian thinkers, Augustine of Hippo and Boethius, grappled with the profound societal and personal collapse of the Western Roman Empire. Both authors, writing in unique and influential literary forms, offer distinct yet converging answers to the question of what truly endures when the world falls apart, ultimately pointing to internal virtues, wisdom, and divine love as the only unshakeable goods.

Key Concepts

  • New Forms of Writing: Augustine's Confessions pioneered the spiritual autobiography, a personal journey of faith and self-reflection, while Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy introduced a philosophical dialogue written under the duress of a death sentence, exploring meaning in suffering. These works set precedents for future literary forms.
  • The Restless Heart (Augustine's Confessions): Augustine's famous assertion that "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee" encapsulates his theological psychology. It suggests that human beings are inherently designed for a relationship with God, and all worldly pursuits ultimately fail to satisfy this intrinsic longing.
  • Sin as Misplaced Love (Augustine's Confessions): Augustine posits that sin is fundamentally loving created things (wealth, fame, pleasure) more than, or instead of, the Creator. He describes his own experience of searching "abroad" for what was "within," mistaking the gifts for the Giver.
  • The Two Cities (Augustine's City of God): In response to the sack of Rome, Augustine developed a profound theological framework distinguishing between the "City of Man" (built on amor sui, self-love and earthly glory) and the "City of God" (built on amor Dei, love of God). This distinction teaches that earthly civilizations are temporal and fallible, while God's city is eternal and indestructible.
  • Enduring Goods Amidst Collapse (Augustine and Boethius): Both thinkers converge on the idea that the only true goods are those that cannot be taken away by external circumstances. For Augustine, this centers on God himself and the virtues rooted in divine love. For Boethius, it's virtue, wisdom, and inner moral integrity, independent of Fortune's whims.

Important Terms

  • Confessions: Augustine's groundbreaking autobiographical work, a prayer, confession of sin, and philosophical journey.
  • Consolation of Philosophy: Boethius's profound philosophical dialogue, written in prison, exploring themes of fortune, evil, and true happiness.
  • Manichaeism: A dualistic religion that Augustine experimented with, which attributed evil to the material world.
  • Rhetoric: The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing; a highly prestigious profession in the Roman world, pursued by Augustine.
  • Tolle, lege: Latin for "Take up and read," the phrase a child's voice uttered, prompting Augustine's pivotal conversion experience.
  • Monica: Augustine's devout Christian mother, whose persistent prayers for his conversion are celebrated in the Confessions.
  • Donatists: A Christian sect in North Africa that Augustine opposed, which held that clergy who had renounced faith during persecution could not administer valid sacraments.
  • Pelagians: A group whose teachings Augustine combated, asserting that humans could achieve salvation through their own efforts and free will, largely independent of divine grace.
  • Amor Sui: Latin for "love of self," which Augustine identifies as the foundational principle of the earthly City of Man.
  • Amor Dei: Latin for "love of God," which Augustine identifies as the foundational principle of the City of God.
  • Eschatology: The part of theology concerned with death, judgment, and the final destiny of the soul and humankind.
  • Lady Philosophy: An allegorical figure who appears to Boethius in prison in Consolation of Philosophy, guiding him through philosophical arguments.
  • Theodoric: The Ostrogothic king of Italy who imprisoned Boethius on charges of treason.

Detailed Notes

I. Reading History Through Faith: A New Kind of Writing

  • Augustine's Confessions: A radically new form of writing, combining prayer, personal confession, and philosophical autobiography. It traces a soul's journey toward truth, laying the groundwork for memoirs and personal essays.
  • Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy: Unique as a philosophical dialogue written by a man condemned to death, debating life's meaning when all external goods are lost.
  • Shared Question: Both authors address the existential crisis of finding meaning and stability amidst the collapse of the Roman world.
  • Shared Answer: They conclude that true goods—virtue, wisdom, and (for Augustine) God—are internal and cannot be seized by external forces.

II. Augustine of Hippo: A Life of Searching

  • Early Life and Ambition:
    • Born AD 354 in Thagaste, Numidia (modern Algeria), to a pagan father and devout Christian mother, Monica.
    • Brilliant and ambitious, he pursued a prestigious career in rhetoric, studying in Carthage, Rome, and Milan.
    • Intellectually and morally restless, he lived with a concubine for 15 years (with whom he had a son, Adeodatus) and explored Manichaeism.
  • Conversion in Milan (AD 386):
    • Influenced by Bishop Ambrose's intellectually robust sermons, which showed him Christianity's philosophical depth.
    • Experienced a dramatic conversion in a garden after hearing a child's voice say "Tolle, lege" ("take up and read"), leading him to read a passage from Paul's letter to the Romans (13:13-14) that brought him immediate peace.
  • Monica's Enduring Influence: His mother, Monica, prayed for his conversion for decades. The Confessions serves as a tribute to her unwavering faith; she died shortly after his conversion, having "completed her work."
  • Later Life and Episcopacy:
    • Returned to Africa, founded a monastic community, and reluctantly became Bishop of Hippo in AD 396.
    • Served for 34 years, preaching daily, writing extensively, and defending orthodox Christianity against major heresies: Manichaeism, Donatism, and Pelagianism.
    • Died in AD 430 as the Vandals besieged Hippo.

III. Augustine: Confessions (AD 397–400)

  • Literary Significance: The first great autobiography in Western literature, structured as a direct prayer to God.
  • The Restless Heart: Its opening lines encapsulate its core theme: "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee."
    • The human heart (the whole person) is inherently designed for God, not for worldly substitutes like wealth, fame, or pleasure.
    • This restlessness is not a flaw but a built-in "hunger" that only God can satisfy.
    • This theme resonates universally, describing the fundamental human search for meaning and fulfillment.
  • Sin as Misplaced Love: Augustine reflects on how he searched "abroad" for God who was "within," loving "fair forms" (created things) instead of the Creator. This defines sin as loving the gifts rather than the Giver.

IV. Augustine: The City of God (AD 413–426)

  • Historical Context: Written in response to the pagan accusation that Christianity caused the sack of Rome in AD 410.
  • Scope: A massive 22-book work covering Roman history, philosophy, theology, and eschatology.
  • The Two Cities: Augustine's central and "devastating" argument:
    • The City of Man (founded on amor sui – self-love): Glories in itself, pursuing its own glory, power, and temporal existence. Rome, though achieving great things, was an example of this city, ultimately destined to fall.
    • The City of God (founded on amor Dei – love of God): Glories in the Lord. Its citizens are intertwined with those of the City of Man on earth, but the City of God is eternal and immune to any earthly destruction.
  • Implications: The fall of Rome should not shake Christian confidence unless they had mistakenly identified Rome with the eternal City of God. This framework encourages Christians to work for earthly peace as a real good but to place ultimate trust and love in God's eternal kingdom.

V. Boethius: Philosopher in Prison

  • Life and Intellectual Project:
    • Born c. AD 480, nearly a century after Augustine.
    • Served as chief minister to the Ostrogothic King Theodoric in Italy.
    • His life's ambition was to translate all of Aristotle and Plato into Latin, to preserve Greek philosophy for the Latin West.
    • His translations and commentaries on Aristotle's logical works were foundational textbooks for over 700 years, shaping medieval philosophy.
  • Downfall and Imprisonment:
    • In AD 523, he was falsely accused of treason for conspiring with the Eastern Roman Emperor.
    • Despite protesting his innocence, he was imprisoned at Pavia, awaiting execution.
  • The Consolation of Philosophy (written in prison):
    • A dialogue in alternating prose and verse between Boethius (in despair) and an allegorical figure, Lady Philosophy.
    • Addresses the profound question of how to find meaning and solace when Fortune has stripped away everything, asking the same fundamental question as Augustine, but from a philosophical rather than theological perspective.
    • The opening "Boethius' Complaint" powerfully expresses his anguish.

Review Questions

  1. Compare and contrast the literary innovations introduced by Augustine's Confessions and Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy. How did these works redefine or expand the scope of writing in their time?
  2. Explain Augustine's concept of the "restless heart" from the Confessions. How does this idea connect to his understanding of sin as "misplaced love," and what solution does he offer for this human condition?
  3. What historical event prompted Augustine to write The City of God? Describe the core distinction between the City of Man and the City of God, and discuss how this framework was intended to reassure Christians during a time of collapse.
  4. Both Augustine and Boethius lived through periods of immense societal upheaval. How did their personal experiences and circumstances influence the central messages of their major works? Provide specific examples.
  5. Despite their different approaches (theology vs. philosophy), Augustine and Boethius converged on a similar answer to the question of what endures when the world falls apart. What are these "true goods," and why are they considered immune to external destruction?
