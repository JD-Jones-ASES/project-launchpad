The Republic: From Kings to Consuls – Study Guide

Overview

This section explores the foundational myths and the historical transition of Rome from a monarchy to a republic. It details the key figures and events that led to the overthrow of the last king, the establishment of core republican institutions like the Senate, Consuls, and Tribunes, and the ongoing struggle between patricians and plebeians that shaped Roman law and governance. The guide also highlights the vital Roman virtues that were believed to underpin the Republic's success.

Key Concepts

  • The Power of Origin Stories: Rome, like other great civilizations, used legends (Romulus and Remus, Aeneas) to explain its origins, define its identity, and transmit core values like pietas (duty), endurance, and honor, justifying its future greatness.
  • Transition from Monarchy to Republic: The tyrannical rule of Tarquinius Superbus and the subsequent outrage over his son's actions (Sextus and Lucretia) led to the expulsion of kings in 509 BC and the establishment of a new government designed to prevent any single person from holding absolute power.
  • Republican Institutions: The Roman Republic was built on a system of shared power, featuring two annually elected Consuls (chief magistrates with veto power), a lifelong advisory Senate (controlling finances and foreign policy), and Tribunes of the Plebs (protecting ordinary citizens).
  • The Struggle of the Orders: A prolonged conflict between the privileged patricians (hereditary nobles) and the common plebeians (ordinary free citizens) for equal political rights, leading to crucial reforms.
  • The Importance of Written Law: The Twelve Tables (Rome's first written law code) were a direct result of plebeian demands for accountability, making the law public, knowable, and binding on all, a fundamental step towards equal justice.
  • Mixed Constitution: Over time, Rome developed a balanced government combining elements of monarchy (consuls), aristocracy (Senate), and democracy (tribunes and plebeian assemblies), which many believed was key to its stability and longevity.
  • Roman Virtues: Beyond institutions, Romans valued moral qualities like virtus (courage, self-discipline for the community), pietas (dutiful devotion to gods, family, state), and gravitas (seriousness, dignity), which they believed made individuals worthy leaders and citizens.
  • Policy of Incorporation: Early Roman expansion involved not just conquest but also offering defeated cities Latin rights or full citizenship, fostering loyalty and integrating populations rather than merely enslaving them.

Important Terms

  • Romulus and Remus: Twin brothers, nursed by a she-wolf, legendary founders of Rome. Romulus killed Remus and became the first king.
  • ab urbe condita: Latin for "from the founding of the city," referring to Rome's traditional founding date of 753 BC.
  • Aeneas: Trojan prince, a hero of a second Roman founding legend, whose journey to Italy symbolized Roman pietas and divine destiny.
  • Pietas: A core Roman virtue meaning dutiful devotion to the gods, to family, and to the state; subordinating personal desires to obligations.
  • Tarquinius Superbus (Tarquin the Proud): The tyrannical seventh and last king of Rome, whose abuses led to the overthrow of the monarchy.
  • Lucretia: A noble Roman woman whose rape by Tarquin's son and subsequent suicide sparked the rebellion against the monarchy.
  • Lucius Junius Brutus: Roman nobleman who led the revolt against Tarquin the Proud and was instrumental in founding the Republic.
  • Republic (res publica): Literally "the public thing"; Rome's system of shared government designed to prevent permanent power in one man's hands, led by two consuls.
  • Consuls: Two annually elected chief magistrates of the Roman Republic, who commanded the army, presided over the Senate, and held imperium.
  • Intercessio (Veto): The power of a consul to block the decisions of the other consul, or a tribune to block a magistrate's action affecting a plebeian.
  • Imperium: The supreme executive power in the Roman Republic, including military command and legal authority, held by consuls and praetors.
  • Dictator: A single magistrate appointed by the Senate in military emergencies, holding supreme power for a maximum of six months, bypassing the consular veto.
  • Senate: The advisory body of experienced former magistrates in the Roman Republic, serving for life, controlling state finances and foreign policy. Its decrees (senatus consulta) held great moral weight.
  • Tribunes of the Plebs: Ten annually elected officials whose persons were sacrosanct (inviolable) and who could veto any magistrate's action affecting a plebeian citizen.
  • Sacrosanct: Legally inviolable; a capital crime to harm the Tribunes of the Plebs.
  • Cursus Honorum: The "path of honors"; the required sequence of political offices a Roman nobleman held to progress in public service (quaestor, aedile, praetor, consul, censor).
  • Patricians: The hereditary noble families who dominated the early Roman Republic, controlling the Senate and major priesthoods.
  • Plebeians: The ordinary free citizens of Rome, who made up the vast majority of the population and struggled for equal political rights.
  • Conflict of the Orders: The long struggle (c. 500-287 BC) between patricians and plebeians for political, legal, and social equality.
  • Secession: The plebeians' tactic of withdrawing from Rome and refusing to participate in its defense until their demands for rights were met.
  • Twelve Tables: Rome's first written law code, produced in 451–450 BC and displayed publicly, making law knowable and binding on all citizens.
  • Licinian-Sextian Laws (367 BC): Legislation that opened the consulship to plebeians.
  • Lex Hortensia (287 BC): Law that declared resolutions of the plebeian assembly binding on all Romans, patrician and plebeian alike.
  • Mixed Constitution: A form of government combining elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy, believed by Romans to provide balance and stability.
  • Virtus: A core Roman virtue meaning courage, self-discipline, and excellence in the service of the community (from vir, man).
  • Gravitas: A core Roman virtue meaning seriousness, weight, and dignity appropriate for public responsibility. The opposite of levitas (frivolity).
  • Latin Rights: Partial Roman citizenship offered to some defeated cities, granting legal protections for merchants but not the full vote, as part of Rome's policy of incorporation.

Detailed Notes

The Stories Rome Told About Its Origins

  • Purpose of Legends: Before written history, Romans told legends not just to record events, but to explain why Rome deserved to rule, revealing its core values.
  • Romulus and Remus Legend:
    • Twin brothers, sons of a Vestal Virgin and the war god Mars.
    • Abandoned on the Tiber, nursed by a she-wolf, raised by a shepherd.
    • Romulus killed Remus in a quarrel and founded Rome in 753 BC.
    • Romans counted years from this date: ab urbe condita.
  • Aeneas Legend:
    • Trojan prince who escaped burning Troy, carrying his father and household gods.
    • Symbolized pietas (dutiful devotion).
    • After wandering, reached Italy and became an ancestor of the Roman people.
    • Virgil later made this the basis of his Aeneid, arguing for Rome's divine destiny.
    • Highlights Roman values: pietas, endurance, honor for ancestors.
  • Why Origin Stories Matter: They answer "Who are we, and what do we stand for?" Even if legendary, they convey real values and civic identity (e.g., Aeneas carrying his father represents honoring parents, gods, and obligations).
  • Livy's Approach: As a historian, Livy often acknowledged uncertainty in legends (e.g., about the Senate's original size), a valued quality in historical sources.

From Monarchy to Republic

  • The Seven Kings: Roman tradition held that six kings ruled after Romulus, some wise, some warlike.
  • Tarquinius Superbus (Tarquin the Proud): The last king; a tyrant who ignored the Senate and executed citizens.
  • The Downfall:
    • Tarquin's son, Sextus, violated the noblewoman Lucretia.
    • Lucretia revealed what happened, made her family swear revenge, and took her own life to preserve honor.
    • Lucius Junius Brutus, a nobleman, seized the moment, rallied the people, and led the expulsion of the Tarquins in 509 BC.
  • Establishment of the Republic:
    • Romans swore never to be ruled by a king again.
    • Power was vested in two annually elected Consuls who shared power.
    • Each consul could block the other's decisions by intercessio (veto), preventing single-person rule.
    • The term res publica ("the public thing") reflects this shift to shared government.

Republican Institutions: Senate, Consuls, and Tribunes

  • Senate:
    • Advisory body of experienced former magistrates, originally patrician.
    • Controlled state finances, foreign policy, and military commands.
    • Senators served for life.
    • Their decrees (senatus consulta) held immense moral weight, almost always obeyed.
  • Consuls:
    • Two annually elected chief magistrates.
    • Commanded the army and presided over the Senate.
    • Both held full imperium (right to command), but each could veto the other.
    • In emergencies, the Senate could appoint a dictator with supreme power for a maximum of six months.
  • Tribunes of the Plebs:
    • Ten annually elected officials, created to protect plebeian citizens.
    • Their persons were legally sacrosanct (inviolable).
    • Could veto any magistrate's action affecting a plebeian.
    • Served as the "common citizen's shield" against abuse of power.
  • Cursus Honorum ("Path of Honors"):
    • The required sequence of public offices for a Roman nobleman.
    • Included: quaestor (treasurer) → aedile (public works/games) → praetor (judge/military) → consul → censor (census/morality).
    • Ensured leaders were tested and experienced.

The Twelve Tables and the Struggle of the Orders

  • Social Division: Early Rome was divided between patricians (hereditary nobles) and plebeians (ordinary free citizens).
  • Conflict of the Orders: The long struggle of plebeians for equal political rights.
  • Plebeian Tactics:
    • Secession: Plebeian soldiers would "walk out" of Rome and refuse to fight.
    • First secession (494 BC) led to the creation of the Tribunes of the Plebs.
  • The Twelve Tables (451–450 BC):
    • Under plebeian pressure, Rome produced its first written law code.
    • Twelve bronze tablets displayed publicly in the Forum.
    • Crucial for accountability: prevented patrician judges from selectively applying unwritten law.
    • Established the principle of public, knowable law, binding on all.
  • Continued Reforms:
    • Licinian-Sextian Laws (367 BC): Opened the consulship to plebeians.
    • Lex Hortensia (287 BC): Made resolutions of the plebeian assembly binding on all Romans, patrician and plebeian alike.
  • Mixed Constitution Achieved: By 287 BC, Rome had a genuinely balanced government, incorporating elements of aristocracy (Senate), monarchy (consuls), and democracy (tribunes/assemblies). This balance was seen as key to Rome's stability.

Roman Virtue: Virtus, Pietas, and Gravitas

  • Romans believed their greatness rested on moral qualities as much as institutions.
  • Virtus:
    • From Latin vir (man).
    • Meaning: courage, self-discipline, excellence in service of the community.
    • Roman heroes' bravery was for the res publica, not personal glory.
  • Pietas:
    • Dutiful devotion to gods, family, and state.
    • Aeneas ("dutiful Aeneas") exemplified this by always prioritizing his obligations.
  • Gravitas:
    • Seriousness, weight, and dignity appropriate for public responsibility.
    • Expected of statesmen: deliberate speech, honor for tradition, serious approach to duties.
    • Opposite was levitas (frivolity), considered un-Roman.

Early Wars and the Expansion of Roman Power

  • Rome expanded its power in the Italian peninsula.
  • Battle of the Latin Way (338 BC): Rome defeated the Latin League.
  • Policy of Incorporation:
    • Rather than enslavement, Rome offered defeated cities Latin rights (partial citizenship) or full Roman citizenship.
    • This policy fostered loyalty and integrated conquered populations, contributing to Rome's growing strength.

Review Questions

  1. Why were Rome's origin stories (Romulus & Remus, Aeneas) important to Roman identity and values, even if partly legendary? What specific virtues or principles did they aim to teach?
  2. How did the reign of Tarquinius Superbus and the incident involving Lucretia lead to the overthrow of the monarchy? What institutional changes were immediately put in place to prevent future abuses of power by a single ruler?
  3. Describe the roles and interrelationships of the Senate, the Consuls, and the Tribunes of the Plebs in the Roman Republic. How did these institutions contribute to Rome's concept of a "mixed constitution" and its overall stability?
  4. Explain the "Conflict of the Orders" between patricians and plebeians. What were the plebeians' most effective tactics, and how did the Twelve Tables mark a significant turning point in this struggle?
  5. Define and discuss the importance of the three core Roman virtues: virtus, pietas, and gravitas. How did these moral qualities shape the expectations for Roman leaders and citizens, and how do they reflect Rome's priorities?
