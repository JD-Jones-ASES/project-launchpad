Study Guide: The Time of Christ to the Council of Nicaea

Overview

Into the world Augustus had made — the Pax Romana — Jesus of Nazareth was born. This chapter examines the historical conditions that made the rapid spread of Christianity possible, the life and ministry of Jesus, the founding of the early Church through Paul's missionary journeys, the Roman persecutions, and the dramatic transformation of the Empire from persecutor to protector of Christianity under Constantine — culminating in the Council of Nicaea (AD 325) and the Nicene Creed that defined Christian orthodoxy for all subsequent centuries. The chapter reads both the New Testament and early Christian writers alongside hostile external sources, showing how both types of evidence converge on the same historical picture.

Key Concepts

  • The Fullness of Time: Paul writes that "when the fullness of time was come, God sent forth his Son" (Galatians 4:4). Early Christian writers recognized that the specific conditions of the first century — the Pax Romana's safe roads, Roman legal protections, koine Greek as a shared language — providentially prepared the world for the Incarnation. The Gospel spread faster in the first century than would have been possible in any earlier period of human history.

  • The Resurrection as Historical Claim: The early Christians were emphatic that the Resurrection was a bodily, physical event — not a spiritual vision or metaphor. Paul's earliest account (1 Cor. 15:3–8, c. AD 54–55) names specific living witnesses. The disciples who had fled in fear at the crucifixion became within weeks a movement willing to die for their testimony. Historians who do not accept the Resurrection must still explain what caused this dramatic transformation.

  • Contextualization — Paul at Athens: Paul's method at the Areopagus — quoting Stoic poets, referencing the altar "To the Unknown God," beginning where his audience already was — illustrates the missionary principle of contextualization: finding the best point of connection between an unfamiliar message and the audience's existing knowledge. He does not compromise the Gospel; he finds the best door through which to introduce it.

  • The Logos and Greek Philosophy: John's Gospel opens with "In the beginning was the Word (Logos)." The Stoics taught that the Logos was an impersonal divine rational principle ordering the universe. John claims this principle became a specific human being in history. The phrase "the Word was made flesh" is the entire Incarnation doctrine in four Greek words. This deliberate engagement with Greek philosophy is part of Christianity's claim to speak to the entire human world, not just Jewish culture.

  • Martyrdom as Witness: The Greek word martys originally meant simply "witness." For the early Church, the supreme witness was one who testified with his life. Ignatius of Antioch sought death not from a death wish but from theological consistency: if Christ's death and resurrection are the central facts of Christian life, then dying in imitation of Christ is not tragedy but fulfillment. Tertullian's "the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church" captures the ironic result: persecutions produced converts, not disappearance.

  • The Hostile Witness Principle: Tacitus called Christianity "a most mischievous superstition" yet confirms Jesus was executed under Pontius Pilate. Pliny the Younger, puzzled about what to do with Christians, describes their practices from the outside with no religious agenda — yet confirms they worshipped Christ as a god and lived ethically. When hostile witnesses confirm the basic facts, those facts carry particular historical weight.

  • Why Nicaea Matters: The Council of Nicaea (AD 325) was not merely a theological debate. It established the principle that the universal Church could meet and define orthodox teaching by consensus, that the emperor could call but not control such a council, and that Christian faith has specific, definable content. The Nicene Creed became the standard by which all subsequent theology was measured.

Important Terms

  • Pax Romana: Latin for "Roman peace." The period of relative stability from Augustus (27 BC) to Marcus Aurelius (AD 180). Its conditions — safe roads, shared language, single legal system — proved essential to the spread of Christianity.
  • Augustus Caesar (27 BC – AD 14): The first Roman emperor; ended the civil wars and established the Pax Romana. Jesus was born during his reign.
  • Herod the Great (d. 4 BC): Rome's client king of Judea; rebuilt the Jerusalem Temple; ruled with notorious cruelty. Jesus was born during his reign (hence the calendar correction placing Jesus' birth c. 4 BC).
  • Pontius Pilate: Roman governor (procurator) of Judea who presided over Jesus' trial and ordered his crucifixion. His existence is confirmed by both Josephus and Tacitus.
  • Josephus (c. AD 37–100): First-century Jewish historian whose Antiquities of the Jews contains the earliest non-Christian reference to Jesus — a "wise man" whose followers reported he appeared alive after crucifixion.
  • Tacitus (c. AD 56–120): Roman historian whose Annals (c. AD 116) confirm that "Christus suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of Pontius Pilatus." Hostile to Christianity — which makes his confirmation more historically significant.
  • Koine Greek: The "common" dialect of Greek spread by Alexander's conquests. The lingua franca of the eastern Roman Empire. The New Testament was written in koine, and Paul could be understood from Egypt to Macedonia without a translator.
  • Pentecost: Jewish festival fifty days after Passover. According to Acts 2, the Holy Spirit descended on the disciples, enabling them to speak in multiple languages. Three thousand were baptized and the Church was born.
  • Paul (Saul of Tarsus, c. AD 5–67): Pharisee, Roman citizen, converted on the Damascus road. Made three missionary journeys through Asia Minor and Greece to Rome. His Epistles are the earliest New Testament writings. Martyred under Nero, c. AD 67.
  • Epistles: Paul's letters to young churches he had founded. The earliest written documents of the New Testament — predating the Gospels by a decade or more. Include Romans, Corinthians, Galatians, and others.
  • Beatitudes: The opening section of the Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5:3–12). From the Latin beatus ("blessed"). Jesus declares blessed those whom the Greco-Roman world considered losers or failures — the meek, the mourning, the persecuted — reversing the conventional moral order.
  • Logos: Greek for "word," "reason," or "rational principle." Used by John in his Gospel's opening line. Connected to the Stoic concept of the divine rational principle governing the universe. John claims this principle became a specific human being — "the Word was made flesh."
  • Nero (r. AD 54–68): Roman emperor who launched the first organized persecution of Christians after the Great Fire of Rome (AD 64). Peter and Paul are traditionally believed martyred under his reign.
  • Diocletian (r. AD 284–305): Roman emperor who launched the Great (Diocletianic) Persecution (AD 303–311) — the most severe Roman persecution of Christians. Retired AD 305; the Empire fell into civil war.
  • Constantine I (r. AD 306–337): The emperor who, after the Battle of Milvian Bridge (AD 312), issued the Edict of Milan (313) and called the Council of Nicaea (325). Whether his conversion was sincere or political, its effects were transformative for world history.
  • Edict of Milan (AD 313): Joint decree by Constantine and Licinius granting religious toleration to all religions throughout the Empire, restoring confiscated Christian property, and ending official persecution.
  • Chi-Rho: The first two Greek letters of Christos (Christ): X and P. Constantine reportedly had his soldiers paint this symbol on their shields before the Battle of Milvian Bridge.
  • Arius (c. AD 256–336): Alexandrian priest who taught that the Son of God was the greatest of created beings but was created — "there was a time when he was not." His teaching was declared heretical at Nicaea.
  • Athanasius (c. AD 296–373): Bishop of Alexandria who defended the full divinity of Christ at Nicaea. Argued that if Christ was not fully God, his death could not accomplish human salvation. Exiled five times: "Athanasius contra mundum" (against the world).
  • Homoousios: Greek for "of one substance." The key word of the Nicene Creed, affirming that the Son is of the same substance as the Father — fully divine, not merely similar to God or a created being.
  • Council of Nicaea (AD 325): First ecumenical council; approximately 318 bishops; called by Constantine to resolve the Arian controversy. Produced the Nicene Creed; declared Arianism heretical.
  • Nicene Creed: The statement of faith produced at Nicaea (325) and refined at Constantinople (381). Still the most widely used Christian confession in the world, recited every Sunday in Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran, and many other churches.
  • Ignatius of Antioch (c. AD 35–108): Third bishop of Antioch; martyred under Trajan. His seven letters written en route to execution are among the earliest Christian documents outside the NT, revealing early church structure and the theology of martyrdom.
  • Pliny the Younger (c. AD 61–113): Roman governor of Bithynia-Pontus whose letter to Trajan (c. AD 112) describes Christian worship practices from the outside — confirming Christians existed in large numbers, worshipped Christ as a god, and lived ethically.
  • Contextualization: The missionary method of finding the best point of connection between an unfamiliar message and the audience's existing knowledge, illustrated by Paul quoting Stoic poets at Athens.

Detailed Notes

The World Into Which Jesus Was Born

  • The Pax Romana
    • Augustus (27 BC – AD 14): ended century of civil war; established two centuries of relative peace
    • Unified the Mediterranean: one government, one road system, one language
    • Safe travel; Roman law protected travelers and citizens
    • Paul would later travel the roads built in previous units of this curriculum
  • Three Key Conditions for Gospel Spread
      1. Political unity: postal system connected Rome to Jerusalem in weeks
      1. Over 50,000 miles of paved Roman roads connecting every major city
      1. Koine Greek: preacher in Greek understood from Egypt to Macedonia without translator
  • The World of Judea
    • Herod the Great: rebuilt Temple, ruled with cruelty, died 4 BC (hence Jesus born c. 4 BC)
    • Roman occupation + Temple priesthood = double authority over Jewish life
    • Jewish expectation: Messiah as conquering king; the one who came was a servant

Jesus: Life, Ministry, and Resurrection

  • Ministry (c. AD 27–30)
    • Baptized by John the Baptist in the Jordan River; beginning of three-year public ministry
    • Twelve apostles chosen; teaching, healing throughout Galilee and Judea
    • Sermon on the Mount: reversed every Greco-Roman value — meek blessed, enemies loved, persecution honored
    • Claimed authority over Torah ("You have heard it was said... but I say"); forgave sins; accepted worship
  • Passion and Resurrection
    • Palm Sunday: triumphant entry into Jerusalem
    • Arrested at chief priests' instigation; Roman trial before Pontius Pilate
    • Crucifixion: Roman method for slaves and enemies of the state
    • Three days later: Mary Magdalene, the Twelve, Paul (1 Cor. 15) report him alive
    • Disciples who fled in fear became within weeks a movement willing to die for their testimony

The Early Church: Pentecost to Paul

  • Pentecost and Peter
    • Acts 2: Holy Spirit descends; disciples speak in multiple languages
    • Peter preaches; three thousand baptized; Church born
    • Stephen: first deacon, first martyr (stoned c. AD 35); Saul of Tarsus watching
  • Paul's Conversion and Journeys
    • Damascus road (c. AD 33–35): encounter with risen Christ; temporary blindness; permanent transformation
    • Three missionary journeys: Antioch → Asia Minor → Greece → Rome
    • Strategy: synagogue first, then wider community; plant church, move on
    • Epistles predating Gospels by a decade: earliest NT writings
    • Arrested in Jerusalem c. AD 57; house arrest in Rome c. AD 60–62; martyred under Nero
  • Paul's Roman Citizenship
    • Protected from summary execution multiple times
    • Right to appeal to Caesar (Acts 25:11): used it to reach Rome
    • "The legal framework the Republic created and the Empire preserved" — direct connection to Unit 3

Primary Texts

  • Sermon on the Mount — Matthew 5
    • Beatitudes invert Greco-Roman honor culture: blessed are the meek, mourning, persecuted
    • Greek makarios: applied to Olympian gods; Jesus applies it to those who suffer
    • "Revolutionary claim about the moral order of the universe" — Aristotle said wealth/fortune needed for flourishing; Jesus said opposite
  • John 1:1–14 — The Logos
    • "In the beginning was the Word (Logos)" — deliberate Stoic philosophical vocabulary
    • Stoics: Logos = impersonal rational principle; John: Logos = Person who can be touched
    • "The Word was made flesh" (ho Logos sarx egeneto): Incarnation in four Greek words
    • Fulfills Greek philosophical longing while radically departing from it: the Greeks' Logos became specific, touchable, historical
  • Acts 17 — Paul at Athens
    • Epicurean and Stoic philosophers at the Areopagus ("Mars' Hill")
    • Paul starts with their altar "To the Unknown God" and their own poets (Aratus)
    • Contextualization: begin where the audience is, find the best door for the Gospel
    • Mixed results: some mocked, some deferred, a few believed (including Dionysius the Areopagite)
  • Acts 26 — Paul before Agrippa
    • Defense as autobiography: from Pharisee to persecutor to Damascus road to missionary
    • Three responses: Agrippa ("almost thou persuadest me"), Festus ("much learning hath made thee mad"), Paul (fully committed)
    • Same Gospel; different approach for different audiences (compare to Athens sermon)

Persecution, Constantine, and Nicaea

  • The Pattern of Persecution
    • Nero (AD 64): first organized state persecution; Great Fire blamed on Christians
    • Domitian, Trajan, Decius, Diocletian: varying intensity throughout Empire
    • Roman legal framework: Christianity not illegal as such, but exclusive loyalty to Christ was permanently vulnerable
    • Trajan's policy: do not hunt them down, but execute those who refuse to sacrifice
  • Ignatius and the Martyr Tradition
    • Bishop of Antioch; arrested under Trajan; seven letters written on journey to Rome
    • "I am the wheat of God, and let me be ground by the teeth of the wild beasts"
    • Martyrdom as theological consistency: if Resurrection is central, dying for Christ is fulfillment, not tragedy
    • Greek martys = "witness"; the supreme witness testifies with his life
  • Pliny the Younger (c. AD 112)
    • Governor of Bithynia-Pontus; asks Trajan how to handle Christians
    • Describes their meetings before dawn, singing hymns to Christ "as to a divinity," ethical oaths, communal meal
    • Perfectly matches Acts 2:42–47 — convergence of hostile external source with internal Christian sources
  • Constantine and the Edict of Milan
    • Battle of Milvian Bridge (AD 312): Chi-Rho sign; "by this sign, conquer"; victory
    • Edict of Milan (AD 313): toleration for all religions; confiscated property restored
    • 313 to 380 (Theodosius): from toleration to official state religion — two generations
  • The Arian Controversy
    • Arius: Son of God is greatest of created beings — "there was a time when he was not"
    • Athanasius: if Christ not fully God, his death cannot save — homoousios ("of one substance")
    • Constantine calls first ecumenical council at Nicaea (AD 325): ~318 bishops
    • Nicene Creed: "begotten, not made, of one substance with the Father"
    • Athanasius exiled five times: "Athanasius contra mundum" — but his position ultimately prevailed

Connections

  • To Chapter 2 (Greece): The koine Greek spread by Alexander's conquests made possible the New Testament, Paul's missionary communications, and the Septuagint. The Stoic Logos concept Paul uses at Athens and John uses in his Prologue connects directly to Zeno's school in Chapter 2.
  • To Chapter 3 (Republic): Paul's Roman citizenship — the legal framework Rome created — protected him on multiple occasions. Roman roads and political unity are direct benefits of the Republic and Empire studied in earlier chapters.
  • To Chapter 4 (Empire): The Pax Romana created by Augustus is the specific historical context into which Jesus was born. The same imperial system that persecuted the Church under Nero and Diocletian eventually became its protector under Constantine.
  • To Chapter 6 (Fall of Rome): Christianity's relationship with the Roman Empire is complex: the same legal system that persecuted the Church also protected Paul. Constantine's conversion was politically motivated as well as personal. When Rome fell, the Church was the most stable institution in the West.
  • Broader Theme — Athens and Jerusalem: The New Testament's engagement with Greek philosophy (Logos in John, Stoic poetry in Acts 17) represents Christianity's claim to speak to the whole human world, not just Jewish culture. "Athens and Jerusalem" — Greek philosophy and Hebrew Scripture — both contributed to the intellectual formation of Christian theology.
