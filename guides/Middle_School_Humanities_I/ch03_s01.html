Reading Rome: Plutarch and Livy on Republican Virtue Study Guide

Overview

This section explores how ancient Roman historians Livy and Plutarch used stories of early Roman heroes to define and promote core Roman virtues. Unlike Greek heroes who sought personal glory, Roman exemplars like Horatius, Cincinnatus, and Regulus prioritized duty, self-sacrifice, and faithfulness to the res publica (the public thing or Republic). Through their moral histories and biographies, these authors aimed to shape the character of their readers, holding up these figures as models of what it meant to be a virtuous Roman citizen.

Key Concepts

  • Roman Republican Virtue: A set of admirable qualities (like courage, duty, and faithfulness) that Romans believed were essential for the survival and prosperity of the Republic. It emphasized self-sacrifice for the community over personal gain or fame, contrasting sharply with the Greek concept of kleos (personal glory).
  • Moral History and Biography: The approach taken by Livy and Plutarch, where history is not merely a record of events but a tool for moral education and character formation. They presented historical figures as "exemplars"—moral examples to be emulated or avoided.
  • Virtus: A core Roman virtue meaning courage, manliness, and excellence, particularly in military or public service. It implies action and bravery in the service of the state, often at great personal cost.
  • Pietas: A deep sense of duty, devotion, and respect towards the gods, one's family, and the state. It encompasses loyalty and a recognition of obligations.
  • Fides: The virtue of faithfulness, trustworthiness, and keeping one's word. Romans considered fides foundational to all social contracts and alliances, essential for civil society to function.

Important Terms

  • res publica: Latin for "the public thing," referring to the Roman Republic or the common good. The welfare of the res publica was paramount for virtuous Romans.
  • kleos: An ancient Greek concept meaning "glory" or "fame," especially that achieved in battle and immortalized in poetry. It was a primary motivation for Greek heroes like Achilles.
  • Dictator: In the Roman Republic, a temporary magistrate appointed with absolute power during times of extreme emergency. By tradition, dictators were expected to resign their power once the crisis was resolved.
  • Consul: One of two annually elected chief magistrates who governed the Roman Republic. They held significant military and civil authority.
  • Etruscans: An ancient civilization located in Etruria (modern Tuscany), north of Rome. They were powerful neighbors and frequent adversaries of early Rome.
  • Pons Sublicius: Rome's oldest bridge, a wooden structure spanning the Tiber River. It played a crucial role in the story of Horatius Cocles.
  • First Punic War (264–241 BC): The first of three major conflicts between the Roman Republic and Carthage, primarily fought for control of Sicily and naval dominance in the Mediterranean.

Detailed Notes

Rome's Stories About Itself

  • Contrasting Heroic Ideals: Roman foundational stories showcased heroes fundamentally different from their Greek counterparts.
    • Greek Heroes: Examples like Achilles sought kleos (personal glory and immortal fame), while Odysseus aimed to return home. Their motivations were often personal.
    • Roman Heroes: Celebrated for duty, self-sacrifice, and putting the good of the res publica above personal wealth, fame, or even life. These stories, whether strictly historical or legendary, articulated Rome's ideals and expectations for its citizens.
  • The Purpose of History: Both Livy and Plutarch viewed history not just as a record but as a tool for moral instruction.
    • Livy: Wrote to provide moral exemplars for a Rome he believed was losing its traditional virtues.
    • Plutarch: Aimed to demonstrate "greatness of character" across Greek and Roman civilizations through comparative biographies.
    • Shared Goal: To use historical narratives to shape the character and morals of their readers.

Livy: Ab Urbe Condita (From the Founding of the City)

  • Author and Work: Titus Livius (c. 64 BC – AD 17) composed a monumental history of Rome from its legendary founding to his own era (142 books, only 35 survive).
  • Historical Approach: Livy was a master storyteller with a clear moral agenda. He openly acknowledged that the earliest accounts were often legendary but deemed them valuable for their moral lessons.
  • Moral Purpose: His preface explicitly states his focus on "their life and their manners" and "their morals," rather than just events or battles. He intended to present heroes as models to emulate and villains as warnings, hoping to inspire a return to past Roman virtues.
  • Horatius at the Bridge (An Exemplar of Virtus):
    • Context: In 508 BC, the Etruscan king Lars Porsena attacked Rome to restore the exiled King Tarquin. The Pons Sublicius was Rome's last defense.
    • The Act: Publius Horatius Cocles, a Roman guard, single-handedly defended the bridge against the Etruscan army while his comrades desperately worked to demolish it behind him.
    • Sacrifice: Horatius expected to die, holding the line to buy time for the Republic. He leaped into the Tiber in full armor and miraculously swam to safety, though Livy notes the uncertainty of this detail.
    • Moral Lesson: This story exemplifies virtus in its Roman sense: courage and self-sacrifice for the community, utterly indifferent to personal glory or reward. His action saved Rome at a critical moment.
    • Comparison to Achilles: While both were great warriors, Achilles fought for personal pride (kleos), withdrawing from battle when slighted. Horatius rushed into mortal danger for the sole purpose of allowing others to escape and save the city, embodying selfless service.

Plutarch: Parallel Lives

  • Author and Work: Plutarch (c. AD 46–120) was a Greek philosopher and biographer who lived under Roman rule. His Parallel Lives compares famous Greek and Roman figures to illustrate character.
  • Purpose and Style: Plutarch explicitly sought to identify and illustrate virtue in history. His work is known for its vivid, anecdote-rich style, which made it highly readable and influential (e.g., a key source for Shakespeare).
  • Cincinnatus: The Man Who Gave Back Power (An Exemplar of Republican Virtue and Pietas):
    • Context: Rome faced a military crisis when the Aequi surrounded a Roman army. Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus, a farmer who had lost much of his land, was named dictator by the Senate.
    • The Act: Cincinnatus immediately left his plow, took command, rescued the trapped army, defeated the Aequi, and then, after only fifteen days, resigned his absolute power and returned to his farm.
    • Moral Lesson: This story powerfully illustrates that power is a temporary burden for the community's needs, not a prize to be seized or kept. It highlights humility, civic duty, and the willingness to relinquish authority for the common good.
    • Legacy: Cincinnatus became an enduring symbol of republican virtue. George Washington was famously dubbed "the American Cincinnatus" for resigning his military commission after the Revolutionary War.
  • Marcus Atilius Regulus: Faithfulness unto Death (An Exemplar of Fides):
    • Context: During the First Punic War (264–241 BC), Roman consul Regulus was captured by Carthage.
    • The Act: He was sent to Rome by the Carthaginians on a solemn oath to negotiate a prisoner exchange and peace treaty, with the condition that he would return to Carthage if negotiations failed. In Rome, he advised the Senate against the Carthaginian terms, arguing they were unfavorable to Rome, knowing this would mean his certain return to torture and death.
    • Sacrifice: True to his oath, Regulus returned to Carthage, where he was brutally tortured to death.
    • Moral Lesson: Regulus's story is the supreme example of fides (faithfulness, trustworthiness). He prioritized his oath and his duty to Rome over his own life and family. Romans believed fides was the absolute foundation of all social order, essential for trust, agreements, and the functioning of society itself. Cicero later highlighted Regulus as the ultimate example of keeping one's word.

Roman Virtue and Christian Virtue: A Comparison

  • Admirable Qualities: Roman virtues like virtus, pietas, and fides (courage, duty, faithfulness, self-sacrifice) are inherently admirable and recognizable across cultures and belief systems. These heroes were willing to give up comfort, power, and even life for something greater than themselves – the Roman Republic.
  • Underlying Motivations: While there are overlaps, the "teacher note" hints at a difference. Roman virtue often had a public dimension, even if not for personal glory, it was for the good of the community and its perceived recognition. Christian virtue, while also emphasizing service and sacrifice, often stresses internal motivation and actions performed for a divine witness, regardless of human observation or reward.

Review Questions

  1. How did the Roman understanding of heroism and virtue, as presented by Livy and Plutarch, differ from the Greek ideal of kleos exemplified by figures like Achilles? Provide specific examples from the text.
  2. What was the shared primary purpose of historical writing for both Livy and Plutarch? How did this purpose influence the way they recounted stories and portrayed historical figures?
  3. Choose one of the Roman exemplars discussed (Horatius, Cincinnatus, or Regulus). Describe their actions and explain which specific Roman virtue they embodied most strongly, referencing details from the text.
  4. Explain the concept of fides within Roman society. Why was it considered so fundamental, and how does the story of Marcus Atilius Regulus serve as its supreme example?
  5. Considering the discussion of Horatius and the "keyconcept" on Roman vs. Christian virtue, what underlying motivations drove these Roman heroes to sacrifice themselves for the res publica? Were they solely driven by external recognition, or was there a deeper sense of duty at play?
