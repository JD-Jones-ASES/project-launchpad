Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide provides a detailed synthesis of Aristotle’s foundational work on moral philosophy, focusing on the nature of the "Chief Good," the function of human beings, and the development of virtue through habituation and the doctrine of the mean.

I. Core Philosophical Concepts

The Search for the Chief Good

Aristotle posits that every human inquiry, action, and pursuit aims at some perceived good. However, goods exist in a hierarchy:

  • Subordinate Goods: Things pursued for the sake of something else (e.g., making bridles for the sake of horsemanship).
  • The Chief Good: A final end that is desired for its own sake and not for the sake of anything else. If no such end existed, desire would be "fruitless and vain." Identifying this goal is critical for conduct, providing a "mark" for the archer to hit.

Politics as the Master Science

Aristotle identifies Politics (politike) as the master science of the human community.

  • Scope: It determines which sciences are studied in a community and coordinates other esteemed faculties like military strategy, domestic management, and rhetoric.
  • Objective: The end of politics is "The Good of Man." While the good of an individual is important, the good of the community or nation is "greater and more perfect to discover and preserve."

The Definition of Happiness (Eudaimonia)

While most agree that the highest good is "happiness" (living and doing well), they disagree on its nature. Aristotle rejects pleasure, wealth, or honor as the ultimate good. Through his Function Argument, he defines happiness based on the characteristic work (ergon) of a human being:

  • Human Function: Not mere life (shared with plants) or sensation (shared with animals), but the "working of the Soul in the way of Reason."
  • Formal Definition: Human good is "a working of the Soul in the way of Excellence," performed over a "complete life." Aristotle famously notes that "one swallow or one fine day does not make a spring," meaning happiness is a stable state, not a fleeting moment.

The Two Types of Virtue (Arete)

Virtue is the excellence that allows a thing to perform its function well. Human excellence is divided into two categories:

  1. Intellectual Virtue: Originates and increases through teaching; it requires experience and time.
  2. Moral Virtue: Result of habituation (ethos). We are not virtuous by nature, but we have the capacity to receive virtues and are perfected through custom.

II. The Doctrine of the Mean

Aristotle defines moral virtue as a "state apt to exercise deliberate choice, being in the relative mean."

Key Principles of the Mean

  • Relative, Not Absolute: The mean is not a mathematical midpoint (like 6 being the mean of 2 and 10). It is "relative to us"—the right amount for the specific person in the specific situation.
  • Avoidance of Extremes: Virtue is the middle state between two vices: Excess (too much) and Defect (too little).
  • Emotional Calibration: Virtue involves feeling emotions (fear, anger, pity, etc.) "when we ought, on what occasions, towards whom, why, and as we should."
  • Non-Universal Application: Some actions (e.g., murder, adultery, theft) and feelings (e.g., envy, malevolence) have no mean; they are inherently bad regardless of the degree.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Aristotle argue that the young are not fit students of Moral Philosophy? Aristotle believes the young lack experience in the "actions of life" and tend to follow their passions. Since the goal of ethics is practice (action) rather than mere knowledge, a student who lives at the "beck and call of passion" will find the study unprofitable.
  2. What is the difference between an "absolute mean" and a "mean relative to us"? An absolute mean is equidistant from extremes and the same for everyone (e.g., 6 is always the mean of 2 and 10). A mean relative to us is the "fitting" amount for an individual; for example, a professional athlete (Milo) requires more food than a novice, so the trainer's "mean" for food differs for each.
  3. According to Aristotle, how do we acquire moral virtues? Moral virtues are acquired through habit and practice. We become just by doing just actions, brave by doing brave actions, and self-mastered by exercising self-mastery.
  4. What is the "Function Argument"? It is the argument that the "good" of any being resides in its characteristic work (ergon). Just as a harp-player’s good is to play the harp well, a human’s good is to perform the "working of the soul in the way of reason" excellently.
  5. What advice does Aristotle give for those struggling to find the mean? One should stay away from the extreme that is more contrary to the mean, take the "least of the evils" as a safe plan, be aware of one's own natural biases, and "force ourselves off in the contrary direction" (like straightening bent timber).

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Stability of Eudaimonia: Compare Aristotle’s view of happiness with the warning of Solon, who claimed no man can be called happy until he is dead. How does Aristotle defend the idea that a virtuous person can maintain a level of "blessedness" despite the "changes and chances" of fortune?
  2. Habit vs. Nature: Aristotle asserts that "not one of the Moral Virtues comes to be in us merely by nature." Analyze his reasoning for this, using his examples of the stone and the development of the physical senses versus the development of character.
  3. The Role of the "Moral Sense": Aristotle concludes that determining the exact point of the mean is difficult and rests with "the Moral Sense" or the judgment of a man of "practical wisdom." Discuss why general rules are insufficient in ethics and why Aristotle believes "exactness must not be looked for" in moral discussions.

V. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Arete: Virtue or excellence; the quality that makes a thing or person perform its function well.
  • Doctrine of the Mean: The principle that every virtue is a middle state between the vices of excess and deficiency.
  • Ergon: Function, work, or characteristic activity of a thing (e.g., the ergon of a knife is to cut).
  • Eudaimonia: Often translated as "happiness," it refers to a state of objective human flourishing, "living well," and "doing well."
  • Habituation: The process of forming character through the repetition of specific actions until they become second nature.
  • Master Science: A science (like Politics) that orders and coordinates other disciplines toward a singular, higher end.
  • Phronesis: Practical wisdom; the intellectual virtue that enables a person to discern the relative mean in specific, real-world circumstances.
  • Politike: Politics; the comprehensive science of the human community and its collective good.
