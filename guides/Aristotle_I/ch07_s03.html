Aristotle on the Ideal State and Education: A Study Guide

This study guide examines the relationship between the individual, the state, and the role of education as presented in Books VII and VIII of Aristotle’s Politics. It explores the philosophical foundations of the "best life" and how a state utilizes education (paideia) to cultivate virtue and sustain its constitution.

Key Concepts and Core Themes

1. The Relationship Between Individual and State Happiness

Aristotle posits that the "best life" for an individual is identical to the "best life" for a state. Both are defined by a life of virtue exercised in accordance with reason. A city cannot be considered genuinely happy based on external goods alone—such as wealth, military power, or territory. Instead, happiness is an activity of the soul; therefore, a city is only as happy as its citizens are virtuous and wise.

2. The Three Sorts of Goods

To achieve happiness, a balance of three types of goods is necessary, though they are not of equal value:

  • External Goods: Wealth, property, power, and reputation. These have a natural limit; excess can be hurtful or useless.
  • Goods of the Body: Health and strength.
  • Goods of the Soul: Fortitude, temperance, justice, and prudence. These are the most valuable; the higher the degree of these qualities, the more useful and noble they become.

Aristotle argues that external goods should be sought for the sake of the soul, not the soul for the sake of external goods.

3. Education (Paideia) as a Political Necessity

Education is not merely a private service or an adjunct to political life; it is the central project of the state.

  • Constitutional Preservation: Every government is preserved by the "manners" or character of its citizens. A democracy requires democratic manners, and an oligarchy requires oligarchic manners.
  • Legislative Duty: The legislator must prioritize the education of youth because neglect of education "is hurtful to the city."
  • Public vs. Private: Since the city has a single "end" (the best life), education ought to be "one and the same" for all citizens and managed as a common care rather than left to private choice.

4. The Curriculum and the Liberal Arts

Aristotle identifies four traditional branches of education and evaluates them based on their utility and their ability to cultivate a "freeborn soul":

Subject Primary Purpose/Benefit
Reading and Writing Useful for the common course of life and for acquiring further instruction.
Gymnastic Exercises Productive of courage and physical health.
Drawing/Painting Sharpens the eye for beauty and enables a man to judge the "beauties of the human form" and the productions of the finer arts.
Music (Mousike) Cultivates the soul’s capacity for "noble leisure" and influences manners/morals.

5. Work, Play, and Noble Leisure (Schole)

A critical distinction in Aristotelian thought is the hierarchy of human activity:

  • Labor/Work: Effort directed toward a necessary end that one does not yet possess. It is often attended by pain or continued exertion.
  • Play/Amusement: A "medicine" for the exhaustion of labor. It provides relaxation but is not an end in itself.
  • Leisure (Schole): The highest form of activity, performed for its own sake. It consists of pleasure, happiness, and an agreeable life.

Aristotle argues that while we must be able to work well, the ultimate goal of education is to teach citizens how to employ their leisure "honourably."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Aristotle, why is it insufficient for a city to possess only wealth and military power?
    • Answer: Because a city, like an individual, cannot be happy without virtue and wisdom. External goods are merely "accidents" of fortune, whereas being wise and just comes from the mind and acts of virtue.
  2. What is the definition of paideia in the context of Greek education?
    • Answer: It encompasses the entire formation of a human being, including the cultivation of character, taste, civic virtue, and emotional education, rather than just academic instruction.
  3. Why does Aristotle believe education should be a "common care" rather than a private matter?
    • Answer: Since the state has one singular end (the best life for its citizens), the training to reach that end must be uniform. Furthermore, the child belongs to the city in the sense that the city’s health depends on the child's eventual character.
  4. How does Aristotle distinguish between "liberal" and "illiberal" arts?
    • Answer: Liberal arts are those fit for a freeman, studied for the sake of oneself, friends, or virtue. Illiberal (or "mean") arts are those practiced for gain or for the service of others, as they "take off from the freedom of the mind" and can deform the body or understanding.
  5. What is the "utilitarian temptation" Aristotle warns against in education?
    • Answer: The temptation to reduce education to mere job training or to measure a curriculum only by its practical utility rather than by how it forms the soul for noble leisure and virtue.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Mirror of the Constitution: Analyze Aristotle’s claim that "the form and manners of each government are peculiar to itself" and that these manners both establish and preserve the state. How does education serve as the mechanism for this preservation?
  2. The Philosophy of Leisure: Aristotle argues that "nature requires that we should not only be properly employed, but to be able to enjoy leisure honourably." Contrast his definition of leisure (schole) with modern concepts of "free time" or "relaxation." Why does he consider music a vital component of this noble leisure?
  3. Virtue as the Source of Utility: Aristotle states that "external goods produce not virtue, but virtue them." Explore this reversal of the common view of success. How does this perspective shift the primary goal of the "Ideal State" from economic or territorial expansion to the moral instruction of its citizenry?

Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
External Goods Resources such as wealth, property, and power that are useful but subordinate to the goods of the soul.
Illiberal Arts Disciplines or employments exercised for gain or for others; considered "mean" because they render the mind "sordid" and unfit for virtue.
Mousike A broad term encompassing music, poetry, song, and dance; used for the education of emotions and the soul.
Noble Leisure (Schole) Free activity directed at what is intrinsically worth doing; the highest end of human life and the primary goal of a liberal education.
Paideia The total formation and education of a citizen, focusing on the cultivation of character and civic virtue.
Play A form of relaxation used as a "medicine" to ease the pain and exertion of labor; not to be confused with leisure.
The Best Life A life of active virtue exercised in accordance with reason; the standard for both individual and state happiness.
