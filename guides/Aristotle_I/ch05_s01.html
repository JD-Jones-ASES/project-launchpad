Aristotle on the State and Citizenship: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide examines Aristotle's political philosophy as presented in "The State and Citizenship." It explores the natural origins of the state, the role of reason in political life, the classification of constitutions, and the ethical implications of citizenship.

I. Key Concepts and Core Themes

Man as a "Political Animal" (Politikon Zoon)

Aristotle asserts that human beings are by nature "political animals." This claim is rooted in teleology—the study of ends or purposes.

  • Teleological Development: Just as an acorn's telos (end) is the oak tree, the human telos is found in the city (polis).
  • The Hierarchy of Community: Human society develops naturally from the household (for basic existence) to the village, and finally to the city.
  • Completion of Nature: The city is the only community large enough to be self-sufficient and organized enough to support justice and "the good life." A human existing outside this community is described as either "a beast or a god."

The Role of Logos

The proof of man’s political nature lies in logos (rational speech). While animals can signal pain or pleasure, only humans possess the capacity to discuss:

  1. Justice and injustice.
  2. The beneficial and the harmful.
  3. Shared norms rather than mere force.

The Purpose of the State

Aristotle rejects the idea that a state exists merely for security, economic cooperation, or class interests. Instead, the state is a natural growth intended for virtue and the good life. It provides the context in which rational and social capacities find their proper expression.

The Six Constitutions

Aristotle classifies governments based on two axes: the number of rulers and whose interest the government serves (the common good versus private interest).

Number of Rulers Correct Form (Common Good) Deviant Form (Private Interest)
One Monarchy Tyranny
Few Aristocracy Oligarchy
Many Polity Democracy
  • Polity: Considered the best practically achievable form for most cities.
  • Tyranny: Identified as the worst form of government.

II. Citizenship and Governance

Citizenship as Activity

Aristotle defines a citizen as one who "shares in the judicial and executive part of the government."

  • Status vs. Activity: Citizenship is not merely a legal status or a document; it is an active participation in the common life of the community.
  • Constitutional Variation: The requirements for citizenship change based on the government type (e.g., wealth in an oligarchy, virtue in an aristocracy).

The "Good Man" vs. the "Best Citizen"

Aristotle observes a potential tension between civic duty and personal morality. A "best citizen" is one who loyally serves their specific constitution. Therefore, in a deviant state like a tyranny, a "best citizen" would be a morally compromised person, creating a rift between civic loyalty and moral principle.

III. Critical Analysis: The Household and Slavery

The Household as a Building Block

The household is the fundamental unit of political life. Aristotle argues that justice must begin within the domestic sphere before it can exist in the city.

The Theory of Natural Slavery

Aristotle defines a "natural slave" as an individual who can recognize reason in others but cannot exercise it himself. He argues such individuals are better off being directed by others.

Refutation of the Doctrine:

  1. Empirical Error: No human group actually lacks the capacity for rational deliberation.
  2. Logical Inconsistency: Aristotle’s own principle—that the human good consists of rational activity—implies that all beings capable of reason should exercise it.
  3. Theological Correction: The Christian tradition, including Thomas Aquinas, corrected this error using the concept of Imago Dei (the image of God), asserting that all humans possess irreducible dignity.

IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What does Aristotle mean by the term telos?
    • Answer: It refers to the natural end, purpose, or goal toward which a thing develops (e.g., a mature human is the telos of an embryo).
  2. How does logos distinguish human political organization from animal social groups?
    • Answer: While animals can signal pleasure and pain, logos allows humans to engage in moral discourse regarding justice, injustice, and the common good.
  3. According to Aristotle, why is the city "natural"?
    • Answer: It is the natural development of the household and village and is the only community where human nature finds its completion and self-sufficiency.
  4. What is the primary difference between a "Correct" and a "Deviant" constitution?
    • Answer: Correct forms aim at the common good, while deviant forms serve only the private interests of the rulers.
  5. Why did Thomas Aquinas reject Aristotle's doctrine of natural slavery?
    • Answer: He utilized the premise of Imago Dei—that every human is made in the image of God and possesses an irreducible dignity that overrides unjust human laws.
  6. What is the "Social Contract" view, and how does it contrast with Aristotle's view?
    • Answer: The social contract view (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau) sees the state as an artificial construction or convention for mutual benefit, whereas Aristotle sees the state as a natural growth essential to human flourishing.

V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Ethics of Participation: Evaluate Aristotle’s claim that citizenship is an activity rather than a status. In the context of modern political apathy, does Aristotle’s definition suggest that a non-participating resident is not truly a citizen?
  2. The Logic of Error: Analyze how Aristotle’s defense of natural slavery contradicts his own philosophical principles regarding logos and the purpose of human life. How does this serve as a lesson for identifying "implicit premises" in philosophical arguments?
  3. The Purpose of Law: Aristotle suggests that because the state exists for justice, an unjust law is "not truly a law at all." Discuss the implications of this view for the relationship between the state and natural law.
  4. Flourishing in Isolation: Critique Aristotle's claim that one cannot achieve the "fully good life" in isolation. Is it possible for human beings to achieve their telos through private virtue, or is political engagement a prerequisite for being "fully human"?

VI. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Aristocracy: A correct form of government ruled by a few who prioritize virtue and the common good.
  • Imago Dei: A theological concept meaning "Image of God"; used to argue for the irreducible dignity of every human being.
  • Logos: Reason, speech, or rational account; the capacity that enables humans to engage in moral and political discourse.
  • Oligarchy: A deviant form of government where the few rule for their own private interest, often based on wealth.
  • Politikon Zoa: "Political animals"; Aristotle’s term for human beings, emphasizing that our nature is fulfilled within a political community.
  • Polity: A correct form of government ruled by the many; considered the most practically achievable form of government for most cities.
  • Telos: The end, purpose, or goal toward which a natural thing develops.
  • Tyranny: A deviant form of government where a single ruler governs for their own private benefit; identified as the worst form of government.
