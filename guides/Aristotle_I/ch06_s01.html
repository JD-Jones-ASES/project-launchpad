Aristotle on Constitutions and Revolution: A Study Guide

This study guide examines Aristotle’s empirical approach to political science, focusing on the practical application of constitutional theory, the importance of the middle class, and the underlying causes of political instability and revolution.

Key Conceptual Overview

Aristotle as Political Scientist

Unlike his predecessor Plato, who envisioned an ideal city based on abstract principles of the Good, Aristotle adopts an empirical methodology. Having studied 158 actual constitutions, he approaches politics as a scientist who observes patterns in history. His work emphasizes that a political theorist must prescribe for real-world cities limited by history, geography, and social composition. For Aristotle, the goal is not to find the "best imaginable" constitution, but the "best achievable" one.

Polity: The Mixed Constitution

Aristotle identifies Polity (politeia) as the most practical constitution for the majority of cities. It is an application of the "doctrine of the mean" to politics—a stable blend of democracy (characterized by broad participation and the rule of law) and oligarchy (characterized by respect for property and merit).

The success of a polity depends on its social foundation: the middle class. Aristotle argues that a large and prosperous middle class moderates the extremes of wealth and poverty, reducing factionalism and encouraging citizens to seek the common good rather than class advantage.

The Anatomy of Revolution (Stasis)

Aristotle identifies revolution not merely as a structural failure of institutions, but as a psychological and social phenomenon. He suggests that the deepest cause of revolution is a perceived injustice in how political power and honor are distributed.

  • Psychological Factors: Envy, ambition, and resentment drive instability.
  • Inequality: Both excessive inequality and a total lack of recognition for merit (too little inequality) are destabilizing.
  • Constitutional Decay: Even a well-designed constitution will fail if its principles are pushed to extremes (e.g., a democracy becoming mob rule).

Constitutional Preservation and Education

For a constitution to endure, laws and institutions are not enough; there must be a citizenry formed in the "spirit of the constitution." This ties politics to ethics: virtue is formed by habit. Citizens must be educated and shaped by the laws from youth so that supporting the constitution becomes second nature.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term Definition
Polity (Politeia) The best practically achievable constitution; a mixed form of government combining democratic and oligarchic elements, supported by a large middle class.
Stasis A state of civil strife, factionalism, or revolution; viewed as a "political disease" that breaks down the unity of the community.
The Mean The virtuous middle ground between two extremes. In politics, it refers to a balance between the interests of the rich and the poor.
Demagogue A leader in a democracy who exploits popular passions and inflames the poor against the wealthy, rather than appealing to reason or the common good.
Empirical Method An approach to knowledge based on the observation of real-world data and history rather than abstract theory.
Deviant Constitutions Forms of government (such as extreme democracy, extreme oligarchy, or tyranny) that serve the interests of the rulers rather than the common good.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Aristotle’s approach to political theory differ from Plato’s?

    • Answer: While Plato focuses on abstract visions of a perfectly just city (the best imaginable), Aristotle uses an empirical approach, studying 158 actual constitutions to determine what is the best achievable city given practical constraints.

  2. Why does Aristotle believe the middle class is essential for a stable polity?

    • Answer: A large middle class moderates the antagonism between the rich and the poor. Where the middle is strong, factions are weaker, and citizens are more likely to seek the common good rather than narrow class interests.

  3. According to Aristotle, what is the primary psychological cause of revolution?

    • Answer: The perception of injustice regarding the distribution of political power and honor, which triggers passions like envy, ambition, and resentment.

  4. What specifically causes instability within an oligarchy?

    • Answer: Oligarchies are destabilized when the rich become too greedy or exclusive, when those shut out from power feel resentment, or when factional conflicts arise among the wealthy themselves.

  5. What is the role of education in Aristotle’s political system?

    • Answer: Education is intended to form the character of the citizens in the spirit of the constitution, ensuring that they develop the habits necessary to sustain and preserve the state over time.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Pragmatism of the Mean: Analyze Aristotle’s claim that "the best constitution is the best achievable, not the best imaginable." How does his application of the "doctrine of the mean" to the middle class support this pragmatic view? Compare this to modern political stability in the 21st century.

  2. The Psychology of Power: Aristotle argues that the ultimate source of political instability lies in human psychology rather than institutional structure. Evaluate this claim. If human passions like envy and ambition are the root causes of revolution, can any institutional design truly guarantee long-term stability?

  3. The Tension Between Stability and Justice: Aristotle’s primary focus in Book V is the preservation of constitutions and the avoidance of stasis. Is there a conflict between the goal of maintaining political stability and the pursuit of absolute justice? Discuss what a citizen's responsibility should be when an existing constitution is fundamentally unjust.

  4. Demagoguery and Democracy: Examine Aristotle's critique of democracy and the role of the demagogue. Based on the source text, explain how a democracy decays into "mob rule" and how the exploitation of popular passion contributes to the breakdown of the political community.
