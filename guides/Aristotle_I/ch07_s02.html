Study Guide: Aquinas, the Liberal Arts, and the Philosophy of Education

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the synthesis between Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology, focusing on the works of Thomas Aquinas, the structure of the medieval liberal arts, and contemporary debates regarding the purpose of education.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

The Aquinian Framework of Law

Thomas Aquinas establishes a hierarchical framework of law where human governance is a participation in Divine wisdom. This structure ensures that education and law are grounded in a stable, rational order rather than mere social convention.

  • Eternal Law: The foundational "plan of government" by which God's wisdom directs all of creation toward its proper ends.
  • Natural Law: The way rational creatures participate in the eternal law. It is not a written code but an orientation of human reason toward the good.
  • Human (Temporal) Law: Laws created by human governors that derive their legitimacy and force from natural law. According to Augustine, nothing is just in temporal law unless it is drawn from the eternal law.

Prudence and Governance

Aquinas identifies prudence (prudentia)—the translation of Aristotle’s phronesis—as the primary intellectual virtue for discerning the right course of action. He categorizes prudence based on the scope of governance:

Type of Prudence Scope of Governance
Prudence simply so called The individual governing themselves (monastic economy).
Domestic Prudence The ruler of a household governing a family.
Political/Regnative Prudence A ruler governing a city or kingdom toward the common good.

Regnative Prudence (prudentia regnativa) is considered the most perfect form of prudence because its scope is universal and its end—the common good of the multitude—is higher and more demanding than private interests.

The Medieval Liberal Arts

The medieval curriculum, or artes liberales, was designed to form the rational and moral capacities of a free person. It was divided into two main branches:

  1. The Trivium (Arts of Discourse): Grammar, Logic, and Rhetoric. These focused on the structure of language, valid reasoning, and the art of persuasion.
  2. The Quadrivium (Arts of Quantitative Reasoning): Arithmetic, Geometry, Music, and Astronomy. These explored the mathematical order of creation in space and time.

The Purpose of Education: Modern Perspectives

The source highlights a tension between education as vocational/civic training versus education as character formation.

  • John Dewey: View education as "life itself," focusing on democratic citizenship, problem-solving, and adaptability through experience.
  • E. D. Hirsch: Advocated for "cultural literacy," a shared body of knowledge necessary for communication and participation in a shared society.
  • Aristotle’s Middle Path: Aristotle prioritizes the formation of character (soul) over both Dewey’s civic utility and Hirsch’s cultural knowledge, though he acknowledges the value in both.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Aquinas define "Eternal Law"?
  2. According to the Summa Theologica, why must all laws be derived from the eternal law?
  3. What is the "law of the fomes," and in what sense does Aquinas consider it a law?
  4. Why does Aquinas argue that prudence must extend to the common good rather than remaining a private virtue?
  5. What are the two virtues Aquinas identifies as most proper to a king?
  6. In the medieval curriculum, what is the specific focus of the Trivium?
  7. How did Hugh of St. Victor view the purpose of the liberal arts in relation to human nature?
  8. What are the four components of the Quadrivium?
  9. According to John Dewey, what should be the primary focus of a school?
  10. What was E. D. Hirsch’s primary critique of "progressive education"?
  11. Why does Aquinas consider "regnative prudence" to be the most perfect form of governance?
  12. How does the concept of "natural law" provide a stable foundation for education compared to "pure pragmatism"?

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Hierarchical Source of Authority: Analyze Aquinas’s argument that all just laws must be derived from the eternal law. How does this theological framework transform Aristotle's natural law, and what are the specific implications for a legislator's responsibility to educate citizens in virtue?
  2. The Degrees of Prudence: Compare and contrast "domestic prudence" and "regnative prudence." Why does Aquinas insist that these are distinct species of habits rather than the same virtue applied to different scales? Use the concept of "formal aspects" and "ends" in your response.
  3. Restoration Through Learning: Discuss Hugh of St. Victor’s claim that the liberal arts serve to restore the "rational image of God" in fallen human beings. How does this view elevate the study of grammar, logic, and mathematics from mere skills to a spiritual and moral endeavor?
  4. The Debate on Educational Ends: Evaluate the modern debate between John Dewey and E. D. Hirsch from an Aristotelian perspective. If the fundamental goal of education is the formation of character, in what ways do both "progressive education" and "cultural literacy" fall short of the classical ideal?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Artes Liberales: The "arts befitting a free person." A curriculum intended for the formation of rational and moral capacities rather than vocational training.
  • Eternal Law: The rational governance of all creation by God's wisdom; the ultimate plan from which all other laws flow.
  • Hugh of St. Victor: A 12th-century Scholastic theologian who argued in the Didascalicon that learning restores the damaged rational image of God in humans.
  • Natural Law: The rational creature's participation in the eternal law; an orientation of reason toward the good that allows humans to discern right conduct.
  • Phronesis (Prudentia): Practical wisdom; the master intellectual virtue used to discern the good and choose the correct means to achieve it.
  • Prudentia Regnativa: "Kingly" or "regnative" prudence; the most complete form of practical wisdom proper to those who govern a city or kingdom.
  • Quadrivium: The four mathematical arts of the medieval curriculum: arithmetic, geometry, music, and astronomy.
  • Trivium: The three language arts of the medieval curriculum: grammar, logic, and rhetoric; the foundational arts of discourse.
