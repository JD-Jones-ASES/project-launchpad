Study Guide: Aquinas and the Philosophy of Law

This study guide explores the legal philosophy of Thomas Aquinas, focusing on his synthesis of Aristotelian thought with theological frameworks. It examines the definition of law, the hierarchical types of law, the precepts of natural law, and the enduring influence of the natural law tradition on political history.

I. Key Concepts and Frameworks

The Definition of Law

Aquinas defines law as "an ordinance of reason for the common good, made by him who has care of the community, and promulgated." This definition consists of four essential elements:

Element Description
Ordinance of Reason Law must be rational and directed by intellect, rather than being an arbitrary decree of will.
Common Good The objective of law is the welfare of the entire community, not the private interests of a ruler.
Made by Authority Law must be established by a legitimate authority who is responsible for the care of the community.
Promulgated For a law to bind rational creatures, it must be publicly declared and made known to those it rules.

The Hierarchy of Law

Aquinas identifies four distinct types of law that form a coherent hierarchy, ranging from divine governance to human statutes.

  1. Eternal Law (lex aeterna): The foundational source of all law; it is God’s rational governance of the entire universe. It is unchangeable and exists outside of time.
  2. Natural Law (lex naturalis): The "participation" of the rational creature in the eternal law. It is the moral order built into human nature and discoverable by reason.
  3. Human Law (lex humana): Specific rules and statutes enacted by human authority. These must be derived from natural law to be valid; if they violate natural law, they are considered a perversion of law.
  4. Divine Law (lex divina): Law revealed through Scripture. It is necessary because human reason is insufficient to grasp everything required for humanity's ultimate end (the beatific vision).

The Precepts of Natural Law

The natural law is based on self-evident principles of practical reason. Aquinas identifies a primary precept and secondary inclinations:

  • The First Precept: "Good is to be done and pursued, and evil is to be avoided."
  • Natural Inclinations: Reason apprehends as "good" those things toward which humans have natural inclinations:
    • Self-Preservation: The preservation of human life and warding off obstacles.
    • Animal Nature: Inclinations shared with other animals, such as sexual intercourse and the education of offspring.
    • Rational Nature: Inclinations unique to humans, such as seeking the truth about God and living in a social community (e.g., shunning ignorance or avoiding offense to neighbors).

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. Why does Aquinas argue that promulgation is essential to the nature of law? Aquinas asserts that law acts as a rule and measure. For a rule to have binding force, it must be applied to those who are to be ruled. This application occurs through notification; therefore, a law only obtains its force when it is publicly declared.

2. How is the natural law "promulgated" if it is not written in human statutes? According to Aquinas, natural law is promulgated by God instilling it into the human mind so that it is known naturally through the light of reason.

3. What is the relationship between the eternal law and the natural law? Natural law is not a separate entity from the eternal law; rather, it is the rational creature’s participation in the eternal law. It is an "imprint" of the Divine light on the human mind, allowing humans to discern good from evil.

4. According to the text, what occurs when a human law is "unjust"? Following the tradition of St. Augustine, Aquinas argues that a law which is not just is "no law at all." It is considered a perversion of law and lacks genuine binding force in conscience.

5. How do the "first principles" of practical reason compare to those of speculative reason? Just as speculative reason is based on the principle of non-contradiction (the same thing cannot be affirmed and denied at the same time), practical reason is based on the notion of the "good." The first principle of practical reason is that "good is that which all things seek after."

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1. The Continuity of the Natural Law Tradition Trace the development of the natural law tradition from Aristotle through Aquinas to the American Founding and the Civil Rights Movement. How does the concept of a "rational moral order discoverable by reason" serve as a foundation for challenging political authority, as seen in the Declaration of Independence or Martin Luther King Jr.’s "Letter from Birmingham Jail"?

2. Classical Tradition vs. Social Contract Theory Compare and contrast the Aristotelian-Thomistic view of the state with the modern social contract tradition (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau). Focus on the origin of the state (natural vs. artificial), the priority of the individual vs. the community, and the ultimate purpose of government (individual interests vs. the common good).

3. The Rationality of Law Analyze Aquinas's claim that law is an "ordinance of reason" rather than a "decree of will." What are the implications of this distinction for the legitimacy of a ruler? If a ruler issues a decree based on private preference rather than reason or the common good, does it meet Aquinas's criteria for law?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Beatific Vision: The ultimate end of human existence, which exceeds natural human capacities and requires Divine Law for guidance.
  • Common Good: The welfare and interest of the entire community, which serves as the primary objective of legitimate law.
  • Divine Law (lex divina): The portion of God's law revealed specifically through Scripture to guide humanity toward their supernatural end.
  • Eternal Law (lex aeterna): The supreme reason and unchangeable plan of God’s governance over the universe.
  • Human Law (lex humana): Particular determinations of laws devised by human reason and enacted by legitimate authority.
  • Natural Law (lex naturalis): The rational creature's participation in the eternal law, enabling the discernment of good and evil through natural reason.
  • Ordinance of Reason: The requirement that law be a rational directive aimed at a specific end (the good), rather than an arbitrary choice.
  • Practical Reason: The use of reason directed toward action and the pursuit of the good, as opposed to speculative reason directed toward the apprehension of truth.
  • Promulgation: The act of publicly declaring or notifying the community of a law, which is necessary for the law to have binding force.
  • Social Contract: The modern political theory that the state is an artificial construction created by individuals to protect their own interests or rights.
