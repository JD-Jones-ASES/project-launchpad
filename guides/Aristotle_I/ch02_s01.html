Aristotle’s Ethics: The Path to Eudaimonia

This study guide examines the ethical framework of Aristotle, focusing on his methodology, the logical structure of the function argument, and the nature of virtue through the doctrine of the mean.

Key Concepts

Aristotle’s Method: Dialectical Analysis

Aristotle’s approach to ethics is distinct from mathematical deduction. Rather than starting with abstract first principles, he employs a dialectical approach that begins with endoxa—the reputable opinions held by most people or by the wise.

  • Treatment of Opinion: Aristotle treats common beliefs as data containing genuine insight.
  • Refinement: His goal is to scrutinize, clarify, and refine these intuitions to identify what is correct and correct what is confused.
  • Connection to Experience: This method ensures ethics remains grounded in ordinary human experience rather than being detached or purely theoretical.

The Function Argument (Ergon)

Located in Book I, Chapter 7, the function argument establishes the definition of human happiness through a logical progression:

  1. Characteristic Function: Every craft, art, and profession has an ergon (a function or purpose). For example, a knife is for cutting and a doctor is for healing.
  2. Excellence as Performance: To be "good" at something is to perform its function excellently (e.g., a good harp-player plays beautifully).
  3. Human Specificity: Humans must have a function specific to them, distinct from the mere life shared with plants or the sensation shared with animals.
  4. The Rational Soul: The distinctive human function is the activity of the soul in accordance with reason.
  5. Definition of Eudaimonia: Human happiness (eudaimonia) is the excellent performance of this rational function.

Conclusion: Human happiness is defined as the active exercise of the soul's capacities in accordance with virtue (arete) over a complete life.

The Doctrine of the Mean

Virtue is defined as a hexis—a stable disposition or settled character trait—that aims at the mean between the vices of excess and deficiency.

  • Relativity: The mean is not a mathematical average; it is relative to the individual and the situation. For example, the appropriate amount of food for an Olympic athlete differs from that of a beginner.
  • Practical Wisdom (Phronesis): The mean is determined by practical wisdom rather than a rigid formula.
  • Emotional Precision: Virtue does not require the suppression of emotion. A virtuous person feels the right emotions at the right intensity, toward the right objects, at the right time, and for the right reasons.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the literal meaning of endoxa, and how does Aristotle use it? Endoxa refers to "reputable opinions" held by the majority or the wise. Aristotle uses these as the starting data for philosophical analysis, refining them rather than dismissing them as mere prejudice.

  2. Why does Aristotle reject "mere life" or "sensation" as the characteristic human function? He rejects them because they are not distinctive to humans; plants share the capacity for life, and animals share the capacity for sensation.

  3. Define hexis and explain its relevance to virtue. Hexis is a stable state or disposition of character. It implies that virtue is not a momentary impulse or a forced act, but a reliable, settled tendency to act and feel in a certain way.

  4. How does eudaimonia differ from hedone? Eudaimonia (flourishing or living well) is an objective state of excellence, whereas hedone refers to sensory pleasure. One can experience hedone while failing at life, or experience eudaimonia while enduring hardship.

  5. What role does phronesis play in the Doctrine of the Mean? Phronesis, or practical wisdom, is the faculty used to determine the mean in specific, varying circumstances where a mathematical formula would be inapplicable.

  6. Does the Doctrine of the Mean encourage mediocrity? Why or why not? No. It is a demanding ideal that seeks "precise excellence." The mean is often a difficult-to-achieve peak between two failings, requiring genuine bravery or joyful generosity rather than lukewarm half-measures.

Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Limits of the Mean: Aristotle suggests that some actions, such as murder or adultery, are always wrong regardless of the circumstances. Evaluate whether these absolute prohibitions undermine or complete the Doctrine of the Mean. Does the existence of "always wrong" actions suggest that the mean is not applicable to all areas of morality?

  2. The Fraudster’s Happiness: Consider the case of a successful fraudster who acquires wealth, honor, and pleasure without being caught. Contrast the common intuition that such a person is "happy" with Aristotle’s definition of eudaimonia. In what ways would Aristotle argue that the fraudster’s life is a failure despite their outward success?

  3. Function and Goodness: Critique the logic of the Function Argument. Is it necessarily true that humans have a single, defining function? Furthermore, does "performing a function well" automatically equate to moral "goodness" for a human being?

Glossary of Key Terms

  • Arete: Virtue or excellence; the quality that allows a thing to perform its function well.
  • Endoxa: Reputable or received opinions; the starting point of Aristotle’s dialectical method.
  • Ergon: Function or task; the characteristic activity of a thing (e.g., the ergon of a knife is cutting).
  • Eudaimonia: Often translated as "happiness," but more accurately meaning "flourishing" or "living well" in an objective, stable sense.
  • Hedone: Pleasure, specifically sensory pleasure.
  • Hexis: A stable disposition or settled state of character.
  • Phronesis: Practical wisdom; the ability to discern the mean and act rightly in specific situations.
