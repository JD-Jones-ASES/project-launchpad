Aristotle’s Ethics: Friendship, Self-Love, and the Contemplative Life

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the final sections of Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics, focusing on the nature of friendship (philia), the philosophical paradox of self-love, the supremacy of the contemplative life (theoria), and the transition into political science.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

The Three Types of Friendship

Aristotle categorizes friendship based on the motive of the relationship. While the lower forms are contingent and fragile, the highest form is stable and essential to human flourishing.

Type of Friendship Primary Motive Characteristics Stability
Utility Profit / Advantage Common among the old or those seeking profit; based on what one can gain from the other. Low: Dissolves once the utility or profit vanishes.
Pleasure Enjoyment / Passion Common among the young; based on immediate gratification or pleasant company. Low: Dissolves as tastes and passions change over time.
Perfect (Virtue) Goodness / Character Based on mutual recognition of virtue; friends wish the good for the other for the other’s sake. High: Permanent as long as the parties remain good; requires time and intimacy.

The Relationship Between Friendship and the State

Aristotle argues that friendship is the "bond of Social Communities" and is even more vital than justice. He identifies parallels between domestic family relationships and political constitutions:

  • Monarchy (Kingship): Analogous to the Father-Son relationship, where the ruler acts for the benefit of the ruled. Its corruption is Despotism (ruling for the ruler's benefit).
  • Aristocracy: Analogous to the Husband-Wife relationship, where rule is based on excellence and assigned to its proper sphere. Its corruption is Oligarchy.
  • Timocracy: Analogous to the relationship between Brothers, where equality is the primary principle. Its corruption is Democracy.

The Paradox of Self-Love

Aristotle distinguishes between two types of self-love:

  1. Vulgar Self-Love: Characterized by those who grasp for money, honors, and bodily pleasures. This "selfishness" follows the irrational part of the soul and is justly reproached.
  2. True Self-Love: Characterized by the virtuous person who assigns to themselves what is "abstractedly noble and honourable." By following reason, the virtuous person benefits both themselves and the community.

Theoria: The Supreme Happiness

The Ethics concludes that the highest form of human happiness is contemplation (theoria). This intellectual activity is deemed superior because:

  • It is the activity of our highest principle (the intellect).
  • It is the most continuous and pleasant activity.
  • It is the most self-sufficient (autarkes), requiring fewer external goods than practical virtues like courage or justice.
  • It is "godlike," as it is the only activity we can reasonably attribute to the divine.

II. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Autarkeia (Self-sufficiency): The quality of a life that is choice-worthy in itself and lacks nothing. In Book X, this is specifically applied to the contemplative life, which requires less external support than the life of moral action.
  • Eudaimonia: Often translated as "happiness," it is defined by Aristotle as "activity of the soul in accordance with virtue."
  • Kindly Feeling: A "dormant" or "inert" form of friendship. It involves wishing someone well (often due to their goodness or a specific feat) but lacks the intimacy, intensity, and shared action required for true friendship.
  • Philia: The Greek term for friendship. It encompasses a broader range than the English word, including familial bonds, civic unity, and deep personal affection.
  • Theoria (Contemplation): The active, sustained intellectual inquiry into truth. It is the highest human activity because it engages the "Intellectual Principle," which Aristotle identifies as the truest "Self."
  • Unity of Sentiment: A "social friendship" where a community agrees on points of common interest and expediency, such as the choice of magistrates or alliances.

III. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Aristotle claim that even the wealthy and powerful need friends?

    • Answer: Prosperity is "slippery and hazardous" without friends to help preserve it. Furthermore, the use of prosperity lies in doing kindnesses, and friends are the "most commendable objects" of such kindness.

  2. What is the "quantity of salt" proverb meant to illustrate regarding perfect friendship?

    • Answer: It illustrates that perfect friendship cannot exist until the parties have spent significant time together and have been "proved" to be fit objects of friendship through intimacy.

  3. How does the "wicked man" view his own past and future?

    • Answer: The wicked man avoids his own company because his memories are unpleasant and his future expectations are equally dark. He is at "variance with himself," desiring things that are hurtful while knowing what is good.

  4. In what way is a friend "another Self"?

    • Answer: The feelings a good man has toward himself—wishing for his own existence, sharing in his own joys and sorrows, and acting for his own sake—are the same feelings he extends to a virtuous friend.

  5. Why does the benefactor often love the recipient more than the recipient loves the benefactor?

    • Answer: Aristotle compares this to an artisan's love for their work. The recipient is the "work" of the benefactor. Since we exist through action, the benefactor "exists" in the recipient. Additionally, doing a kindness is laborious and honorable, and we value most what cost us labor.

  6. Why is the contemplative life considered "higher than mere human nature"?

    • Answer: Because it is lived not in so far as one is a "composite" human (with passions and physical needs), but in so far as there is a "divine Principle" (the intellect) within the person.

IV. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Fragility of Utility vs. The Permanence of Virtue: Analyze Aristotle’s claim that only friendships based on goodness are stable. Why are friendships of utility and pleasure considered "accidental" or "matters of result," and what does this reveal about Aristotle’s view of human character?

  2. The Domestic Origins of the State: Discuss how Aristotle uses family relationships (father/son, husband/wife, brothers) as models for political constitutions. How does this comparison support his argument that the state is a "community of shared life" rather than just a legal arrangement?

  3. The Ethics of Self-Interest: Aristotle argues that the good man is "most truly Self-loving." Construct an argument explaining how Aristotle reconciles this "self-love" with acts of extreme altruism, such as dying for one's friends or country.

  4. Practical Virtue vs. Contemplation: Compare "Secondary Happiness" (the life of moral virtue) with "Perfect Happiness" (the life of contemplation). If contemplation is the highest good, why does Aristotle still insist on the importance of external goods and the political community?

  5. The Bridge to Politics: At the end of Book X, Aristotle transitions from the individual to the state. Explain why, according to Aristotle, the study of ethics is incomplete without the study of legislation and political constitutions.
