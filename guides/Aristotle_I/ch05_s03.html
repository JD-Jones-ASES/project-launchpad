Aristotle on the State and Citizenship: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This study guide examines the transition from Aristotle’s ethical inquiries regarding the individual to his political inquiries regarding the community. It explores the natural origins of the state, the internal structure of the household, the definition of citizenship, and the classification of various forms of government.

I. Key Concepts and Core Themes

The Natural Origin of the City-State

Aristotle posits that the city-state (polis) is not a man-made convention for the sake of convenience, but a natural growth. He reasons from "original seeds" to show a progression of human association:

  1. The Couple: Formed for the natural impulse of propagation.
  2. The Household (Oikos): Established for the satisfaction of daily needs.
  3. The Village: A collection of families seeking lasting, mutual advantage.
  4. The City (Polis): The perfection of these smaller communities. It is founded so that humans might live, but it continues so that they may "live well."

Man as a "Political Animal" (Politikon Zoon)

The claim that "man is by nature a political animal" suggests that human nature is only fully realized within the organized life of a city. According to Aristotle, anyone who exists outside of society by nature—and not by mere accident—is either "inferior or superior to man" (i.e., a beast or a god).

The Building Blocks of Justice: The Household

The city is composed of households. Therefore, the justice of the city is rooted in the justice of domestic relationships. Aristotle identifies three primary relationships within the oikos:

  • Master and Slave: A relationship Aristotle controversially describes as "natural," arguing that those with minds capable of reflection should govern those with only corporeal strength.
  • Husband and Wife: A domestic government for the benefit of the governed or the common benefit.
  • Parent and Child: A relationship based on the elder governing the younger.

The Definition of a Citizen

Citizenship is not determined by residence or legal rights alone (as even resident foreigners have these). Rather, a "complete citizen" is defined by active participation. Specifically, a citizen is one who:

  • Shares in the judicial and deliberative functions of the community.
  • Participates in "ruling and being ruled" in turn.

The Classification of Constitutions

Aristotle classifies governments based on two criteria: Who rules (one, few, or many) and In whose interest they rule (the common good or private interest).

Number of Rulers Rule for the Common Good (Correct) Rule for Private Interest (Deviant)
One Monarchy (Kingdom) Tyranny
Few Aristocracy Oligarchy
Many Polity (State) Democracy

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the "highest human purpose" for which the city exists?

    • Answer: The city exists not merely for the sake of living or survival, but for the sake of "living well" or living happily.

  2. According to Aristotle, how does the city differ from a mere "tariff for trade" or a mutual defense alliance?

    • Answer: A city is defined by a shared commitment to virtue and the common good. If the inhabitants only trade or defend one another but do not care about the moral character of their neighbors, they have an alliance, not a city.

  3. Why does Aristotle characterize the slave as an "animated instrument"?

    • Answer: He views the slave as a "possession" that is used to facilitate life, much like a tool (instrument) is used by an artist to complete a work.

  4. How does Aristotle distinguish between a "Polity" and a "Democracy"?

    • Answer: In Aristotle’s classification, a Polity is the correct form of rule by the many for the common good, whereas Democracy is a deviant form where the poor majority rule only in their own private interest.

  5. What was the central irony of the relationship between Aristotle and his student, Alexander the Great?

    • Answer: Aristotle theorized that the natural political unit was the small, self-governing city-state, yet his student Alexander built a massive empire that swept away those very city-states.

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Evolution of Human Association: Analyze Aristotle’s argument that the city is a "natural production." How does his method of tracing the city back to its "first elements" (the household and the village) support his conclusion that man is a politikon zoon?

  2. The Paradox of Natural Slavery: Aristotle argues that the city exists for virtue and the good life, yet he defends the institution of slavery. Examine the "caution" provided in the text regarding this doctrine. How do Aristotle's own principles—specifically that the human good consists in rational activity—provide a basis for refuting his views on natural slavery?

  3. Active vs. Passive Citizenship: Contrast Aristotle’s definition of a "complete citizen" with modern concepts of citizenship. If a citizen is defined as one who "shares in ruling and being ruled," what are the implications for a society where most people are legal residents but do not participate in judicial or deliberative functions?

  4. The Metric of Good Government: Discuss Aristotle’s claim that the most important distinction in political science is not how many people rule, but the purpose for which they rule. Why does he consider a Monarchy (rule of one) superior to a Democracy (rule of many) if the monarch rules for the common good?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Aristocracy: Literally "rule by the best" (aristoi); a correct form of government in which a virtuous few rule for the benefit of all.
  • Democracy: In Aristotle's classification, a deviant form of government where the poor majority rule in their own private interest.
  • Monarchy: Rule by a single virtuous person for the common good; also referred to as a "Kingdom."
  • Oikos: The Greek household, encompassing family, slaves, and property; treated as the fundamental building block of the city.
  • Oligarchy: A deviant form of government where a wealthy few rule in their own self-interest.
  • Politikon Zoon: Literally "political animal"; refers to the idea that human beings are creatures whose nature is fulfilled only within a city (polis).
  • Polity: Aristotle's term (politeia) for a constitutional government where the many rule for the common good; considered the best practically achievable form for most cities.
  • Tyranny: The worst deviant form of government, characterized by a single person ruling for their own private gain.
  • Virtue: The ultimate aim of the city's laws and the necessary component of the "good life" that the state is meant to facilitate.
