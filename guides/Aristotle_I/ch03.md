# Study Guide: The Virtues and Justice

This study guide provides a comprehensive synthesis of the philosophical explorations of virtue and justice as outlined in the works of Aristotle and Thomas Aquinas. It covers the foundations of moral responsibility, the structural nature of virtue, the specific complexities of justice, and the medieval synthesis that integrated classical philosophy with theological frameworks.

---

## I. The Foundations of Moral Responsibility

Before evaluating character, one must determine if an agent is genuinely responsible for their actions. Aristotle establishes that moral evaluation—praise or blame—is only applicable to **voluntary actions**.

### Voluntary vs. Involuntary Action
*   **Voluntary Action:** Originates within the agent; the agent has knowledge of the particular circumstances. These actions express a person's deliberate character.
*   **Involuntary Action:** Done under **compulsion** (the moving principle is external, and the agent contributes nothing) or through **ignorance** of relevant circumstances.
*   **Proairesis (Deliberate Choice):** This is the specific act of will involving rational deliberation. It distinguishes a morally responsible agent from someone acting on mere impulse.

### The Role of Ignorance
Aristotle makes a critical distinction regarding ignorance:
1.  **Ignorance of Circumstances:** May excuse an action or render it involuntary (e.g., a soldier mistaking a civilian for an enemy).
2.  **Ignorance of Moral Principles:** Does not excuse an agent. One cannot claim "I didn't know lying was wrong" to escape moral blame.

## II. The Structure of Virtue: The Mean

Aristotle defines virtue as a stable disposition (**hexis**) to choose the intermediate point between two extremes. This is known as the **Doctrine of the Mean**.

### Elements of the Virtuous Pattern
Every moral virtue consists of three elements:
1.  **A Sphere:** The domain of feeling or action (e.g., fear, pleasure, money).
2.  **Two Vices:** The excess and the deficiency within that sphere.
3.  **The Mean:** The appropriate response given the circumstances, the person, and the purpose.

| Sphere | Deficiency (Vice) | The Mean (Virtue) | Excess (Vice) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Fear of Death | Cowardice | **Courage** | Rashness / Recklessness |
| Bodily Pleasure | Insensibility | **Temperance** | Intemperance / Licentiousness |
| Worth/Honor | Pusillanimity | **Greatness of Soul** | Vanity |

**Note:** The mean is not a mathematical midpoint; it is relative to the person and situation, identified through **Phronesis** (practical wisdom).

## III. Justice: The Master Virtue

Justice occupies a unique position because it is essentially **relational**. While other virtues (like temperance) concern the agent’s own soul, justice concerns what is owed to others and the community. Aristotle calls it "complete virtue."

### Forms of Justice
Aristotle distinguishes between two primary forms of particular justice:

| Type | Focus | Principle |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Distributive Justice** | Allocation of shared goods (offices, honors, civic benefits). | **Proportional:** Based on merit, contribution, or worth. |
| **Corrective Justice** | Restoring fairness after a wrong (crime, contract breach). | **Arithmetic:** Stripping the wrongdoer of gain and compensating the victim. |

### Natural vs. Conventional Justice
*   **Natural Justice (Physis):** Standards of justice that hold everywhere by nature, independently of human enactment.
*   **Conventional Justice (Nomos):** Laws, customs, or decrees that vary by community and are established by agreement.

## IV. The Thomistic Synthesis

Thomas Aquinas "baptized" Aristotelian philosophy, integrating it into a theological framework where God is the final cause of human action.

### The Cardinal Virtues
Aquinas identifies four "hinge" virtues upon which the moral life turns:
1.  **Prudence:** Directs practical reasoning (perfects the rational power).
2.  **Justice:** The stable will to give each their due (perfects the will).
3.  **Fortitude:** Stabilizes the soul against fear of danger (perfects the irascible faculty).
4.  **Temperance:** Moderates the pull of pleasure (perfects the concupiscible faculty).

### The Theological Virtues
While cardinal virtues perfect natural capacities, Aquinas adds three **theological virtues** infused by grace to direct humans toward a supernatural end: **Faith, Hope, and Charity**.

### The Mean of Reason
Aquinas refines the "Mean." He distinguishes between the **real mean** (arithmetic) and the **rational mean** (determined by reason). He argues that some virtues, like justice and prudence, cannot be "excessive" because they are defined by what right reason requires; you cannot be "too just."

---

## V. Modern Perspectives on Justice

Modern philosophy continues to use Aristotelian conceptual tools to debate the nature of a fair society:
*   **John Rawls (*A Theory of Justice*):** Uses the "Veil of Ignorance" to argue for principles that protect the least advantaged. This abstracts from the concrete community Aristotle emphasized.
*   **Robert Nozick (*Anarchy, State, and Utopia*):** Argues for "entitlement theory," emphasizing individual rights and just acquisition, though largely abandoning Aristotle’s focus on the common good (teleology).

## VII. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Architecture of Responsibility:** Analyze Aristotle’s account of voluntary action. To what extent does ignorance of circumstances or moral principles mitigate a person's responsibility? Use the examples of the negligent driver and the soldier provided in the text.
2.  **The Relational Nature of Justice:** Compare Aristotle’s "distributive justice" with modern concepts of "redistributive justice." Why does the text caution against conflating the two, and how does Aristotle’s focus on merit differ from modern equality of outcome?
3.  **Synthesis or Transformation?** Evaluate Aquinas’s reception of Aristotle. Does Aquinas merely "copy" Aristotle, or does his theological framework fundamentally alter the nature of Aristotelian virtue? Focus on the principle of *gratia non tollit naturam, sed perficit* (grace does not destroy nature, but perfects it).
4.  **The Standards of Justice:** Discuss the tension between natural and conventional justice. If justice is merely conventional (*nomos*), on what basis can a legal system be criticized as unjust? Use Aristotle's view on the trial of Socrates as a starting point.

## VIII. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Akrasia:** Weakness of will; acting against one's knowledge of what is right due to desire.
*   **Andreia:** Courage; the mean between cowardice and rashness.
*   **Arete:** Virtue or excellence; the quality that enables a thing to perform its function well.
*   **Cardinal Virtues:** The four principal moral virtues: Prudence, Justice, Fortitude, and Temperance.
*   **Dikaiosyne:** Justice; the virtue concerned with our relationships with others.
*   **Eudaimonia:** Happiness or human flourishing; the highest good and activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.
*   **Hexis:** A stable state or settled disposition of character (translated by Aquinas as *habitus*).
*   **Logos:** Reason, speech, or rational account; the basis of human political community.
*   **Nomos:** Law, custom, or convention; established rules of a specific community.
*   **Phronesis:** Practical wisdom; the intellectual virtue required to identify the mean in particular cases.
*   **Physis:** Nature; the inherent character of a thing or what holds true universally.
*   **Positive Law:** Law enacted by human authority (statutes, decrees).
*   **Proairesis:** Deliberate choice; the union of rational deliberation and the act of will.
*   **Telos:** The end, purpose, or goal of a thing.
*   **Veil of Ignorance:** John Rawls’s thought experiment for choosing principles of justice without knowing one’s social standing.
