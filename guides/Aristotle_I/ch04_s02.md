# Friendship and the Contemplative Life: A Study Guide

This study guide explores the synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology regarding the nature of friendship and the purpose of the contemplative life. It focuses primarily on the work of Thomas Aquinas and the modern reflections of C.S. Lewis.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### The Problem of Inequality in Friendship
Aristotle posited that genuine friendship requires equality and reciprocity. Because of the "radical inequality" between humans and God, Aristotle concluded that friendship with the divine was impossible; a god needs nothing from humans, and humans have nothing of equal value to return.

### The Thomistic Synthesis
Thomas Aquinas overturns Aristotle’s conclusion not by rejecting his logic, but by introducing the concepts of the **Incarnation** and **Grace**. Aquinas argues that while humans cannot rise to God's level, God "condescends" to the human level. Through grace, God communicates His happiness to humanity, creating a form of "fellowship" or "communication" that allows for a reciprocal relationship.

### Charity (Caritas) as Friendship
Aquinas identifies *caritas* (charity) as the specific form of friendship between man and God. 
*   **Definition:** The theological virtue of love directed toward God, and toward others for God’s sake.
*   **Distinction:** Unlike the love of "concupiscence" (desiring something for one's own benefit, like wine or a horse), charity is a love of "benevolence," where one wishes good to the other.
*   **Universality:** Charity extends even to enemies because they are loved in relation to God, whom the friend of God loves.

### The Order of Charity
Aquinas argues that love must be ordered based on proximity to God. The hierarchy of charity is as follows:
1.  **God:** Loved above all things as the principle of good.
2.  **Self:** Specifically one's spiritual nature, because the individual is a direct partaker in God's good.
3.  **Neighbor:** Loved as a fellow partner in the divine good.

### Contemplation and the "Mixed Life"
While Aristotle viewed the contemplative life (*bios theoretikos*) as the highest happiness because of its self-sufficiency, Aquinas transforms this idea:
*   **The Ultimate End:** The highest contemplation is the **Beatific Vision**—the direct contemplation of God in heaven, made possible by grace.
*   **The Mixed Life:** In this life, the most perfect state is not pure contemplation, but a "mixed life." This is captured in the Dominican motto: *contemplata aliis tradere* ("to contemplate and to give to others what has been contemplated"). The good is meant to be communicated and shared, not enjoyed in solitude.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Agape** | Selfless divine love; the Greek root for the Latin *caritas*. |
| **Beatific Vision** | The direct contemplation of God enjoyed by the blessed in heaven; the ultimate end of human existence. |
| **Caritas (Charity)** | The theological virtue of love directed toward God and others; the "animating principle" of all other virtues. |
| **Contemplata aliis tradere** | "To pass on to others what one has contemplated"; the Christian ideal of the mixed life of action and contemplation. |
| **Imago Dei** | The "image of God" found in human beings, which serves as the ground for loving one's neighbor. |
| **Philia** | The Greek term for friendship, often based on shared vision or virtue. |
| **Reciprocity** | The mutual exchange or return of love required for friendship in the Aristotelian sense. |

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Why did Aristotle conclude that humans cannot be friends with God?**
    Aristotle believed friendship requires equality and reciprocity. He argued the gap between the divine and the human is too vast for a real exchange to occur.

2.  **According to Aquinas, what makes "communication" between God and man possible?**
    It is made possible through God’s grace and the communication of His own happiness (beatitude) to human beings.

3.  **How does Aquinas distinguish between the love of friendship and the love of "concupiscence"?**
    The love of friendship involves benevolence (wishing good to the other), whereas concupiscence involves wishing a thing's good for oneself (e.g., loving wine).

4.  **Why does Aquinas claim that a person should love themselves more than their neighbor?**
    He argues that unity surpasses union. A person's share of the Divine good is more "potent" to them than another's partnership in that good; however, this refers to the spiritual nature, not selfish desire.

5.  **What are two of the reasons Aquinas gives for the contemplative life being superior to the active life?**
    (Select two): It belongs to the best part of man (intellect); it is more continuous; it is more delightful; it is more self-sufficient; it is loved for its own sake; it consists in leisure/rest.

6.  **How does C.S. Lewis define the "spark" of friendship (*philia*)?**
    He describes it as the moment two people realize they share a vision of a truth or goodness that others do not see, summarized by the phrase: "What! You too?"

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Bridge of Grace:** Analyze how Aquinas uses the theological concepts of the Incarnation and Grace to solve Aristotle’s philosophical dilemma regarding friendship with God. Does this "overturning" of Aristotle preserve or contradict Aristotelian logic?

2.  **The Order of Love:** Explain Aquinas’s hierarchy of charity (God, self, neighbor). How does this ordering prevent "charity" from becoming mere "selfishness," and how does it change the way we view our obligations to "the neighbor"?

3.  **The Evolution of the Contemplative Life:** Compare Aristotle’s ideal of the self-sufficient philosopher with Aquinas’s ideal of the "mixed life." Discuss the theological significance of the shift from "hoarding" truth to "communicating" truth (*contemplata aliis tradere*).

4.  **Lewis and the Natural Loves:** Using the framework of *The Four Loves*, discuss C.S. Lewis’s argument that natural loves (like *philia* or *eros*) are insufficient without *agape*. How does this reflect the broader Aristotelian-Thomistic idea that virtue and grace perfect nature?
