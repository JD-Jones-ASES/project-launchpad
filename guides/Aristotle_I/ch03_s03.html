Study Guide: Aristotle on the Virtues and Justice

This study guide explores the foundations of moral responsibility, the nature of courage, and the multifaceted concept of justice as presented in Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics.

I. Key Concepts and Themes

The Foundations of Moral Responsibility

Aristotle establishes that moral evaluation—the assignment of praise or blame—is only appropriate for voluntary actions. An action is considered voluntary when the initiative lies within the agent and they have knowledge of the relevant circumstances.

  • Involuntary Actions: These occur under compulsion (external force) or by reason of ignorance.
  • Borderline Cases: Aristotle recognizes "mixed" actions, such as a person complying with an extortionist to avoid a greater evil. While the person does not "want" the action, it is technically voluntary because the choice to act comes from within the agent at the moment of decision.
  • Proairesis (Deliberate Choice): This is the specific act of will that produces moral action. It distinguishes virtuous behavior from mere impulse or habit. Virtue requires not just doing the right thing, but choosing it for the right reasons.

Courage: The Paradigm Virtue

Courage is the "mean" state regarding the feelings of fear and boldness. It is specifically concerned with the most fearful of all things: death.

  • The Object of Courage: While many things are fearful (poverty, disease, disgrace), the "Brave man" is primarily concerned with death in the most honorable circumstances, specifically in war.
  • The Mean and its Extremes:
    • Cowardice (Excess): Fearing the wrong things, in the wrong way, or at the wrong time.
    • Rashness (Deficiency of Fear/Excess of Confidence): Often a form of boastfulness; rash people may appear brave before danger but fail when it arrives.
    • Courage (The Mean): Withstanding fearful things as right reason directs, motivated by honor.

Justice: The Master Social Virtue

Aristotle regards justice as "perfect virtue" because it is the exercise of virtue toward others rather than oneself. He distinguishes between several types of justice:

  1. Universal Justice: Law-abidingness and fairness that encompasses the whole of virtue in a social context.
  2. Particular Justice:
    • Distributive Justice: The fair allocation of honors or wealth based on merit (proportionate equality).
    • Corrective Justice: Restoring a fair balance after a transaction or injury (arithmetic equality).
  3. Natural vs. Conventional Justice:
    • Natural Justice: Principles that have the same force everywhere and do not depend on human enactment.
    • Conventional (Positive) Law: Rules that could originally be decided one way or another but become fixed once enacted (e.g., specific religious sacrifices or legal fees).

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1. According to Aristotle, what are the two primary causes of involuntary action? Answer: Compulsion (external force) and ignorance of relevant circumstances.

2. Why does Aristotle argue that a man being extorted at knifepoint is acting voluntarily? Answer: Because the "moving principle" or the initiative to perform the act is within the person at the time of the action, even though the choice is made to avoid a worse alternative.

3. What is the difference between "Distributive" and "Corrective" justice? Answer: Distributive justice deals with sharing honors or wealth according to merit (geometric proportion), while Corrective justice focuses on rectifying a wrong or imbalance between two parties regardless of their personal merit (arithmetic proportion).

4. Why is death in war considered the primary "object-matter" for the brave man? Answer: Because death is the greatest of all evils (being the end of all things), and war provides the most honorable and dangerous circumstances in which to face it.

5. How does Aristotle define the "Unjust man"? Answer: The unjust man is one who violates the law, one who is "grasping" (seeks more than his fair share of good things), and one who is "unequal" or unfair.

6. What is the role of "right reason" in the exercise of courage? Answer: Right reason directs a person to stand up against fearful things as they ought and at the right time, with the ultimate goal being "what is honourable."

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Role of Intent in Moral Evaluation: Aristotle argues that "virtue is about the whole pattern of deliberate choice, not isolated acts." Analyze how the concept of proairesis (deliberate choice) separates a truly virtuous person from someone who merely performs the "correct" action by accident, habit, or instruction.

  2. Justice as the "Complete Virtue": Aristotle claims that justice is unique because it is "exercised towards one's neighbor." Discuss why Aristotle views justice as the "master virtue of social life" and how it differs from individual virtues like temperance or generosity.

  3. The Tension Between Natural and Conventional Justice: Using Aristotle’s distinction between natural justice and "positive law," evaluate the moral implications of a citizen facing an "unjust law." How does Aristotle’s framework allow for the critique of a community's established statutes?

  4. The Nuances of Courage: Aristotle distinguishes the "Brave man" from the "Rash man" and the "Coward." Compare and contrast these three states, paying particular attention to their motivations and how they react to "sudden emergencies."

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

Term Definition
Corrective Justice A form of justice concerned with restoring balance in transactions or after an injury; it treats the parties as equals and uses arithmetic progression.
Distributive Justice The fair distribution of honors, wealth, or other community resources based on the merit of the individuals involved.
Involuntary Action Actions performed under external compulsion or due to ignorance, for which a person is not held morally responsible.
Natural Justice A form of justice that has universal force and does not depend on being accepted or enacted by any specific human authority.
Positive Law (Also Conventional Justice) Laws that are "posited" or enacted by human authority, such as specific decrees or statutes that vary by location.
Proairesis Deliberate choice; the specific act of will involving rational deliberation that results in moral action.
The Mean The virtuous middle ground between the extremes of excess and deficiency.
Voluntary Action An action where the initiative lies within the agent and the agent has knowledge of the circumstances; the basis for praise and blame.
