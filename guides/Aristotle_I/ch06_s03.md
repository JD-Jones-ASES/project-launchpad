# Aristotle on Constitutions and Revolution: A Study Guide

This study guide explores the political philosophy of Aristotle as presented in Books IV and V of the *Politics*. It examines his empirical approach to governance, his theory of the "Polity" as the best practical state, the causes of political instability and revolution, and the mechanisms for preserving a constitution.

---

## Key Concepts and Themes

### 1. The Empirical Approach to Political Science
Aristotle transitioned from the "ideal theory" of his predecessors to a practical, empirical study of government. He and his students compiled descriptions of 158 different constitutions to understand how real cities functioned.
*   **The Statesman as Craftsman:** Aristotle compares the political philosopher to a physician or a gymnastic trainer. A physician who only knows how to treat a perfectly healthy person is useless in practice; similarly, a legislator must know not only the "best imaginable" state but also what is best suited to specific circumstances.
*   **Constitutional Variety:** There is no single form of democracy or oligarchy. Legislators must understand the specific varieties that emerge from a city's particular circumstances to create effective laws.

### 2. The Best Practical Constitution: The Polity
Aristotle identifies the "Polity"—a mixed constitution—as the most stable form of government for most cities.
*   **The Doctrine of the Mean:** Just as virtue in individuals is a "mean" between extremes, political stability is found in the "middle."
*   **The Role of the Middle Class:** 
    *   **The Very Rich:** Often arrogant, contemptuous of law, and unable to obey.
    *   **The Very Poor:** Often envious, rascally, and unable to command, behaving like slaves.
    *   **The Middle Class:** Invested in stability, governed by reason rather than passion, and capable of acting as a buffer between the rich and the poor to prevent factionalism.

### 3. Political Pathology: The Causes of Revolution
Aristotle views revolution (*stasis*) as a result of a perceived violation of justice (*dikaiosyne*).
*   **Grievances of Equality and Superiority:** Revolutions occur when those who believe they are equals are treated as inferiors, or when those who believe they are superior are treated as equals.
*   **Psychological Root:** Politics is driven by what people believe, feel, and desire regarding the distribution of profit and honor.
*   **Secondary Causes:** Haughtiness, fear, eminence, contempt, and disproportionate increase in one part of the state.

### 4. Preservation of the State
To maintain stability, a state must actively counter the forces of decay.
*   **The Rule of Law:** Guarding against even small illegalities, as minor expenses eventually consume a family's income.
*   **Avoidance of Excess:** A democracy that becomes "too democratic" or an oligarchy that becomes "too oligarchic" will eventually destroy itself.
*   **Education:** Aristotle argues that the "greatest of all means" for preserving a constitution is adapting education to the form of government. Citizens must be formed in the habits and desires that support the specific justice of their constitution.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Why does Aristotle argue that a legislator must know more than just the "ideal" form of government?**
Aristotle believes a legislator must be like a craftsman or physician; they must understand the full range of what is possible and what is best suited to specific, often imperfect, circumstances. Knowing only the ideal state is useless for improving or preserving actual cities.

**2. According to Aristotle, what is the defining difference between "government" and "laws"?**
Government is the ordering of the state, particularly regarding the regulation of magistrates and the placement of supreme power. Laws are the rules that direct the conduct of those magistrates in their office and the punishment of offenders.

**3. Why are the "very rich" and the "very poor" considered dangerous to political stability?**
The very rich are often too arrogant to obey and seek to command with "despotic power," while the very poor are often too "mean" and envious, knowing only how to be commanded like slaves. This creates a city of "slaves and masters" rather than a community of friends.

**4. What is the "psychological" root of revolution according to Aristotle?**
The root is a grievance about justice (*dikaiosyne*). People become seditious when they feel they have been denied the profit or honor they believe is rightly due to them, whether they are seeking equality or superiority.

**5. What are the three qualifications necessary for those holding high office?**
*   Affection for the established constitution.
*   Abilities and competence equal to the requirements of the office.
*   Virtue and justice correspondent to the nature of that particular state.

**6. How does Aristotle use the "analogy of the nose" to explain constitutional decay?**
He explains that a nose can deviate slightly from perfect straightness (becoming hooked or flat) and still be beautiful. However, if the deviation is pushed to excess, the part loses its identity as a nose. Similarly, a democracy or oligarchy can deviate from its perfect form and remain functional, but if pushed to the extreme, the government will cease to exist.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Ethics of Politics:** Analyze how Aristotle’s "Doctrine of the Mean" from his ethical writings serves as the foundation for his "Polity" in the *Politics*. How does the balance of the middle class reflect the pursuit of virtue in a political community?
2.  **Education as the "Immune System":** Aristotle claims that education is the greatest means of preserving a constitution. Discuss the implications of this for a modern state. Should education be designed to "fit" the current government, or should it remain independent?
3.  **The Pathology of Power:** Compare and contrast the causes of revolution in democracies versus oligarchies. Based on Aristotle’s analysis, is political instability primarily an economic issue or a psychological one?
4.  **The Practical Statesman:** Using the physician analogy, evaluate Aristotle's advice for preserving a state. How does he balance the need for "strict laws" with the need for "wise conduct" by magistrates?

## Glossary of Important Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Dikaiosyne** | **Justice:** The virtue of rendering to each what is due. In politics, it concerns the distribution of power, honor, and wealth. |
| **Phronesis** | **Practical Wisdom:** The intellectual virtue of knowing the right course of action in specific, particular situations. |
| **Polity** | A mixed constitution that represents a "mean" between democracy and oligarchy; often characterized by a strong middle class. |
| **Demagogue** | Literally a "leader of the people" who inflames popular passions and prejudices to gain power rather than appealing to reason. |
| **Stasis** | Civil strife or sedition; the internal conflict that leads to the breakdown of a constitution and eventual revolution. |
| **Ekklesia** | The citizen assembly in Athens where members met to deliberate on the city’s laws and policies. |
| **Mean** | The middle state between two extremes (excess and deficiency) that defines both individual virtue and political health. |
| **Dynasty** | A corrupted form of government where power is concentrated in the hands of a few powerful families or magistrates who have held office for too long. |
| **Emolument** | Profit or gain derived from public office; Aristotle suggests that making offices unpaid can prevent the poor from seeking office for greed and the rich from pillaging the state. |
