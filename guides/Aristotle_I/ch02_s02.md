# Aquinas, Aristotle, and the Pursuit of the Good Life: A Study Guide

This study guide examines the synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology developed by Thomas Aquinas, the subsequent revival of virtue ethics in the 20th century, and the intersection of these ideas with contemporary positive psychology.

---

## I. Key Concepts and Core Themes

### The Synthesis of Reason and Revelation
Thomas Aquinas did not view Aristotle as an opponent, but as "the Philosopher"—a foundational authority on natural reason. Aquinas’s project in the *Summa Theologica* was to receive Aristotelian ethics and "perfect" them through the lens of Christian revelation. He argued that while Aristotle correctly identified the structure of human happiness, he lacked the revealed truth necessary to identify its ultimate fulfillment.

### Natural vs. Supernatural Happiness
The defining distinction in Aquinas’s ethics is the shift from a single-tier to a two-tier account of human flourishing:

| Type of Happiness | Description | Source/Means |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Imperfect Happiness** | *Eudaimonia*: Virtuous activity over a complete life; involves health, friendship, and external goods. | Achieved through natural powers, habituation, and practical wisdom. |
| **Perfect Happiness** | *Beatific Vision*: The direct contemplation of God as He is in Himself; the final rest of the restless human heart. | Exceeds natural capacity; given as a gift of divine grace. |

### The Nature of Human Action
Aquinas distinguishes between two types of activities occurring in a person:
*   **Human Actions:** These proceed from a deliberate will and reason. Man is "master of his actions" only in this category.
*   **Actions of a Man:** These are actions found in a person that are not proper to "man as man" (e.g., involuntary or irrational movements).

### Grace and Nature
The foundational principle of Aquinas’s thought is *gratia non tollit naturam, sed perficit*: **"Grace does not destroy nature, but perfects it."** In this framework, natural ethics (Aristotle) serves as a "staircase" ordered toward a supernatural destination that transcends ethics.

## II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

**1. Why does Aquinas argue that no "created good" (like wealth or honor) can constitute perfect happiness?**
Aquinas asserts that the human will is oriented toward the "universal good," just as the intellect is oriented toward "universal truth." Because every created thing has only "goodness by participation" (it is limited), it cannot satisfy a will of infinite capacity. Only the infinite good—God—can fully "lull the appetite."

**2. How does Aquinas redefine the Aristotelian concept of the "end" as a cause?**
While an "end" is the last thing in the order of execution (the finish line), Aquinas argues it is the *first* thing in the "order of the agent's intention." In this way, the goal acts as a final cause that moves the person to act.

**3. What is the "capacity for God" (*capax Dei*) mentioned by Bernard of Clairvaux?**
It is the intuition that the human heart has a vacuum or a capacity that nothing less than God can fill, echoing Augustine’s sentiment that the heart is "restless" until it rests in God.

**4. According to Aquinas, why is a human being "more noble" than an animal, even though an animal can reach its end through natural powers alone?**
A nature that can reach a perfect, infinite good (but needs help/grace to do so) is superior to a nature that can only reach a lower, imperfect good on its own. Dependence on God for perfect happiness is a sign of human dignity, not a defect.

**5. What triggered the 20th-century revival of virtue ethics?**
The revival began with Elizabeth Anscombe’s 1958 article "Modern Moral Philosophy," which argued that Kantian deontology and Utilitarianism were incoherent without the teleological (goal-oriented) framework of Aristotle.

## III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

**1. The Aristotelian Framework in a Theological Context**
Analyze how Aquinas uses Aristotle’s "function argument" and the concept of "final ends" to build a case for the existence of God as the ultimate good. To what extent does Aquinas remain "Aristotelian" when he concludes that human happiness must transcend this life?

**2. Virtue Ethics vs. Modern Moral Frameworks**
Compare and contrast the Aristotelian focus on character and "natural goodness" (as discussed by Philippa Foot) with the Kantian focus on duty and the Utilitarian focus on pleasure. Why did Alasdair MacIntyre argue that modern culture needs to recover the Aristotelian framework?

**3. Flourishing: From Ancient Philosophy to Modern Psychology**
Discuss the parallels between Aristotle’s *eudaimonia* and the findings of the "positive psychology" movement (Seligman, Csikszentmihalyi). How does the empirical discovery that "feeling good is not the same as living well" validate or complicate Aristotle’s account of virtue?

## IV. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Beatific Vision (*visio Dei*):** The direct vision or contemplation of God as He is, which Aquinas identifies as the only source of perfect human happiness.
*   **Deontology (Kantian Ethics):** A moral framework that grounds ethics in the rational principle of duty rather than the pursuit of happiness or human nature.
*   **Eudaimonia:** Aristotle’s term for happiness, defined as the soul's activity in accordance with virtue over a complete life.
*   **Grace:** The free gift of God that elevates the soul beyond its natural capacity to participate in divine life.
*   **Natural Powers:** The inherent capacities of a human being (reason and will) to achieve goods in the present life without direct divine intervention.
*   **Positive Psychology:** A modern psychological movement focused on human "flourishing"—meaningful relationships and the exercise of strengths—rather than just the treatment of mental illness.
*   **Summa Theologica:** Thomas Aquinas's masterwork, which provides a comprehensive synthesis of Christian theology and Aristotelian philosophy.
*   **Teleology:** A framework that understands things in terms of their "end," "goal," or "purpose."
*   **Utilitarianism:** The moral philosophy that defines the right action as that which produces the greatest happiness (defined as pleasure) for the greatest number.
