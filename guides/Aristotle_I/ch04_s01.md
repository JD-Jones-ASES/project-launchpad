# Study Guide: Friendship and the Contemplative Life in Aristotle’s Ethics

This study guide explores the philosophical examination of friendship, the nature of the self, and the pursuit of the highest human happiness as presented in Aristotle’s *Ethics*. It synthesizes the relationship between personal virtue, civic life, and the intellectual pursuit of truth.

---

## Key Concepts and Overview

### The Philosophical Importance of Friendship
Aristotle posits that friendship (*philia*) is not merely a personal preference but a vital subject for philosophical analysis for three primary reasons:
*   **Friendship as Virtue:** It is a stable disposition to relate to others correctly.
*   **Social Necessity:** Aristotle asserts that "no one would choose to live without friends, even if he had all other goods."
*   **Character Illumination:** Relationships reveal an individual's values and the kind of person they are becoming.

### The Three Types of Friendship
Aristotle classifies friendship based on what the parties involved value in one another. Only one form is considered "perfect" (*teleios*).

| Type | Basis of Relationship | Duration/Stability | Typical Context |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Utility** | Mutual benefit or what each party provides. | Contingent; dissolves when utility ceases. | Business partners, colleagues, acquaintances. |
| **Pleasure** | Enjoyment or the feelings the relationship brings. | Contingent; dissolves when the pleasure fades. | Common among the young. |
| **Character (Perfect)** | Goodness of character; valuing the other for who they are. | Stable; character is enduring. | Requires virtue and time to develop; rare. |

### Civic Friendship and the State
Unlike modern theories that view the state as a mechanism for power or competing interests, Aristotle views the political community as an extension of natural bonds.
*   **Civic Friendship:** A healthy community requires goodwill among citizens and a shared investment in one another's welfare.
*   **Family Analogies:** Political structures reflect family relationships:
    *   **Monarchy:** Father-child relationship.
    *   **Aristocracy:** Husband-wife relationship.
    *   **Republic:** Brother-brother relationship.
*   **Institutional Love:** Good government is essentially a form of love made institutional.

### The Two Senses of Self-Love
Aristotle resolves the tension between love of self and love of neighbor by distinguishing between corrupt and proper self-love:
1.  **Corrupt Self-Love:** Gratifying base appetites, seeking the greater share of money, honors, or pleasure at others' expense.
2.  **Proper Self-Love:** Seeking the nobler actions and the goods of the soul. The person who loves what is best in themselves—reason and virtue—will naturally act generously toward others because acting nobly is what they love.

### The Contemplative Life (*Bios Theoretikos*)
In Book X of the *Ethics*, Aristotle identifies the life of intellectual contemplation (*theoria*) as the highest form of happiness. While the life of practical virtue is a "secondary happiness," the contemplative life is superior because:
*   **Highest Part of Man:** It exercises the intellect.
*   **Self-Sufficiency (*Autarkes*):** It requires fewer external goods than the active life.
*   **Continuity:** It can be pursued more uninterruptedly than social activities.
*   **Stability:** The pleasures of philosophy are the most enduring.
*   **Divinity:** It is the most godlike activity.

## Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Why does Aristotle consider friendship a "social necessity"?**
    Aristotle claims that human life is essentially social, stating that even an individual possessing all other goods would not choose to live without friends.

2.  **What distinguishes "perfect" friendship from the other two types?**
    Perfect friendship is based on goodness of character and valuing the person for who they are, rather than for utility or pleasure. It is stable and requires time and virtue to develop.

3.  **How does Aristotle describe the relationship between a republic and family bonds?**
    He suggests that a republic is an extension of the natural bond of affection between brothers.

4.  **Explain the difference between the "corrupt" and "proper" senses of self-love.**
    Corrupt self-love is the pursuit of money and appetites at the expense of others. Proper self-love is the pursuit of virtue and reason, which leads to noble actions and generosity toward others.

5.  **Why is the contemplative life considered more "self-sufficient" than the life of practical virtue?**
    The contemplative person requires fewer external resources and fewer other people to engage in their highest activity (thinking) compared to a statesman or a soldier who needs specific social contexts and resources to exercise their virtues.

6.  **Does Aristotle define the contemplative life as a life of passivity?**
    No. He defines it as the active work of reasoning about deep questions, similar to the life of a dedicated researcher or scholar.

## Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **The Tension of Happiness:** Aristotle argues that the life of contemplation is the highest happiness, yet he spends most of the *Ethics* discussing social virtues and citizenship. Is there a contradiction between the "active" life of justice and the "solitary" life of the philosopher? Use Aristotle's definition of "secondary happiness" to support your argument.

2.  **Civic Friendship vs. Modern Governance:** Compare Aristotle’s view of the state as a "form of love made institutional" with modern views of the state as a mechanism for managing competing interests. What are the practical implications of a society lacking "civic friendship"?

3.  **The Ethics of Self-Love:** Evaluate Aristotle’s claim that a person must love themselves properly before they can love others. How does this compare to the concept of *ordo amoris* (ordered love), and how does it challenge the idea that self-love is inherently selfish?

4.  **The Divine Objection:** Aristotle suggests that living the contemplative life is "to live not as a human being but as something divine." Discuss whether this should be viewed as an aspirational goal or a warning against transcending the human condition.

## Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Autarkes (Self-Sufficiency):** A mark of the highest good; the quality of requiring few external goods or others to achieve happiness.
*   **Bios Theoretikos:** The contemplative life; a life devoted to intellectual inquiry and theoria.
*   **Civic Friendship:** The goodwill and shared investment in mutual welfare that binds citizens together in a healthy political community.
*   **Ordo Amoris:** A concept (later explored by Augustine) regarding the ordered love of all things according to their proper rank; relevant to Aristotle's hierarchy of self-love and friendship.
*   **Philia:** Generally translated as friendship; a stable disposition to relate to others and a necessary component of the virtuous life.
*   **Proper Self-Love:** Love for the highest part of oneself (reason and virtue), which motivates noble actions and benefits others.
*   **Teleios (Perfect):** Referring to the highest form of friendship based on character, which is complete and enduring.
*   **Theoria:** Intellectual contemplation; the active pursuit of truth through reasoning.
