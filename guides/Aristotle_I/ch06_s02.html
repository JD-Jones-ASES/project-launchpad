Study Guide: Constitutions and Revolution—Aquinas, the American Founding, and Tocqueville

This study guide examines the philosophical foundations of legal obligation, the relationship between human and natural law, and the influence of classical political thought on modern democratic systems. It focuses on the synthesis of Thomas Aquinas’s legal theory and the subsequent "Aristotelian anxieties" present in the American founding and the observations of Alexis de Tocqueville.

I. Key Concepts and Theoretical Frameworks

Aquinas on the Nature and Derivation of Law

Thomas Aquinas establishes a hierarchical relationship between four types of law: eternal, natural, human, and divine. Human law derives its authority specifically from its alignment with natural law. This derivation occurs in two distinct modes:

  • Derivation by Conclusion: Human laws that follow logically from natural law principles (e.g., "one must not murder" follows from "one should do harm to no man"). these laws carry the force of both human and natural law.
  • Derivation by Determination: Human laws that specify details where the natural law is general (e.g., determining specific punishments for a crime or establishing traffic directions). These laws carry force only from human authority.

Criteria for Just and Binding Laws

For a human law to genuinely bind a citizen’s conscience, Aquinas argues it must meet three criteria of justice:

  1. End: The law must be ordained toward the common good, rather than the private advantage or "cupidity" of the ruler.
  2. Author: The law must be created by a legitimate authority and must not exceed the power committed to that lawgiver.
  3. Form: Burdens (such as taxes or duties) must be distributed across the community with "an equality of proportion."

The Ethics of Disobedience

Following Augustine’s dictum that "a law that is not just seems to be no law at all," Aquinas categorizes unjust laws into two types:

  • Contrary to Human Good: Laws that fail the criteria of End, Author, or Form. These are "acts of violence" rather than laws. They do not bind in conscience, though compliance may be necessary to avoid "scandal or disturbance."
  • Opposed to Divine Good: Laws that command acts contrary to divine law (e.g., idolatry). These must never be observed, as one must "obey God rather than man."

Principles of Legal Change

Aquinas maintains a conservative stance on changing laws, arguing that the force of law is derived largely from custom and habit.

  • The Cost of Change: Altering a law diminishes its binding power by disrupting the habit of obedience.
  • Justification for Change: Laws should only be changed if the "common weal" is compensated by a "very great and evident benefit" or in cases of "extreme urgency" where the existing law is clearly unjust or harmful.

Aristotelian Influence on the American Founding

The American Founders integrated Aristotelian concepts primarily through intermediaries like Montesquieu:

  • Mixed Government: Aristotle’s "polity" (blending democratic, oligarchic, and aristocratic elements) was reformulated into the American doctrine of separation of powers and checks and balances.
  • Factions: In Federalist No. 10, James Madison echoes Aristotle’s diagnosis that factions are "sown in the nature of man."
  • Structural vs. Social Solutions: While Aristotle relied on a large middle class for stability, Madison relied on structural mechanisms (size of the republic, staggered elections) to moderate passions.

Tocqueville’s "Aristotelian Anxieties"

Alexis de Tocqueville identified two primary threats to modern democracy that mirror classical concerns:

  1. Tyranny of the Majority: The danger that the majority will treat its will as absolute sovereignty, ignoring the common good and the rights of minorities.
  2. Soft Despotism: A state where citizens become indifferent to public life, focusing solely on private comfort. This results in an all-encompassing administrative state where citizens become "timid and industrious animals" led by a shepherd-like government.

II. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. According to Aquinas, what is the "first rule of reason" from which human law must derive?
  2. What is the difference in moral gravity between a law derived by "conclusion" versus one derived by "determination"?
  3. Under what specific circumstances does Aquinas suggest a person might obey an unjust human law?
  4. Why did Aquinas believe that human law should not be "quickly changed"?
  5. What are the three criteria a law must meet to be considered "just" and binding in conscience?
  6. How does Madison’s solution to the problem of faction in Federalist No. 10 differ from Aristotle’s solution?
  7. What is "soft despotism," and why did Tocqueville fear it?
  8. According to the text, what is the "form" criterion regarding the justice of a law?
  9. Which ancient author’s work on "mixed government" was reformulated by Montesquieu into the doctrine of separation of powers?
  10. In what scenario does Aquinas state a law "must nowise be observed"?

III. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The Limits of Legal Obligation: Analyze Aquinas’s argument that an unjust law is a "perversion of law" rather than a law at all. Compare this to his advice regarding "avoiding scandal." In a modern context, how does one determine if the "burden of proof" for civil disobedience has been met?
  2. Stability through Structure vs. Virtue: Contrast the Aristotelian view of political stability (which emphasizes the middle class and civic formation) with James Madison’s reliance on constitutional structure. Evaluate whether structural mechanisms alone can sustain a democracy if civic virtue—as Tocqueville feared—is lost.
  3. The Paradox of Legal Change: Aquinas argues that even if a "better" law is conceived, it may not be prudent to implement it. Discuss the relationship between custom, habit, and the authority of law. When does the cost of disrupting social habit outweigh the benefit of a more "just" or "efficient" statute?
  4. Democratic Degeneration: Examine Tocqueville’s concept of "soft despotism." How does this "gentle suffocation of political energy" compare to Aristotle’s concerns regarding the failure of political formation and the education of citizens for their constitution?

IV. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Common Weal: The public good or general welfare of the community.
  • Conscience: The internal "Divine court" where moral obligations are recognized; Aquinas argues just human laws bind here.
  • Derivation by Conclusion: The process of deriving a specific law as a logical consequence of a general natural principle (e.g., no murder follows from no harm).
  • Derivation by Determination: The process of particularizing a general natural law principle into a specific rule (e.g., deciding which side of the road to drive on).
  • Equality of Proportion: The principle that social burdens (taxes, etc.) must be distributed fairly and equitably among citizens based on their relation to the whole.
  • Factions: Groups of citizens united by a common passion or interest adverse to the rights of others or the permanent interests of the community.
  • Polity: Aristotle’s term for a stable constitution that balances democratic and aristocratic elements.
  • Scandal: In a legal/moral context, the disruption or social disorder that might occur if a law—even an unjust one—is publicly defied.
  • Soft Despotism: Tocqueville’s term for a modern form of state control that is not brutal but diminishes civic energy by making citizens concerned only with private comforts and dependent on an administrative state.
  • Tyranny of the Majority: A situation in democracy where the majority uses its power to oppress minorities or ignore the common good, treating its will as the ultimate standard of justice.
