Study Guide: The Evolution of Virtue and Justice from Aristotle to Aquinas and Beyond

This study guide examines the transition of Aristotelian ethics into the theological framework of Thomas Aquinas and explores how these classical questions of justice continue to shape modern political philosophy.

I. The Thomistic Synthesis of Aristotle

In the 1260s, Thomas Aquinas began the Summa Theologica, integrating the recently recovered Latin translations of Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics into Christian theology. Aquinas did not reject Aristotle’s pagan philosophy; instead, he sought to "baptize" it, arguing that reason (Aristotle) and faith (Christianity) are complementary.

The Nature of Virtue (Habitus)

Aquinas adopts Aristotle’s definition of virtue as a hexis—a stable state or settled disposition—which he translates as the Latin habitus.

  • Definition: A deep, reliable orientation of the soul that enables one to act, feel, and choose well.
  • The Theological Dimension: While Aristotle saw virtue as fitting us for earthly life, Aquinas argues that rightly ordered virtues open humanity toward a final supernatural end (God).

The Four Causes of Virtue

Aquinas identifies the "perfect essential notion" of virtue by looking at its four causes:

  1. Formal Cause: Defined as a "good quality" or "habit."
  2. Material Cause (Subject): The "mind" (the subject in which virtue exists).
  3. Material Cause (Object): The matter about which the virtue is concerned.
  4. End (Final Cause): Operation or "living righteously."
  5. Efficient Cause: For infused virtues, the efficient cause is God, who "works in us, without us."

II. The Classification of Virtues

Aquinas organizes the moral life around the "hinge" virtues of antiquity and the "infused" virtues of revelation.

The Cardinal Virtues

The term "cardinal" comes from the Latin cardo (hinge). These four virtues are the foundations upon which all other moral virtues depend.

Virtue Greek/Latin Term Faculty/Subject Definition
Prudence Phronesis / Prudentia Rational (Essence) Practical wisdom; the ability to discern the right action in specific circumstances.
Justice Dikaiosyne / Iustitia The Will The stable will to give each person what is rightly due to them.
Fortitude Andreia / Fortitudo Irascible Faculty Courage; the disposition to face dangers appropriately without fleeing or being rash.
Temperance Sophrosyne / Temperantia Concupiscible Faculty The moderation of appetite and pleasure within proper bounds.

The Theological Virtues

While the cardinal virtues perfect our natural capacities, Aquinas introduces three theological virtues—Faith, Hope, and Charity—which are infused by grace and direct humanity toward a supernatural end.

III. The Mean of Virtue

Aquinas refines Aristotle’s "mean of virtue," distinguishing between how virtue relates to reason versus how it relates to its matter.

  • Real Mean: A quantitative midpoint (like arithmetic).
  • Rational Mean: A mean determined by "right reason" relative to the individual and the circumstances.
  • The Extremes: To reason, virtue is an "extreme" of conformity and excellence. To the matter (passions/operations), virtue is a "mean" between excess and deficiency.
  • Exception: Virtues like justice and prudence have no "excess" because they are defined by what reason requires; one cannot be "too just."

IV. The Virtue of Justice

Aquinas defines justice as "the perpetual and constant will to render to each his right" (ius suum cuique tribuere).

Key Characteristics of Justice

  • Other-Directed: Unlike other virtues, justice essentially requires a relationship between distinct persons; one cannot be strictly unjust to oneself.
  • The Will: Justice is not a feeling or sentiment but a formed commitment of the rational appetite (the will).
  • General vs. Particular: Aquinas maintains the distinction between general justice (virtuous living within a community) and particular justice (rendering specific dues to others).

V. Justice in Modern Philosophy

Aristotle’s questions regarding the common good and individual rights remain central to 20th-century debates.

  • John Rawls (A Theory of Justice, 1971):

    • Concept: The "Veil of Ignorance."
    • Argument: Rational agents should choose principles of justice without knowing their own status (wealth, talent, class).
    • Aristotelian Connection: Resonates with the idea that justice aims at the common good rather than the advantage of the powerful.

  • Robert Nozick (Anarchy, State, and Utopia, 1974):

    • Concept: "Entitlement Theory."
    • Argument: Justice consists of respecting individual rights; redistribution is unjust if it violates what people have justly acquired.
    • Aristotelian Connection: Shares the view that justly acquired property is genuinely one's own, though it rejects the teleological framework of community flourishing.

VI. Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Aquinas define the term habitus in the context of virtue?
    • Answer: It is a stable disposition or settled orientation of the soul that enables a person to act, feel, and choose well.
  2. What does the phrase "God works in us, without us" refer to in the definition of virtue?
    • Answer: It refers to the efficient cause of "infused virtue," which is granted by God rather than acquired solely through human effort.
  3. Why does Aquinas argue that prudence is the "directing principle" of the other moral virtues?
    • Answer: Because prudence is the virtue of the rational faculty that discerns the "good as defined by reason," which the other virtues then put into order in operations or passions.
  4. Distinguish between the "irascible" and "concupiscible" faculties as subjects of virtue.
    • Answer: The irascible faculty is the subject of Fortitude (strengthening against fear/toil), while the concupiscible faculty is the subject of Temperance (curbing passions that incite against reason).
  5. In Aquinas’s view, why is justice considered a "will" rather than a "power"?
    • Answer: Because "will" denotes the voluntary act and the choice required for a habit to be virtuous; justice is a commitment to render to each their due.
  6. What is the "Veil of Ignorance" in John Rawls’s theory?
    • Answer: A thought experiment where individuals choose social principles without knowing their own place in society, ensuring protections for the least advantaged.

VII. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

  1. The "Baptism" of Aristotle: Analyze how Aquinas integrates Aristotelian reason with Christian faith. In what ways does Aquinas believe that faith "completes" what reason achieves in the realm of virtue?
  2. The Rational Mean: Explain the distinction between a "real mean" and a "rational mean." Use the examples of Justice and Temperance to illustrate why virtue is considered an "extreme" in relation to reason but a "mean" in relation to its matter.
  3. Natural vs. Supernatural Ends: Compare the ultimate goal of virtue in Aristotle’s Ethics with that of Aquinas’s Summa Theologica. How does the addition of the theological virtues (Faith, Hope, Charity) change the "final cause" of human action?
  4. Modern Applications of Justice: Evaluate the debate between John Rawls and Robert Nozick using Aristotelian concepts. Does Aristotle’s emphasis on the "common good" align more closely with Rawls’s fairness or Nozick’s entitlement?

VIII. Glossary of Important Terms

  • Cardo: Latin for "hinge"; the root of "cardinal" virtues.
  • Efficient Cause: The agent that brings a thing (like infused virtue) into being.
  • Habitus: The Latin equivalent of the Greek hexis; a stable state of character.
  • Hexis: A stable disposition to respond well or badly.
  • Infused Virtue: Virtues (like the theological virtues) given by God's grace rather than through human habituation.
  • Ius suum cuique tribuere: "To render to each his right"; the classic definition of justice.
  • Mean of Virtue: The point of conformity with reason, situated between the extremes of excess and deficiency.
  • Teleological Framework: A system of thought, like Aristotle’s, centered on the "telos" or final end/purpose of a community or individual.
  • Veil of Ignorance: Rawls’s thought experiment designed to determine fair principles of justice by removing knowledge of one's personal circumstances.
