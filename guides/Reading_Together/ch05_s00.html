Chapter 5: No Wings

Overview

This section finds the children stranded at the very top of a church tower, having lost their ability to fly. Panic and anxiety set in as they grapple with their predicament and the incriminating "soda-water syphon." Their desperate cries for help are severely misinterpreted by the villagers, leading to a tense rescue mission where the adults expect to confront dangerous criminals rather than a group of terrified children.

Key Concepts

  • Consequences of Magic: The children experience the immediate aftermath of their magical flight, discovering they are stuck in a very un-magical and dangerous situation. This highlights the limits and unpredictable nature of the magic they encounter.
  • Panic and Resourcefulness: Initially, the children are overcome with fear and frustration, particularly Jane. However, Cyril quickly takes charge, devising a plan to signal for help, showcasing their resilience and ability to strategize under pressure.
  • Miscommunication and Misinterpretation: The chapter heavily features how the children's desperate yells are drastically misunderstood by the adults below. Their cries are taken as signs of murder, ghosts, or a dangerous lunatic, revealing the disconnect between their reality and the villagers' perceptions.
  • The Burden of the Syphon: The soda-water syphon, a seemingly minor object, becomes a major source of anxiety and potential trouble for the children. Cyril's constant fear of being accused of stealing it adds a layer of suspense and complication to their rescue.
  • Adult Bias and Assumptions: The adults approach the "crisis" with preconceived notions, expecting violence and criminality. This bias leads them to arm themselves and treat the children as potential "desperadoes," illustrating how fear can shape perception.

Important Terms

  • Blind-cord: The string or cord used to raise or lower a window blind. (Relevant when the maid pauses with her hand on it.)
  • Belfry: The part of a church tower where the bells are hung. (Where the owls and starlings are startled, and where the children are found.)
  • Gamekeeper: A person employed to look after wildlife on a country estate and to watch for poachers. (The Cook's Cousin, who leads the rescue party and is armed.)
  • Desperadoes: Dangerous criminals or outlaws. (The term the gamekeeper uses to describe what he expects to find at the top of the tower.)
  • Parapet: A low wall along the edge of a roof or tower, meant to stop people from falling off. (Where the children are standing when the door is opened.)
  • Entreaty: An earnest, pleading request. (The children's desperate pleas to be taken down.)
  • Allay: To calm or reduce someone's worry or fear. (Robert tries to allay the Vicar's wife's anxiety.)
  • Flags: Large, flat stones used for paving floors, especially in old churches. (The children step out onto these in the church-porch.)
  • Vicarage: The house where the Vicar (a clergyman in charge of a parish) lives. (Where the maid and other servants are, and where the children are taken after rescue.)
  • Bell-ringers' loft: The room in a church tower where people stand to pull the ropes that ring the bells above. (Part of the tower the rescue party climbs through.)

Detailed Notes

Trapped in the Tower

  • Realization and Panic: After their magical flight, the children find themselves stranded at the very top of an English church tower. The magic has worn off, leaving them in a dangerous and unfamiliar situation.
  • Initial Reactions:
    • Anthea tries to calm Jane, who is crying and wants to go home.
    • Cyril is gloomy, immediately concerned about the syphon and the possibility of being sent to prison for stealing it.
    • Robert suggests throwing the syphon away, but Cyril points out the danger of hitting someone.
    • Jane desperately wants her tea, highlighting her youth and distress.
    • Robert, in a moment of desperation, suggests letting Jane cry loudly enough to attract attention, which Anthea quickly dismisses due to the syphon.
  • The Syphon's Predicament: Cyril takes responsibility for the soda-water syphon, buttoning it inside his jacket, constantly worried about being caught with it. The children's fear of accusation overrides their immediate need for rescue, creating a moral dilemma.

A Desperate Plan for Rescue

  • Cyril's Strategy: Cyril devises a plan to yell for help, instructing Robert to make a "railway engine" yell and himself to do a "coo-ee" like his father's. The girls are told to yell as they please.
  • First Yells: Their combined "fourfold yell" breaks the silence of the evening, immediately attracting attention from the Vicarage.

Adult Misinterpretations and Fear

  • The Maid's Reaction: A maid at the Vicarage, startled by the first yells, flees, faints, and then reports seeing a ghost. This shows the immediate, superstitious fear evoked by the unfamiliar sounds.
  • The Vicar's Alarm: The Vicar interprets the subsequent yells as a murder in the church and prepares to confront a "lunatic" he suspects stole a "tongue" (likely implying a previous incident or local rumor). He arms himself with a stick and calls for his man-servant, Andrew.
  • Vicar's Wife's Panic: The Vicar's wife is terrified for her husband and reinforces the idea of a "dangerous lunatic," urging Andrew to "catch it."
  • Andrew's Reluctance: Andrew is hesitant and cautious, suspecting a "trap" and expressing reluctance to go alone. His actions are driven by self-preservation, feigning bravery later.

The Rescue Mission

  • Confirmation of Location: Andrew shouts, and the children confirm their location: "CHURCH! TOWER! TOP!" and that they "CAN'T! DOOR LOCKED!"
  • Assembling the Rescue Party: The Vicar, Andrew, and the Cook's Cousin (a gamekeeper armed with a gun) form the rescue party. The Vicar's wife pleads for caution.
  • Differing Theories:
    • The Vicar remains convinced it's the "lunatic" from earlier.
    • Andrew believes it's a "trap."
    • The gamekeeper, though calm and armed, expects "vicious characters" or "desperadoes," showing the pervasive suspicion.
  • The Ascent:
    • The gamekeeper leads the way, lantern in one hand, gun in the other.
    • Andrew follows, pretending bravery but actually fearing a "trap" from behind.
    • The climbing is described in detail: corkscrew staircase, bell-ringers' loft (with bell-ropes like giant caterpillars), belfry, ladder, and a small stone stair leading to the bolted door.

The Confrontation at the Door

  • Keeper's Caution: The gamekeeper kicks the door and shouts. Cyril, hoarse from yelling, replies. The keeper interrogates them, asking how many, if they are armed, and threatening to use his gun if they try "tricks."
  • Children's Compliance: The children, desperate, promise to come down quietly.
  • Vicar's Observation: The Vicar notes a "female voice," starting to question the "dangerous lunatic" theory.
  • Opening the Door: The keeper, still cautious, makes them move to the other side of the tower before unbolting the door, which is stiff from disuse. He then throws it open, expecting criminals.

The Reveal and Descent

  • A "Pack of Kiddies": The gamekeeper is astonished to find only children, lowering his gun in surprise.
  • The Vicar's Interrogation: The Vicar severely questions them, demanding an explanation.
  • Children's Plea: The children, particularly Jane, are more concerned with getting down than explaining, making urgent "entreaties."
  • Cyril's Ongoing Syphon Struggle: Cyril continues to struggle with the syphon, trying to keep it hidden even during the descent, nearly falling.
  • The Descent: The descent is difficult in the dark. The gamekeeper helps, but Cyril must remain "independent" because of the syphon.
  • Arrival at the Bottom: They finally reach the church-porch. The gamekeeper grabs Cyril and Robert, still wary, believing they might try to run.

In the Vicarage Study

  • Vicar's Wife's Relief and Scolding: The Vicar's wife rushes in, relieved to find her husband safe but immediately scolds the children for "frightening everybody out of their wits."
  • Children's Fabricated Story: Robert, trying to "allay" anxiety and get home, invents a story: they went up, fell asleep, and woke to find the door locked. Jane meekly agrees they are "ashamed."
  • Unanswered Questions: The Vicar is still puzzled about who locked the door.
  • Desire to Go Home: Robert and Anthea express their lateness and concern for Martha, hoping for a carriage.
  • Cyril's Secret: Cyril remains hunched over, protecting the syphon, while the adults question them. The true magical explanation for their presence remains unspoken.

Review Questions

  1. What was the children's main dilemma after realizing they were trapped? How did their individual reactions (Anthea, Cyril, Robert, Jane) differ in the initial moments of panic?
  2. How did the "soda-water syphon" become a central source of anxiety for Cyril throughout this chapter? Discuss why it was so important for him to keep it hidden, and what it symbolizes in their predicament.
  3. Describe the various ways the adults (the maid, the Vicar, his wife, and Andrew) misinterpreted the children's yells for help. What do these differing interpretations reveal about their assumptions and fears?
  4. The gamekeeper takes a very cautious and even aggressive approach when confronting the sounds from the tower. Explain his initial assumptions and how he changed his demeanor upon discovering the "pack of kiddies."
  5. Why do the children choose to tell a fabricated story about how they got locked in the tower, rather than the truth? What might be the potential consequences of this decision, both immediate and long-term?
