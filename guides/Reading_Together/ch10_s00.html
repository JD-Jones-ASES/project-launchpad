Chapter 10: Scalps - A Study Guide

Overview

This section opens with Cyril's innocent, yet ultimately disastrous, wish for "Red Indians" in England, inspired by a book. The children quickly learn from the Psammead that their wish has been granted, leading to agonizing uncertainty about its terrifying implications. Faced with the possibility of dangerous scalping Indians, Anthea takes drastic, resourceful measures to protect her youngest sibling, the Lamb, while grappling with the boys' skepticism and her own moral compromises.

Key Concepts

  • The Peril of Thoughtless Wishes: Cyril's casual wish, made without considering the real-world consequences or the Psammead's literal interpretation, plunges the children into a serious predicament. This highlights the ongoing theme of the Psammead's wishes often creating more problems than they solve.
  • Leadership and Resourcefulness in Crisis: Anthea emerges as the primary leader in this crisis, demonstrating quick thinking, determination, and a willingness to make difficult choices to protect her younger brother. Her actions, though morally questionable (breaking property, stealing from a charity box), are driven by a strong protective instinct.
  • Sibling Dynamics and Conflict: The children exhibit distinct personalities under pressure. Anthea is decisive and protective, Jane is supportive but anxious, and the boys (especially Cyril, due to guilt) are skeptical and critical, underestimating the danger. This creates tension and highlights their different coping mechanisms.
  • Moral Dilemmas and Justification: Anthea's actions of breaking the jug and the missionary-box raise questions about whether the "greater good" (saving the Lamb) justifies breaking rules and property. The story suggests that while her intent is noble, the methods are certainly problematic.
  • Denial as a Coping Mechanism: Cyril's persistent refusal to believe in the Red Indians, despite the Psammead's warning and Anthea's certainty, stems from his guilt over making the wish. This denial prevents him from engaging constructively with the problem.
  • Uncertainty and Fear of the Unknown: A significant part of the children's distress comes from not knowing what the wish entails, where the Indians might be, and when they might appear. This prolonged anxiety is more debilitating than a direct, immediate threat.

Important Terms

  • The Last of the Mohicans: A famous adventure novel by James Fenimore Cooper (1826) set during the French and Indian War. Cyril's reading of this book directly inspires his wish for Red Indians.
  • Psammead (Sand-fairy): The ancient magical creature that grants wishes, often with unexpected and challenging results. In this section, it is irritable and confirms a wish has been granted, hinting at its dangerous nature.
  • Two-shilling pieces (florins): British coins worth two shillings each. The children initially wanted 100 pounds in these coins.
  • Queen Victoria: The Queen who ruled Britain from 1837 to 1901. Her image appeared on British coins of the era.
  • Boot-tree: A foot-shaped wooden form inserted into boots or shoes to help them keep their shape. Anthea uses one as a tool to break the water-jug.
  • Missionary-box: A small, locked collection box kept in homes for donating money to missionaries or charity. Breaking it open was considered a serious offense.
  • Seven-and-fourpence, Four shillings, Half-a-crown, Eight shillings, Coppers: References to British currency. Seven-and-fourpence means seven shillings and four pence. A half-a-crown is two shillings and sixpence. "Coppers" refer to copper-coloured coins of small value, like pennies.
  • Liberty box: A decorative box, likely from Liberty's, a famous and fashionable London department store, offered by Anthea as a bribe to Martha.
  • Trencher: A flat wooden board traditionally used as a plate for serving food.
  • Damask: A rich, patterned fabric. In the text, "deepening of the damask of Martha's complexion" is a figurative way to describe her face getting redder (from agitation or anger).
  • Scalping: The act of removing the scalp (skin and hair from the top of the head). In the context of "The Last of the Mohicans," this is a violent act associated with certain Native American tribes during warfare.

Detailed Notes

The Unintended Wish

  • Cyril's Inspiration: The chapter begins with Cyril engrossed in "The Last of the Mohicans," a book about adventure and war involving Native Americans. This leads him to idly wish for "little Red Indians" in England for them to "fight."
  • The Psammead's Confirmation: The children visit the Psammead, hoping for their previously discussed wish of 100 pounds in florins. However, the Psammead, grumpy and sleepy, informs them they've already had their wish. It reminds them of their earlier request to have wishes granted wherever they happened to be.
  • Ominous Warning: The Sand-fairy offers a cryptic and unsettling message: "A nice thing you've let yourselves in for!" before burrowing away, leaving the children in suspense.

The Agonizing Uncertainty

  • Forgotten Wish: No one can recall what wish was made that morning, leading to intense anxiety and fear. They spend hours in dreadful anticipation, convinced something terrible is about to happen based on the Psammead's warning.
  • Anthea's Revelation: It isn't until Jane trips over Cyril's abandoned copy of "The Last of the Mohicans" that Anthea suddenly remembers Cyril's breakfast-time wish for Red Indians.
  • The Threat of Scalping: The immediate fear is that these "Red Indians" will be scalping people, potentially even their baby brother, the Lamb. Jane attempts to downplay the danger, suggesting the Indians might be far away or that the wish's effects might reverse at sunset, but Anthea quickly dismisses these hopeful notions based on past experiences with the Psammead's wishes.

Anthea's Desperate Plan

  • Protecting the Lamb: Anthea's main goal is to get the Lamb (and Martha) out of danger. She believes the Indians will target their house and that the farmer's cart, which won't return until after sunset, is their best chance for safety.
  • Fundraising through Destruction:
    • The Water-Jug: Anthea decides she needs money. Her first act is to break her mother's water-jug. Interestingly, despite attempting to drop it three times, it remains intact, forcing her to use her father's boot-tree to deliberately smash it – a "heartless" act.
    • The Missionary-Box: Next, she uses a poker to break open the missionary-box, a locked collection box for charity. Jane objects, calling it "wrong," but Anthea justifies it as a "matter of life and death." They collect "over eleven shillings" from the box and their own money.
  • Arranging Transport:
    • The children had previously arranged to ride with the farmer to Rochester for two shillings each (total eight shillings).
    • Anthea hastily changes the plan, telling the farmer they can't go but asks him to take Martha and the Lamb instead. She pays him only a "half-a-crown" (two shillings and sixpence) instead of the full eight shillings, displeasing him.
  • Convincing Martha:
    • Martha is initially cross and suspicious when Anthea approaches her.
    • Anthea confesses to breaking the jug and offers to pay for a new one if Martha will buy it from her cousins' china-shop that day, citing their mother's potential return.
    • Martha sees through Anthea's attempt to "get the precious Lamb off your hands."
    • To seal the deal, Anthea offers Martha her valuable "Liberty box" as a bribe. Martha agrees, but warns the girls against further mischief.
    • Anthea hurries Martha and the Lamb into their best clothes and ensures they leave with the farmer.

Relief and Rising Skepticism

  • Anthea's Emotional Release: Once Martha and the Lamb are safely gone, Anthea collapses in tears, a mixture of joy and relief that her aim has been accomplished. Jane is surprised by her sister's sudden emotional shift after such a show of bravery.
  • The Boys' Disbelief: At dinner, Anthea attempts to tell the boys her story, but the mundane setting and food make the idea of Red Indians seem "flat and unbelievable."
    • Cyril, perhaps feeling guilty for his wish, dismisses Anthea's fears, suggesting it was actually a wish for a fine day. He questions why, if Indians were wished for, they wouldn't already be present, implying wishes are always immediate.
    • Anthea defends herself, pointing to the Psammead's explicit warning and her own difficult actions, which weigh heavily on her conscience.
    • Cyril continues his denial, calling Anthea's actions "utter rot" and threatening to report her for breaking the missionary-box, a "treason-crime." Robert tells him to "shut up."
  • Cyril's Guilt-Driven Denial: The narrative explains that Cyril's intense disbelief is a coping mechanism. He feels responsible for the potential Indians and therefore "did not wish to believe in them," illustrating how guilt can lead to denial and problematic behavior.

Review Questions

  1. How does Cyril's initial wish reflect a lack of understanding about the Psammead's power and the real-world implications of his desires? What are some examples of past wishes that should have taught him caution?
  2. Analyze Anthea's actions after realizing the wish was for Red Indians. What does her behavior reveal about her character, leadership qualities, and her relationship with the Lamb?
  3. Discuss the moral dilemmas presented by Anthea's plan to protect the Lamb. Was she justified in breaking the water-jug and the missionary-box, given the perceived threat? What are the potential long-term consequences of these actions for her and her siblings?
  4. How do the reactions of Cyril, Robert, and Jane to Anthea's revelation and plan differ? What do these reactions tell us about their individual personalities and how they cope with crisis, especially when compared to Anthea?
  5. The story ends with Cyril denying the existence of the Red Indians despite his underlying fear and guilt. What psychological phenomenon does this demonstrate, and why is it problematic for the children's ability to deal with the actual consequences of the wish?
