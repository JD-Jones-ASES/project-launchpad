Study Guide: Chapter 3 - Being Wanted

Overview

This section finds the children disillusioned with their previous wishes for beauty and boundless wealth, neither of which brought them true happiness. Amidst the chaos of a boisterous baby brother, they grapple with the consequences of their magical encounters and debate how to use their daily wish from the Psammead more effectively. Their renewed hope for a "sensible" wish is quickly complicated by the practical challenges of daily life and a surprising oversight regarding the Psammead's location.

Key Concepts

  • Unintended Consequences of Wishes: The children realize that even wishes granted by a magical creature can lead to dissatisfaction or new problems, highlighting the theme that superficial desires (beauty, wealth) don't equate to happiness.
  • The Burden of Daily Life: Despite having access to magic, mundane responsibilities like caring for a baby, mending clothes, and dealing with everyday spills continue to intrude upon and complicate their fantastical adventures.
  • Differing Perspectives: The children display varied attitudes towards the Psammead and its wishes, with some (Robert, Anthea) growing wary and others (Cyril, Jane) remaining optimistic about finding the "right" wish.
  • The Importance of Practicality: After two failed wishes, the children decide to aim for a more practical and tangible benefit – a specific amount of money – reflecting a shift towards more grounded desires, even if still motivated by material goods.
  • The Fleeting Nature of Opportunity: The oversight of not marking the Psammead's location underscores how easily even extraordinary chances can be lost or complicated by simple human forgetfulness or distraction.

Important Terms

  • Hash: Leftover meat chopped up small and cooked again with potatoes or vegetables. Historically considered a plain or boring meal.
  • Petticoat: An undergarment worn under a dress or skirt. Wearing only a petticoat in public would be considered highly improper or akin to going out in one's underwear.
  • Sundial: A garden instrument that tells time by the shadow cast by the sun on a marked plate. A common feature in traditional gardens.
  • Darned: Mended a hole in fabric by weaving thread back and forth with a needle. A traditional method of repairing clothes, necessary before inexpensive clothing was widely available.
  • Shilling: A former British coin worth twelve old pennies. Twenty shillings made one pound.
  • Vulgar fractions: An old-fashioned term for ordinary fractions like 3/4 or 1/2. "Vulgar" in this context means "common" or "ordinary," not rude.
  • Army and Navy Stores: A famous department store in London that historically sold a wide variety of goods, from toys to furniture, and was known for its thick mail-order catalogues.
  • Psammead (Sand-fairy): The magical creature discovered by the children, which grants them one wish each day that lasts until sunset. (Introduced in previous chapters, but central to the plot here).

Detailed Notes

The Lingering Disappointment

  • Morning After: The children wake up without the enthusiasm they felt previously, having realized that their wishes for "Beauty" and "Boundless Wealth" did not bring them lasting happiness. The wealth they acquired was impractical and difficult to use, resulting in only a few trivial purchases.
  • Contrast with Mundane Life: Despite the magical events, daily life continues, with meals, chores, and the occasional "cold mutton or hash" reminding them that not everything is magical or pleasant.

Breakfast Chaos and the Baby's Antics

  • Oversleeping: Everyone oversleeps, leading to a rushed and late start to breakfast.
  • The Baby (Lamb) Problem: Any attempts to discuss the Psammead are thwarted by the Baby's extreme liveliness and disruptive behavior.
    • He wriggles out of his high chair, chokes, and turns purple.
    • He hits Cyril with a tablespoon and cries when it's taken away.
    • He plays with his bread-and-milk, demands unauthorized snacks, sings, and puts his feet on the table.
    • The Goldfish Incident: The Baby knocks over a bowl of goldfish, spilling water and fish everywhere, causing widespread commotion and distress for everyone except the Baby.
  • Consequences and Chores:
    • The goldfish are rescued and the mess is cleaned up.
    • The Baby is taken away to be redressed.
    • Most children have to change clothes due to the water spill.
    • Jane faces a dilemma: her dress from the previous day is torn, and Martha, the housekeeper, forbids her from wearing her beautiful petticoat as an outer garment, deeming it "not respectable." Jane is forced to mend her torn dress.

The Psammead Debate

  • Mending Session: The children (minus the Baby) gather on the grass-plot around the sundial while Jane mends her dress, finally creating an opportunity for conversation.
  • Conflicting Opinions:
    • Robert and Anthea's Suspicion: They are the first to voice their distrust of the Psammead, suggesting it might be "spiteful" and intentionally sabotaging their wishes so they don't do any good. They propose leaving the Psammead alone and simply playing on their own.
    • Cyril and Jane's Optimism: They are more hopeful, believing the Psammead isn't malicious, but rather their previous wishes were "silly" ("boundless wealth" and "beautiful as the day"). They advocate for thinking harder and choosing a "really useful wish" to make the day jolly.
  • "Talking by Fours": The narrator describes the children's unique ability to talk simultaneously yet still listen to each other, comparing it to soldiers marching "by fours."

The New Wish: Fifty Pounds

  • A "Sensible" Wish: After discussion, the children decide on a new, more practical wish: "fifty pounds in two-shilling pieces." This is seen as a manageable and useful amount of money.
  • Martha's Intervention: As they prepare to go to the gravel-pit to make their wish, Martha intercepts them, insisting they take the Baby with them, reminding them of their promise to their mother.
  • The Baby's Charm: Despite initial reluctance from Robert, the Baby quickly makes himself agreeable through his laughter, rides on Robert's back, and attempts to feed Jane stones. His presence becomes less of a burden and more of a joyful addition.
  • Future Planning for the Baby: Jane suggests dedicating wishes to ensure the Baby's future, like in fairy tales. However, Anthea points out the Psammead's wishes only last until sunset, making long-term benefits impossible. They settle on buying the Baby an expensive rocking-horse (from the Army and Navy Stores catalogue) with some of the wished-for money.
  • Excitement and Plans: Full of hope, they envision using the money to drive to Rochester with Mr. Crispin, taking Martha if necessary, and carefully making a list of desired purchases beforehand.

The Critical Oversight

  • Journey to the Gravel-Pits: The children head towards the gravel-pits, where they expect to find the Psammead.
  • Sudden Realization: A crucial thought suddenly strikes them, causing their faces to go blank. They remember that the previous day, when they wished for "boundless wealth," the Psammead had instructed them to run outside the quarry to avoid being buried under the gold.
  • The Missing Psammead: In their haste to escape the gold, they had forgotten to mark the Psammead's exact spot with a ring of stones, as they had done before.
  • The Search Begins: Jane remains hopeful that they will "soon find him," but the search for the Sand-fairy proves difficult, despite their efforts and the convenient excuse of the Baby needing to be put down. The section ends with them realizing the gravity of their forgetfulness.

Review Questions

  1. How do the children's experiences with the wishes for "Beauty" and "Boundless Wealth" change their perspective on what they truly desire?
  2. Discuss the role of "The Lamb" (the Baby) in this section. How does his presence both hinder and eventually enrich the children's day and their plans?
  3. Compare and contrast the different attitudes of Robert/Anthea and Cyril/Jane towards the Psammead and the effectiveness of its wishes. What does this reveal about their individual personalities?
  4. Why is the decision to wish for "fifty pounds in two-shilling pieces" considered more "sensible" than their previous wishes? What does this imply about their learning process?
  5. Analyze the significance of the children forgetting to mark the Psammead's location. What does this incident tell us about the nature of their adventures and the practical challenges they face?
