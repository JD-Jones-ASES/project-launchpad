Study Guide: Chapter 2 - Golden Guineas

Overview

This section picks up the morning after the children's first encounter with the Sand-fairy, revealing that their fantastic dream was, in fact, real. After confirming the truth of their adventure and making arrangements for the baby to be out of the way, the children return to the gravel-pit with a new, agreed-upon strategy for wishing. They successfully obtain two wishes: for their gifts to go unnoticed by servants, and for vast riches in the form of gold, setting the stage for new adventures and challenges.

Key Concepts

  • The Reality of Imagination: The central revelation that the shared dream about the Sand-fairy was real blurs the lines between fantasy and reality for the children, proving that extraordinary events can indeed occur in their world.
  • Consequences of Unprepared Wishes: Cyril's reminder about the "peerless beauties" wish highlights the importance of careful planning and agreement before making wishes, demonstrating a learned lesson from previous magical experiences.
  • The Psammead's Peculiar Nature: The Psammead is further characterized as a grumpy, literal, and somewhat exhausted magical creature capable of granting powerful wishes but not without its own quirks and conditions (e.g., its restless whisker, its own odd dreams).
  • The Practicalities of Wealth: While the wish for "millions of gold" is granted in an overwhelming display, the immediate discovery that the coins are "not sovereigns" introduces a practical problem that challenges the children's initial excitement, hinting at the complexities and limitations of their newfound riches.
  • Children's Perception of Servants: Jane's imaginative theory about Martha pretending the Baby is her "little duke" reveals the children's innocent yet class-conscious perspective on the lives and motivations of their domestic staff.

Important Terms

  • Apple-pie beds: A practical joke where someone folds the sheets in a bed so that the person who gets in can't stretch out their legs.
  • Booby-traps: Tricks set up to surprise or startle someone, often involving an object falling or moving unexpectedly.
  • Sand-fairy (Psammead): The unique, spider-shaped, wish-granting creature with bat's ears and snail's eyes introduced in the previous chapter, which the children sometimes call "Sammyadd."
  • Carrier: A person who transports goods and/or passengers between villages or towns using a horse and cart, akin to an early form of local delivery and public transport.
  • Tilt: A canvas or cloth cover stretched over hoops on a cart or wagon, designed to protect passengers or goods from weather like rain and sun.
  • Smelting furnace: A very hot oven or chamber used in metallurgy to melt metal ore, separating the pure metal from impurities.
  • Sovereigns: A specific type of British gold coin, historically minted in the United Kingdom. The fact that the gold the children receive is not sovereigns indicates a potential issue with its usability or value.
  • Golden Guineas: The chapter title refers to another historical British gold coin, slightly different in value from a sovereign. This implies the children might have expected to receive these, or that the received gold is of an unexpected type.

Detailed Notes

The Morning After: Dreams and Reality

  • Anthea's Awakening: Anthea wakes from a vivid dream about a rainy Zoological Gardens. Robert wakes her with a wet bath-towel trick, a characteristic example of his "ingenious" sibling antics.
  • The Shared Dream: Anthea, Jane, and Robert discover they've all had the same extraordinary dream about finding a Sand-fairy (which Jane calls "Sammyadd") and being granted wishes. Robert specifically recalls their first disastrous wish to be "beautiful as the day."
  • Cyril's Confirmation: Cyril, the eldest brother, dismisses their "dream" talk, revealing he already knows it's all true. He emphasizes the need for careful planning and agreement among them before making any more wishes, especially after the "peerless beauties" debacle.
  • Anthea's Certainty: Anthea remains somewhat skeptical until she hears Martha (the servant) discuss mundane "Dream-book" interpretations. She concludes that since servants only dream predictable things, the Sand-fairy must be real, as it's too fantastical for a servant's dream.

Martha and the Lamb's Departure

  • The Lamb Goes to Rochester: Martha is taking the Baby ("the Lamb") to visit her cousins in Rochester, dressed in his finest cream-coloured silk coat and hat.
  • Children's Relief and Speculation: The children are happy to see the baby and Martha depart, ensuring their privacy for wish-making. Jane fancifully suggests that Martha will pretend the Lamb is her own "little duke" to impress her cousins, highlighting the children's imaginative understanding of social roles.
  • Travel by Carrier: Martha and the Lamb leave via a carrier's cart, with a white tilt and red wheels, picked up at the Cross Roads. The children wave them off, eagerly anticipating their return to the gravel-pits.

The Return to the Psammead

  • Strategic Approach: The children, despite their hurry, wisely take the safe lower road to the gravel-pit, avoiding dangerous climbs. They easily locate the spot where the Psammead disappeared, marked by a ring of stones.
  • Sibling Bickering and Reconciliation: While digging, Robert expresses doubt about the Psammead's reality, leading to a brief, warm argument with Cyril and Jane. Anthea intervenes, using flattery to calm Robert and nominate him as the spokesperson for their wishes.
  • Uncovering the Psammead: They uncover the creature's familiar, strange form: spider-shaped, brown, hairy, with long arms/legs, bat's ears, and snail's eyes. This sight removes all doubt that it was merely a dream.
  • Polite Greetings: Anthea, ever polite, asks about the Psammead's "left whisker," which had a "restless night," showing the creature's quirky sensitivity.

The Wishes are Made

  • The "Extra" Wish: Robert, empowered by Anthea's flattery, asks if the Psammead can grant an "extra" small wish besides the regular daily one.
  • Psammead's Grumpiness: The Psammead initially expresses annoyance, mentioning it thought it had dreamed them because of their loud squabbling. Jane tries to distract it by asking about its dreams, almost costing them a wish.
  • Wish 1: Invisibility to Servants: Their first agreed-upon wish is for the servants "not to notice the gifts" the Psammead gives them. Anthea whispers a correction, adding "Are kind enough to give us," which Robert repeats. The Psammead grants this easily, noting "People don't notice things much, anyway."
  • Wish 2: Riches Beyond Dreams: Robert then articulates their main wish: "to be rich beyond the dreams of something or other." Jane supplies "Avarice."
  • Details of the Gold: The Psammead confirms it can't go "beyond dreams" and asks for quantity and form (gold or notes). They ask for "millions" in gold. The Psammead agrees to fill the gravel-pit, warning them to "get out before I begin, or you'll be buried alive in it."

The Golden Transformation

  • A Dazzling Spectacle: As the children flee, Anthea manages a polite farewell. They look back to witness an astounding sight: the entire gravel-pit is filled to the brim with new, shining gold coins, blindingly bright like the midday sun.
  • Overwhelming Abundance: The gold lies in vast heaps, covering the sand-martins' nests and forming banks, gleaming like a "smelting furnace" or "fairy halls."
  • Initial Awe and Play: The children are initially speechless, then excitedly begin to play with the gold, letting it run through their fingers and enjoying the "wonderful music" of its chinking.
  • The Problem: Robert picks up a coin and, after inspecting it, states in a changed voice: "It's not sovereigns." This final line reveals a critical, unforeseen complication to their immense wealth.

Review Questions

  1. How did the children come to the collective realization that their dream about the Sand-fairy was real? What specific detail convinced Anthea, who was initially unsure?
  2. Before making their wishes, what rules or agreements did the children establish, and why were these rules important given their previous experiences?
  3. Describe the Psammead's personality as it interacts with the children in this chapter. Provide at least two specific examples of its words or actions that illustrate its character.
  4. What were the two wishes the children made in this section, and how did the Psammead react to each? What might the Psammead's comments reveal about the nature of wishes or people?
  5. At the very end of the chapter, Robert makes a crucial discovery about the newly acquired gold. What is this discovery, and what potential problems or challenges might it present for the children in the future?
