A Castle and No Dinner Study Guide

Overview

This chapter finds the children grounded as punishment, but Robert is sent out to make the day's wish. His attempts to quickly wish for something suitable lead to an unexpected magical outcome: the family's house is replaced by a medieval castle under siege, dropping them into a living historical romance. Robert soon encounters the besieging army, whose anachronistic leader and archaic language present both wonder and confusion.

Key Concepts

  • Consequences of Wishes: The central theme continues as every wish, even one made with good intentions, leads to unforeseen and often inconvenient or dangerous circumstances. Robert's wish to spare the others the trip to the Psammead results in an extreme and literal interpretation.
  • Adult vs. Child Perspectives: Martha, the caregiver, views the children's past mishaps as "naughtiness" requiring punishment, believing she's acting "for their own good." The children, however, experience it as unfair misfortune, highlighting the common disconnect in understanding between adults and children.
  • The Psammead's Literalism and Power: The Sand-fairy demonstrates its immense power by granting a wish remotely, though it requires significant effort. Its literal interpretation of wishes (e.g., wishing the others to "have a wish" leading to them making one collectively) remains a constant source of trouble.
  • Fantasy and Reality in Historical Romances: Robert's admiration for the "glorious leader" and the besieging camp is shaped by the idealized, often historically inaccurate, depictions found in his beloved historical romances. This blend of fantasy and reality influences his perception, allowing him to overlook inconsistencies.
  • Language and Communication Barriers: The old-fashioned language spoken by the medieval soldiers and their leader creates a significant communication challenge for Robert, who must translate their archaic phrases into modern understanding, often with humorously practical results.

Important Terms

  • Audacious: Bold and daring in a way that is cheeky or shocking. (e.g., "they are that audacious, they'll be walking in with their heads knocked off...")
  • Forrard: A dialect pronunciation of “forward,” meaning ahead in their work. (e.g., "...soon as we've got a bit forrard with our work.")
  • Battlements: The notched wall along the top of a castle, with raised sections and gaps that soldiers could shoot through while staying protected. (e.g., "...with battlements and lancet windows...")
  • Lancet windows: Tall, narrow windows with pointed arch tops, common in medieval castles and churches. (e.g., "...with battlements and lancet windows...")
  • Moat: A deep, wide ditch filled with water surrounding a castle, making it harder for enemies to attack. (e.g., "...across the moat that now lay where the garden had been...")
  • Steel-caps: Helmets made of steel, worn by soldiers. Here, specifically referring to the rounded helmets from the English Civil War (the 1640s). (e.g., "...two men in steel-caps were coming towards him.")
  • Halidom: An old oath meaning “by my holy relic” or “by all that is holy.” The sort of thing knights said. (e.g., "By my halidom,' said one, a brave varlet this!'")
  • Varlet: An old word for a young servant or attendant. Also used as a mild insult meaning “rascal.” (e.g., "`...a brave varlet this!'")
  • Belike: An old English word meaning “probably” or “perhaps.” (e.g., "Some outlandish treachery, belike.'")
  • Hither: An old word meaning “to this place” or “here.” “Come hither” means “come here.” (e.g., "`what brings thee hither?'")
  • Zooks: A mild old-fashioned oath, short for “Gadzooks,” itself a softened version of “God's hooks.” (e.g., "`...none hindereth, and nought lets us to follow. Zooks!'")
  • Knave: Originally a boy or servant, but by the Middle Ages it meant a dishonest person. (e.g., "`Where dwellest thou, young knave?'")
  • Forthwith: Immediately, right away, without any delay. (e.g., "And to the leader Robert was dragged forthwith - by the reluctant ear.")
  • Peninsular War: A war fought between 1807 and 1814 in Spain and Portugal, where British forces fought against Napoleon's French army. (e.g., "...the sword was of the pattern used in the Peninsular War.")
  • Cuirass: A piece of armour covering the chest and back, made of a front plate and a back plate buckled together. (e.g., "The cuirass was of the time of Charles I...")
  • Heraldry: The system of designing and identifying coats of arms — the special symbols and colours used by noble families. (e.g., "...knew no more of heraldry or archaeology...")
  • Archaeology: The study of human history and prehistory through the excavation of sites and the analysis of artifacts and other physical remains. (e.g., "...knew no more of heraldry or archaeology...")
  • Cromwellian: Relating to Oliver Cromwell, who led the Parliamentary side in the English Civil War (1642–1651). (e.g., "...men in Cromwellian steel-caps had said a few low eager words.")
  • Scathe: Harm, injury, or damage. (e.g., "`Have no fear; thou shalt take no scathe,' he said.")
  • Men-at-arms: Professional soldiers, especially medieval knights' armed followers. (e.g., "...among these rough men-at-arms?")
  • Grammercy: An old exclamation meaning “grant me mercy” or “thank you very much.” (e.g., "`Grammercy,' began Robert, 'for thy gracious words.'")

Detailed Notes

The Day's Punishment and Robert's Task

  • The children are kept indoors as a punishment for the previous day's disastrous wish involving wings.
  • Martha, the caregiver, believes she's doing her duty, seeing their actions as "naughtiness" rather than misfortune, though she dislikes punishing them due to the noise and her own sympathy.
  • She plans a treat (cake for tea) and later play with Baby to ease the punishment.
  • Robert is the only one allowed out, for a mere half-hour, specifically to make the day's wish.

Encountering the Psammead

  • Robert easily finds the Sand-fairy, which is unusually out in the open due to the hot weather, stretching and cleaning itself in a sand-pool.
  • The Psammead greets Robert, expressing relief that the children weren't "smashed up" by the wings, but immediately inquires about the others.
  • Robert explains their punishment and the limited time he has to make a wish.

Robert's Difficult Wish

  • Under pressure, Robert struggles to think of a suitable wish for everyone, especially since they are stuck indoors.
  • His mind fills with selfish, trivial wishes (toffee, stamp album, clasp-knife, football, leg-guards, beating up a school bully).
  • Desperate, he wishes for the Psammead to grant a wish to one of the others without them having to come to it.
  • The Psammead grants this "tremendously hard" remote wish with great effort, collapsing like a pricked bubble. It warns Robert to hurry home to prevent a "silly" wish.

The Journey Home and the Shocking Transformation

  • Robert races home, filled with anxiety about what the others might have wished for in his absence. He considers possibilities like pets, chocolate, or even a wasted wish for him to "hurry up."
  • He contemplates wishes that would be amusing indoors but finds few options.
  • As he approaches home, he discovers his house and garden are gone, replaced by a massive, stately medieval castle with battlements, lancet windows, and eight towers.
  • The former garden and orchard are now a moat and a camp filled with tents and men in armour, indicating the castle is under siege. Robert exclaims, "Oh, crikey! They've wished for a castle, and it's being besieged!"

First Contact with the Besieging Army

  • Robert sees a pale, dust-colored handkerchief waving from a castle window, identifying it as Cyril's.
  • Waving back proves unwise, as two men in "steel-caps" (Cromwellian-era helmets) approach him.
  • Robert decides not to run, fearing it would be futile and irritate his "foe."
  • The men speak in archaic English, calling him a "brave varlet," which Robert, familiar with historical romances, interprets as a compliment despite the potential insult.
  • They question his "garb" and "intent," suspecting him of treachery or bearing tidings to the besieged. Robert simply states he wants to go home.

Meeting the Glorious Leader, Wulfric de Talbot

  • Robert is dragged by the ear to the leader of the besieging force.
  • The leader, Wulfric de Talbot, is a magnificent figure, resembling characters from historical romances.
  • His armour and weapons are a comical jumble of different historical periods (13th-century shield, Peninsular War sword, Charles I cuirass, Second Crusade helmet), which Robert, lacking historical knowledge, perceives as "perfectly correct" and glorious.
  • Wulfric de Talbot reassures Robert, promising he will "take no scathe" (harm).
  • He questions Robert about his origin and purpose, using formal, archaic language ("Whence comest thou, and what is thine intent?").
  • Robert struggles to understand the vocabulary but correctly infers Wulfric is asking what he's doing there.
  • Despite the language barrier, Robert feels Wulfric, being part of a wish himself, might understand the true story of the Psammead and the wishes better than any adult he's met before. He attempts to explain, beginning with a dramatic line from a historical romance.

Review Questions

  1. How does Martha's perspective on punishment differ from the children's experience of it, and what does this reveal about adult-child relationships in the story?
  2. Explain the circumstances that lead to Robert's unusual wish for the Psammead to grant a wish remotely. What does this incident reveal about the Psammead's powers and its interactions with the children?
  3. Describe Robert's initial reaction upon discovering the castle. How does his familiarity with historical romances shape his understanding and feelings about the sudden, fantastical change?
  4. Discuss the historical inaccuracies in Wulfric de Talbot's appearance and the besieging camp. Why do these inconsistencies not bother Robert, and what point might the author be making about how children perceive fantasy?
  5. Analyze the communication difficulties Robert faces when speaking with the men-at-arms and Wulfric de Talbot. How does Robert manage to navigate these linguistic barriers, and what is the humorous effect of his attempts?
