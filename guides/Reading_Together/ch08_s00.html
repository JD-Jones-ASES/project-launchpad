Study Guide: Bigger Than the Baker's Boy

Overview

This section finds the children frustrated with their previous wishes, prompting Cyril to devise a new strategy: to play as usual, hoping a truly useful wish will emerge spontaneously. Their game of bandits is interrupted by a baker's boy, leading to an unexpected confrontation where Robert is soundly defeated. In a fit of anger and humiliation, Robert wishes to be "bigger than him," a wish the Psammead grants with immediate, enormous, and comical consequences.

Key Concepts

  • The Ineffectiveness of Deliberate Wishing: The children realize that their carefully planned wishes for things like wealth or beauty have not brought them lasting satisfaction or truly useful outcomes.
  • Cyril's "Spontaneous Wish" Theory: Cyril hypothesizes that the best wishes come unexpectedly during normal play, rather than through strained effort. This introduces a new approach to dealing with the Psammead.
  • Consequences of Impulsivity: Robert's quick temper and impulsive actions (like ambushing the baker's boy and then wishing in anger) consistently lead to trouble and often to comical or inconvenient outcomes.
  • Literal Interpretation of Wishes by the Psammead: The Psammead, as usual, grants wishes literally, transforming Robert into a giant when he expresses a desire to be "bigger" than his opponent, without considering the practical implications.
  • The Narrator's Self-Awareness: The narrator occasionally interjects with comments about the storytelling process, such as the difficulty of describing fights or the tendency of "rows to keep on happening," adding a meta-narrative layer.
  • Childhood Conflict and Fair Play: The chapter explores themes of childhood squabbles, the unfairness perceived in fights, and the differing interpretations of "chivalry" or gentlemanly conduct.

Important Terms

  • Jackape: Short for "jackanapes," meaning a cheeky, impertinent person or a mischievous troublemaker. Robert is called this for being sarcastic.
  • Humbugging: Joking around, being dishonest, or talking nonsense. Cyril assures Robert he isn't "humbugging" about his idea.
  • Water-butt: A large barrel placed outside to collect rainwater from the roof gutters. Cyril stands on one to make his important speech.
  • The Conquest: Refers to the Norman Conquest of 1066, when William the Conqueror invaded England from France. Cyril compares recalling history facts to thinking of wishes.
  • Pax: A Latin word meaning "peace." British schoolchildren used it as a truce call during fights. Cyril calls for "pax" after the skirmish with Robert.
  • Cricket ball: The hard leather ball used in cricket, England's most popular summer sport. It's roughly baseball-sized but heavier. Robert claims to have a lump "as big as a cricket ball" over his eye.
  • Brigandish: Looking like a brigand, which is a bandit or robber. The children's improvised hats with turkey feathers are described as having a "brigandish" effect.
  • Mail-cart: A sturdy baby carriage originally modeled after the carts used to deliver mail. The Lamb's mail-cart is used as a baggage-wagon in their game.
  • Banditti: The Italian plural of "bandit," meaning a gang of robbers or outlaws. This term is used to describe the children in their game.
  • Baker's boy: A young man employed by a bakery to deliver bread and goods door to door—home delivery was the norm in 1902. He becomes the unexpected antagonist.
  • Skipping-rope: A jump rope. British children call it a "skipping-rope" because the activity is called "skipping." Robert uses Jane's skipping-rope to lasso the baker's boy.
  • The Boys of England: A popular adventure magazine for boys, published weekly from 1866 to 1899. The narrator references it when discussing how hero stories usually go.
  • Chivalry: The medieval code of honour followed by knights—being brave, courteous, and protective of the weak. The baker's boy is described as lacking this quality.
  • Mansion House: The official residence of the Lord Mayor of London. There was a famous busy crossing nearby. Robert's giant size is compared to a big policeman who used to be at this crossing.
  • Norfolk suit: A style of boys' clothing with a belted jacket and matching knee-length trousers, named after the Duke of Norfolk. Robert's Norfolk suit grows with him.
  • Eton collar: A broad, stiff white collar worn outside the jacket, named after Eton College. Robert's large size combined with his small Eton collar makes him look comical.

Detailed Notes

Cyril's Frustration and New Strategy

The chapter opens with Cyril expressing profound dissatisfaction with the wishes they have made so far. He recounts their previous wishes—for wings, beauty, wealth, castles, and the "rotten gipsy business"—all of which he deems ultimately useless, leaving them "no forrader" (no further ahead). Robert interjects that at least "things happened," but Cyril insists they weren't the "right things."

Cyril then presents his new theory: truly useful wishes don't come from deliberate effort. Instead, they "crop up" spontaneously when they are "rotting about in the usual way" and "real earnest wishes come into the heads of the beholder." He compares trying to force a wish to forgetting a history fact when asked directly, even though you know it. He suggests that if they just play naturally, a good wish is bound to arise.

This discussion is interrupted by a minor scuffle between Robert and Cyril, ending with a damp Robert and a truce ("pax") being called by Anthea.

The Bandit Game

Following Cyril's theory, the children agree to play an old game, settling on "bandits." Despite their agreement, the game struggles to gain spirit because the underlying pressure of needing a wish still lingers. They try to embrace their roles:

  • They arm themselves with bows and arrows, umbrellas, and cricket stumps.
  • White cotton hats are decorated with turkey feathers for a "brigandish" look.
  • The Lamb's mail-cart is transformed into a "baggage-wagon," with the Lamb conveniently asleep inside. They set off towards the sand-pit, consciously staying near where they found the Psammead, just "in case."

Confrontation with the Baker's Boy

The game is dragging when a real-world interruption occurs: a "baker's boy" arrives with loaves in a basket. Seeing an opportunity for some "bandit" action, Cyril and Robert confront him with classic bandit lines: "Stand and deliver!" and "Your money or your life!"

However, the baker's boy, described as "unusually large," does not share their playful spirit. He dismisses them roughly, saying, "Chuck it now, d'ye hear!" and pushes them aside. Robert, undeterred, tries to lasso him with Jane's skipping-rope, but it trips the boy instead of going around his shoulders. The basket overturns, scattering loaves onto the dusty road.

A fight erupts between Robert and the baker's boy. The narrator describes the chaos, acknowledging this is the second fight in the chapter. The skipping-rope becomes an accidental weapon, hitting shins and ankles. The narrator humorously admits inability to describe the fight properly, confessing a lack of talent for such descriptions, unlike the writers of "The Boys of England" magazines. He also truthfully reports Robert's defeat, admitting that Robert was "badly beaten" for the second time that day, with a black eye, pulled hair, and a kick to the knee, highlighting the baker's boy's lack of "fair play and gentlemanly behaviour."

Anthea's Intervention and Robert's Humiliation

As Cyril prepares to join the fight, Jane clings to his legs, crying and begging him not to get beaten "too," much to Robert's chagrin. Anthea then rushes in, tearfully imploring the baker's boy to stop hurting her brother, offering a "despicable apology" and stating it was "only play."

The baker's boy, however, is "a stranger to the breast of chivalry." He roughly pushes Anthea away and continues to chase Robert with kicks and insults, ultimately landing him in a heap of sand with a final kick. He then leaves, calling them "a pack of silly idiots."

The Aftermath and Robert's Wish

The children gather around the sobbing Robert in the sand-pit. Their emotions are high: Robert is raging, Cyril is angry with Jane, Robert is furious with Anthea (for her intervention and apology), and the girls are miserable.

In his fury and humiliation, Robert digs his hands and toes into the sand, lamenting his defeat simply because the baker's boy was "bigger than me." He shows off his new "purple bruise" and declares, "I only wish I was bigger than him, that's all." As he speaks, his hand touches something furry in the sand – the Psammead.

The Psammead Grants a Literal Wish

Predictably, the Psammead grants Robert's wish immediately and literally. Robert instantly grows "much, much bigger" than the baker's boy. He becomes "ten or eleven feet high," taller than the biggest policeman the narrator has ever seen. His clothes (a Norfolk suit and Eton collar) fortunately grow with him. He stands up, still showing his now-gigantic bruise.

The sight of Robert, now a flushed, enormous giant still bearing traces of his recent fight, strikes the others as comical. Cyril remarks, "The Sammyadd's done us again," but Robert corrects him, "Not us - ME," highlighting the personal nature of this particular wish's consequence. He complains about how "silly it feels," but Cyril quickly retorts, "I can jolly well see how silly it looks," setting the stage for the next predicament.

Review Questions

  1. Why is Cyril frustrated with the wishes they've made so far, and what new strategy does he propose for making effective wishes?
  2. Describe the children's "bandit" game and explain why it was difficult for them to play it with full spirit.
  3. Detail the confrontation between Robert and the baker's boy. What actions does each boy take, and what is the outcome for Robert?
  4. How does Anthea attempt to resolve the conflict, and how does the baker's boy react to her intervention? What does this reveal about his character?
  5. What exactly does Robert wish for at the end of the chapter, and how does the Psammead grant this wish? What are the immediate and comical consequences of this wish?
