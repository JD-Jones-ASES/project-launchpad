Chapter 11: The Last Wish — A Study Guide

Overview

This chapter marks a critical turning point as the children face their final day with the Psammead, though they don't realize the gravity of it. Their initial wishes for personal gain are quickly abandoned when their mother announces her unexpected return. A fateful, impulsive wish by Jane, prompted by news of a local jewel burglary, causes Lady Chittenden's stolen diamonds to appear in their mother's room. The children grapple with the terrifying consequences, debating whether to confess the truth about the Psammead, ultimately deciding to conceal it from their skeptical parents, leading to an intense confrontation as their mother discovers the jewels and contemplates calling the police.

Key Concepts

  • The Power and Peril of Wishing: The Psammead's wishes are powerful and immediate, but they often come with unforeseen, complicated, or even dangerous consequences. Jane's wish for jewels, while well-intentioned, instantly turns her mother into an unwitting "receiver of stolen goods," highlighting the responsibility that comes with such magic.
  • Unintended Consequences and Moral Dilemmas: The children constantly face situations where their wishes lead to problems far worse than their original desires. This chapter presents their greatest moral dilemma yet: whether to confess the unbelievable truth about the Psammead or conceal it, potentially implicating their mother in a crime.
  • Childhood vs. Adult Perspectives: The children firmly believe in the Psammead, but they are acutely aware that adults would find their story unbelievable, even "mad." Cyril's vivid description of "Bedlam" underscores the divide between their magical reality and the logical, often punitive, world of grown-ups.
  • The End of Magic and Return to Reality: The impending return of Mother and Father signals the end of the children's magical adventures. The need to "please mother" replaces selfish desires, and the escalating crisis with the stolen jewels forces them to confront real-world legal and ethical problems without the easy fix of another wish.
  • Family Loyalty and Secrecy: Despite their fear and the escalating danger, the children stick together. They decide as a group to keep the Psammead a secret, driven by fear of punishment and disbelief, showing a strong bond but also a tendency to protect each other even when it might be counterproductive.

Important Terms

  • Florins: British silver coins, each worth two shillings, used in the early 20th century. The children want "modern" ones to ensure they are accepted in shops.
  • Litter and stabling: Litter refers to the straw or bedding used in a horse's stall. Stabling refers to the act of housing horses in a stable or barn.
  • Topaz: A semi-precious gemstone, typically yellow or blue, owned by the children's mother.
  • Sapphire: A precious gemstone, usually blue, found in a ring given to Mother by Father.
  • Garnet: A semi-precious gemstone, usually deep red, mentioned as part of Mother's limited jewelry collection.
  • Brooch: A decorative pin or clasp, often set with jewels, worn on clothing. Mother has a small one containing great-grandpapa's hair.
  • Tiara: A jewelled ornamental crown or headband worn by women on very formal occasions. Lady Chittenden had one, which Jane refers to as a "tarrer."
  • Receiver of stolen goods: A legal term for a person who knowingly accepts or possesses property that has been stolen. Cyril correctly identifies this as a serious crime, fearing their mother might unwittingly become one.
  • Bedlam: The popular nickname for Bethlem Royal Hospital, a famous historical psychiatric asylum in London, known for its chaotic and inhumane conditions. Cyril uses it to scare the others about being declared insane.
  • Maniacs: A derogatory term for people who are wildly mentally ill, often used to describe patients in asylums. Cyril exaggerates this to frighten his siblings.
  • Asters: Daisy-like flowers that typically bloom in late summer and autumn, often in shades of purple, pink, or white.
  • Zinnias: Brightly coloured garden flowers that bloom from summer into fall.
  • Bower: A pleasant, shady retreat or shelter, often made of leafy branches or climbing plants. The children transform their house into a "flowery-boweryness" with flowers.
  • Bonnet: A woman's hat, typically tied under the chin with ribbons. By 1902, the term could be used more loosely for any lady's hat.
  • Ring-stand: A small stand, often found on a dressing table, designed to hold rings.
  • Pincushion: A small, stuffed cushion used for holding pins and needles, a common item on a lady's dressing table.

Detailed Notes

The Last Chance for a Wish

  • Awareness of the End: The children, as readers, know this is the eleventh and final chapter, implying a sense of closure to their adventures with the Psammead. However, the characters themselves are unaware that this is truly their last chance for a wish, instead seeing it as simply the last day before their parents return.
  • Initial Wish Ideas: With their parents' return imminent, the children's brains are "full of the most beautiful and sensible ideas" for wishes, including practical ones like "one hundred pounds in modern florins" or "a pony each." The pony idea even comes with an ingenious plan for economy (wishing it back daily to save on stabling).

Mother's Return and a Shift in Priorities

  • Unexpected News: A letter from Mother arrives, announcing that she and Father will be home that very afternoon. This joyous news instantly "scattered all the before-breakfast wish-ideas."
  • Wishing for Mother's Happiness: The children unanimously decide that the day's wish must be "something to please mother and not to please themselves."
  • Warning from Anthea: Anthea wisely reminds everyone of yesterday's incident (not detailed in this excerpt, but implies a problem with casual wishing) and the rule that wishes come true "wherever we happen to be when we say 'I wish'," urging caution.

Martha's Ominous News and Lady Chittenden's Loss

  • Martha's Gossip: Martha, the housemaid, enters with a dramatic story about a burglary at Peasmarsh Place.
  • Lady Chittenden's Jewels: Lady Chittenden, whom the children know as an unpleasant, childless woman, has had all her "thousands of thousands of pounds" worth of diamonds and jewels stolen – including a necklace, a "river" (river of diamonds), bracelets, a "tarrer" (tiara), and rings.
  • Contrast with Mother: Anthea highlights the stark contrast between Lady Chittenden's excessive wealth and their own mother's modest jewelry collection (topaz necklace, sapphire ring, garnet star, pearl brooch). This contrast sets the stage for Jane's unfortunate wish.

Jane's Fateful Wish

  • Robert's Future Ambition: Robert dreams of becoming a wealthy explorer in Africa to buy Mother "no end of diamonds."
  • Jane's Innocent Wish: Inspired by Martha's tale and Robert's ambition, Jane dreamily exclaims, "Wouldn't it be jolly... if mother could find all those lovely things, necklaces and rivers of diamonds and tarrers... in her room when she came home? I wish she would."
  • Immediate Realization of Disaster: The others "gazed at her in horror." They instantly understand the terrible implication: Jane has wished for Lady Chittenden's stolen jewels to appear in their Mother's room. Robert voices the fear of "the police" and Mother becoming a "receiver of stolen goods."

The Desperate Search and the Debate on Truth

  • Failure to Undo the Wish: The children frantically search for the Psammead to try and take back the wish, but it cannot be found. They also search Mother's room but find no jewels.
  • Robert's Naive Hope: Robert, ever optimistic, suggests Mother might assume the jewels "have been in the house for years."
  • Cyril's Bleak Realism: Cyril, however, is scornful, pointing out the severe legal consequences: "then mother will be a receiver of stolen goods, and you know jolly well what THAT'S worse than."
  • Anthea's Plea for Honesty: Anthea initially believes that telling Mother "the truth" will make "everything all right."
  • Cyril's Argument Against Truth: Cyril vehemently disagrees, predicting adult disbelief: "Could anyone believe about a Sammyadd unless they'd seen it? She'll think we're pretending. Or else she'll think we're raving mad, and then we shall be sent to Bedlam." He frightens Jane with vivid descriptions of being "shut up in an iron cage with bars and padded walls."
  • Decision to Keep the Secret: Anthea concedes to Cyril's logic, acknowledging that the truth about the Psammead is "not true enough for grown-up people to believe it." They resolve to keep silent.

Preparing for Mother's Arrival

  • A Diversion of Flowers: To try and make the house pleasant and perhaps distract from the impending disaster, the children fill all available pots and vases with flowers (asters, zinnias, roses), making the house a "perfect bower."

Mother's Return and the Discovery of the Jewels

  • Joyful Reunion, Underlying Tension: Mother arrives, delighted with the flowery house. The children struggle to avoid telling her about the Psammead, a habit they usually maintain.
  • Children's Desperate Attempts to Stall: As Mother heads upstairs to her bedroom, the children cling to her, trying to prevent her from going up and discovering the jewels.
  • Mother's Dismissal: Mother, unaware of their true motivation, briskly dismisses their antics and proceeds to her room.
  • The Unveiling:
    • She first discovers a beautiful pearl and diamond ring on her dressing table, believing Father must have left it.
    • Upon opening a drawer, she finds an "almost priceless diamond necklace."
    • In the wardrobe, she discovers a tiara and several brooches.
    • More jewelry continues to appear in various parts of the room.
  • Mother's Changing Reaction: Her initial delight turns to confusion, then growing concern and unease as the sheer quantity and value of the jewels become apparent.

The Partial Confession and the Looming Crisis

  • Mother's Interrogation: Noticing Jane's distress, Mother gravely asks her to tell the truth.
  • Jane's Blurted Truth: Jane, obediently but disastrously, replies, "We found a Fairy."
  • Cyril's Quick Thinking: Cyril quickly interrupts, shutting Jane down. He then desperately links the jewels to Lady Chittenden's burglary, suggesting the thieves might have hidden them there for later retrieval, not knowing Mother would return. This provides a "plausible" (to an adult) explanation.
  • Mother's Distraction and Decision: Mother, though still perplexed about how the jewels got there, is now convinced they are the stolen property. Distraught, she declares, "I must send for the police at once," wishing Father were present.
  • Robert's Attempt to Stall: Robert tries to buy time, knowing Father won't be home before sunset, by suggesting they wait for him. However, Mother is too agitated to wait.
  • The Cliffhanger: The chapter ends with Mother determined to call the police, leaving the children in a state of terror and facing severe consequences.

Review Questions

  1. Why was the day described in this chapter particularly significant for the children regarding their relationship with the Psammead, even though they didn't fully realize it? How did the news of their parents' return alter their initial plans for the day's wish?
  2. Explain how Martha's dramatic story about Lady Chittenden's misfortune directly led to Jane's critical wish. What specific concerns did the other children immediately voice after Jane made her wish?
  3. Describe the internal conflict and debate among the children about whether to tell their mother the truth about the Psammead. What specific arguments and fears did Cyril use to convince his siblings that revealing the truth was not an option?
  4. Trace Mother's emotional journey as she discovers the various pieces of jewelry in her bedroom. How do her initial feelings upon finding the first item evolve as she uncovers more, and what causes this shift in her reaction?
  5. At the chapter's conclusion, the children are in a desperate situation. What immediate action does Mother decide to take, and what potential legal and personal implications might this have for the family, especially for the children, given their secret?
