Study Guide: A Siege and Bed

Overview

This section finds the children trapped in a gloomy banqueting hall, facing an invisible and unfeelable dinner, highlighting their dire hunger during the magical siege. They soon discover a peculiar rule of their magic: food becomes real if they physically possess it or put it in their mouths. This discovery allows them to magically eat a hearty dinner. Afterwards, they prepare their makeshift castle defenses, observing the besieging army and debating the reality and danger of their situation, culminating in the formal demand for surrender and the start of the "attack."

Key Concepts

  • The Magic of Possession: The chapter introduces a new magical rule related to the Psammead's wishes. Food, though initially invisible or unfeelable, becomes tangible and edible if the children physically possess it (e.g., in their pockets) or bring it into their mouths. This demonstrates how their wishes (to have food) manifest through direct physical interaction.
  • Imagination vs. Reality: The narrative constantly blurs the line between the children's vivid make-believe game and the growing sense of reality and danger. While they initially treat the siege as a game, the sight of the enemy, the trumpet's call, and the formal demand for surrender begin to erode this perception, particularly for Jane and, to a lesser extent, Robert.
  • Childhood Roles and Reactions: Each child displays distinct personality traits in the face of the siege. Cyril is the clever, analytical one who figures out the "food magic" and quickly adapts. Robert is the brave leader, eager to prove himself and direct defenses. Anthea is practical and comforting, trying to reassure Jane while contributing to the preparations. Jane is the most fearful, constantly questioning the reality and safety of the game.
  • Building Suspense: The chapter effectively uses details to heighten tension, moving from the initial magical solution to hunger to the clear visual and auditory signals of the approaching attack (e.g., battering-ram, cleaning weapons, trumpet blasts, the summons). The children's varied reactions further emphasize the growing sense of dread.

Important Terms

  • Hank: A coil or loop of something, like yarn, string, or rope. (e.g., "hank of tarred twine")
  • Tarred twine: String coated with tar to make it waterproof, often used for outdoor and marine purposes.
  • Cobbler's wax: A sticky wax used by shoemakers to coat their thread, making stitches hold tightly in leather.
  • Suet roly-poly: A traditional English dessert made by spreading jam on pastry made with suet (hard beef fat), rolling it up, and steaming it.
  • Reconnoitre: To explore or survey an area, especially to gather information about an enemy's position. It's a military term.
  • Battering-ram: A heavy beam of wood, often with a metal head, used to smash down castle doors and gates during a siege.
  • Intrepid: Very brave and fearless, especially in the face of danger.
  • Bodiam Castle: A real medieval castle in East Sussex, England, built in 1385, famous for its wide moat. Anthea references it.
  • Crossbows: Weapons that shoot short arrows (bolts) using a horizontal bow mounted on a wooden stock.
  • Longbows: Very large bows, about six feet tall, used by English archers. They required great strength to draw.
  • Javelins: Light spears designed to be thrown at an enemy rather than used for thrusting.
  • Apace: Quickly, at a fast pace. (e.g., "a heap of stones grew apace")
  • Pennoned: Decorated with pennons—small triangular flags attached to the tips of knights' lances.
  • No quarter: Mercy shown to a defeated enemy, especially by sparing their lives. To give "no quarter" means no one will be spared.
  • Beleaguered: Surrounded by enemy forces; under siege. (e.g., "the beleaguered fortress")

Detailed Notes

The Invisible Dinner and Cyril's Discovery

  • The children are in the gloomy banqueting-hall, hungry, with the dinner rendered invisible and unfeelable by the Psammead's magic.
  • Cyril finds forgotten biscuits in his pocket, which are real, though crumbled and tasting odd from their contact with tarred twine, fir-cones, and cobbler's wax.
  • He divides them fairly, prompting Robert to question why these biscuits are real but the main dinner isn't.

The "Ownership" Rule of Magic

  • Cyril theorizes that the biscuits are real because "WE had them," suggesting a personal possession makes them tangible.
  • Jane adds that food in one's pockets is real, while Robert suggests food in one's mouth would also be real.
  • Cyril experiments by "biting" the air above the table, and a real piece of bread appears in his mouth. He discovers that even if the rest vanishes, he knows he has it.
  • All the children follow suit, magically eating slices of mutton and, later, plain suet roly-poly directly from the air above the table.
  • Martha is aghast at the mess left on the plates, having no idea of the magical feast.

Preparing for the Siege

  • After dinner, renewed with courage, Robert insists on reconnoitring from a castle tower.
  • From the tower, they see the besieging camp around the moat: tents, men sharpening weapons, re-stringing bows, and polishing shields.
  • They spot a battering-ram being dragged towards the castle, causing Cyril to feel pale with fear.
  • The children discuss the castle's defenses (moat, drawbridge) and the absence of soldiers, leading Cyril to create a dramatic scenario of "intrepid survivors" (themselves).
  • Anthea recalls historical defense tactics, like pouring boiling lead, mentioning specific features of Bodiam Castle.
  • Jane expresses her fear, questioning if it's "only a game."

Arming and Provisioning the Garrison

  • They find large, impractical medieval weapons (swords, lances, crossbows, longbows) that are too heavy for them.
  • They settle on daggers (to be thrown or dropped) and stones as viable weapons.
  • Heaps of stones and daggers are gathered in the gate-tower room.
  • Anthea has a clever idea: asking Martha for biscuits for tea and specifically requesting they be put in their pockets. This bypasses the invisibility charm, as the "air" turns into real biscuits once in their possession.
  • They also prepare pots of cold water to pour on attackers, substituting for the unavailable hot lead.
  • The afternoon passes quickly, with Robert being the only one who consistently feels the danger is real.

The Attack Begins

  • As tea-time approaches, they eat biscuits (with water from the well) and save some reserves.
  • A sudden, loud trumpet blast signals the attack, confirming Robert's fears.
  • They rush to the windows, seeing the besiegers moving. Robert bravely (and cheekily) puts his tongue out at an enemy figure.
  • Jane is still very scared, while Cyril's pallor turns to redness as he tries to be brave.
  • Anthea tries to reassure Jane that the besiegers are "civilized" and that the scene is picturesque, like a "splendid coloured picture," with colorful flags and armor.
  • A trumpeter sounds a loud blast, followed by a man demanding the castle's surrender "in the name of our Lord the King, and of our good lord and trusty leader Sir Wulfric de Talbot," threatening "fire and sword and no quarter."
  • Robert defiantly shouts "No!" to the surrender demand.
  • He urges the others to cheer and rattle daggers to show courage, despite their high, weak cheers.
  • Another shout from the camp confirms the attack has begun.
  • The room grows dark, and Jane notes the "dreadfully thin" moat, with sunset approaching.

Review Questions

  1. How does the concept of "ownership" or "possession" influence the magic in this chapter? Provide specific examples of how the children use this understanding to their advantage.
  2. Discuss the different reactions of Cyril, Robert, Anthea, and Jane to the escalating "siege." How do their individual personalities shape their engagement with the game and their perception of danger?
  3. What role does imagination play in the children's experience of the siege? At what points does the line between "make-believe" and "real" become most blurred, and what causes these shifts?
  4. Anthea attempts to reassure Jane that the besiegers are "civilized" and that the scene is picturesque. How does this description conflict with the actual demands and preparations of the besiegers, and what does it reveal about the children's perspective?
  5. How does the chapter build suspense and tension towards the end, despite the children's initial belief that it's "only a game"? Identify specific sensory details and events that contribute to this increasing tension.
