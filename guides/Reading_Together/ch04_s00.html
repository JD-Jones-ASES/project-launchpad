Chapter 4: Wings - Study Guide

Overview

This section details the children's failed attempts to communicate the existence of the Psammead to their mother and an enjoyable outing with their generous Uncle Richard. The core of the chapter focuses on Anthea's secret, early morning visit to the Psammead, where she seeks advice on making wishes, specifically inquiring about the wisdom of wishing for wings, after their past wishes have led to "horrid" outcomes.

Key Concepts

  • The Difficulty of Communication: Despite their intention, various mishaps prevent the children from successfully telling their mother about the Psammead, highlighting the challenges of sharing extraordinary secrets and the unpredictable nature of childhood.
  • The Nature of Wishes and Unintended Consequences: The children's previous wishes have consistently gone awry. This chapter introduces the idea of a more thoughtful approach, with Anthea seeking advice to avoid further "horrid" outcomes, particularly concerning a wish for wings.
  • The Psammead's Cryptic Wisdom: The ancient Sand-fairy, though initially grumpy and resistant to giving direct advice, offers subtle warnings and non-advice that underscore the potential dangers and complexities of magic and wishes. Its story of the Ninevite boy hints at the peril of unchecked ambition.
  • Childhood Independence and Resourcefulness: Anthea's determination to wake early, her meticulous preparation, and her decision to confront the Psammead alone demonstrate her growing sense of agency and a desire to take control of their magical experiences.
  • Generosity and Childhood Choices: Uncle Richard's unrestricted gift-giving allows the children freedom of choice, revealing their varied desires—from practical toys to unexpected "instructive" puzzles like Robert's Nineveh box, illustrating the unpredictable path of children's interests.

Important Terms

  • Indian ink: A deep black ink originally from China, used for drawing and writing.
  • Wagonette: A four-wheeled, horse-drawn carriage with bench seats facing each other, typically open at the back.
  • Nineveh: The ancient capital of the Assyrian Empire, located in modern-day Iraq, renowned as one of the greatest cities of the ancient world.
  • Medway: A river in Kent, England, flowing through towns like Maidstone and Rochester. Boating on it was a popular leisure activity.
  • Latin proses: Exercises where students were required to write passages or compositions in Latin, a standard part of education for English children during this historical period.
  • Sennacherib: A historical king of ancient Assyria (reigned approximately 705-681 BC), known for his military campaigns and building projects, including the expansion of Nineveh.

Detailed Notes

The Rainy Day and the Failed Letters

  • Weather: The day is extremely wet, making it impossible to go outside and causing discomfort for the water-sensitive Sand-fairy.
  • Children's Plan: The children decide to write letters to their mother.
  • Anthea's Letter:
    • Robert accidentally knocks a full ink-pot into Anthea's "secret drawer" (a gum and cardboard arrangement).
    • This happens when Anthea has the drawer open, and the Lamb simultaneously breaks his squeaking bird, jabbing Robert with a wire.
    • Her letter is ruined by ink, with only a partial message and a penciled apology/explanation about the ink spill.
  • Robert's Letter: He hadn't started writing, busy drawing a ship. After the ink incident, he helps Anthea clean up and promises her a new secret drawer, but his letter remains unwritten.
  • Cyril's Letter: He writes quickly but then goes to set slug traps. His letter is lost and never found.
  • Jane's Letter:
    • The only letter sent.
    • She struggles immensely with spelling "Psammead," consuming most of her writing time.
    • Unable to tell the full story due to time constraints, she sends a brief note and a postscript asking her mother what she would wish for.
  • Conclusion: Despite their intentions, the children collectively fail to inform their mother about the Psammead due to various mishaps and distractions.

Uncle Richard's Visit to Maidstone

  • The Outing: The next day, Uncle Richard takes all the children (except the Lamb) to Maidstone in a wagonette.
  • Uncle's Generosity: He is described as the "very best kind of uncle," allowing the children to choose any toys they want without price restrictions or insistence on "instructive" items.
  • Children's Choices:
    • Robert: Hastily chooses a box depicting winged bulls and men, expecting animals, but it turns out to be a "Sunday puzzle about ancient Nineveh" – an unintentionally instructive choice.
    • Cyril: Selects a model engine.
    • Girls: Choose two dolls and a china tea-set to share.
    • Boys' Shared Gift: A bow and arrows.
  • Afternoon Activities: They go boating on the beautiful Medway river and have tea at a pastrycook's.
  • No Wishes: By the time they return home, it is too late in the day to make any wishes.
  • Secrecy: The children do not tell Uncle Richard about the Psammead, the reason for which remains unexplained even to themselves.

Anthea's Secret Early Morning Plan

  • The Hot Day: The day after Uncle Richard's visit is exceptionally hot.
  • Anthea's Determination: Anthea makes herself wake up at five o'clock in the morning.
  • Waking Method: The narrator explains Anthea's technique for self-waking: lying flat, repeating the desired wake-up time, and performing a chin-to-chest, head-to-pillow motion as many times as there are 'ones' in the target hour. Success depends on truly wanting to wake up.
  • Morning Routine:
    • She interprets the black-and-gold dining-room clock's incorrect eleven strikes as three minutes to five.
    • She uses cold water on her face and hands as a "fairy charm" to prevent going back to bed.
    • She meticulously folds her nightgown by the seams, indicating her well-brought-up nature.
    • She quietly creeps downstairs, choosing to exit through the dining-room window for romanticism and to avoid Martha.
  • Encountering the Psammead:
    • Anthea runs directly to the sand-pit and digs out the Psammead.
    • The Psammead is extremely cross, complaining about the "arctic" weather and the early hour.
    • Anthea shows kindness by covering it with her pinafore and offering to let it sit on her lap for warmth, which it accepts, remarking on her thoughtfulness.
  • Seeking Advice:
    • Anthea explains that all their previous wishes have turned out "rather horrid."
    • She wants the Psammead's advice, believing its age makes it wise, to avoid wasting the "splendid, glorious chance" of having wishes.
    • The Psammead initially refuses to give advice, stating it only gives gifts.
    • It offers a piece of "non-advice": "think before you speak," but dismisses it as unoriginal.
    • Anthea specifically asks if "wings" would be a silly wish.
    • The Psammead's response: "You might do worse. Only, take care you aren't flying high at sunset." It mentions a "little Ninevite boy" from King Sennacherib's time who flew too high, hinting at danger.

Review Questions

  1. Why did none of the children successfully tell their mother about the Psammead, despite their intentions? What does this series of events suggest about the challenges of keeping or sharing secrets in childhood?
  2. How does Uncle Richard's philosophy of gift-giving ("without any restrictions about price, and no nonsense about things being instructive") contrast with typical adult approaches? What is ironic about Robert's choice, and what does it reveal about unintended outcomes?
  3. Describe Anthea's elaborate method for waking herself up at five o'clock. What character traits (e.g., determination, discipline, romanticism) are highlighted by her early morning actions and her decision to speak to the Psammead alone?
  4. What is the Psammead's stance on giving advice, and how does it try to avoid doing so? What warning does it give Anthea when she asks about wishing for wings, and what might the story of the "Ninevite boy" foreshadow?
  5. Compare Anthea's interaction with the Psammead in this chapter to previous encounters. Why do you think she felt it was important to talk to it privately, and what does she hope to gain from this more personal conversation?
