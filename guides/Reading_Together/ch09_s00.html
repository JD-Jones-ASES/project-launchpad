Chapter 9: Grown Up - Study Guide

Overview

This chapter details the consequences of the children's increasingly casual approach to wishing. Cyril, seeking convenience, secures a promise from the Psammead that wishes can be made anywhere, anytime. However, this new freedom quickly leads to disaster when Cyril, frustrated by the baby Lamb's destructive antics, rashly wishes for him to "grow up now," transforming the beloved toddler into an independent, adult young man.

Key Concepts

  • The Psammead's New Wishing Rule: Cyril negotiates with the Psammead to allow wishes to be made spontaneously, wherever the children are, rather than needing to visit the creature in the sand-pit. While seemingly convenient, this removes a layer of deliberation and caution from wishing.
  • The Danger of Rash Wishes: The chapter powerfully illustrates the unpredictable and often negative consequences of wishing without careful thought. Cyril's impulsive wish for the Lamb to "grow up" stems from a moment of irritation and immediately spirals into a crisis, highlighting that wishes often grant the literal interpretation, not the desired intent.
  • The Loss of Innocence/Childhood: The transformation of the Lamb signifies more than just physical growth; it represents a jarring loss of his babyhood and the innocence associated with it. The children are confronted with a stranger who shares the Lamb's body but not his mind or personality, a profound and unsettling change.
  • Responsibility and Consequences: The children are forced to grapple with the immediate and overwhelming responsibility for their wish. They must figure out how to manage a grown-up individual who was, moments before, their baby brother, and how to explain his sudden appearance to their parents. This underscores the moral weight of their magical abilities.
  • The Irony of Adult Freedom: The grown-up Lamb, now an independent young man, desires solitude, a trip to London, and a visit to his club – all adult pursuits. This contrasts sharply with the children's desperate need to get him home and care for him, showcasing the incompatibility of adult independence with the children's caregiving role.

Important Terms

  • Tin baths: Large metal tubs used for bathing before modern indoor plumbing became common. Water would be heated and carried to the tub.
  • Megatherium: An extinct genus of giant ground sloths from thousands of years ago, which the Psammead fondly remembers.
  • Wash-hand basin: A bowl or sink, often kept in a bedroom, used for washing hands and face before modern bathrooms were widespread.
  • Waterbury watch: A type of cheap, mass-produced pocket watch made by the Waterbury Clock Company, common and affordable during the era.
  • Flannels: A suit or trousers made of flannel, a soft woven fabric, often worn by young gentlemen for casual outings during the Edwardian era.
  • Straw hat: A hat woven from straw, commonly worn by men in summer during the Edwardian era.
  • Languid: Describing a slow, relaxed, and often lazy manner, used here to characterize the grown-up Lamb's voice and demeanor.
  • Club (Gentlemen's Club): A private social establishment in London where members (typically wealthy men) could eat, drink, read, and socialize.
  • Baptismal names: The formal names given to a person at their christening or baptism, often multiple names.
  • Stand Sam: An old British expression meaning to pay for everyone's food or drinks.
  • Blow-out: British slang for a big, satisfying, and often indulgent meal.
  • Pall: A dark, heavy cloth spread over a coffin or tomb; used metaphorically here to describe a feeling of gloom or despair falling over the children.

Detailed Notes

Cyril's Early Morning Visit and New Wishing Rules

  • A New Approach to Wishes: Cyril, remembering past difficulties, decides to approach the Psammead with a strategic request. He wants the convenience of wishing anywhere, anytime, without needing to make a special trip to the sand-pit.
  • The Psammead's Reluctance and Agreement: The Psammead, initially cold and annoyed, warns Cyril that this new freedom will likely lead to more thoughtless wishes, citing the castle incident. Despite its grumbling, it eventually agrees, tired of the children and their requests, offering a curt "Have it your own way. Good-bye." This sets the stage for rapid, less considered wishes.

The Lamb's Destructive Tendencies

  • The Children in the Woods: The five children are in the woods, supposedly looking for nuts. Cyril is already upset because the Lamb has broken his own watch (using it as a spade).
  • Another Broken Watch: While playing, the Lamb accidentally breaks the glass of their father's "second-best Waterbury watch," which Cyril had borrowed without permission. This second act of destruction pushes Cyril's patience to its limit.
  • Cyril's Frustration: Overwhelmed by the Lamb's babyish behavior and the consequences (two broken watches), Cyril expresses extreme frustration with how long babies stay babies.

The Rash Wish and Its Terrible Consequence

  • Cyril's Impulsive Wish: In a moment of intense exasperation, Cyril bitterly exclaims, "I wish to goodness he would [grow up]—" and then immediately adds, "Grow up now!"
  • Anthea's Vain Warning: Anthea, understanding the power of their wishes, cries out "Oh, take care!" but it's too late; her words and Cyril's wish come out simultaneously.
  • The Lamb's Transformation: True to its promise, the Psammead grants the wish. The Lamb undergoes a horrifying, rapid transformation. His face thins, lines appear, eyes deepen, and a small dark moustache sprouts. The most unsettling aspect is that his body remains that of a two-year-old in a smock, but his face is that of a man.
  • The Children's Horror: The other children are terrified. They desperately wish for it to stop, but their wishes are "quite vain."
  • Full Transformation: The change continues, and the Lamb fully transforms into a "very proper-looking young man" in flannels and a straw hat, still sporting the small moustache. He then gracefully settles against a tree trunk, clearly weary, and falls asleep.

The Dilemma of the Grown-Up Lamb

  • A "Council" in the Bracken: The children hold a hurried discussion about their predicament. The central question is: Has the Lamb's mind grown up along with his body, or is he still a baby mentally?
  • Anthea's Fears: Anthea voices the terrifying possibilities: if his mind is grown up, he won't let them look after him; if he's still a baby inside, managing him in an adult body will be impossible.
  • Practical Concerns: Jane worries about dinner, and Robert suggests tying the Lamb to a tree. Cyril, accepting blame, acknowledges the dire situation.

Interaction with the "New" Lamb

  • Waking the Lamb: Jane, chosen by lot, gently wakes the grown-up Lamb by tickling his nose.
  • Adult Demeanor: The Lamb speaks in a "languid tone," calling the children "kiddies," and suggests they go home. He expresses a desire to go to London for lunch at his club.
  • Rejecting His Past: He sternly tells Anthea not to call him "Lamb," insisting on his baptismal names like "Hilary," "St Maur," or "Devereux," calling "Lamb" a "relic of foolish and far-off childhood." This confirms his mind has indeed grown up.
  • Adult Independence: He dismisses Anthea's plea about their father not wanting him to go to London. He rejects Cyril's offer of a "blow-out" in Maidstone, preferring "solitude."
  • Patronizing Attitude: He tells Jane, "little girls should be seen and not heard" and offers them a penny if they are "good." He completely disregards Cyril's attempts at a "man to man" conversation.
  • The Children's Despair: The children realize they have lost their baby brother to this independent, dismissive young man. Their responsibility for him is now coupled with his refusal to be cared for. The thought of returning home without him, or with him in this state, fills them with "blank misery."

Review Questions

  1. What was Cyril's initial motivation for asking the Psammead for a new wishing arrangement, and how did this change in rules contribute to the subsequent events?
  2. Describe the sequence of events that led to Cyril's wish for the Lamb to "grow up." What specific frustrations pushed him to make such an impulsive wish?
  3. How did the Lamb's transformation unfold, and what were the most unsettling aspects of it for his siblings? Why was the children's subsequent wishing to stop the transformation "vain"?
  4. Once the Lamb was fully grown up, what were the children's main concerns and dilemmas? How did his adult behavior and personality make their situation more complicated than if he had remained a baby mentally?
  5. Analyze the grown-up Lamb's interactions with his siblings. What do his words and actions (e.g., rejecting his old name, desiring solitude, offering a penny) reveal about his new identity and his view of his family?
