Study Guide: The Comparison

The Solon-Publicola synkrisis is one of Plutarch's most sustained pieces of comparative judgment, and one of his more interesting verdicts. Plutarch ranks Publicola above Solon on almost every measure — a decisiveness the chapter itself flags as unusual. The reasoning is worth following with care, axis by axis, because the synkrisis is also a small treatise on what political virtue consists of: invention, prudence, durability, and the capacity to know when to yield.

Key Overview

The chapter walks through eight axes of comparison. Axis 1: happiness and the end of life. Plutarch uses Solon's own saying about Croesus — that no one's life can be called happy until it has ended well — and turns it back on Solon. Publicola died in office, mourned by all Rome; Solon died in exile, having watched his cousin Pisistratus overturn his constitution. Round to Publicola.

Axis 2: wealth honestly used. Both men kept their wealth honestly, but Plutarch adds that Publicola spent his nobly in doing good to the distressed. Round to Publicola.

Axis 3: the refusal of tyranny. Solon refused absolute power outright. Publicola did even more, in Plutarch's reading: he accepted limited power (the consulship in its first, less-constrained form) and converted it into a "popular office." Round to Publicola.

Axis 4: the cancellation of debts. Here Solon decisively wins. The seisachtheia was an original act of civic legislation without parallel in the early Mediterranean. Publicola did not face the same problem and did not enact a comparable reform. Round, decisively, to Solon.

Axis 5: originality and dependence. Solon "was entirely original, and followed no man's example." Publicola consciously imitated Solon — the elective offices, the right of appeal, the institution of treasurers. Round to Solon.

Axis 6: the endurance of the work. But the verdict flips here. Solon left Athens after his laws were complete; within a generation his cousin Pisistratus had seized the citadel. Publicola stayed in Rome and saw his constitution survive every challenge in his lifetime — and Plutarch can say, with three centuries of hindsight, that it "maintained the state in good order down to the civil wars." Round to Publicola.

Axis 7: military and political effectiveness. Solon's exploits were small (Salamis was an ingenious stratagem more than a great victory). Publicola won several major battles and "utterly subverted and dissolved a potent monarchy." Round to Publicola.

Axis 8: the use of yielding. Publicola made peace with the Etruscan king Lars Porsenna by ceding some territory. Plutarch defends the move: "judgment is to be made of actions according to the times" — "by foregoing a part he saves the whole." Round narrowly to Publicola.

Major Figures and Terms

synkrisis — Plutarch's comparative essay appended to each pair of Lives; here, one of the longest and most decisive.

Croesus — Lydian king to whom Solon first delivered the maxim Plutarch turns back on Solon himself.

Lars Porsenna — Etruscan king who invaded Rome to restore Tarquin; the peace involved Publicola's cession of territory.

Pisistratus — Solon's cousin, tyrant of Athens from 561 BC; embodiment of the constitution "running under a tyrant's permission."

lex libertatis — Publicola's collective constitutional package.

Important Passages

The opening of Plutarch's synkrisis: "There is something singular in the present parallel, which has not occurred in any other of the lives; that the one should be the imitator of the other, and the other his best evidence." It tells us why this synkrisis is unique.

The cancellation of debts: "A mere law to give all men equal rights is but useless if the poor must sacrifice those rights to their debts." Plutarch sees property as political reality.

The yielding maxim: "judgment is to be made of actions according to the times" — Plutarch's defense of prudent compromise.

The chapter's summary: "Solon was the greater inventor; Publicola the greater executor. Solon designed the form; Publicola made it work in practice."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. List the eight axes of comparison in Plutarch's synkrisis and identify the winner of each round.
  2. How does Plutarch use Solon's own saying about Croesus as a measure of the two men? What is the reflexive structure?
  3. Why does Solon win the seisachtheia round so decisively, and what does Plutarch mean about property being a political reality?
  4. Why does Plutarch defend Publicola's peace with Lars Porsenna rather than read it as a humiliation?
  5. State the chapter's overall paraphrase of Plutarch's verdict in one sentence.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch's verdict that Publicola outranks Solon is the most one-sided judgment in the Lives. Is it convincing, or does it underweight Solon's role as the inventor of the form?
  2. The synkrisis distinguishes between the originator and the executor. Is invention or execution the greater political achievement? Cite specific incidents from each Life.
  3. The optional essay asks whether mercy (seisachtheia) and severity (execution of the Aquillians) are different kinds of political virtue or incompatible. What is the strongest case for each side?

Connections to Later Chapters

The synkrisis's themes recur explicitly: the republican refusal of one-man rule tested by Themistocles, Pericles, and finally Caesar; Marcus Brutus (Chapter 11) reenacting his ancestor's deed in killing Caesar; the Solonian property classes as the foundation of Pericles's popular courts in Chapter 6.
