Study Guide: The Comparison

Plutarch's synkrisis of Pericles and Fabius is one of his most balanced. Neither leader was perfect; both were exceptional; the question — as the chapter opening promised — is what kind of excellence is more politically valuable. The synkrisis works through eight axes of comparison, weighing each in turn, and then refuses to declare a single winner. What it offers in place of a verdict is something more useful: a vocabulary for thinking about kinds of political greatness, and a habit of asking, of any political moment, what kind of leader the moment requires.

Key Overview

Axis 1: The difficulty of the inherited situation. Pericles took over a city "in its most flourishing and opulent condition, great and growing in power." Fabius took over a city facing catastrophic invasion. The two situations are not comparable in difficulty. Round one to Fabius.

Axis 2: The number of victories. Pericles "erected nine trophies for as many victories obtained by land and by sea." Fabius won only one set battle (against the Ligurians). On this narrow military measure, Pericles is the more decorated commander.

Axis 3: The rescue of Minucius. Plutarch singles out this episode as one of the great Roman moments — "a noble act, combining the highest valor, wisdom, and humanity." He goes further: "no action of Pericles can be compared" to it. Round three to Fabius.

Axis 4: Foresight. Pericles "admonished the Athenians, and told them beforehand the ruin the war would bring upon them, by their grasping more than they were able to manage." Fabius "was not so good a prophet, when he denounced to the Romans that the undertaking of Scipio would be the destruction of the commonwealth." Plutarch's epigram: "Pericles was a good prophet of bad success, and Fabius was a bad prophet of success that was good." Round four to Pericles.

Axis 5: Mildness toward colleagues. Fabius's gentleness with Minucius (he saved his rival's army) against Pericles's ostracism of Cimon and Thucydides son of Melesias. Round five to Fabius.

Axis 6: Influence and reach. Pericles's authority in Athens was greater than Fabius's in Rome. Round six to Pericles — but with a complication: it would have been better for Rome if Fabius's authority had been greater, since Rome suffered Cannae partly because his hand was not strong enough.

Axis 7: Liberality and incorruptibility. Pericles took no gifts and was famously incorruptible. Fabius gave his own money to ransom Roman soldiers from Hannibal. A draw.

Axis 8: The building program. Pericles's program is described by Plutarch as unequaled even by anything in Rome "to the time of the Caesars." This is an astonishing acknowledgment from a writer working under the Roman imperial peace. Axis eight clearly to Pericles.

The verdict that refuses to resolve. Pericles wins more rounds and the more dramatic ones (foresight, building, range of victories). Fabius wins the most morally weighty (rescue of Minucius, mildness toward rivals, harder political situation). What Plutarch is showing is that these two men represent two kinds of political greatness that cannot be ranked on a single scale.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's closing comparative essay for each pair of Lives.
  • The Eight Axes — difficulty of inheritance, number of victories, rescue of Minucius, foresight, mildness toward colleagues, influence and reach, liberality and incorruptibility, the building program.
  • "Good prophet of bad success / bad prophet of success that was good" — Plutarch's epigram for Axis 4.
  • The leader of opportunity (Pericles) — the leader of a city in flower.
  • The leader of emergency (Fabius) — the leader of a city in ruin.
  • "To the time of the Caesars" — Plutarch's chronological frame for the building comparison; the acknowledgment is significant given his Roman imperial context.

Important Passages

On the rescue: Plutarch says of Fabius's rescue of Minucius: "no action of Pericles can be compared" — calling it "a noble act, combining the highest valor, wisdom, and humanity."

On foresight (the epigram): "Pericles was a good prophet of bad success, and Fabius was a bad prophet of success that was good." Pericles foresaw the ruin Athenian overreach would bring; Fabius failed to see that Scipio's African strategy would in fact end the war.

On the building program: Plutarch describes Pericles's Athenian construction as "unequaled even by anything in Rome to the time of the Caesars" — a striking concession from a Greek writing under Roman imperial peace.

On the deeper claim: "Pericles is the leader of a city in flower: his contribution was to push the city as far as it would go... Fabius is the leader of a city in ruin: his contribution was to hold the city together when everything else was breaking... A polity needs leaders of both kinds at different times."

On Athens's tragedy: "It had Pericles when it needed him; the political tragedy might have been that, when it later needed a Fabius (during the Peloponnesian War, after Pericles's death), it had no one of sufficient stature for the role."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. List the eight axes Plutarch uses in this synkrisis and name the winner of each (where Plutarch declares one).
  2. State Plutarch's epigram on foresight in full.
  3. Why does Plutarch say "no action of Pericles can be compared" to the rescue of Minucius?
  4. Explain the complication Plutarch adds to Axis 6 about Pericles's greater authority.
  5. Why is the Axis 8 acknowledgment of Pericles's building program "astonishing"?
  6. What does Plutarch mean by "the leader of opportunity" and "the leader of emergency"?
  7. What political tragedy of Athens does Plutarch identify in light of the synkrisis?
  8. What was Rome's good fortune in 217 BC?
  9. State, in your own words, the habit of judgment Plutarch recommends to the reader.
  10. Why does Plutarch refuse to declare a single winner in this synkrisis?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch's epigram on foresight is witty, but is it just? Is being wrong about a single great opportunity (Scipio's Africa) really comparable in weight to being right about a long slow ruin (the cost of Athenian overreach)? Argue both sides.
  2. On Axis 5, Plutarch finds Fabius decisively better than Pericles in his treatment of rivals. But Pericles operated within a constitutional ostracism system; Fabius's rescue was an individual choice. Are these really comparable acts?
  3. The synkrisis ends with a habit of judgment, not a verdict. Why is that the right pedagogical conclusion for a chapter built on temperaments? What does it teach the student to do that a clean ranking would not?

Connections to Later Chapters

The synkrisis sets the model for every later one in the book: a structured weighing of axes, a willingness to award rounds to either side, and a refusal to collapse the comparison into a single number. The leader-of-opportunity / leader-of-emergency typology recurs in the Alcibiades and Coriolanus chapter (Chapter 7), where neither man fits cleanly into either category and the city pays for the mismatch. The writing prompt at the close of the chapter — write your own synkrisis, 600–1,000 words, pick an axis, cite two incidents from each Life, render judgment — sets up the kind of work the rest of the book asks of the reader.
