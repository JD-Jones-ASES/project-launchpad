Study Guide: Where to Go Next

You have read ten of Plutarch's twenty-three pairings. The book has tried to give you the most famous pairs and the ones that best teach the synkrisis method, but Plutarch wrote thirteen more pairs plus four single Lives. This closing section maps the territory ahead: which Lives to read first, what to read in the Moralia, which historical sources deepen each Life, and how to keep practicing synkrisis after you close the book.

Key Overview

The most accessible continuation is to pick up the Modern Library two-volume Dryden-Clough translation and work through the remaining pairs in order. Four pairings are flagged for priority. Aristides and Cato the Elder — the men of austere republican virtue, a moral counterweight to Alcibiades and Coriolanus. Lysander and Sulla — the destroyers, whose synkrisis compares them with the Spartan reformers of Ch. 10. Phocion and Cato the Younger — the principled losers, whose synkrisis is one of the lost comparisons in the corpus. Demetrius and Antony — the brilliant ruined men, and the source for Shakespeare's Antony and Cleopatra.

The Lives are only half of Plutarch's surviving work. The Moralia is the other half — fifteen volumes in the Loeb, with the Penguin Essays edited by Robin Waterfield as a good starting selection. Four highlights: "On the Education of Children" (a founding text of the humanist tradition), "How to Tell a Flatterer from a Friend" (one of the deepest ancient analyses of self-knowledge), "On the Malice of Herodotus," and "On the E at Delphi" (a dialogue set at Plutarch's own sanctuary).

You can also deepen any Life by going to its sources: Caesar's Commentaries plus Suetonius and Appian for the Caesar; Arrian for Alexander; Thucydides for Pericles and Alcibiades; Herodotus for Themistocles; Plato's Seventh Letter and Cassius Dio for Dion and Brutus.

The most important inheritance is not the contents of the Lives but the discipline of writing synkrisis. You have written ten of them. The exercise is portable: name the axis of comparison, adduce two or three specific incidents from each life, render judgment, keep it short. Two further-practice pairings are proposed: Lincoln and Washington (the two consciously Plutarchan American presidents), and two figures from your own family or community.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Dryden-Clough translation: the Modern Library two-volume edition used by the book.
  • Aristides and Cato the Elder: the austere republican virtuous.
  • Lysander and Sulla: the destroyers; explicit synkrisis with the Spartan reformers.

  • Demetrius and Antony: the brilliant ruined men; source of Shakespeare's Antony and Cleopatra.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How many of Plutarch's twenty-three pairings have you read, and how many remain?
  2. Name the four pairings recommended for priority continuation, and give the moral category each represents.
  3. Which of those four pairs has a lost synkrisis, and which is the source for a Shakespeare play?
  4. List two highlights of the Moralia along with what each contributes.
  5. State the four-step structure of the portable synkrisis exercise.

Discussion Questions

  1. Of the four flagged pairings, which would you read first and why? Consider both the moral category and how it connects to material you have already studied.
  2. The Phocion-Cato the Younger synkrisis is lost. If you had to reconstruct it from the two Lives alone, what axis of comparison would you choose?
  3. The chapter argues that the synkrisis discipline does not require famous figures — "a teacher and a parent, two mentors, two friends who took different paths." Try drafting the four steps for two non-famous figures you actually know.
  4. Why might Lincoln and Washington be "consciously Plutarchan" in a way later American presidents are not? Pick one specific decision from each life and try the synkrisis exercise.

Where this leads

This section is the deliberate close of the book — the handoff from reading Plutarch to using him. The Roman plays of section 12.1, the political vocabulary of section 12.2, and the Christian biographical tradition of section 12.3 are all evidence of what Plutarch can do once a culture takes him seriously. The point of section 12.4 is that you, the individual reader, can do the same on your own scale. The lives are the work; the synkrisis is the skill; both together are the inheritance.
