Study Guide: The Life of Caesar

Plutarch's Life of Caesar is the closest portrait we have of how the Roman Republic actually ended. Caesar is harder to summarize than Alexander: brilliant, brave, magnanimous, indomitable, charming, ruthlessly funny — and the man who killed a million Gauls, crossed the Rubicon, and whose end was prepared by the very mercy he had practiced.

Key Overview

  • Origin. Born 100 BC into a patrician family of modest political standing. The Julii claimed descent from the Trojan Aeneas and through him from Venus, but in the late Republic they were neither wealthy nor powerful.
  • The pirate story. Cilician pirates demanded twenty talents; Caesar laughed and told them to ask for fifty. He lived among them as a guest, read them his poems, and told them cheerfully he would crucify them all. They thought he was joking. After release he raised a fleet, captured them, and crucified them — though he had their throats cut first so the crucifixion would be painless. At twenty-five he was already the man who would say a thing with a smile and then do it.
  • The political climb. Heavy spending on games, massive debt, and then the First Triumvirate of 60 BC with Pompey and Crassus — a private arrangement whose combined clout could carry any vote. As consul in 59 BC he gave Pompey's veterans land, confirmed Crassus's tax contracts, and secured himself a five-year proconsular command in Gaul.
  • The Gallic War (58-50 BC). Four legions, eight years, the Helvetii, Germans, and Belgae; two Rhine crossings, two British invasions; perhaps a million dead and a million enslaved by Plutarch's figures; a vast new province Roman for five hundred years. Caesar paid off his debts, built a private fortune, and acquired veterans personally loyal to him.
  • The Rubicon. The Senate demanded he disband his army and face prosecution. The Rubicon was a small stream marking the boundary between his province and Italy proper; crossing it in arms was civil war. Plutarch preserves the hesitation — "he wavered much in his mind, when he considered the greatness of the enterprise" — then: "The die is cast."
  • The civil war and dictator perpetuo. Caesar defeated Pompey at Pharsalus (48 BC); Pompey fled to Egypt and was murdered there; Caesar established Cleopatra on the Egyptian throne and defeated Pompey's supporters in Africa and Spain. By 45 BC he was sole master of Rome. In early 44 BC he was declared dictator perpetuo — essentially king without the title. His reforms: the Julian calendar, court reorganization, veterans' land grants, reduction of the grain dole, the extension of Roman citizenship to provincials.
  • Clementia and the conspiracy. Caesar pardoned many civil-war enemies and readmitted them to the Senate (clementia Caesaris). Several pardoned senators, including Marcus Brutus — descendant of the Brutus who had expelled the Tarquins — organized the conspiracy.
  • The Ides of March. March 15, 44 BC, in the Theater of Pompey. Tillius Cimber grabbed his toga; the others drew daggers. He was struck twenty-three times. When he saw Brutus's sword he covered his face with his robe and fell at the foot of Pompey's statue. His last words, according to Plutarch, were Greek: "kai su, teknon?" — "You too, my son?" Shakespeare's "Et tu, Brute?" is the Latin of the Greek. The Republic did not revive; the civil wars that followed (44-31 BC) ended with Octavian as Augustus.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Pompey the Great — Defeated at Pharsalus, murdered in Egypt.
  • Crassus — The richest man in Rome; Caesar's other Triumvir.
  • Cleopatra — Established on the Egyptian throne by Caesar; later allied with Antony and dead at Actium 31 BC.
  • Marcus Brutus — Chief plotter, pardoned by Caesar, made consul; descendant of the Tarquin-expeller.
  • Octavian / Augustus — Caesar's great-nephew; founder of the imperial system.
  • First Triumvirate (60 BC) — Caesar, Pompey, Crassus.
  • Rubicon — Small stream between Gaul and Italy proper.
  • Pharsalus (48 BC) — Decisive defeat of Pompey.
  • Dictator perpetuo — "Dictator for life"; effective monarchy without the regal name.
  • Clementia Caesaris — Caesar's signature magnanimity.

Important Passages

  • He was already the man who would say a thing with a smile and then do it. — Verdict on the pirate story.
  • His thoughts began to work... he wavered much in his mind, when he considered the greatness of the enterprise. — The Rubicon hesitation.
  • The die is cast. — At the Rubicon.
  • He covered his face with his robe, and without any further resistance, fell... at the foot of the statue of Pompey, which was thus stained with the blood of his enemy. — The fall.
  • kai su, teknon? — You too, my son? — The Greek last words.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Describe Caesar's family background.
  2. Tell the pirate story and what it shows about Caesar at twenty-five.
  3. Name the three Triumvirs and what the alliance accomplished for Caesar.
  4. Give the dates, scale, and human cost of the Gallic War.
  5. What was the Rubicon, and what did Caesar say crossing it?
  6. How did the civil war end? Name the decisive battle, Pompey's fate, Cleopatra's role.
  7. What is dictator perpetuo, and why was it intolerable?
  8. Name three of Caesar's reforms.
  9. What is clementia Caesaris, and how did it shape the conspiracy?
  10. Describe the assassination, Caesar's last words, and Shakespeare's version.

Discussion Questions

  1. The section says Caesar's end was "prepared by the very mercy he had practiced." Is that tragic, ironic, or a vindication of Brutus?
  2. Is the Rubicon hesitation more morally important than the crossing?
  3. The Gallic dead and the clementia are both Caesar. How do you hold them in one judgment?
  4. Is Plutarch's restraint on the moral question a virtue or a failure?
  5. Was Brutus's role a vindication of the Republican tradition or a misreading?
