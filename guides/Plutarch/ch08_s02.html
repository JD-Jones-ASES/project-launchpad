Study Guide: The Life of Alexander

Plutarch's Life of Alexander is the Lives' most enigmatic portrait. Alexander conquered the largest empire of the ancient world in twelve years, broke the political institutions of the Greek and Persian worlds in one campaign, and died at thirty-two with no heir designated. Plutarch opens the Life with his most famous methodological manifesto: he is writing biography, not history; the small detail matters more than the great battle.

Key Overview

  • The inheritance. Born 356 BC, son of Philip II and Olympias. Philip had defeated Athens and Thebes at Chaeronea (338 BC), forced the Greek city-states into a federated league, and was planning the league's Persian expedition when he was assassinated in 336 BC. The campaign fell to Alexander at twenty.
  • The opening manifesto. Plutarch announces that his design is not to write histories but lives; the most glorious exploits do not always furnish the clearest discoveries of virtue or vice; a matter of less moment — an expression or a jest — may inform us better of character. The portrait-painter analogy is the figure: attention to the lines of the face where character is seen. The discipline applies to both Alexander and Caesar.
  • Bucephalas. Alexander, perhaps twelve, watched the unmountable horse, realized it was bolting at its own shadow, turned the horse toward the sun, and mounted easily. Philip wept and told his son to look out a kingdom worthy of himself, "for Macedonia is too little for thee."
  • Aristotle. Tutored Alexander perhaps 343-340 BC; gave him the full philosophical and literary curriculum of fourth-century Greece — ethics, politics, biology, literature, medicine. Alexander slept with the "casket edition" of Homer's Iliad under his pillow throughout the Asian campaign.
  • The Persian campaign. Four battles over four years. Granicus (334 BC, cavalry charge nearly cost his life). Issus (333 BC; Darius fled; conspicuous chivalry to Darius's mother and wife — when she bowed before Hephaestion, Alexander said, "It is no matter, Mother, for he too is Alexander"). Tyre (332 BC, seven-month siege ending in massacre or enslavement). Gaugamela (331 BC, decisive for the throne). Then seven more years east — trans-Oxiana, Bactria, the Indus valley, the Hyphasis, where the army refused. The Gedrosian desert retreat killed perhaps half his men.
  • Small acts. Granicus: the cloak for wounded Lysimachus. The Egyptian desert: the helmet of water for a dying soldier. Corinth: Diogenes' "Stand out of my sun" and Alexander's "If I were not Alexander, I would be Diogenes." The moral nadir: the killing of Clitus, the old friend who had saved Alexander's life at Granicus, in a drunken rage in Samarkand in an argument about whether Alexander was now too Persian; three days of mourning followed.
  • Persianization and death. Alexander adopted Persian dress, took Persian wives (Roxane most famous), demanded proskynesis; veterans resented every step. Hephaestion's death in 324 BC produced extravagant grief. In June 323 BC Alexander developed a fever in Babylon after a long drinking party and died eleven days later, age thirty-two. Asked to whom he would leave the empire: "To the strongest." Within twenty years it was divided among his generals.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Philip II — Alexander's father; victor of Chaeronea; assassinated 336 BC.
  • Aristotle — Alexander's tutor.
  • Bucephalas — The horse Alexander tamed at twelve.
  • Darius III — Persian king Alexander defeated at Issus and Gaugamela; later assassinated by his own satraps.
  • Hephaestion — Alexander's closest friend; his death produced extravagant grief.
  • Clitus — Old friend who saved Alexander's life at Granicus; killed by Alexander in Samarkand.
  • Roxane — Sogdian princess; Alexander's most famous Persian wife.
  • Proskynesis — Persian prostration before the king; Greeks considered it the mark of slavery.

Important Passages

  • It must be borne in mind that my design is not to write histories, but lives. — The manifesto.
  • Macedonia is too little for thee. — Philip after Bucephalas.
  • It is no matter, Mother, for he too is Alexander. — At Issus when Darius's mother bowed to Hephaestion.
  • Stand out of my sun. — Diogenes to Alexander.
  • To the strongest. — Alexander's dying line.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What political inheritance did Alexander receive in 336 BC?
  2. State Plutarch's opening methodological manifesto.
  3. What did Alexander see in Bucephalas that no one else did?
  4. Who tutored Alexander and what curriculum did he provide?
  5. Name the four Persian-campaign battles in order, and identify the decisive one.
  6. What did Alexander say when Darius's mother bowed to Hephaestion?
  7. What is the killing of Clitus, and where does the moral arc place it?
  8. What is proskynesis, and why did its demand provoke resistance?
  9. How and where did Alexander die, and what did he say about the succession?
  10. What became of the empire after his death?

Discussion Questions

  1. The Life's moral arc is not improvement — clement at the start, increasingly imperious afterward. Defend or contest this with two incidents.
  2. Is "Aristotle shaped him; the Asian campaign deformed him" a true description, or an overstatement?
  3. The Tyre massacre and the chivalry to Darius's family are both Alexander. How do you hold them together in one judgment?
  4. Why might a biographer who prizes small detail leave the verdict on Alexander harder to summarize than usual?
  5. Pick one small act and argue that it is the central key to the Life.
