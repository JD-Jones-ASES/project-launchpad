Study Guide: The Lost Comparison

This is the second of the two lost synkriseis of this book. Plutarch wrote a comparison of Alexander and Caesar; we do not have it. By common consent, this is the most painful single loss in the manuscript tradition of the Parallel Lives — these are the two most famous men in Western history, and we do not know what Plutarch's verdict on them was. As in Chapter 5, the synkrisis the student writes at the end is fully the student's own.

Key Overview

The historical fact. The pair appears together in the manuscripts; the closing comparison did not survive transmission. The loss is irreparable. Chapter 5 was the first lost-synkrisis pair in this book; this is the second.

What Plutarch likely would have weighed. Reconstructed from the surviving synkriseis, whose shape is highly consistent.

  • Origin. Alexander inherited a kingdom and a planned expedition; Caesar started as a modest patrician. Plutarch credits the lower start — verdict leans Caesar.
  • Military genius. Alexander's greater scope; Caesar's more strategic difficulty. Probably a draw.
  • Treatment of defeated. Alexander's chivalry to Darius's family and his Tyre and Thebes massacres; Caesar's clementia and his million Gauls. Political edge to Caesar; moral weight to neither.
  • Restraint. Alexander's imperiousness, the proskynesis demand, the killing of Clitus, the grief at Hephaestion's death; Caesar's moderate drinking, disciplined prose, political composure. Tilts Caesar.
  • Educational formation. Aristotle versus Greek tutors plus lifelong reading. Favors Alexander.
  • Political form broken. Alexander ended Greek polis freedom for Hellenistic monarchy; Caesar ended the Republic for dictatorship. The Hellenistic Greek world was more cosmopolitan than the polis world; the Roman collapse was less clearly improving. Slight edge to Alexander.
  • The end. Alexander dead of fever at thirty-two; Caesar assassinated at fifty-six, mid-program. Different but both unfinished. Probably even.

What Plutarch could not have weighed. Two aspects of how these men are now read fall outside Plutarch's frame.

  • The long political afterlife. Augustus's system lasted four centuries; Alexander's empire fell apart within twenty years. The afterlife of the conquests is part of how we judge them.
  • The Christian reception. Dante placed Brutus and Cassius in the lowest circle of Hell; the Christian moral imagination used Caesar as the type case of overweening political ambition.

The writesynkrisis as exercise. The book's most demanding essay. Plutarch's verdict is lost, so the student has no model; the men are perhaps the most famous in Western history, so the student must resist deference; the pair has the richest reception history in the book. The essay must (a) state its axis in the first sentence, (b) cite two specific named incidents from each Life, (c) render judgment — the lost synkrisis freed Plutarch of the obligation but not the student — and (d) stay under 1,000 words. Possible axes: the conqueror's restraint; the political form broken; the end; the educational formation. The student may propose another.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's closing comparative essay; preserved for nineteen pairings, lost for four.
  • The four lost synkriseis — Themistocles-Camillus, Pyrrhus-Marius, Alexander-Caesar, Phocion-Cato the Younger.
  • The likely Plutarchan axes — origin, military genius, treatment of defeated, restraint, educational formation, political form broken, the end.
  • What Plutarch could not have weighed — the long political afterlife and the Christian reception.
  • Clementia Caesaris — Caesar's signature magnanimity; central evidence for the treatment axis.
  • Proskynesis — Persian prostration; Alexander's demand of it is central evidence for the restraint axis.
  • Writesynkrisis — the chapter-end comparative-essay exercise; here, the book's most demanding instance.

Important Passages

  • The synkrisis of Alexander and Caesar is lost. The pair appears together in the manuscripts; the closing comparison did not survive transmission. The loss is irreparable. — The frame.
  • The most painful single loss in the manuscript tradition of the Parallel Lives. — The verdict on the loss.
  • We can reconstruct, in outline, the axes Plutarch probably used. — The methodological move.
  • The afterlife of the conquests is part of how we judge them. — The reminder that the modern reader has axes Plutarch did not.
  • The lost synkrisis freed Plutarch of the obligation to verdict; it does not free you. — The closing injunction.

Discussion Questions

  1. The mix of axes favors each man on some. Make the case for an overall verdict.
  2. Is it fair to weigh Augustus's institutional success in Caesar's favor, when Alexander's empire fragmenting was equally an aftermath he did not live to see?
  3. Pick one of the four axes and write the first sentence of your synkrisis. Name two incidents from each Life you would cite.
  4. Could the right answer be that the two cannot be ranked?
  5. Should reception evidence (Dante, Augustine) weigh in your synkrisis?
