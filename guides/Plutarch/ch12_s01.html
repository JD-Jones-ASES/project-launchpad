Study Guide: Plutarch in Shakespeare

This section makes one of the great connections in the history of European literature visible. If you have read the Lives in this book, you have already read Shakespeare. Three of the great Roman plays — Julius Caesar, Antony and Cleopatra, and Coriolanus — are drawn almost line-for-line from Plutarch, with Shakespeare supplying the poetry and Plutarch supplying the events, the scenes, and the moral architecture.

Key Overview

The chain of transmission begins with Sir Thomas North's 1579 English translation. North did not work from the Greek; he translated from Jacques Amyot's French version of 1559. The result was a vigorous, slightly old-fashioned English Plutarch that became one of the most-read books in Elizabethan England. Shakespeare almost certainly owned a copy and used it constantly. Long stretches of North's prose reappear as Shakespearean blank verse.

Julius Caesar (1599) follows Plutarch most closely. The framework — soothsayer's warning, Brutus-Cassius quarrel, Senate scene, funeral oration, Philippi, Brutus's self-killing — is entirely Plutarch's. Shakespeare's most famous additions are theatrical (Calpurnia's dream, the storm's omens, Caesar's ghost), but the moral core stays Plutarchan.

Antony and Cleopatra (1606) is in many ways an even closer translation. Enobarbus's "The barge she sat in" speech transposes Plutarch's set-piece description of Cleopatra into iambic pentameter with hardly an alteration of fact. What Shakespeare adds is tragic interiority: Plutarch's Antony is a man undone by passion; Shakespeare's is a man dragging a world down with him.

Coriolanus (1608) is the third Roman play. The scene of Volumnia kneeling at the gates of Rome (read in section 7.3) is rendered as a near-literal translation. The play asks, in Plutarchan terms, what kind of man cannot live in his own city — a question more discomfiting than the answers in Caesar or Antony.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Sir Thomas North (1535-1604): English translator whose 1579 Plutarch became an Elizabethan classic.
  • Jacques Amyot: French translator of 1559 whose version served as North's source.
  • Antony's funeral oration (Julius Caesar III.ii.74-251): the single most striking instance of borrowing; expansion of a brief Plutarchan notice.
  • Volumnia: Coriolanus's mother; her plea at the gates closely translates Plutarch's report.
  • Plutarchan habit: the structural method of comparing two figures across a single work — visible in Hamlet vs. Fortinbras, Henry V vs. the French king.

Important Passages

  • "Sir Thomas North did not work from the Greek; he translated from the French version of Jacques Amyot." — establishes the indirect chain Greek -> French -> English.
  • "Every major incident is Plutarch's." — on Julius Caesar's source dependence.
  • "Plutarch's Antony is a man brought down by his weakness for a woman; Shakespeare's is a man bringing his world down with him." — the interpretive contrast.
  • "The very habit of comparing two historical figures across an entire play... is, at its root, a Plutarchan habit." — extends the influence beyond the Roman plays.
  • "Plutarch will tell you what people did; Shakespeare will give you what people felt. Each completes the other." — the chapter's practical pedagogy.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Sir Thomas North translate from Amyot's French rather than from Plutarch's Greek directly?
  2. What three Shakespeare plays are drawn almost line-for-line from Plutarch, and to what years are they dated?
  3. Which scene in Coriolanus does the chapter describe as a near-literal translation of Plutarch?
  4. What does Shakespeare add to Plutarch's account of Antony, and what does Plutarch supply that Shakespeare cannot?
  5. How is the structural method of Hamlet a Plutarchan inheritance?

Discussion Questions

  1. If Shakespeare's poetry is original but his "events, scenes, and moral architecture" are Plutarch's, who deserves the larger share of literary credit for the Roman plays? Does the question even matter?
  2. The expansion of Antony's funeral oration is described as "one of the great moments of literary creation," yet the seed is Plutarch's. What does this example teach you about the relationship between source and art?
  3. Why might Coriolanus's proud rigidity be "harder to admire" than Brutus's principle or Antony's passion? Does this make Coriolanus a worse play, or a different one?
  4. Try imagining the experiment in the closing paragraphs: a synkrisis of Shakespeare's Brutus and Plutarch's Brutus. What would the two axes of comparison be?
