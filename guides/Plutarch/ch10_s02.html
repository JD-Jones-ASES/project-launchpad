Study Guide: The Spartan Kings — Agis and Cleomenes

The Lives of Agis IV and Cleomenes III are read as a single continuous narrative covering two reigns roughly fifteen years apart. Plutarch treats them together because their projects were the same and because Cleomenes consciously completed what Agis began. Agis tried to restore the Lycurgan order by persuasion and was murdered by an aristocratic faction before he had finished. Cleomenes did the same work by military coup, briefly restored Sparta's greatness, and died an exile in Egypt. Two reformers, two methods, one project — and a contrast in moral and political costs that occupies the heart of the section.

Key Overview

  • Inherited decline. Agis IV inherited the throne at about nineteen. The Sparta he received was Spartan in name only: the Lycurgan equality had collapsed, the citizen body was down to about 700 families, and most "Spartans" were too poor to attend the common mess.
  • Agis's three reforms. Cancellation of all debts; redistribution of land into 4,500 plots for Spartans and 15,000 for the perioikoi; restoration of the agoge for the new citizen body.
  • The opening gesture. Agis surrendered his own enormous personal fortune — six hundred talents in ready money plus his lands — into the redistribution pool. The gesture made wealthy resistance look like hypocrisy.
  • The festival of fire. Debt cancellation passed; the debt records were burned in the public square. The land redistribution stalled, opposed by Agis's co-king Leonidas and the aristocracy.
  • The death of Agis. Condemned in absentia on a trumped-up charge, strangled in prison at twenty-four, with his mother Agesistrata and grandmother Archidamia strangled beside him. Plutarch's verdict: Agis had the moral vision without the political ruthlessness the program needed.
  • Cleomenes's coup. In 227 BC, after years of campaigning had given him the army's loyalty, Cleomenes returned to Sparta with picked troops, had the ephors murdered at dinner, and presented his program to the assembly the next morning.
  • The fuller program. Debt cancellation; redistribution into 4,000 Spartan and 15,000 perioikic plots; immediate restoration of the agoge; abolition of the ephorate, replaced by a smaller council of patronomoi; Cleomenes himself as sole effective ruler.
  • Resurgence and Sellasia. The new citizen army won battle after battle against the Achaean League. By 224 BC Sparta had effective hegemony over much of the Peloponnese. The League called in Antigonus Doson of Macedon; at Sellasia in 222 BC the Spartan army was decisively defeated.
  • Exile and death. Cleomenes fled to Egypt under Ptolemy III. After Ptolemy IV imprisoned him, a desperate escape attempt failed; Cleomenes committed suicide, and his body was hung up in public and crucified. He was perhaps forty.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Agis IV: Spartan king, c. 244–241 BC. Reformer killed before his program was complete.
  • Cleomenes III: Successor, king 235–222 BC. Carried Agis's program by coup; defeated at Sellasia.
  • Leonidas: Agis's co-king. Headed the aristocratic opposition that engineered Agis's death.
  • Agesilaus: The "craft of Agesilaus" baffled Agis, in Plutarch's phrase — a political operator within the reform coalition who undermined it.
  • Agesistrata and Archidamia: Agis's mother and grandmother, strangled beside him in prison.
  • The ephors: The five annually elected magistrates who, since Lycurgus, served as a check on the kings. Cleomenes had them killed at dinner and abolished the office.
  • Patronomoi: The smaller council with which Cleomenes replaced the ephorate.
  • Perioikoi: The free non-citizen subjects of Sparta. Both Agis and Cleomenes included them in their redistribution plans, expanding the citizen body.
  • Antigonus Doson: King of Macedon who defeated Cleomenes at Sellasia in 222 BC.
  • Ptolemy III and Ptolemy IV: Successive kings of Egypt. Ptolemy III allied with Cleomenes; his weak successor Ptolemy IV imprisoned him.

Important Passages

Plutarch's account of Cleomenes's coup is the centerpiece of the section: "The blow was struck at the ephors as they were dining together. They were murdered without warning. Several other men of the opposite faction were banished. The next morning Cleomenes appeared in the assembly, announced what he had done, and presented the program." The passage compresses into a paragraph the entire contrast that Plutarch wants to draw between the two reigns. Agis had appealed to the assembly; Cleomenes presents the assembly with a fait accompli secured by force.

Plutarch's verdict on the contrast is unusually pointed: Cleomenes "proceeded to effect the revolution with only too much boldness and violence, and unjustly slew the Ephors, whom he might, by superiority in arms, have gained over to his party." The use of the knife, Plutarch writes, "is neither good surgery nor wise policy." Effectiveness has been bought at a real moral price.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What three reforms did Agis IV propose, and what made his opening gesture politically powerful?
  2. How did Agis handle the ephors, and what was the "festival of fire"?
  3. Why did the land redistribution stall under Agis, and how did he die?
  4. How did Cleomenes's method differ from Agis's, and what role did the army play?
  5. What did Cleomenes change about the ephorate, and what does that change reveal about his rule?
  6. What happened at Sellasia in 222 BC, and what became of Cleomenes afterward?
  7. How does Plutarch's overall verdict on Cleomenes balance effectiveness against moral cost?

Discussion Questions

  1. Could Agis have succeeded with more political skill — without resorting to Cleomenes's violence? Or is purely persuasive reform always doomed in a society where wealth has been long concentrated?
  2. Cleomenes restored the Lycurgan order by means Lycurgus would not have used. Is the restored order still a Lycurgan order in any meaningful sense?
  3. Plutarch is clearly more sympathetic to Agis than to Cleomenes — but his story shows Cleomenes accomplishing what Agis could not. Does Plutarch ever resolve this tension, or is it left to the reader?
