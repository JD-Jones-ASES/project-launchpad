Study Guide: The Comparison (Synkrisis)

The synkrisis of Demosthenes and Cicero is one of Plutarch's most discriminating. Both men were great orators; both fought to preserve their cities' political freedom; both lost; both died at the hands of their political opponents. The comparison turns on subtler matters of style, character, and office, on seven distinct axes. The result is a layered verdict: Demosthenes wins on the qualities of the speech and the manner of the death; Cicero wins on the qualities of the public official.

Key Overview

Plutarch's seven axes work as follows. Axis 1 (oratorical method): focus vs. breadth — round to Demosthenes. Axis 2 (tone): Demosthenes austere and serious; Cicero given to mockery that ran into scurrility — round to Demosthenes. Axis 3 (boasting): Demosthenes sparing; Cicero's "immeasurable boasting" reveals an "uncontrollable appetite for distinction" — round to Demosthenes. Axis 4 (political office and integrity): Demosthenes never held a major office; Cicero served as quaestor in Sicily and proconsul in Cilicia and Cappadocia, proved unusually honest, and as consul handled the Catiline crisis — round to Cicero. Axis 5 (money): Demosthenes was accused (and Plutarch half-believes) of bribery; Cicero refused presents from many sources — round to Cicero. Axis 6 (character of the exile): Cicero's cause was more honorable but he passed it idly; Demosthenes used his productively — round, narrowly, to Demosthenes. Axis 7 (the death): Cicero's was pitiful; Demosthenes's was composed, prepared, and defiant at the altar of Poseidon — round to Demosthenes.

Overall the verdict tilts Greek but with significant qualifications. Demosthenes is the more focused orator and more composed character; Cicero is the more capable public official and the more morally clean man in office. Plutarch most admires Demosthenes's singleness of purpose; he most criticizes Cicero's vanity and the political ineffectiveness of the Philippics against Antony.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's formal closing comparison; the chapter's seventh-axis structure is one of the most elaborate in the Lives.
  • The seven axes — oratorical method, tone, boasting, political office and integrity, money, character of exile, the death.
  • Harpalus / king of Persia — the two parties whose alleged bribes implicate Demosthenes on Axis 5.
  • Sicily / Cappadocia / Roman friends in exile — the three contexts where Cicero refused gifts.
  • Antipater — the Macedonian general at whose cruelty Demosthenes laughed before dying at the altar of Poseidon.
  • The long influence — the medieval and humanist reception of Cicero's philosophical writings that Plutarch could not see, but which weighs on Cicero's side for modern readers.

Important Passages

  • The synkrisis on focus (Axis 1): Demosthenes "applied all the faculties he had, natural or acquired, wholly that way."
  • The synkrisis on tone (Axis 2): Demosthenes's oratory was "wholly composed for real effect and seriousness"; Cicero's love of mockery "ran him into scurrility."
  • The synkrisis on boasting (Axis 3): Cicero's "immeasurable boasting of himself in his orations argues him guilty of an uncontrollable appetite for distinction."
  • The synkrisis on the death (Axis 7): "Demosthenes demands our admiration; and still more admirable was his using [the poison], freeing himself from arms and soldiers, and laughing to scorn the cruelty of Antipater."
  • The complication of the long influence: "The synkrisis barely acknowledges that Cicero's philosophical writings (which Demosthenes did not produce) became the foundation of the medieval Western tradition. Plutarch could see only Cicero the orator; the later Cicero (the schoolbook of every European schoolchild for fifteen centuries) was not yet visible to him."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. List Plutarch's seven axes and state the winner on each.
  2. What does Plutarch mean by "singleness of purpose," and why does he find it more morally weighty than philosophical breadth?
  3. On Axis 6, Cicero's exile had the more honorable cause but the round still goes to Demosthenes. Why?
  4. Why does Plutarch's verdict tilt Greek yet leave the comparison "without a clear winner"?
  5. What modern complication weighs on Cicero's side that Plutarch could not see?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch finds focus a higher virtue than breadth. Argue from incidents in the two Lives whether this is fair, or whether Cicero's combination of philosophy, oratory, and provincial administration is the harder achievement.
  2. Plutarch's verdict turns significantly on the manner of dying. Is the manner of dying really a measure of the man, or is it sometimes a matter of luck and circumstance?
  3. The synkrisis treats Cicero's wit as a political liability. Is that always true, or are there moments where wit is the most powerful weapon an orator has?
  4. The chapter ends with a writing prompt asking you to write your own synkrisis on one of four axes. Pick the axis you find most interesting and outline your argument before drafting.

Connections to Later Chapters

The pair's reception story — Cicero as the schoolbook of fifteen centuries, Demosthenes recovered by Renaissance humanists — was previewed in Topic 9.1 and will be developed in Chapter 12. Plutarch's structural concern with the tension between immediate political failure and long-term cultural success runs across all the Lives, but it is most starkly visible here. The writing exercise at the chapter's end (600–1,000 words; state the axis in the first sentence; cite two specific incidents from each Life; render judgment) is the moment to apply Plutarchan method to your own thinking.
