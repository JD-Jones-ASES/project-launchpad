Study Guide: The Life of Lycurgus

Plutarch's Life of Lycurgus opens with a candid admission: scarcely anything about Lycurgus is asserted without contradiction. Yet the constitution is not in doubt. The Lycurgan Sparta — iron money, common tables, the agoge — is historical reality. The Life is a biography of the constitution as much as of the man.

Key Overview

  1. The renunciation of the throne. Of the Eurypontid royal house, Lycurgus could have made himself king when his brother Polydectes died. Instead he saved his unborn nephew Charilaus ("the joy of the people") from the queen's scheme to abort him and presented the child publicly as Sparta's new king. He then sailed abroad — Crete, Asia Minor, Egypt — studying constitutions.

  2. The three foundational reforms. The Senate (gerousia) of twenty-eight elders plus the two kings balanced royal power against the assembly. The redistribution of land into equal plots ended hereditary wealth. The iron money — too heavy and useless to hoard, hide, or trade — starved luxury out of the city.

  3. The syssitia and Alcander. The common tables abolished the private dinner. Wealthier Spartans rioted; a young man named Alcander struck Lycurgus and blinded an eye. Lycurgus did not retaliate — he took Alcander into his house, made him wait at table, and turned him into a disciple. This, Plutarch says, is the moral center of the Life.

  4. The agoge. From age seven every male citizen entered a graded public education whose aim was character, not skill. Endurance, obedience, and laconic speech were made instinctive before the boy could question them. The famous image is the boy who let a hidden fox tear out his bowels rather than admit the theft.

  5. The lawgiver's last act. Lycurgus extracted an oath from Sparta to keep his laws until he returned, went to Delphi for ratification, sent the oracle's confirmation back — and then starved himself to death in exile so the oath would bind Sparta forever.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Lycurgus — Spartan lawgiver of the Eurypontid royal house; brother of Polydectes.
  • Charilaus — "The joy of the people"; Lycurgus's nephew, presented publicly as king.
  • Gerousia — Senate of twenty-eight elders plus the two kings, "a central weight, like ballast in a ship."
  • Iron money — Currency so heavy that twenty pounds required a closet and a yoke of oxen.
  • Syssitia — Common tables: messes of fifteen contributing monthly to a shared daily meal.
  • Alcander — The young man who blinded Lycurgus's eye, then became his disciple.
  • Agoge — Spartan public education from age seven; cement of the constitution.
  • Iren — Older boy supervising each agoge company.
  • Delphi — The oracle Lycurgus consulted to ratify his laws — and refused to return from.

Important Passages

  • "Who would rob another of such a coin? . . . a thing which it was not easy to hide, nor a credit to have, nor indeed of any use to cut in pieces?" — On the iron money.
  • "The rich, being obliged to go to the same table with the poor, could not make use of or enjoy their abundance." — On the syssitia.
  • "The whole course of their education was one continued exercise of a ready and perfect obedience." — On the agoge.
  • The boy "suffered [the fox] to tear out his very bowels with its teeth and claws, and died upon the place, rather than let it be seen." — On endurance.
  • "Like some deep and thoroughly ingrained tincture, retaining their hold upon the nation." — On the durability of the constitution.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Lycurgus refuse the throne, and what did he do instead?
  2. What three reforms did Lycurgus introduce, and what single result did they converge on?
  3. What was the gerousia, and what political function did it serve?
  4. How did the iron money succeed in banishing vices from Sparta?
  5. What were the syssitia, and why did the wealthier Spartans riot against them?
  6. Why is the Alcander story called "the moral center" of the Life?
  7. At what age did Spartan boys enter the agoge, and what was its central pedagogical aim?
  8. What is the role of the iren in the agoge?
  9. What does the story of the stolen fox illustrate?
  10. How did Lycurgus arrange his own death so as to bind Sparta to his laws permanently?

Discussion Questions

  • Plutarch insists the Spartan constitution would not have held without the Spartan lawgiver. Is that consistent with his claim that education was the real cement?
  • Lycurgus's reforms made Sparta the most warlike state in Greek history. Is the warlike result a feature of the system or an unintended consequence?
  • A lawgiver who kills himself in exile to bind his city by oath is extraordinary. Is it admirable, manipulative, or both?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Life sets up the synkrisis in section 3.4, where Plutarch argues that Lycurgus's institutions outlasted Numa's because they were rooted in the agoge — education as the real cement. The themes of self-restraint and renunciation recur in Solon (Chapter 4). The helots, mentioned only in passing here, will return as one of the sharpest charges against Lycurgus in the comparison.
