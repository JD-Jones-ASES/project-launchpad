Study Guide: The Lost Comparison

This study guide accompanies Section 5.4 of Plutarch: Lives in Parallel — the chapter that closes the pairing of Themistocles and Camillus without a Plutarchan synkrisis to read. The manuscripts confirm Plutarch wrote one, but the text was lost in the early Middle Ages, along with three other comparisons (Pyrrhus-Marius, Alexander-Caesar, and Phocion-Cato the Younger). The chapter's writing exercise therefore takes a different shape: the student writes a synkrisis without a Plutarchan model. This guide orients you to the historical fact of the loss, to what Plutarch would most plausibly have argued, and to the demands of the writesynkrisis exercise itself.

Key Overview

The historical fact. Of the twenty-three pairings of the surviving Parallel Lives, four lack their original comparison. The Themistocles-Camillus synkrisis is the first such loss the reader encounters in this book; the second is the Alexander-Caesar pairing in Chapter 8. The manuscripts confirm the comparison once existed; the body of the text is gone.

What Plutarch would plausibly have argued. The section sketches axes Plutarch is likely to have used, on the basis of the surviving synkriseis whose shape is highly consistent.

  • Origin and education. Both rose by their own merits, but Themistocles started lower. The verdict on this axis would have tilted toward Themistocles.
  • The single decisive act. Salamis and the recovery of Rome from the Gauls are essentially equivalent in stakes — both saved a city from existential threat. Plutarch would probably have noted that Salamis was the wider achievement: in its immediate aftermath it was the deliverance of all Greece, not just Athens.
  • The methods used. Themistocles bribed, deceived, and exploited religious omens; he sent a false message to Xerxes to force the battle at Salamis. Camillus sent the treacherous schoolmaster back to his own city in chains. The contrast is sharp; on methods the verdict tilts toward Camillus.
  • The treatment of defeated enemies. Camillus wept at the fall of Veii, returned the Falerian schoolchildren, punished the man who tried to deliver them. Themistocles left no comparable scenes of restraint. Round to Camillus.
  • The exile. Both were exiled by ungrateful cities; both refused to betray their cities. But Themistocles ended in service to Persia, the very power he had defeated, while Camillus mobilized a force from Ardea against the Gauls and was recalled. The asymmetry favors Camillus.
  • The afterlife of the work. The fleet became the foundation of Athenian naval empire and full democracy. Restored Rome became the city that would dominate the Italian peninsula. Plutarch would likely have called this even.

The likely overall verdict. Given Plutarch's known preferences — for clean methods, for restraint in victory, for a later life that matches early promise — the balance would probably have tilted toward Camillus. Themistocles was perhaps the more brilliant strategist; Camillus was the more admirable man. But this is a guess. Plutarch sometimes surprised; the Solon-Publicola synkrisis tilted Roman against expectations, and the Aristides-Cato synkrisis tilted Greek for similar reasons of personal virtue.

The writesynkrisis as exercise. The section closes by handing the assignment to the student. The exercise is not the imitation of a Plutarchan verdict but the supply of one Plutarch did not leave behind. The text is explicit: do not simply rehearse the likely Plutarchan verdict. The point of this assignment is to write your own synkrisis — to do the work Plutarch did not leave behind. The essay must (a) state its axis in the first sentence, (b) cite two specific named incidents from each Life, (c) render a clear judgment, and (d) stay under 1,000 words. Possible axes include the brilliance of the strategist versus the integrity of methods; the treatment of defeated enemies; the kind of foresight; and the shape of the ending. The student may also propose an axis not in the list.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's closing comparative essay; preserved for nineteen pairings, lost for four.
  • The four lost synkriseis — Themistocles-Camillus, Pyrrhus-Marius, Alexander-Caesar, Phocion-Cato the Younger.
  • The likely Plutarchan tilt — Toward Camillus, on the strength of methods, restraint, and the shape of the ending.
  • The five axes the section sketches — origin, decisive act, methods, treatment of defeated enemies, exile (plus afterlife as a sixth-equal axis).
  • The four prompt axes — strategist's brilliance vs. method's integrity; treatment of defeated enemies; kind of foresight; shape of the ending.
  • The four essay requirements — state axis in first sentence; cite two named incidents per Life; render judgment; stay under 1,000 words.

Important Passages

  • We know he wrote one — the manuscripts are clear that a comparison originally followed the pair — but the text was lost in the early Middle Ages. — The frame of the section.
  • Themistocles was perhaps the more brilliant strategist; Camillus was the more admirable man. — The text's best guess at the likely tilt.
  • Plutarch sometimes surprised. — The reminder that the guess is only a guess.
  • You should not simply rehearse the likely Plutarchan verdict. — The negative instruction at the heart of the assignment.
  • Plutarch's silence is your opportunity. — The closing line of the section.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the historical status of the Themistocles-Camillus synkrisis, and what other three are lost with it?
  2. On which axis would Plutarch's verdict likely have tilted toward Themistocles, and why?
  3. On which axes would the verdict likely have tilted toward Camillus?
  4. How does the section assess the two decisive acts (Salamis and the recovery of Rome)?
  5. What is the section's best guess at the overall verdict, and on what grounds?
  6. Why is the guess only a guess?
  7. What does the section say the student should not do?
  8. What are the four explicit requirements of the writesynkrisis essay?

Discussion Questions

  1. The section says Plutarch's known preference is for "clean methods, restraint in victory, and a later life that matches earlier promise." How would you argue that one of these three preferences is mistaken, on the evidence of Themistocles and Camillus?
  2. Is the Salamis-versus-Gallic-Sack equivalence the right one? Could a reader plausibly claim that Salamis is the greater act because of wider stakes, or that the Sack recovery is the greater because Rome's near-extinction was more total?
  3. Plutarch's lost verdict is sometimes a constraint and sometimes an opportunity. Without his text, is the assignment freer (you choose your own axis and verdict) or harder (no model to argue against)? Give an example of each.
  4. Pick one of the four axes the prompt offers and write the first sentence of your synkrisis. Then state which two incidents from each Life you would cite. (This is the planning step the essay requires.)
  5. Could the right answer to "who was the greater city-saver?" be that the question is wrong — that the two men did different things in different conditions and cannot be ranked? What would Plutarch make of such a refusal?
