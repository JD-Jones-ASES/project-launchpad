Study Guide: The Life of Theseus

Plutarch's Life of Theseus opens with the most famous passage in the Parallel Lives — the geographer's map — and then proceeds to tell the story Athenians told about themselves. Read the section both as a narrative (a hero's journey from Troezen to Athens to Crete and back) and as Plutarch's first sustained portrait of a founder. Watch how the two strands — generous political founder, rash private man — sit uneasily side by side.

Key Overview

Theseus's life unfolds in three movements. First, the birth and youth: his father Aegeus, king of Athens, fathered him on Aethra at Troezen through the arrangement of her father Pittheus, after a gnomic Delphic oracle. Aegeus hid a sword and sandals under a stone outside Troezen as tokens, and told Aethra to send the boy to Athens once he could lift the stone. When Theseus came of age, he lifted it and set out — but he chose the dangerous overland route, where he killed the famous bandits Sciron, Sinis, Procrustes, and Periphetes, each by his own method. By the time he reached Athens he was already famous.

Second, the great exploits. In Athens, he narrowly escaped poisoning by Aegeus's new wife Medea, who knew who he was. He then went voluntarily to Crete as part of the tribute owed to King Minos, killed the Minotaur in the Labyrinth with the help of Minos's daughter Ariadne, abandoned her on Naxos, and forgot to change his returning ship's sail from black to white. Aegeus, seeing the black sail, threw himself into the sea — which is named after him, the Aegean.

Third, the political founding. As king of Athens, Theseus carried out the synoecism of Attica — persuading the scattered villages, without war, to accept a single city and a single government. Critically, he laid down regal power voluntarily, kept only war-command and protection of the laws, and invited strangers to share citizenship with natives. Plutarch presents this as a generous, voluntary, inclusive founding — the moral contrast with Romulus that the synkrisis will later draw out.

Theseus's later life darkens. The Amazon war, the death of his son Hippolytus by Phaedra's machinations, and growing political unrest in Athens — fanned by the demagogue Menestheus — drive him into exile. He retires to the small island of Skyros, where King Lycomedes pushes him off a cliff. He is buried on Skyros, and only centuries later does the Athenian general Cimon recover his bones and bring them home to a great public shrine.

Important Passages

The geographer's map. The opening of the Theseus is the methodological foundation of the Lives. Plutarch admits the legendary status of the material and asks for "candid readers."

The synoecism passage. "He summoned the inhabitants of all the country to assemble together… to the more powerful he promised a state without monarchy, a democracy, or people's government, in which he should only be continued as their commander in war, and the protector of their laws… and by way of example, voluntarily laid down his regal power, and consulted with them about a new constitution… Thus he united the divided people, and made one body politic and one city of them." Mark this passage. The verbs — exhorted, persuaded, promised, voluntarily laid down — are the moral grammar of Theseus's founding. He builds Athens not by force but by argument and self-restraint.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why did Theseus choose the overland route to Athens instead of going by sea?
  2. What political arrangement did Theseus offer to the powerful when persuading them into the synoecism?
  3. How did the sail mix-up cause Aegeus's death?
  4. In Plutarch's account, what does Theseus do with Ariadne after she helps him?
  5. What two events bracket Theseus's political career — the founding act and the cause of his exile?
  6. Who recovered Theseus's bones, and what was the political significance of bringing them home?
  7. Plutarch's Theseus has one trait that makes him an exemplary founder and another that makes him a poor husband. Name both.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch praises the synoecism as a generous, voluntary founding. How much of this generosity is part of the historical reality, and how much is the Athenian moral self-portrait?
  2. Theseus's killing of the bandits made him famous before he had founded anything. Is this the same kind of glory as the political founding, or a different kind? Which does Plutarch value more?
  3. The Athenians who had once accepted Theseus's synoecism grew weary of him. What does it suggest about founding — generally — that even an exemplary founder can be politically rejected by his own people?
  4. The recovery of the bones happened centuries after Theseus's exile. Why might Athens have wanted, in the 5th century BC, to bring its legendary founder physically home?

Connections to Later Chapters

  • The synoecism passage is the moral foil for Section 2.3's account of Romulus's far more violent founding. The synkrisis in 2.4 turns on the contrast.
  • Theseus's treatment of women (Ariadne abandoned, Antiope abducted, Helen seized when old) becomes one of the central axes of the synkrisis in 2.4 — and one of the places Theseus loses to Romulus.
  • The death of Hippolytus, paired with Romulus's killing of Remus, is Plutarch's third synkrisis axis: the killing of the relative.
  • The mysterious death and recovery of the bones echoes Romulus's mysterious disappearance — both founders end in circumstances their cities had to interpret.
