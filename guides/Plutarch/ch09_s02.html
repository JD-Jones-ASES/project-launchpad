Study Guide: The Life of Demosthenes

Plutarch's Demosthenes is the model of the self-made political orator. Born in Athens in 384 BC, robbed of his inheritance, he taught himself to speak through one of the most famous training regimens in the ancient world, then spent his career warning his city about Philip of Macedon. He lost. But the loss is told as a story of integrity, and the death at the altar of Poseidon is the moment that makes the speeches matter.

Key Overview

Demosthenes's life moves in five stages. First, an early loss and a self-made recovery: orphaned at seven, robbed by guardians, at twenty he sued them — winning, and in the process learning to speak. Second, the deliberate self-construction: he copied Thucydides eight times, built an underground study, shaved half his head so he could not leave, practiced with pebbles and before a mirror, and ran uphill while reciting. Third, the political emergence: in the 350s he turned from legal speechwriting to public oratory and began the campaign against Philip, marked by the First Philippic (351 BC). Fourth, the failure at Chaeronea (338 BC), the Harpalus scandal, and the exile. Fifth, the recall during the Lamian War and the death by poison at the temple of Poseidon at Calauria in 322 BC.

The Life's signature note is that the orator's greatness is built from weakness — the man with a stutter who became the greatest Greek speaker. The character is serious, austere, patriotic, prepared to fight on a losing side. He has limitations: not a battle commander (Chaeronea), not above the temptations of money (Harpalus), conscious of his own importance. But what he most has is integrity at the end.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Demosthenes (384–322 BC) — Athenian orator; central figure of the Life.
  • Philip of Macedon — acceded 359 BC; reformed the army with the sarissa pike and disciplined phalanx; the opponent of the Philippics.
  • Alexander — Philip's son; at eighteen led the cavalry charge that broke the Theban Sacred Band at Chaeronea.
  • Thucydides — the historian Demosthenes copied eight times to shape his style.
  • Harpalus — Alexander's friend who fled to Athens with embezzled royal funds; the bribery accusation behind Demosthenes's conviction and banishment.
  • Antipater — Macedonian general who, after the Lamian War, demanded the death of the anti-Macedonian leaders.
  • Archias — the Macedonian agent sent to bring Demosthenes back from Calauria.
  • First Philippic — 351 BC; "Will you never act, men of Athens, until news of some disaster forces you?"
  • Chaeronea (338 BC) — battle where Athens and Thebes combined against Philip were defeated; Demosthenes fled the field.
  • Lamian War — Greek rising after Alexander's death in 323 BC; the Greeks lost.
  • Calauria — the island whose temple of Poseidon was Demosthenes's sanctuary and place of death.

Important Passages

  • The self-construction: "He had built an underground study where he constantly went to exercise his voice, and form his style; he would continue there for two or three months together, shaving one half of his head, that he might not be able, however he should desire it, to leave the place."
  • The First Philippic: "Will you never act, men of Athens, until news of some disaster forces you? Philip is not dead, nor has he gone home; nothing has happened to relieve us. Yet you, the Athenians, are sitting here as if you were on holiday. You must change your way of war, or you will be conquered."
  • The death: "You may now act the part of Creon in the tragedy if you like, and cast forth this body of mine unburied. Friendly Poseidon, for my part, I leave thy temple while still alive; but Antipater and his Macedonians have not so much as left thy temple unpolluted."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What did Demosthenes's lawsuit against his guardians teach him?
  2. List Demosthenes's three self-imposed exercises and the weakness each addressed.
  3. Why does Plutarch emphasize that Demosthenes copied Thucydides eight times?
  4. Reconstruct what happened at Chaeronea, including Alexander's role and Demosthenes's behavior.
  5. How does Plutarch handle the Harpalus affair, and what is his final judgment?
  6. How does Demosthenes turn the moment of his death into a kind of last speech?

Discussion Questions

  1. Demosthenes fled the field at Chaeronea. Does this disqualify him as a political leader, or is the orator's role different from the soldier's?
  2. Plutarch finds the Harpalus conviction "probably partly deserved and partly political." Is that a satisfying verdict, or a Plutarchan dodge?
  3. The death scene is the most carefully staged moment in the Life. What does Plutarch want us to feel — pity, admiration, both?
  4. Demosthenes's self-construction is one of Plutarch's great stories of deliberate self-improvement. Does it still work as a model for modern readers?

Connections to Later Chapters

This Life is the Greek panel of the diptych whose Roman panel is the Life of Cicero (Section 3); the two are weighed in the synkrisis (Section 4). Plutarch's comparison leans on moments staged here: the self-construction (Axis 1), the absence of boasting (Axis 3), Harpalus (Axis 5), the productive exile (Axis 6), and the composed death at the altar of Poseidon (Axis 7). When you read the synkrisis, return to the death scene — Plutarch's most rewarded moment.
