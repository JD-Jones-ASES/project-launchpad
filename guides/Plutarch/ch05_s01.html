Study Guide: Background — Persia and the Gauls

This study guide orients readers to Chapter 5 of Plutarch: Lives in Parallel, the pairing of Themistocles and Camillus. After three chapters on city-builders — founders, lawgivers, reformers — the Parallel Lives turns to a different kind of statesman: the man who saves a city already on the edge of destruction. Two existential threats are introduced; two saviors are introduced; and a single moral pattern is foregrounded that will haunt both biographies — the city that turns on the man who saved it.

Key Overview

Two crises. In 480 BC, Xerxes — son of the Darius defeated at Marathon — marched on Greece with an army modern scholarship places at 200,000-250,000 men and a fleet of perhaps 600-800 ships. By September the Persians had burned Athens. Refugees were stranded on Salamis and at Troezen. The fate of Greek civilization hung on one sea battle. In 390 BC (traditional date), a Celtic warband under Brennus crossed the Alps, destroyed a Roman army at the Allia, walked into Rome, sacked and burned most of it, besieged the Capitoline hill, and was eventually paid off in gold.

Two saviors. Themistocles (c. 524 - c. 459 BC) had spent two decades persuading Athens to become a sea power. He converted the silver revenues of the Laurium mines into a fleet of one hundred ships, evacuated the civilian population, and engineered the victory at Salamis. Camillus (c. 446 - 365 BC) captured Veii after a ten-year siege, was exiled, was recalled from Ardea to defeat the Gauls, and kept the Romans from relocating to the empty Veii — earning the title second founder of Rome.

One pattern. Both saviors were driven into exile by the cities they saved. Themistocles was ostracized — a ten-year banishment voted by the Athenian assembly. Camillus was charged with appropriating the bronze gates of an Etruscan city and chose exile over trial. The chapter foregrounds the question Plutarch keeps circling: how do cities treat the men who save them?

Major Figures and Terms

  • Themistocles — Athenian general; architect of Athenian naval power; victor of Salamis; ostracized; died in Persia, possibly by suicide.
  • Camillus (Marcus Furius Camillus) — Roman dictator (five times); captor of Veii; rescuer of Rome from the Gauls; called second founder of Rome.
  • Xerxes — Persian Great King; son of Darius; invaded Greece in 480 BC; watched his fleet sink at Salamis from a throne on a hill.
  • Brennus — Gallic chieftain who led the warband that sacked Rome; later famous in Livy's account for the line Vae victis — "Woe to the conquered."
  • Salamis — Narrow strait between the island and the Attic coast; site of the decisive sea battle of September 480 BC.
  • The Allia — River just north of Rome; site of the Roman defeat that opened the city to the Gauls.
  • The Laurium silver mines — Source of the unexpected Athenian revenue Themistocles diverted into building the navy.
  • The Capitoline hill — The one Roman citadel that held out during the Gallic Sack while the rest of the city burned.
  • Veii — Etruscan city twelve miles north of Rome; taken by Camillus after a ten-year siege.
  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's closing comparison at the end of each Lives pairing. Four are lost; this is one of them.
  • Ostracism — Athenian procedure by which the assembly could vote a citizen into ten-year exile without trial.

Important Passages

  • The fate of Greek civilization hung on a sea battle. — The chapter's framing of what Salamis decided.
  • Romulus had built it, Camillus had built it back. — The traditional Roman account of why Camillus is called the second founder.
  • The savior, then the victim. — The pattern the chapter names as one of the great patterns of ancient political life.
  • The trophy of Miltiades would not let him sleep. — Previewed for Section 5.2; the line that captures Themistocles's ambition.
  • A great general should rely on his own virtue, and not on other men's vices. — Previewed for Section 5.3; Camillus to the Falerian schoolmaster, the moral centerpiece of his Life.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What contrast does Chapter 5 draw between its pairing and the pairings of Chapters 2-4?
  2. By what date and with what force had Xerxes invaded Greece, and what was the condition of Athens before Salamis?
  3. Who led the Gauls against Rome, and roughly when did the Sack occur?
  4. Why is Camillus called the second founder of Rome?
  5. What pattern of civic ingratitude do the two Lives share?
  6. How does the lost synkrisis change the assignment at the end of this chapter?
  7. Who is the Second Witness for Chapter 5, and what work does he provide?
  8. What are the five patterns the introduction tells the reader to watch for?

Discussion Questions

  1. The chapter calls "the city turns on its savior" one of the great patterns of ancient political life. Is the pattern peculiar to the ancient world, or does it recur in modern democracies? Find a modern example to test the claim.
  2. Both saviors were politically ambitious before the crisis they resolved. Is foresight inseparable from ambition? Could a less ambitious man have done what Themistocles or Camillus did?
  3. The chapter introduction warns that both men used methods that "could plausibly have lost." What is the right relationship between strategic boldness and moral accountability when the stakes are existential?
  4. The synkrisis for this pair is lost. Does that make the comparison freer (because no Plutarchan verdict constrains it) or harder (because no Plutarchan model guides it)?
