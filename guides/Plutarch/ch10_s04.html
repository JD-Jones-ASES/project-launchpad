Study Guide: The Joint Comparison

Plutarch's synkrisis of the four reformers is the only joint comparison in the Parallel Lives. He weighs Agis, Cleomenes, Tiberius, and Gaius Gracchus together on seven axes — natural endowment, integrity in money, scale of the project, opposition faced, the deaths, civil violence, procedural innovation — and closes with an unusually explicit four-way ranking. Each man wins a different axis. None is dismissed. The result is one of Plutarch's most carefully discriminated judgments, and behind it sits a political question he raises but does not finally answer: can redistributive reform be achieved peacefully when wealth has been long concentrated?

Key Overview

  • A unique synkrisis. It is the only joint comparison Plutarch wrote, weighing four men across two centuries and two cultures against one common project of redistributive reform.
  • Axis 1, natural endowment and education. The Gracchi had "a genius to virtue beyond all other Romans" with a generous education; the Spartans had "stronger natural gifts" since they were exemplary despite the corruption around them. The Spartan achievement on this axis is slightly more impressive — they were swimming against a stronger cultural current.
  • Axis 2, integrity in money. Agis decisively. He distributed his own private fortune of six hundred talents; the Gracchi merely kept clean hands in office. Both pass; Agis goes further.
  • Axis 3, scale of the project. A draw. The Spartan project was deeper — a "thorough reformation" restoring the city to its ancient state. The Roman was more surgical — "the settlement of cities and mending of highways," recovery of public lands, equestrian-juror reform.
  • Axis 4, opposition faced. The Gracchi. They fought "men of the greatest influence" without any foundational precedent to lean on; the Spartans had Lycurgus's "great and glorious precedent" at their backs.
  • Axis 5, the deaths. Agis's death the noblest. He "willingly submitted to his fate, rather than any citizen should be in danger of his life." Cleomenes "generously fell by his own hand" after revenge failed. The Gracchi's flight is defended as "an honorable retreat from endangering of others."
  • Axis 6, civil violence. Decisively to the Gracchi. "Neither the one nor the other was the first to shed the blood of his fellow-citizens." Cleomenes is condemned for unjustly killing the ephors when he might have gained them by superiority in arms. The knife verdict: "To use the knife, unless in the extremest necessity, is neither good surgery nor wise policy, but in both cases mere unskillfulness; and in the latter, unjust as well as unfeeling."
  • Axis 7, procedural innovation. A draw. Tiberius's deposition of Octavius is "the greatest crime that can be laid to Tiberius's charge." Cleomenes's coup is on the same axis but more violent.
  • The four-way ranking. Plutarch closes with an unusually specific verdict: "I should affirm Tiberius to have excelled them all in virtue; that young Agis had been guilty of the fewest misdeeds; and that in action and boldness Caius came far short of Cleomenes." Tiberius most virtuous; Agis least guilty; Cleomenes most effective; Gaius least effective.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis: Plutarch's brief comparative essay closing a pairing of Lives. Uniquely here it closes four Lives at once.
  • The seven axes: Endowment and education; integrity in money; scale of project; opposition faced; the deaths; civil violence; procedural innovation.
  • The "knife" line: Plutarch's verdict on political violence in reform — "To use the knife, unless in the extremest necessity, is neither good surgery nor wise policy."
  • Plutarch's four-way ranking: Tiberius most virtuous, Agis least guilty, Cleomenes most effective, Gaius least effective.
  • Solon and Publicola: The implicit counter-figures — earlier Greek and Roman reformers whose names hover behind the chapter's central question: could a more skillful reformer have done better?

Important Passages

The synkrisis is structured as a sequence of rounds, but the closing verdict resolves to a four-way ranking that Plutarch quotes himself delivering: "From the account, therefore, which has been given, you yourself may perceive the difference; which if it were to be pronounced of every one singly, I should affirm Tiberius to have excelled them all in virtue; that young Agis had been guilty of the fewest misdeeds; and that in action and boldness Caius came far short of Cleomenes." The ranking is unusual for Plutarch in being so explicit; it is also, characteristically, refused as a single scale. Tiberius wins one axis, Agis another, Cleomenes a third, Gaius the unflattering fourth. The reader is left with a more textured picture than any single ranking could give.

The "knife" line carries the chapter's broader moral weight: violence in reform is "mere unskillfulness," except in the most extreme necessity. Plutarch's praise of the Gracchi on this axis is exactly that they did not shed first blood; his criticism of Cleomenes is that he chose violence when superiority in arms might have brought the ephors over by simple political pressure.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why is this synkrisis structurally unique in the Parallel Lives?
  2. On which axis does Plutarch find Agis decisively superior, and why?
  3. On which axis does Plutarch find the Gracchi decisively superior, and why?
  4. Whose death does Plutarch consider the noblest, and how does he characterize the others?
  5. What is the "knife" line, and how does it apply to Cleomenes specifically?
  6. What is the greatest charge Plutarch lays against Tiberius, on the axis of procedural innovation?
  7. State Plutarch's final four-way ranking and identify which axis each man wins.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch refuses to rank the four reformers on a single scale. Is that refusal a strength of his moral analysis or an evasion? What would a single ranking have to ignore?
  2. The chapter ends with an open political question: can redistributive reform be achieved peacefully? The four cases together suggest "probably not, at least in societies where wealth has been long concentrated." Do the four cases really support this conclusion, or is the sample too small?
  3. The writing exercise asks you to test Plutarch's ranking. Pick one axis on which you would shift his judgment and defend the change.

Connections to Later Chapters

The double-pair synkrisis closes a chapter that has already reached back into Chapter 3 (Lycurgus) for the Spartan baseline. Forward it opens into the broader Roman political collapse the rest of the book tracks. Plutarch's verdict against unnecessary violence will recur in his treatment of figures like Sulla and Caesar — for whom the "knife" is no longer extraordinary but routine. The reader who has worked through this synkrisis carries forward a more discriminating moral vocabulary for the next twenty Lives.
