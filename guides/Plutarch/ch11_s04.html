Study Guide: The Comparison

The synkrisis of Dion and Brutus is the book's closing comparison and one of Plutarch's most considered verdicts. He weighs two men who gave their lives to free their cities from one-man rule, who failed in their immediate political objective, and who left enduring philosophical reputations. The synkrisis works through six axes — motive, the character of the tyranny, resources, conduct of the war, choice of allies, aftermath — and refuses, in the end, to declare a single winner. What it offers instead is the deeper question of whether the act of tyrannicide remains morally worth doing when politically it does not achieve its aim. That question is the curricular climax of the whole book.

Key Overview

Axis 1: Personal motivation. Dion had been personally wronged by Dionysius II — banishment, the forced remarriage of his wife, the confiscation of his estate. He acted with a mix of principle and personal grievance. Brutus had been pardoned, befriended, and elevated by Caesar. He acted purely on principle. Plutarch's verdict: "The chief glory of both was their hatred of tyranny, and abhorrence of wickedness. This was unmixed and sincere in Brutus." Round one decisively to Brutus.

Axis 2: The character of the tyranny. Dionysius II was "a tyrant" who "vexed Sicily with a thousand oppressions." Caesar's supremacy, "once established and victorious, had indeed the name and appearance, but fact that was cruel or tyrannical there was none." This is Plutarch's most controversial claim in the synkrisis: that Caesar after his victory was not actually a tyrant in substance, only in form — and that the killing answered a constitutional shape, not current cruelty. Round two to Dion: his target was the worse man.

Axis 3: The resources for the project. Dion "provided not only arms, ships, and soldiers, but likewise friends and partners for the enterprise" and "laid out of his own substance." Brutus and Cassius, fleeing Rome under sentence of death, took up arms partly to save themselves. The Dion project was a deeper personal commitment. Round three to Dion.

Axis 4: The conduct of the war. Dion was "a commander without fault, improving to the utmost those counsels which he himself gave." Brutus showed "little wisdom in engaging in the final battle" and, having failed, "gave all up, and abandoned his hopes." Round four to Dion.

Axis 5: The choice of allies. Dion "trusted himself with ill men" — Callippus, the man who killed him, was a man Dion himself had brought from Athens. Brutus chose better: the conspiracy included "a great many men, not one of whom deceived him." Round five to Brutus.

Axis 6: The aftermath. Brutus was buried with honor by Antony. Caesar Augustus, the synkrisis closes with — encountering Brutus's bronze statue in Cisalpine Gaul, stopped before it and praised the Gauls for not having moved it. Dion, when killed, had no avenger. Round six to Brutus.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's closing comparative essay; this is the tenth and last in the book.
  • The six axes — motive, character of the tyranny, resources, conduct of war, choice of allies, aftermath.
  • "Unmixed and sincere" — Plutarch's phrase for Brutus's motivation; the axis-1 verdict.
  • "Vexed Sicily with a thousand oppressions" — Plutarch on Dionysius II.
  • "The name and appearance, but fact that was cruel or tyrannical there was none" — Plutarch's controversial claim about Caesar's regime in substance.
  • "A commander without fault" — Plutarch's verdict on Dion as a general.
  • Callippus — The Athenian Dion trusted; the killer who decided Axis 5 against Dion.
  • "Not one of whom deceived him" — Plutarch's phrase for Brutus's conspirators.
  • The Augustus-statue incident — Augustus's preservation of Brutus's bronze statue in Cisalpine Gaul; the closing image of the synkrisis.

Important Passages

The synkrisis's controversial claim is on Axis 2. Plutarch declares that Caesar's rule "had indeed the name and appearance, but fact that was cruel or tyrannical there was none." For an essay closing a chapter about justified tyrannicide, that is an extraordinary admission — the killing answered a constitutional form rather than current cruelty. The honesty of the admission is part of what makes the synkrisis morally serious.

The second weight-bearing passage is the moral-political verdict on the deed itself: tyrannicide can be morally justified and politically futile, and the Republic did not return because "the Romans instantly regretted Caesar, and grew enraged and implacable against those that killed him." Brutus's case is, as Plutarch presents it, morally as clean as such a case ever gets. And the case still failed politically. Whether the act remains worth doing under those conditions is the question Plutarch leaves the reader — and the question the chapter's writing exercise asks the student to answer.

The third passage is the closing image: Caesar Augustus, encountering the bronze statue of Brutus in Cisalpine Gaul, stops before it and praises the local Gauls for not having moved it. Plutarch ends the synkrisis with the gesture in which the founder of the imperial system Brutus had tried to prevent acknowledges, quietly, the moral standing of the man who had tried to prevent him.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. List the six axes of the synkrisis and name the winner of each.
  2. Quote Plutarch's verdict on Brutus's motive in Axis 1.
  3. What is the controversial claim Plutarch makes about Caesar's regime in Axis 2?
  4. Why does Plutarch award Axis 3 to Dion? Quote the operative phrase.
  5. State Plutarch's criticism of Brutus's conduct at the final battle.
  6. Who is Callippus, and why does he matter for Axis 5?
  7. Describe the closing image of the synkrisis (the Augustus-statue incident).
  8. State Plutarch's overall verdict between the two men in your own words.
  9. Quote the line about the Romans' regret over Caesar.
  10. List the four axes the writing prompt offers as alternatives, plus the option to propose one's own.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch's Axis 2 admission that Caesar's rule was not in substance tyrannical is extraordinary in a chapter celebrating tyrannicide. Does the admission damage Brutus's moral case, or does it merely shift the case from "Caesar is currently cruel" to "the constitutional form is intolerable"? Argue both sides.
  2. The synkrisis splits 3–3 across the six axes (Dion takes 2, 3, 4; Brutus takes 1, 5, 6). Is this neat balance the actual moral picture, or a structural artifact of the synkrisis form? What would change in the verdict if Plutarch had weighted some axes more heavily than others?
  3. Tyrannicide is, in Plutarch's verdict, morally justifiable and politically futile. Is the moral justification of an act independent of its political success — or does political failure retroactively damage the moral case? Choose a position and argue it from Plutarch's text.

Connections to Later Chapters

The synkrisis closes the book's ten-pair sequence. The writing prompt at the end of section 4 is the tenth and last synkrisis exercise: choose one axis (justification of tyrannicide, principle versus motive, use of force, political failure, or one of your own), cite two specific incidents from each Life, render judgment, stay under a thousand words. The reader is asked to place this final synkrisis beside the one written for Chapter 2 (Theseus and Romulus) — the contrast in sharpness of axis, specificity of incidents, and confidence of judgment is the curricular point of the whole spine. The chapter's reception history (Dante's hostile reading, Shakespeare's heroic reading, the American Founders' republican reading) is taken up directly in Chapter 12.
