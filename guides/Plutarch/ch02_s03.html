Study Guide: The Life of Romulus

Plutarch's Life of Romulus tells the legend you may already partially know — twin brothers, a she-wolf, a brother killing a brother — but tells it skeptically, with multiple competing versions, and with growing moral unease. This section also introduces the first formal Second Witness exercise of the book: Livy's parallel account, written about a century before Plutarch. Read both Plutarch and Livy together; the comparison is itself part of the lesson.

Key Overview

The setting is Alba Longa, an ancient Latin city ruled by Numitor. Numitor's brother Amulius usurped the throne, exiled Numitor, killed Numitor's son, and made Numitor's daughter Rhea Silvia a Vestal virgin so that she could have no rival heir. But Rhea Silvia bore twins to the god Mars: Romulus and Remus. Amulius ordered the babies thrown into the Tiber. The basket carrying them washed ashore, where they were suckled by a she-wolf and then found and raised by the shepherd Faustulus.

Grown into young men, the twins restored Numitor to the throne and decided to found their own city. Here the trouble begins. Romulus marked the walls on the Palatine; Remus stood on the Aventine. They agreed to let the gods decide between them by a sign of birds. Remus saw six vultures first; Romulus saw twelve a little later. Each claimed the omen. From words they fell to blows, and Remus was killed — by Romulus himself, in the more common version Plutarch records. Livy makes it more direct: Remus jumped derisively over Romulus's new wall and Romulus killed him in a rage.

Rome's founding population was unusual: an open settlement of fugitives, outcasts, runaway slaves, and exiles. To find them women, Romulus invited the neighboring Sabines to a festival and, on a prearranged signal, his men seized the Sabine women as wives. This is the famous "rape of the Sabine women." Plutarch is careful: he reports the seizure as real violence, then reports the comparatively decent Roman conduct that followed (the women were not assaulted in the modern sense but forced into marriage; many came in time to identify with their new families), and finally records the women's own intervention — running between the swords when their fathers came back in arms — which brought peace and merged the two peoples.

Romulus reigned about thirty-seven years. He disappeared in a storm at the Capraean Marsh and was deified as Quirinus — or, in a colder tradition Plutarch refuses to drop from the page, he was assassinated by the senators, his body cut to pieces and concealed. Plutarch notes wryly that legends about disappearances tend to multiply because the cult of the founder is more useful than the inconvenient story of how he died.

Major Figures and Terms

Name Role
Romulus Legendary founder of Rome; son of Mars; killer of his brother Remus.
Remus Romulus's twin; killed in the founding quarrel over the bird-omen.
Numitor Rightful king of Alba Longa; deposed by Amulius, restored by his grandsons.
Amulius The usurping brother who set the whole story in motion.
Rhea Silvia Numitor's daughter; forced into Vestal celibacy but bore the twins to Mars.
Faustulus The shepherd who raised the twins after the wolf had suckled them.
The Sabines Neighboring people whose women were seized to populate Rome.
Hersilia The one Sabine woman Romulus kept for himself (mentioned by Plutarch in the synkrisis).
Quirinus The deified Romulus; the god into whom he was transformed in the apotheosis tradition.
Livy The great Roman historian (Augustan age); the Second Witness whose account is read alongside Plutarch's.
Capraean Marsh The site of Romulus's disappearance in the apotheosis tradition.

Important Passages

The founding quarrel. "Romulus marked out the walls of the new town on the Palatine hill; Remus took up his station on the Aventine. They had agreed to leave the choice between them to the gods, through a sign of birds. Remus, watching, first saw six vultures; Romulus, somewhat later, saw twelve. A quarrel ensued… From words they fell to blows, and in the confusion Remus was killed; by whose hand, the tradition is not certain, but the more common account makes Romulus himself the killer."

Mark the careful phrasing: "the tradition is not certain." Plutarch will not pretend to a knowledge he does not have, but he will not let Romulus off the hook either.

The Sabine women. "When the time was come to fight, the Sabine women, with their hair flying and their garments torn, ran into the very midst of the swords and the slaughter… separating husbands and fathers… The Sabine men, when they saw their daughters and sisters and wives, were so moved that they laid down their arms." This is Plutarch holding three things together: violence, women's agency, and a real political union that grew out of an ambiguous beginning.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What did Amulius do to prevent any rival heir, and how did the gods get around it?
  2. What did Remus and Romulus see in the bird-omen, and why did the dispute matter?
  3. According to Plutarch, who killed Remus, and how does he qualify his statement?
  4. Name three differences between Livy's account and Plutarch's account of the same events.
  5. What was Romulus's solution to populating his new city, and what was the consequence?
  6. What did the Sabine women do when their fathers came back in arms, and what was the political outcome?
  7. What are the two traditions about Romulus's death, and why does Plutarch refuse to choose between them?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch records the seizure of the Sabine women as violence and the subsequent union as a real political good. Is this honest historiography, or is Plutarch softening the moral problem?
  2. Livy lets the great story stand (the wolf, the killing of Remus by Romulus in a rage). Plutarch is more guarded. Which historian's method do you trust more, and why?
  3. Plutarch's character contrast — Theseus persuades, Romulus commands; Theseus shares power, Romulus reigns alone — is sharp. Is this an accurate reading of the two founders, or is Plutarch already setting up the synkrisis?
  4. The cult of Quirinus persisted for centuries; the senator-assassination story was the colder alternative. What does it mean that Plutarch records the colder alternative anyway?

Connections to Later Chapters

  • The killing of Remus, paired with Theseus's role in the death of Hippolytus, is Axis 3 of Plutarch's synkrisis in 2.4 — the killing of the relative. Plutarch gives Theseus the better excuse there.
  • The seizure of the Sabine women, paired with Theseus's many seizures of women (Ariadne, Antiope, Helen), is Axis 5. Plutarch decides this one for Romulus, partly on the strength of the political union that followed.
  • The two-deaths-of-Romulus puzzle is one of the clearest examples of the "some say, others say" method introduced in Section 2.1.
  • The Second-Witness comparison with Livy is the first formal exercise in reading multiple ancient sources against each other — a skill the book returns to several times.
