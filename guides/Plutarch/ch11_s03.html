Study Guide: The Life of Brutus

Plutarch's Life of Brutus is one of the most morally serious in the whole work. Brutus is the austere Roman Stoic — philosophically formed under Cato the Younger, politically schooled under the bronze image of his ancestor the first Brutus, intellectually respected even by his opponents. The Life follows him through an impossible civil-war choice, an unprecedented act of friendship-pardon at Caesar's hands, a conspiracy joined out of inherited obligation rather than personal grievance, a single great public act in the Theater of Pompey, two battles at Philippi, and a death by his own hand at forty-three. The Life closes the second half of the chapter's pairing, and the synkrisis that follows it is, by the chapter's own claim, in some ways the moral climax of the whole book.

Key Overview

Inheritance and formation. Marcus Junius Brutus was born in 85 BC. His father had been killed by Pompey in 77 BC. His mother Servilia was the half-sister of Cato and a long-time mistress of Caesar — with the gossip that Brutus himself might have been Caesar's natural son. The bronze statue of Lucius Junius Brutus, expeller of the Tarquins and founder of the Republic, stood in the family home. Brutus took the inheritance literally: his political identity was the inherited obligation to oppose monarchy. His education was Stoic and Academic, his teacher his uncle Cato, his correspondent Cicero. Even his opponents acknowledged his intellectual seriousness.

The civil war and the pardon. When civil war broke out in 49 BC, Brutus faced an impossible choice: Pompey had killed his father, Caesar was his mother's lover. He chose Pompey on political grounds, served through Pharsalus, and after Caesar's victory was pardoned with what was apparently full friendship — promoted, given commands, promised the consulship for 44 BC. The two men were close in Caesar's last years. Brutus accepted the pardon and the offices but remained, intellectually, a Stoic republican who could not reconcile himself to one-man rule.

Dictator perpetuo, the conspiracy, the Ides. In early 44 BC Caesar became dictator perpetuo — dictator for life. Cassius approached Brutus; Brutus's hesitation is reported by Plutarch with care; the political logic was that his inherited name made him the natural leader. He joined; the conspiracy expanded to roughly sixty senators; the date was the Ides of March. Plutarch's account of the killing is one of his most carefully composed scenes: Tillius Cimber pulled at Caesar's toga as if making a petition; Casca struck the first blow; when Caesar saw Brutus's drawn sword, he pulled his gown over his head and fell at the foot of the statue of Pompey. The Greek line Plutarch attributes to Caesar is "kai su, teknon?" — "You too, my child?" — the source for Shakespeare's "Et tu, Brute?"

The political failure and the death. The Senate fled rather than applauding. Antony's funeral oration the next day turned the Roman people against the conspirators. Brutus and Cassius retreated to the Greek East. At Philippi in late 42 BC the first battle was indecisive — Brutus defeated Octavian but Cassius, defeated by Antony and falsely informed Brutus had also been defeated, killed himself. The second battle twenty days later was a clearer defeat. Brutus withdrew to the hills with a few companions, spent his last night reciting Greek tragedy, and at dawn fell on his sword with Strato holding it. He was forty-three.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Lucius Junius Brutus — Marcus Brutus's ancestor; the founder of the Republic from Chapter 4; his bronze statue stood in the family home.
  • Servilia — Brutus's mother; Cato's half-sister; Caesar's long-time mistress.
  • Cato the Younger — Brutus's uncle and Stoic teacher; the great Stoic moralist of the late Republic.
  • Cicero — Brutus's correspondent and intellectual contemporary; complained in his letters about Brutus's Cypriot interest rates.
  • Cassius — Brutus's brother-in-law; instigator of the conspiracy.
  • Pharsalus (48 BC) — Caesar's defeat of Pompey; after this battle Brutus was pardoned.
  • Dictator perpetuo — Caesar's title of permanent dictatorship from early 44 BC; the political move that triggered the conspiracy.
  • Tillius Cimber, Casca — Conspirators named in the assassination sequence.
  • The statue of Pompey — At whose foot Caesar fell.
  • "kai su, teknon?" — Plutarch's Greek for Caesar's dying words to Brutus.
  • Antony's funeral oration — The rhetorical instrument that turned Rome against the conspirators.
  • Philippi (42 BC) — Two battles in northern Greece; the Republican defeat.
  • Strato — Brutus's companion who held the sword on the last morning.

Important Passages

Two passages from this Life have shaped Western political imagination more than almost any others in Plutarch. The first is the reconstruction of Caesar's death: "When he saw Brutus with his sword drawn in his hand, he pulled his gown over his head, and made no more resistance, and fell at the foot of the statue of Pompey." The image of the dictator falling at the feet of his rival's statue is one of the most ironically charged scenes in ancient historiography. The second is the account of Brutus's last morning: "Then taking every one of those that were with him by the hand, he spoke to them with a cheerful and serene countenance, and said that he was abundantly satisfied to have failed in nothing that concerned the public, but accused fortune of his country's ruin." He thanked his companions for their loyalty, asked Strato to hold his sword, and fell on it with both hands.

The third passage — implicit rather than quoted — is the moral self-assessment Brutus offers in his final hours: he had failed in nothing that concerned the public. Whether that judgment is correct is one of the questions the synkrisis takes up.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Give Brutus's birth year and the year his father was killed, with the name of the killer.
  2. Identify Brutus's mother and the political scandal that attended her.
  3. What object stood in the Brutus family home, and what did it represent?
  4. Name Brutus's philosophical teacher and the school of his formation.
  5. State the moral complication in Brutus's provincial record.
  6. Explain Brutus's impossible choice in 49 BC and how he resolved it.
  7. List four specific honors Caesar gave Brutus after Pharsalus.
  8. Where did Caesar fall, and at whose feet?
  9. Give Plutarch's Greek for Caesar's dying words and identify their Shakespearean rendering.
  10. Describe Brutus's death — manner, name of companion, age.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch's Brutus is intellectually serious and morally upright, but the Life records his hard treatment of Cypriot debtors. Does this complication damage the picture of him as the moral high water mark of the late Republic, or is it a normal mark of late-republican aristocratic practice that the Life acknowledges without indicting?
  2. Plutarch's reconstruction has Caesar resist briefly until he sees Brutus's sword and then surrender the resistance. What does this small dramatic detail do for the meaning of the killing — and how much weight should a reader put on something Plutarch could not literally have known?
  3. Brutus's last night is composed almost as a Stoic exercise: serene countenance, recitation of Greek tragedy, statement that he had failed in nothing that concerned the public, death with both hands on the sword. Is this the heroic Stoic ending Plutarch's Brutus deserved, or is the scene a literary composition that softens the political failure?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Life of Brutus is the second half of the pairing; the synkrisis in section 4 weighs Brutus against Dion across six axes. The Life closes the chapter's central question — whether the moral weight of tyrannicide survives its political failure — at exactly the point where Plutarch makes Brutus say in his own voice that he failed in nothing that concerned the public. The synkrisis will test that claim. The chapter as a whole closes the book, and the writing prompt at the end of section 4 asks the reader to compose a final synkrisis of their own that pulls together what the spine of ten exercises has taught.
