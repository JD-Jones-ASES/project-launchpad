Study Guide: The Life of Coriolanus

Caius Marcius — later surnamed Coriolanus for his single-handed capture of the Volscian city of Corioli — is the moral opposite of Alcibiades in style and the structural twin of Alcibiades in fate. Where Alcibiades was supple, Coriolanus was rigid. Where Alcibiades flattered the people he despised, Coriolanus refused to flatter and was destroyed by his refusal. Both ended in exile and turned on their cities. Plutarch's Life presses the question of whether the rigidity of personal virtue can itself be a political vice.

Key Overview

The mother's son. Plutarch's introduction is built around two facts. The first is that good natural endowments, without proper discipline, are like rich soil without culture: they produce, alongside their better fruits, much that is faulty. The second is that Coriolanus's whole moral life was oriented toward his mother Volumnia's approval. He won military distinctions "not that glory might be the end of his daring, [but] the end of his glory was his mother's gladness." He married a woman his mother chose and continued to live in her house. This bond is the deepest fact about him. He could not flatter the Roman plebs because he could not bring himself to seek any approval except his mother's. The same rigidity that made him fearless in battle made him a political liability.

Corioli and the refusal of spoils. The first great test of his career was the Roman war with the Volscians. During the siege of Corioli he pursued the retreating enemy back through the gates and fought alone among them until reinforcements arrived; the city fell. He was given the surname Coriolanus as a permanent reward. In the same campaign, when wounded and ordered to the rear by the consul Cominius, he refused with the line "weariness was not for conquerors." After the victory the consul offered him a tenth of the spoils. He refused the wealth and asked only for the release of a Volscian friend who had been taken prisoner. The army praised him; Plutarch comments that to refuse advantage is rarer and finer than to use it well.

The bread crisis and the failed consulship. The Roman plebs had recently won, by threatening to leave the city en masse, the office of tribune of the people — a magistracy whose holders could veto patrician decisions. Coriolanus regarded the office as a catastrophic compromise. When a famine struck and Sicilian grain ships arrived, he argued in the Senate that the grain should not be sold to the plebs except at the old high price and except on condition that the tribunate be abolished. He wanted to use the famine to roll back the constitutional reform. Word of the speech reached the plebs. The tribunes demanded his arrest; he stood trial; he was convicted of attempting to overthrow the constitution; he was banished.

The march on Rome. He went to the Volscians — the very people whose city he had taken — and offered them his services. They accepted. Within a year he was at the head of a Volscian army moving on Rome, winning town after town from his former countrymen. The Romans, terrified, sent embassies. He rejected the senators. He rejected the priests in their sacred garments. He nearly rejected the matrons of Rome.

Volumnia's appeal. At the head of the matrons came his mother Volumnia, his wife Virgilia, and his small son. Plutarch records Volumnia's speech at length. She says even silence would inform him of their misery, that the matrons ought to have rejoiced at his coming but cannot, that no man is so unfortunate as to hate the city which is his mother — and that he has undertaken her ruin. Coriolanus listened in silence. He embraced his mother. He turned his army around. He withdrew from Roman territory. Plutarch calls this the last and best moment of his life. The Roman matrons returned to the city in honor; the Senate later built a temple at the spot. (The scene is the basis of Volumnia's great speech in Shakespeare's Coriolanus V.iii.)

The Volscian killing. The Volscians, robbed of their war, were furious. His Volscian rival Tullus Aufidius — who had welcomed him in exile — turned on him. At a public assembly Aufidius accused him of betrayal; the crowd turned; assassins struck; Coriolanus was killed on the spot. He had refused to ransom himself to either side.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Caius Marcius Coriolanus — Roman patrician general; legendary date in the fifth century BC.
  • Volumnia — Coriolanus's mother; the formative moral influence of his life; the one person who could stop his army at the gates of Rome.
  • Virgilia — Coriolanus's wife, chosen by his mother; with their small son joined Volumnia's procession.
  • Cominius — Roman consul under whom Coriolanus served against the Volscians; offered him the tenth of the spoils.
  • Corioli — Volscian city captured single-handedly by Marcius; source of the surname.
  • The tribunate of the people — plebeian magistracy with veto power; Coriolanus tried to abolish it during the famine.
  • Tullus Aufidius — Volscian leader who welcomed Coriolanus in exile and engineered his death after the withdrawal from Rome.

Important Passages

On formation: "A generous and worthy nature without proper discipline, like a rich soil without culture, is apt, with its better fruits, to produce also much that is bad and faulty… [Marcius's] force and vigor of his soul, and a persevering constancy in all he undertook, led him successfully into many noble achievements; yet, on the other side, also, by indulging the vehemence of his passion… he rendered himself incapable of acting and associating with others."

On his motive: Glory's end was "his mother's gladness."

On weariness: When the consul Cominius wanted to send him to the rear, he refused with the line "weariness was not for conquerors."

On Volumnia's appeal: "If we hold our peace, my son, and determine not to speak, the state of our garments and the misery of our looks will inform thee what life we have led at home, since thy banishment… I, who have borne thee, the most thy mother that ever was, must behold thee in arms against thy mother, thy wife, and thy children… My son, no man is so unfortunate as to hate the city which is his mother. But thou hast undertaken her ruin."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What two facts does Plutarch use to introduce Coriolanus's character?
  2. State the agricultural simile Plutarch applies to Coriolanus's formation.
  3. How did Marcius earn his surname?
  4. What line did Coriolanus speak when ordered to the rear during the Volscian war?
  5. What did he ask for in place of a tenth of the spoils, and what does Plutarch say about the refusal of advantage?
  6. What was the tribunate of the people, and why did Coriolanus oppose it?
  7. State the two conditions Coriolanus attached to the sale of Sicilian grain during the famine.
  8. List the three embassies the Romans sent to his camp and identify who finally moved him.
  9. Summarize the central claim of Volumnia's appeal.
  10. How did Coriolanus die, and at whose instigation?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch presents the bond between Coriolanus and Volumnia as both his greatest virtue (the moral counterweight) and his great vulnerability (the political incapacity). Can a single relationship be both at once? Argue from the speech at the gates of Rome and the lifelong refusal to flatter.
  2. Plutarch says the rigidity that made Coriolanus fearless in battle made him a political liability. Is rigidity always a political vice in a leader? When, if ever, is it the right virtue?
  3. Coriolanus refused to ransom himself to either side. Was the silent embrace of his mother followed by the Volscian killing the redemption of his Life, the proof of its tragedy, or both?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Volumnia scene is the model passage for the Christian–Shakespearean reception of Plutarch. Shakespeare's Coriolanus (1607) is drawn almost verbatim from this section through North's 1579 translation, and the modern English understanding of the proud, doomed hero owes its shape to this passage. Topic 7.4 (the synkrisis) will set Coriolanus's openly haughty contempt for the plebs against Alcibiades's flattering contempt and weigh which kind of failure is the more politically destructive. Plutarch's verdict — that the man who refuses to flatter is more politically dangerous than the man who flatters — is one of the surprises of the Lives.
