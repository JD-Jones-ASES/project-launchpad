Study Guide: Background — Two Temperaments

The sixth chapter of Plutarch: Lives in Parallel introduces a pairing unlike the earlier ones. The men we have read about so far — Solon, Themistocles, Aristides, the rest — were leaders in moments of acute crisis, who either built or saved their cities and then receded. Pericles and Fabius Maximus are different. Both wielded sustained political authority over decades, and both became proverbial for a distinctive temperament. The chapter is about character itself as a political fact — about what kind of man each leader was, and what difference it made.

Key Overview

The visionary and the delayer. Pericles (c. 495–429 BC) presided over the Golden Age of Athens: the Parthenon, the patronage of philosophy and tragedy, the constitutional adjustments that completed the democracy. He was ambitious, intellectually adventurous, eloquent at a distance. Fabius Maximus (c. 280–203 BC) led Rome during the worst year of its history. After Hannibal crossed the Alps and shattered Roman armies at the Trebia, at Lake Trasimene, and (climactically) at Cannae in 216 BC, where 80,000 Romans died in a single day, Fabius held the line by refusing battle altogether. His patient strategy — harassing supply lines, shadowing the enemy, never offering the decisive engagement — earned him the surname Cunctator, "the Delayer." It also saved Rome.

The central question. Plutarch puts before the reader a recurring problem of political life: which kind of leader is better suited to public office — the visionary, who sees what could be, or the delayer, who insists on what must not be lost? The chapter does not answer cleanly. Pericles did things; Fabius prevented things. Both contributions are politically essential, but they are different kinds of greatness, and the synkrisis will resist ranking them on a single scale.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Pericles — Athenian statesman, leader of the demos; rebuilder of the Acropolis; chief architect of Periclean-era Athens and its imperial reach.
  • Fabius Maximus Verrucosus — Roman patrician, twice consul before being made dictator after Trasimene; known as the Cunctator.
  • Anaxagoras of Clazomenae — Philosopher who introduced Nous (mind/intelligence) as the ordering principle of the universe; tutor to Pericles.
  • Damon — Music teacher to Pericles who, by Plutarch's account, was really a political theorist using music lessons as cover; later ostracized.
  • Mnesiphilus — A Solonian political mentor who taught Pericles "a sort of political shrewdness and practical sagacity."
  • The Olympian — Pericles's nickname for his godlike public manner: elevated speech, composed demeanor, refusal to be drawn into private chatter. A deliberate political style.
  • Delian League — Anti-Persian alliance founded after the Persian Wars; in practice became the Athenian empire. Pericles moved its treasury to Athens.
  • Minucius — Fabius's ambitious cavalry commander, elevated to equal command by popular vote; later rescued by Fabius from one of Hannibal's traps.
  • Cunctator — "The Delayer." Originally a slander, later an honorific.
  • Long Walls — Fortifications connecting Athens to its port of Piraeus; backbone of Pericles's war strategy of withdrawal and naval supremacy.

Important Passages

The introductory section quotes very little directly, but it flags two passages to watch in the Lives proper. The first is Plutarch's account of Pericles's intellectual formation — the moments when specific Periclean habits (serenity, eloquence, disinterest in superstition) are traced to particular teachers. The second is the Funeral Oration (Thucydides II.34–46), the Second Witness for the Greek side, "one of the founding documents of Western political thought." The text warns the reader that it is both the most sustained ancient case for democracy as a way of life and a piece of Athenian war rhetoric. Both readings are correct.

The Roman side has no comparable Second Witness in this volume; Livy's account is too extensive to excerpt, and Polybius is referred to in Further Reading.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What distinguishes the Pericles–Fabius pair from the earlier pairs in the Lives?
  2. Name the three teachers Plutarch identifies in Pericles's intellectual formation.
  3. What was the Delian League, and how did it complicate Pericles's legacy?
  4. What does the nickname "Cunctator" mean, and what was its original tone?
  5. Identify the three Roman defeats that established the depth of Rome's crisis under Hannibal.
  6. What is the "Olympian style," and why does Plutarch emphasize that it was deliberate?
  7. According to the chapter, what is the "political cost of patience"?
  8. Why is Thucydides's Funeral Oration the Second Witness for the Greek side?
  9. How do the deaths of Pericles and Fabius contrast?
  10. State the central question Plutarch asks of the reader in this chapter.

Discussion Questions

  1. The chapter says character itself is a political fact. Is that claim still defensible in modern politics, where parties, institutions, and media often constrain the influence of individual temperament?
  2. The Roman public initially treated "Cunctator" as a slander. What other historical or contemporary examples can you think of where a leader's right strategy looked like cowardice or weakness at the time?
  3. The case for Pericles relies in part on what Athens did — empire, monumental architecture, patronage of culture. Is the visionary's record separable from the methods by which the vision was financed?

Connections to Later Chapters

The pairing with Alcibiades (Chapter 7) is direct: Alcibiades is the Athenian who inherits a Periclean city already stretched by its ambitions, and squanders what he inherits. The pairing with Coriolanus (Chapter 7's Roman half) brings the question of temperament back to its political extreme — what happens when a Roman general's character is so rigid that the city he served can no longer hold him. The synkrisis method itself, foregrounded here, recurs in every later chapter.
