Study Guide: The Comparison (synkrisis of Lycurgus and Numa)

Plutarch's synkrisis of Lycurgus and Numa is more interpretive than his comparison of the founders. Unlike Theseus and Romulus, the two lawgivers are not separated by personal flaws — both lived austerely, both ruled justly. The comparison is between their philosophies of government: not which man was better, but which kind of city is better. Plutarch organizes it around seven axes, with a verdict whose hinge is durability.

Key Overview

The seven axes and their results:

  1. How each came to power. Numa accepted a kingdom he did not desire; Lycurgus resigned one he could have had. "It was glorious to acquire a throne by justice, yet more glorious to prefer justice before a throne." Round one, narrowly, to Lycurgus.

  2. The character of the work. Lycurgus pulled down a wealthy oligarchy; Numa softened a martial people. Plutarch judges Lycurgus's task harder: asking a rich man to give up gold is harder than asking a warrior to give up his sword. Round two to Lycurgus.

  3. The use of force. Lycurgus fought and lost an eye; Numa never struck a man. Round three to Numa.

  4. The humaneness of the institutions. The Spartan krypteia — sending young citizens out once a year to kill any helot they could find — is "cruel and iniquitous." Numa, by contrast, granted slaves a license to sit with their masters at the Saturnalia. Round four to Numa.

  5. The virtue each aimed at. Lycurgus aimed at fortitude; Numa at justice. Plutarch's generous formulation: neither was vicious; they aimed at different virtues. Round five is properly a draw.

  6. The treatment of women. Spartan girls' exercises and public processions struck Greek readers as scandalous; Numa's Roman matrons preserved modesty (wine forbidden, divorce essentially unknown for two and a half centuries). Round six to Numa.

  7. The endurance of the work — the decisive axis. Lycurgus's institutions lasted more than five hundred years, "like some deep and thoroughly ingrained tincture." Numa's peace died with him: "no sooner did he expire his last breath than the gates of Janus's temple flew wide open." Round seven, and the overall verdict, to Lycurgus.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's formal comparison, here organized around seven axes.
  • Krypteia — Spartan annual hunt of helots; the cruelty Plutarch names against Lycurgus.
  • Saturnalia — The Roman festival at which Numa allowed slaves to sit with masters.
  • Helots — Enslaved Messenian and Laconian peasants on whom Spartan citizens depended.
  • Fortitude / justice — Contrasting virtues Plutarch identifies as the targets of the two lawgivers.
  • The agoge — Lycurgus's public education; the identified cement of the durable constitution.

Important Passages

  • "It was glorious to acquire a throne by justice, yet more glorious to prefer justice before a throne." — Axis 1.
  • "It was not so much his business to persuade his citizens to put off their armor or ungird their swords, as to cast away their gold or silver." — Axis 2.
  • "While the one effected all by persuasions and his people's love for him, the other, with danger and hazard of his person, scarcely in the end succeeded." — Axis 3.
  • "Numa did not out of cowardice or fear affect peace, but because he would not be guilty of injustice." — Axis 5.
  • "Like some deep and thoroughly ingrained tincture, retaining their hold upon the nation." — On the durability of Lycurgus's institutions.
  • "Numa's whole design and aim, the continuance of peace and good-will, on his death vanished with him." — The central diagnosis.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why is the synkrisis of Lycurgus and Numa more interpretive than the comparison of the founders?
  2. How does Plutarch resolve Axis 1?
  3. On Axis 2, why does Plutarch say Lycurgus's task was harder?
  4. On Axis 3, how does Plutarch put the contrast in a single sentence?
  5. What is the krypteia, and how does Plutarch use it against Lycurgus on Axis 4?
  6. What virtues does Plutarch identify as the aims of each lawgiver in Axis 5?
  7. Why does Plutarch judge Numa's arrangements "better adapted to the female sex" on Axis 6?
  8. What is the decisive axis, and how does Plutarch resolve it?
  9. According to Plutarch, what is the institutional reason Lycurgus's work endured and Numa's evaporated?
  10. What two closing observations complicate Plutarch's overall verdict?

Discussion Questions

  • Numa wins more rounds numerically, but Plutarch's overall verdict tips to Lycurgus. Is durability the most important measure of a lawgiver's success?
  • Plutarch argues that warlike peoples are not harder to keep peaceful than peaceful peoples are to keep disciplined. Do you agree?
  • The closing paradox — Rome grew by abandoning Numa's piety; Sparta collapsed by abandoning Lycurgus's discipline — asks whether civic peace and political greatness can be the same thing. Can they?

Connections to Later Chapters

The synkrisis opens an axis that runs through the rest of the Lives: education is the cement of any constitution. The claim foreshadows Solon (Chapter 4), whose laws were written to bind without him. Plutarch's closing acknowledgment of Numa's unique pacific achievement — harmony "without arms or any violence" — is one of the most generous notes in any of his synkriseis.
