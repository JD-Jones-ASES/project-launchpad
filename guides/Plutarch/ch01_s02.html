Study Guide: The Parallel Lives — Method and Purpose

Collections of biographies were not new in Plutarch's day — Suetonius wrote the Lives of the Twelve Caesars, and Diogenes Laertius later wrote the Lives of the Eminent Philosophers. What no one else did was pair Greeks with Romans. This section explains the Plutarchan pair, the comparative essay (synkrisis) that closes each pairing, and what survives of the project. Understanding the architecture before you enter the Lives is essential, because the structure is the argument: the form itself is a way of thinking.

Key Overview

  • The Plutarchan pair. Each unit of the Parallel Lives is one Greek Life, one Roman Life, and one synkrisis (formal comparison). Twenty-three such pairings survive — forty-six biographies plus nineteen surviving synkriseis.
  • Pairing logic. Plutarch pairs by theme (founders, lawgivers, orators), not by date. Subjects often live centuries apart; what unites them is the question their lives raise.
  • The synkrisis is evaluative. It is not a tidy summary but a judgment essay: which of these two men was the greater? On which axis? By what standard?
  • Compression is the point. A typical synkrisis is four to six pages in modern editions — less than a tenth of the Lives it concludes.
  • What survives. Twenty-three pairs (forty-six Lives), nineteen synkriseis, and four single Lives (Aratus, Artaxerxes II, Galba, Otho). Four pairings lost their synkriseis; one entire pairing (Scipio and Epaminondas, presumed) is gone.
  • Why the form matters. Holding two lives side by side, on a chosen axis, is a way of thinking. Learning to do it well is the chief purpose of the book.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Suetonius: Plutarch's older contemporary, author of the Lives of the Twelve Caesars — a Roman-only series, no pairings.
  • Diogenes Laertius: Wrote the Lives of the Eminent Philosophers about a century after Plutarch, also unpaired.
  • Synkrisis (Greek, "comparison"): Plutarch's evaluative comparison essay closing each pairing.
  • Theseus and Romulus: The opening pair — legendary founders of Athens and Rome.
  • Lycurgus and Numa: Lawgivers who shaped Sparta and Rome from their foundations.
  • Pericles and Fabius: Crisis-managers separated by two centuries; their contrast is the point.
  • Demosthenes and Cicero: Orators whose words tried to save threatened republics.
  • The Four Lost Synkriseis: Themistocles–Camillus; Pyrrhus–Marius; Alexander–Caesar; Phocion–Cato the Younger.
  • The Four Singles: Aratus of Sicyon, Artaxerxes II of Persia, Galba, and Otho — surviving Lives with no preserved pair.

Important Passages

The note on synkrisis compression is the structural heart of the section. Plutarch "has just spent forty pages walking you slowly through a life; now in four pages he renders judgment." The two moves — the long descriptive Life and the short evaluative synkrisis — are part of a single intellectual operation. The Lives gather evidence; the synkrisis weighs it. The whole work is therefore an alternation between description and judgment, observation and verdict. The student who reads the Lives without reading the synkriseis has read half the book.

The list of typical synkrisis axes is a checklist worth memorizing: (1) origin and ability — who came from the harder starting point; (2) virtues — courage, justice, restraint, friendship, compared incident by incident; (3) failings — where each man fell short of his own standard; (4) the end of the life — how each died and what that death tells us; (5) a summative judgment — sometimes a clear verdict, sometimes deliberate ambivalence. When you write your own synkriseis at the end of each chapter, this is the shape your essay should take.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What single innovation distinguishes Plutarch's biographical project from those of Suetonius and Diogenes Laertius?
  2. How many pairings, biographies, and surviving synkriseis make up the Parallel Lives as we have them?
  3. Give three of Plutarch's pairings and the theme that unites each pair.
  4. Which four pairings lost their synkriseis, and how does this book handle two of those lost essays?
  5. Name the four "single" Lives and explain why they sit outside the paired project.
  6. What are the five standard axes of a Plutarchan synkrisis?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch never explains his pairing logic. Reading the examples (Theseus/Romulus, Lycurgus/Numa, Pericles/Fabius, Demosthenes/Cicero), what unstated rules do you think govern his pairings? Could a pair fail?
  2. The text says learning to write synkrisis is "the discipline ancient and early-modern education prized above almost any other." Why might holding two lives side by side, on one axis, sharpen judgment more than describing one life alone?
