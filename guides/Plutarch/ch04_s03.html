Study Guide: The Life of Publicola

Publius Valerius — later given the cognomen Publicola, "friend of the people" — was a Roman patrician of an ancient family whose ancestor had negotiated peace between Romulus's Romans and the Sabines after the abduction of the Sabine women. Plutarch's Life is set in the most consequential half-decade in Roman history: the expulsion of the Tarquins in 509 BC, the bloody first year of the new Republic, and the construction of the legal scaffolding that gave the new government its durability. The Life is also our principal Greek-language source on Lucius Junius Brutus the Liberator (not to be confused with Marcus Brutus of Chapter 11), and the chapter pairs Plutarch's account with Livy's Roman one as the Second Witness for the Roman side.

Key Overview

Five episodes carry the Life. The expulsion of the Tarquins, triggered by Lucretia's rape and suicide; the discovery of the royalist conspiracy, in which a slave named Vindicius reveals that Brutus's own sons are plotting to restore Tarquin, and Brutus orders their execution; the legislation of the early Republic, in which Publicola installs the right of appeal, the lowering of the fasces, the quaestorship, and the death penalty for would-be tyrants; the demolition of his own house overnight when some Romans began to call him a king-in-waiting; and his death in office and public funeral, paid for from the public treasury because he had died poor.

The arc is unlike Solon's. Where Solon left Athens to let his laws develop their own authority, Publicola stayed in Rome and worked the constitution through its first crises. The chapter wants us to weigh the two patterns — Plutarch's synkrisis in Section 4 will tilt decisively toward the man who stayed.

Major Figures and Terms

Publius Valerius Publicola — patrician aristocrat, four-time consul, principal architect of the Valerian laws.

Lucius Junius Brutus — kinsman of the Tarquins who feigned half-wit; led the revolt; first consul; ordered the execution of his own sons; killed in single combat at the Arsian wood.

Lucretia — noblewoman raped by Tarquin's son Sextus; her suicide triggered the revolt.

Collatinus — Brutus's first colleague; Tarquin's nephew, resigned, opening the way for Publicola.

Vindicius — slave who exposed the royalist conspiracy to Publicola.

Lars Porsenna — Etruscan king who invaded to restore Tarquin; the peace involved Publicola's cession of some territory.

provocatio — right of citizen appeal against a consul's death sentence.

fasces — bundle of rods around an axe carried by lictors; lowered in assembly to signal subordination to the people.

quaestor — magistrate over the public treasury, an office Publicola created.

Valerian laws (lex libertatis) — the collective name for Publicola's republican legislation.

Important Passages

The execution of Brutus's sons, in Plutarch's careful prose: Brutus "is said not to have turned aside his face, nor allowed the least glance of pity to soften and smooth his aspect of rigor and austerity." Plutarch's gloss: an action "either divine or brutish, but neither seemed common, or the result of humanity."

The demolition of the house earned Publicola his cognomen — friend of the people. "While it was still night, sending for a number of workpeople, pulled down his house and leveled it with the ground."

Livy's contrasting emphasis: Brutus "had not been a more zealous assertor of liberty than he was afterwards its guardian." For Livy the founding belongs to Brutus; for Plutarch the institutional engineering belongs to Publicola.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Describe the sequence of events from Lucretia's suicide to the founding oath of 509 BC. Who acts at each stage?
  2. What is Plutarch's verdict on Brutus's execution of his own sons, and how does it differ from the Roman tradition's verdict?
  3. List the four Valerian laws and explain how each was designed to prevent any consul from sliding back toward kingship.
  4. Why did Publicola demolish his hilltop house, and how did the gesture earn him his cognomen?
  5. In what ways does Livy's account of the founding differ in emphasis from Plutarch's, and how does the chapter reconcile the two?

Discussion Questions

  1. Is Plutarch's "either divine or brutish" verdict a satisfactory response to Brutus's execution of his sons, or is it an evasion?
  2. Plutarch credits Publicola with saving the Republic from "death by mercy" in its first year. Is the use of severity in the founding moment a different kind of political virtue from the generosity of Solon's seisachtheia, or the same virtue applied to different problems?
  3. The chapter notes that Publicola was four-time consul — unusual in the early Republic. Does repeated officeholding compromise the very republican principle he was installing, or strengthen it?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Valerian laws — especially provocatio — are the foundation of the Roman citizen's relationship to the law for the rest of the Republic and the early Empire. Brutus the Liberator's deed will be consciously reenacted by Marcus Brutus in Chapter 11, who kills Caesar in deliberate imitation of his ancestor. The Solon-Publicola synkrisis in Section 4 will rank Publicola above Solon on almost every measure — a verdict whose reasoning the next section unpacks axis by axis.
