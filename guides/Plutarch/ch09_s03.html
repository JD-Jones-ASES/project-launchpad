Study Guide: The Life of Cicero

Plutarch's Cicero is the most fully human portrait in the Lives — brilliant, brave at intervals, vain, witty (sometimes too witty), self-promoting, deeply learned. He saves the Republic in 63 BC; is exiled for the very executions that saved it; comes back; writes; dies horribly on the road from Rome under Antony's proscription. The death scene is one of the most heartbreaking in the whole work.

Key Overview

The Life moves in seven stages. First, the Italian-equestrian origin: born 106 BC at Arpinum, an eques, a novus homo. Second, the education: leading jurists in Rome, then Athens and Rhodes among Stoics, Academics, and Epicureans — a philosophical breadth Demosthenes lacked. Third, the early legal career: the bold defense of Sextus Roscius (80 BC) against a case implicating Sulla's freedman, then a brief voluntary exile in Greece. Fourth, the cursus and the Catiline crisis: quaestor (75 BC), praetor (66 BC), consul (63 BC), and the four Catilinarian speeches that drove Catiline from Rome and produced the executions on Cato's motion. Fifth, the exile (58 BC) and the triumphant recall (57 BC) — payment for those executions ordered without the provocatio. Sixth, the years under the First Triumvirate and civil war, ending in retirement and the great philosophical writings. Seventh, the Philippics against Antony, the proscription, the death on the litter, and the display of head and hands on the Rostra.

Two reservations color Plutarch's portrait: Cicero's love of his own eloquence (he constantly praises his own speeches in his speeches), and his wit (politically hazardous; he made enemies he did not need to make). But the failures sit alongside the suppression of Catiline, the moral courage of the Philippics, and the most significant body of philosophical and political prose in Latin.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BC) — Roman orator, statesman, philosopher.
  • Arpinum — Italian town sixty miles southeast of Rome.
  • eques / novus homo — equestrian class; "new man"; first novus homo in many decades to reach the consulship.
  • Sextus Roscius of Ameria — defendant in Cicero's first major case (80 BC), against prosecution backed by Sulla's freedman Chrysogonus.
  • Catiline — frustrated aristocrat who plotted to overthrow the Republic in 63 BC.
  • Catilinarian Orations — four speeches exposing the conspiracy.
  • Cato the Younger — moved the Senate motion to execute the captured conspirators.
  • provocatio — the right of appeal Publicola had established (Ch. 4); bypassed in the executions.
  • Clodius — pushed the 58 BC bill that retroactively criminalized the executions and triggered exile.
  • Philippics — fourteen speeches against Antony after Caesar's assassination, modeled on Demosthenes.
  • Antony / Octavian / Lepidus — the Second Triumvirate that proscribed Cicero.
  • Fulvia — Antony's wife, who stabbed Cicero's tongue with a hairpin.
  • Rostra — the speaker's platform where Cicero's head and hands were displayed.

Important Passages

  • The opening of the First Catilinarian: "Quousque tandem abutere, Catilina, patientia nostra? — How long, Catiline, will you abuse our patience? How long will your madness still mock us? To what end will your unbridled audacity vaunt itself?"
  • The death: "When the murderers approached, Cicero gave orders that they should set down the litter, and stretching out his neck from the litter, asked the soldiers to make their blow clean. He was sixty-three years old. They cut off his head; and, by order of Antony, his hands also, which had written the Philippics."
  • The aftermath: "Cicero's head and hands were brought to Antony, who exposed them on the Rostra — the public speaker's platform from which Cicero had spoken so often, the place where the Philippics had been delivered. Fulvia, Antony's wife, took the severed head, drew out the tongue with a hairpin, and stabbed it."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What did being a novus homo mean for Cicero's career?
  2. How did Cicero's training abroad differ from Demosthenes's, and what later effect did it have?
  3. Reconstruct the Catiline conspiracy and Cicero's handling of it.
  4. Why did the very action that saved the Republic later send Cicero into exile?
  5. What is the deliberate echo when Cicero calls his speeches against Antony the Philippics?
  6. Why is the exposure of head and hands on the Rostra a denial of speech and writing themselves?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch's reservations about Cicero are vanity and politically hazardous wit. Character flaws or occupational features of a public speaker?
  2. Cicero acted without provocatio and was later punished for it. Is the rule of law worth following in an emergency?
  3. Plutarch finds Cicero's exile "idle." Fair reading or moralizing?
  4. The death scene is engineered for horror. Compare to Demosthenes's death — what is Plutarch's intended effect?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Life closes by pointing toward the synkrisis (Section 4): the austere serious Demosthenes, or the brilliant variable Cicero? Plutarch's seven-axis comparison leans on specific moments staged here — Cicero's wide philosophical education (Axis 1), his constant self-praise (Axis 3), his integrity as quaestor in Sicily and proconsul in Cilicia (Axes 4–5), his "idle" exile (Axis 6), and the pitiful death on the litter (Axis 7). When reading the synkrisis, return to these moments — they are the evidence Plutarch is weighing.
