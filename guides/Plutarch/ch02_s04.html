Study Guide: The Comparison (Synkrisis of Theseus and Romulus)

This section is the architectural centerpiece of the chapter. The Lives are the evidence; the synkrisis is where Plutarch — and now you — render judgment. Read Plutarch's synkrisis once for the moves, a second time for the verdicts, and a third time for the structure. It is the model your own essay should imitate.

Key Overview

The synkrisis is a brief, dense, evaluative essay closing each paired life. It is short — about a tenth the length of the two Lives combined — and it does not retell the narrative. Instead, it names incidents already in the reader's mind and weighs them on multiple axes.

Plutarch's synkrisis of Theseus and Romulus moves through six axes:

  1. Origin and the moral weight of action. Theseus acted from free will (no necessity drove him to leave peaceful Troezen); Romulus acted from necessity, to escape present servitude. Plutarch quotes Plato: Romulus "grew valiant purely out of fear." Round one to Theseus.

  2. The scope of the original deeds. Romulus's first great act was killing one king (Amulius); Theseus killed four bandits (Sciron, Sinis, Procrustes, Periphetes) — deeds done for the benefit of others, including travelers who would never know who freed the road. Round two to Theseus.

  3. The killing of the relative. Theseus's role in the death of Hippolytus was driven by passion in response to Phaedra's false complaints; Romulus killed Remus in cold political calculation over the founding. Plutarch judges Theseus has the better excuse. Round three to Theseus.

  4. The political founding. Plutarch reverses direction. Romulus started from genuinely tiny beginnings, freed the Latin people from their oppressors, and built a city from the ground up. Theseus's founding was the rearrangement of existing Attic villages — not the building of a new thing but the unification of old ones. Round four to Romulus.

  5. The treatment of women. Plutarch is severe with Theseus here. The catalog is damning: Ariadne abandoned on Naxos; Antiope the Amazon; Anaxo the Troezenian; Helen, taken when Theseus was old and Helen a child. Theseus's seizures look like personal lust repeated over a lifetime. Romulus's single seizure of the Sabine women became a political union; Romulus kept only Hersilia for himself, distributed the rest, and presided over the women's reconciliation with their husbands' new state. Plutarch's verdict: Theseus's seductions were "out of wantonness and lust," Romulus's seizure "a commendable and politic exploit to establish a society." Round five, decisively, to Romulus.

  6. The signs of divine favor. Romulus was preserved by "the special favor of the gods" from the beginning; the oracle given to Aegeus before Theseus's conception "seems to demonstrate that the birth of Theseus was not agreeable to the will of the gods." Round six (less decisive) to Romulus.

The verdict overall? Plutarch refuses a clean answer. Each axis is judged sharply, but the lives are too complex to reduce to one. By the end, however, the judgment slightly tips toward Romulus: the political founding is the largest single thing either man did, the treatment of women goes badly for Theseus, and Theseus's personal failings accumulate where Romulus's, terrible as the killing of Remus is, are fewer in number.

Major Figures and Terms

Term Meaning
Synkrisis The closing comparative essay of a paired Life; brief, dense, multi-axis, judgmental.
Axis A single dimension along which the two lives are weighed. Plutarch uses six in this synkrisis.
Aeschylus Quoted at the opening of the Life: "Whom shall I set so great a man to face? or whom oppose? who's equal to the place?" — the line Plutarch uses to introduce the pairing question.
Plato (quoted) Cited on Axis 1: Romulus "grew valiant purely out of fear."
Hersilia The one Sabine woman Romulus kept for himself; a key detail in Plutarch's defense of Romulus on Axis 5.
Hippolytus Theseus's son, killed through Phaedra's false accusations; the Theseus side of Axis 3.
Remus Romulus's twin, killed in the founding quarrel; the Romulus side of Axis 3.
The four bandits Sciron, Sinis, Procrustes, Periphetes — the road-clearing exploits weighed on Axis 2.
The four women Ariadne, Antiope, Anaxo, Helen — Theseus's catalog of seductions on Axis 5.

Important Passages

On Axis 1 (origin). Plutarch quotes Plato to land the verdict: Romulus "grew valiant purely out of fear." The man who acts from choice outranks the man who acts from necessity.

On Axis 5 (women). Plutarch's verdict is sharp: "It is to be suspected that these things were done [by Theseus] out of wantonness and lust," whereas Romulus's seizure of the Sabine women "was a commendable and politic exploit to establish a society." Notice the moral grammar — wantonness is personal, politic is communal.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. List the six axes Plutarch uses in the synkrisis, and identify the winner on each.
  2. Why does Plutarch judge Theseus's killing of Hippolytus a more excusable fault than Romulus's killing of Remus?
  3. Why does Plutarch judge Romulus the greater political founder, even though Theseus's synoecism was peaceful?
  4. What does Plutarch mean by "wantonness and lust" applied to Theseus, and how does he defend Romulus's seizure of the Sabine women?
  5. What are the four compositional rules Plutarch's synkrisis follows? Which is hardest to obey in your own writing?
  6. Why does Plutarch refuse a single overall verdict, even though he renders sharp judgments on each axis?
  7. Toward which founder does the verdict slightly tip, and why?

Discussion Questions

  1. Is it fair for Plutarch to judge Romulus's act from necessity less admirable than Theseus's act from free choice? What does this reveal about Plutarch's moral framework?
  2. Plutarch defends Romulus's seizure of the Sabine women on the grounds that it produced political union. Is consequence the right measure for an act of violence? Would the same logic exonerate other founding violences?
  3. Plutarch credits Romulus more on Axis 4 (the building of a city from nothing) than Theseus (the unifying of villages). Some readers think synoecism — peacefully uniting scattered people — is the harder political achievement. With whom do you agree?
  4. The synkrisis ends without a single verdict. Is this intellectual honesty or an avoidance of judgment? Could a strict ranking ever be appropriate for two such different lives?

Connections to Later Chapters

  • The four-move pattern of a synkrisis (axis, evidence, judgment, brevity) was introduced in Chapter 1; here it is put to its first major test in Plutarch's own practice.

  • Plutarch's reading of Romulus's founding as the greater achievement returns whenever the book asks how to weigh institutional building against political unification.
