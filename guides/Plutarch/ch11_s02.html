Study Guide: The Life of Dion

Plutarch's Life of Dion is, as the chapter introduction warns, in part a biography of Plato's political ambitions. Dion was Plato's most committed Sicilian student, and the project of his life — to turn the Syracusan tyranny into a Platonic constitutional state — was directly the project of his teacher. The Life follows that project through two generations of tyrants, three Platonic visits, ten years of exile, a successful liberation, three years of confused rule, and a murder at the hands of a man Dion had trusted. The verdict that closes the Life is, in different ways, both Plato's and Plutarch's: a good man can be undone by failures of practical judgment, and a Platonic education is not by itself the political skill it might be mistaken for.

Key Overview

The Platonic project, two generations. Dion was born around 408 BC into the Syracusan nobility. His sister's marriage to the tyrant Dionysius I gave him court access. Plato's first visit in 387 BC — Plato 39, Dion 20 — captured Dion entirely. The first attempt to influence Dionysius I failed and ended with Plato sold into slavery and ransomed by friends. Dion stayed at court and waited a generation. When Dionysius I died in 367 BC and was succeeded by his son Dionysius II, Dion urged Plato to return. The famous second visit (367–365 BC) was the formal experiment in Platonic education of a tyrant — geometric instruction, dialectical conversation, the cultivation of intellectual discipline — and the young tyrant was simply bored. The experiment ended with Dion accused of plotting, banished, his wife forced to remarry, and his estate confiscated.

The return. For ten years Dion lived in exile, mostly in Athens, attending Plato's Academy and preparing. In 357 BC, with Dionysius II away from Syracuse, he landed with about a thousand mercenaries. The city rose with him. But the next three years were inconclusive: a tactical failure to demolish the citadel fortifications, a political split between Platonic constitutionalists and democratic populists in Dion's coalition, and an austere personal style that alienated many Syracusans. By 354 BC Dion was the effective ruler of a divided city.

The murder. Callippus — an Athenian Dion had brought on the expedition and elevated to prominence — organized the killing. Plutarch's account of the assassination is one of the most moving in the Life. Dion was reading at home; the assassins struggled with him; they could not strangle him; they could not get a sword through his throat; one of them was thrown through a window and returned with a short sword from outside; with that they killed him. He was approximately 54. Plato's Seventh Letter gives the harsh verdict: Dion had trusted bad men, and good men should know better.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Hipparinus — Dion's father, one of the leading Syracusan politicians before the tyranny began.
  • Dionysius I — Tyrant of Syracuse; Dion's brother-in-law through his sister's marriage; the man who had Plato sold into slavery.
  • Dionysius II — Son of Dionysius I; perhaps thirty at his accession; the failed pupil of Plato's second visit; the man who banished Dion.
  • Philistus — Old loyalist of Dionysius I; Dion's chief opponent at the Syracusan court.
  • Callippus — Athenian whom Dion had brought from Athens, elevated in the new regime, and who organized Dion's murder.
  • The Seventh Letter — Plato's probably-genuine letter giving his own account of his Syracusan engagements and his verdict on Dion's death.
  • The 357 BC expedition — Dion's small force of roughly a thousand mercenaries, landed while Dionysius II was absent.

Important Passages

The Life of Dion has two passages that have echoed through Western political writing. The first is Plutarch's account of the assassination — the slow, almost domestic horror of an old strong man being killed in his own house by friends he had brought from Athens, the assassin thrown through the window to fetch the sword from outside. Plutarch presents the scene as the moral climax of the Life: it is one thing to fail at politics, another to be killed by the man you trusted most.

The second is Plato's verdict in the Seventh Letter, which Plutarch's account presupposes. Plato does not say that Dion was unlucky or that fortune turned against him. He says that Dion had trusted bad men, and good men should know better. The moral burden is assigned to the victim. It is one of the hardest verdicts in ancient ethical writing, and it survives in Plutarch's Life as a comment not just on Dion but on the entire Platonic enterprise of turning philosophical training into political competence.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Give Dion's dates and the city of his birth.
  2. Explain the family relationship Dion had to Dionysius I.
  3. State the year of Plato's first visit to Syracuse and the ages of the two men.
  4. How did Plato's first Syracusan stay end?
  5. Who succeeded Dionysius I, and how did Dion attempt to use the succession?
  6. List Dion's three specific grievances after his banishment.
  7. Where did Dion spend his ten years of exile, and what did he do there?
  8. State the year and size of the force with which Dion landed in Sicily.
  9. Identify Dion's tactical error in the city and its consequence.
  10. Name Dion's murderer and explain the painful detail of how the killing was carried out.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plato's verdict that Dion had trusted bad men is morally severe — it assigns to Dion himself responsibility for being deceived. Is this the right verdict, or is there an aspect of the situation Plato refuses to acknowledge?
  2. The Life shows a man whose philosophical formation made him an awkward democratic politician — austere, remote, uncomfortable with give-and-take. Does the Life suggest that Platonic political theory is structurally bad at preparing actual political leaders? Argue from the text.
  3. Dion's three concrete grievances — banishment, the loss of his wife to forced remarriage, the confiscation of his estate — are real injuries. To what degree do they morally compromise the philosophical character of his return? The synkrisis will say his motive was thereby mixed; argue whether mixed motive is in fact a defect.

Connections to Later Chapters

The Life of Dion is the first half of the chapter's pairing; section 3 follows with the Life of Brutus, and section 4 closes with the synkrisis that weighs the two men. Dion's relationship to Plato is the philosophical anchor for the entire pairing — it sets up the question (which the synkrisis will press) of whether philosophical education makes a competent political actor, or whether it produces a man whose moral seriousness becomes, in practice, a political handicap. The killing of Dion by Callippus also sets up one of the axes of the synkrisis: Dion trusted ill men; Brutus chose his conspiracy better. The next Life takes up the Roman counterpart who came from a different philosophical tradition and met a different end.
