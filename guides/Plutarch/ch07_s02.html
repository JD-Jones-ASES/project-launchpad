Study Guide: The Life of Alcibiades

Plutarch's Life of Alcibiades is one of the most psychologically vivid of the Lives. Alcibiades (c. 450 – 404 BC) was the most gifted and the most dangerous Athenian of his generation: a ward of Pericles, a student of Socrates, a man of staggering beauty, theatrical brilliance, and ungovernable ambition. He served Athens, betrayed Athens, was recalled, fell again, and died in foreign exile the same year his city surrendered. Plutarch's portrait is impossible to dismiss and impossible fully to admire.

Key Overview

Beauty, brilliance, ungovernable energy. Plutarch opens with physical description. Alcibiades's beauty was famous across the Mediterranean and persisted from boyhood into old age; his slight lisp became a fashionable affectation; his energy was limitless. Among the many strong passions of his character, ambition and the desire of superiority were the most prevailing. Two boyhood stories crystallize the man. As a child he bit a wrestling opponent's hand; reproached for biting "like a woman," he answered "no, like a lion." As a boy at dice in the street, when a loaded cart bore down on his throw, he threw himself in its path and dared the driver to drive on. The lion-bite and the cart-incident are Plutarchan character-revealing moments: Alcibiades will not be made to lose, and will, if necessary, stake his life on a refusal.

Socrates. The brightest light in the Life is Alcibiades's friendship with Socrates. At Potidaea (432 BC) the wounded Alcibiades was saved by Socrates, who stepped over him and defended him with his shield. At Delium (424 BC) the situation reversed: Alcibiades, on horseback, stayed with Socrates as the Athenians fled and protected him from pursuit. Each man, in his own crisis, was defended by the other.

Wealth, flamboyance, and the Olympic chariots. Alcibiades was extravagant. He kept seven chariot teams at the Olympic games — an unprecedented number — and had Euripides write him a victory ode. He cut off the tail of his own famously beautiful dog (worth seventy minae) and explained: "I wished the Athenians to talk about this, that they might not say something worse of me." He boxed the ear of Hipponicus on a dare; he grabbed his wife Hipparete from the archon's court when she tried to file for divorce and carried her home through the marketplace.

The Sicilian Expedition and the betrayal. In 415 BC he persuaded Athens to launch its largest expedition ever — 134 triremes, 5,100 hoplites, 30,000 men — against Syracuse. The night before sailing, someone mutilated the Herms (statues of Hermes at Athenian doorways). His enemies accused him of the sacrilege and of the profanation of the Eleusinian Mysteries. He demanded an immediate trial; they delayed. A recall summons was sent after the fleet. He disappeared, was sentenced to death in absentia, and turned up in Sparta. In Sparta he exercised in the gymnasium, ate the black broth, wore the rough cloak — and gave the three strategic gifts (send Gylippus to Syracuse, fortify Decelea, revolt Athens's allies) that destroyed Athenian power. Then he seduced the wife of King Agis II and had to flee to Persia.

Recall, victory, second fall. When the Athenian oligarchic revolution of 411 BC failed, the new democratic government recalled him to command the fleet at Samos. He won a series of naval victories that nearly turned the war around. In 407 BC he briefly returned to Athens, with even the religious charges dropped, and led a procession to Eleusis restoring the rite the Spartan occupation of Decelea had interrupted. Then a subordinate lost a sea battle near Notium while Alcibiades was absent, and the Athenians removed him. He retired to Thrace.

The end. After the final Athenian defeat at Aegospotami (405 BC) he fled to Phrygia. The Spartans (now dominant) demanded his death; Lysander conspired with the Persians; his house was set on fire one night; he emerged with sword in hand and was killed by arrows. He died in 404 BC, the same year Athens surrendered. The two endings are entangled.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Pericles — Alcibiades's guardian after Cleinias's death at Coronea.
  • Socrates — the philosopher whose battlefield bond with Alcibiades is the brightest light in the Life.
  • Hipparete — Alcibiades's wife, daughter of Hipponicus; carried home from the archon's court when she tried to divorce him.
  • The Herms — statues of Hermes mutilated the night before the fleet sailed; the religious crisis used to convict Alcibiades.
  • Eleusinian Mysteries — the sacred rite alleged to have been profaned at private parties; second charge against Alcibiades.
  • Decelea — Attic fort fortified permanently on Alcibiades's advice; bled Athens by interrupting agriculture and silver mining.
  • Tisaphernes — Persian satrap to whom Alcibiades attached himself after fleeing Sparta.
  • Notium — naval engagement lost by a subordinate, used by Athens as the pretext for removing Alcibiades from command.
  • Aegospotami (405 BC) — the final Athenian naval defeat; collapse of Athenian power.
  • Lysander — Spartan general who conspired with the Persians for Alcibiades's death.

Important Passages

On Alcibiades's character: "His conduct displayed many great inconsistencies and variations… among the many strong passions of his real character, the one most prevailing of all was his ambition and desire of superiority."

On the wrestling-bite: Pressed in wrestling, he bit his opponent's hand. Reproached — "You bite, Alcibiades, like a woman" — he replied, "No, like a lion."

On the cart: Playing at dice in the street, when a loaded cart was driven at his throw, he threw himself face down in its path and "bade the carter pass on now if he would."

On the dog's tail: "I wished the Athenians to talk about this, that they might not say something worse of me."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Identify the two grand passions Plutarch names in his opening description of Alcibiades.
  2. Recount the wrestling-bite story and state what it reveals about the man.
  3. Describe each of the two battlefield episodes between Alcibiades and Socrates.
  4. What was unusual about Alcibiades's appearance at the Olympic games?
  5. List two flamboyant or cruel acts Plutarch records and explain the manipulation each represents.
  6. What two religious charges were used to drive Alcibiades into exile in 415 BC?
  7. State the three pieces of strategic advice he gave the Spartans and the effect of each.
  8. Why was he forced to flee from Sparta?
  9. What happened at Notium, and what political consequence followed for Alcibiades?
  10. Describe the manner of his death and the year of Athens's surrender.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch's portrait is built around character-revealing childhood incidents. Are such anecdotes a reliable instrument of biography, or are they too easily shaped to fit a thesis? Argue from the wrestling-bite and the cart.
  2. The chapter says the relationship with Socrates is the brightest light in Alcibiades's Life. Is one such relationship enough to redeem a public career, or is it a private exception that the politics swamp?
  3. Plutarch's verdict is "tempered" — not hero, not monster. What does that intermediate judgment do that a clean acquittal or condemnation cannot? When is tempered judgment the right verdict in political biography?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Sicilian Expedition picks up the imperial Athens of Chapter 6 (Pericles) and dramatizes its overreach. The friendship with Socrates points forward to Plutarch's broader interest in philosophical formation — Alcibiades is Plutarch's strongest case for the limits of moral instruction on a brilliant nature. The synkrisis in Topic 7.4 will weigh his style of contempt for the multitude against Coriolanus's open haughtiness, and Plutarch's verdict there is one of the surprises of the book.
