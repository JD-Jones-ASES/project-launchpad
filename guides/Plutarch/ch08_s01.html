Study Guide: Background — Conquest and Monarchy

Alexander of Macedon and Julius Caesar of Rome are the two most famous men in Western history. Each, in his own world, was a conqueror who shattered the political form he inherited; each turned a republic or its functional equivalent into a personal empire; each died short of fifty. Their pair is Plutarch's most famous and most demanding, and the closing comparison — the synkrisis — is the most painfully lost text in the corpus.

Key Overview

  • The pair. Alexander (356-323 BC) inherited the throne of Macedon at twenty, led an army from Greece across Asia Minor, Egypt, and Mesopotamia to the Indus, founded Greek-speaking cities, and died at thirty-two in Babylon; his empire fell to pieces among his generals within a few years. Caesar (100-44 BC), a patrician of modest standing, conquered Gaul in eight years, won a civil war in three, became dictator perpetuo, and was assassinated on the Ides of March.
  • The theme. Conquest and the slide toward monarchy. Alexander destroyed Persian rule and Greek polis freedom in the same campaign; Caesar destroyed the Roman Republic. Each was, in his own way, the end of a particular political form.
  • Plutarch's question. What kind of man makes such an end? Are the conquerors morally distinguishable from the systems they break? What balance of personal virtue, talent, ruthlessness, and accident produces a world-historical figure?
  • The lost synkrisis. Second in this book (the first was Themistocles-Camillus in Chapter 5). The manuscript tradition preserves the pair but not the comparison. The section calls this the most painful single loss in the corpus — two of the most famous men in Western history, both conquerors, both broke their cities' institutions, both died young, and we do not know what Plutarch's verdict was. The chapter's writesynkrisis exercise is therefore the student's own.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Alexander the Great (356-323 BC) — King of Macedon at twenty; conqueror of the Persian Empire; dead at thirty-two of fever in Babylon.
  • Julius Caesar (100-44 BC) — Roman patrician, conqueror of Gaul, victor over Pompey, dictator perpetuo, assassinated on the Ides of March.
  • The Rubicon — Small Italian river marking the boundary between Caesar's province and Italy proper; crossing it in arms was an act of civil war.
  • Synkrisis (lost) — Plutarch's closing comparative essay on Alexander and Caesar; the most painful manuscript loss in the corpus.
  • The four lost synkriseis — Themistocles-Camillus, Pyrrhus-Marius, Alexander-Caesar, Phocion-Cato the Younger.
  • Christian reception. Alexander as a model of princely virtue (the Alexander Romance); Caesar more ambivalent — Augustine treated the Rubicon as overweening ambition, Dante placed Brutus and Cassius in the lowest circle of hell.
  • Founder reception. Adams admired Cicero and thought Caesar contemptible; Hamilton found in Caesar a model of executive greatness.

Important Passages

  • Each man broke the political institutions of his society to make himself supreme. — The chapter's thesis on conquest and monarchy.
  • What kind of man makes such an end? — Plutarch's moral question.
  • The synkrisis of Alexander and Caesar is the most painful of the lost synkriseis. — The framing of the loss.
  • This Chapter's synkrisis exercise is fully your own. — The handover from author to reader.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What did Alexander and Caesar each accomplish, in outline?
  2. State the chapter's theme and how it applies to each man.
  3. Which pair was the first lost synkrisis in this book, and which is the second?
  4. Why does the section call the Alexander-Caesar loss the most painful in the corpus?
  5. What does the section say to watch for at the opening of the Life of Alexander?
  6. Name two early displays of character the section flags — one for each man.
  7. What decisive geographic crossings does the section name?
  8. How do Alexander's and Caesar's uses of clemency differ in style?
  9. How does the section describe the two death scenes?
  10. How does the Christian moral imagination's reception of Alexander differ from its reception of Caesar?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch asks whether conquerors are morally distinguishable from the systems they break. Stake out a position and defend it with one detail from each Life.
  2. Adams and Hamilton both knew their Plutarch and reached opposite verdicts on Caesar. What in the Life of Caesar might support each reading?
  3. Why does the methodological discipline (biography, not history) matter especially for a man whose public deeds were so enormous?
  4. The chapter omits Second Witnesses. Does the omission strengthen or weaken Plutarch's portrait of either man?
  5. Does the loss of the synkrisis make the chapter freer (no verdict to argue against) or harder (no model to imitate)?
