Study Guide: Plutarch and the American Founders

The men who founded the American Republic read Plutarch in childhood and quoted him through their lives. The Parallel Lives were one of the small set of books that shaped the political imagination of the founding generation — alongside Cicero, the Bible, Tacitus, and a handful of British constitutional writers. This section traces the schoolbook, the favorite figures, and the four ways the Founders absorbed and used Plutarch.

Key Overview

In the eighteenth century, Plutarch was a fixed item of the Latin-school reading list. Boys read him in translation — usually North's 1579 version or the Langhorne brothers' more recent 1770 translation — alongside Cicero and Tacitus. The plain prose was thought suitable for young readers; the moral seriousness was thought appropriate for forming public character.

Different Founders had different favorites. John Adams filled his diary with paired Plutarchan comparisons (Washington with Caesar, Hamilton with Alcibiades, Adams himself with Cicero). Alexander Hamilton preferred Caesar — the brilliant outsider rising to executive power — and his enemies accused him of wanting to be Caesar; Hamilton thought the comparison flattering. Patrick Henry's "Give me liberty or give me death" was a Brutus citation, drawing on the moral type of Cato the Younger at Utica or Brutus at Philippi. Nathan Hale's "I only regret that I have but one life to lose" paraphrased Addison's Cato, whose central character is the Plutarchan Cato the Younger.

The chapter identifies four legacies the Founders took from Plutarch. First, the figure of the citizen-statesman: the man of private virtue who takes public office reluctantly and retires when his term is done. Washington's refusal of a third term and his return to Mount Vernon were Plutarchan moves recognized as such by his contemporaries. Second, the warning against the brilliant ambitious man — Plutarch's Alcibiades and Caesar were cautionary examples. Third, the discipline of comparison: the synkrisis form shaped how the Founders thought, and the Federalist Papers are Plutarchan in spirit, with Madison's Federalist 10 comparing ancient Sparta, ancient Rome, and modern Britain. Fourth, the legitimation of revolutionary action: the Plutarchan Brutus killing the tyrant Caesar was the precedent for deposing a king.

The influence continued past the founding. Emerson wrote one of the great English essays on Plutarch in Representative Men (1850). Lincoln read the Bible, Shakespeare, and Plutarch — the same three books, in roughly the same order, that Adams and Jefferson had read.

Major Figures and Terms

  • John Adams: diarist of Plutarchan comparisons; wrote in 1781 that Plutarch was "in the hands of every American boy of sense."
  • Alexander Hamilton: preferred Caesar; accused by enemies of wanting to be Caesar.
  • Patrick Henry: "Give me liberty or give me death" — a Brutus citation.
  • Nathan Hale: paraphrased Addison's Cato, itself drawn from the Plutarchan Cato.
  • George Washington: imitated the Plutarchan citizen-statesman by refusing a third term and returning to Mount Vernon.
  • James Madison: wrote Federalist 10, a Plutarchan exercise in republican comparison.
  • Langhorne translation (1770): the more modern English version used alongside North in eighteenth-century classrooms.

Important Passages

  • "You will find Plutarch in the hands of every American boy of sense." — Adams to John Quincy, 1781.
  • "His [Hamilton's] enemies (Jefferson especially) accused him of wanting to be Caesar. Hamilton himself thought the comparison flattering."
  • "The political rhetoric of the American Revolution is, to a striking extent, Plutarchan rhetoric."
  • "Washington consciously imitated [the citizen-statesman model]. His refusal to take a third term as president, his return to Mount Vernon, his refusal to be made king — all are Plutarchan moves."
  • "The case for the American Revolution, in much of its contemporary literature, was built on Plutarchan precedent."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Which two English translations of Plutarch were standard in eighteenth-century classrooms?
  2. List the three Plutarchan figures John Adams used most often as comparisons in his diary.
  3. Why did Hamilton's enemies (especially Jefferson) accuse him of wanting to be Caesar, and how did Hamilton respond?
  4. What four legacies does the chapter say the Founders took from Plutarch?
  5. Name the three books Lincoln read most, and explain why the order matters.

Discussion Questions

  1. Adams worried about Hamilton; Jefferson worried about Hamilton; Hamilton's friends worried about Aaron Burr. Why was the Plutarchan vocabulary so useful for these worries?
  2. Why might the "discipline of comparison" be especially well suited to designing a constitutional republic? Compare the synkrisis form to the structure of Federalist 10.
  3. Washington's refusal of a third term is described as a "Plutarchan move." What would have been the alternative — and what would the alternative have signaled?
  4. The chapter says the modern American republic does not consciously read Plutarch much anymore, yet much of its political imagination still does. Try to identify one current political ritual or argument that has Plutarchan roots.

Connections to Later Chapters
