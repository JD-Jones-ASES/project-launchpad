Study Guide: Background — The Tyrannicide

The eleventh and last of Plutarch's ten pairings opens with a pair of men whose project is the same and whose moral weight is exact: each killed a tyrant. The chapter is about tyrannicide — the deliberate, justified killing of a sovereign who has betrayed the office — as both a political act and a personal practice. Plutarch is sympathetic to Dion of Syracuse and to Marcus Brutus of Rome, but the chapter does not flinch from the costs of what they did, nor from the question of whether what they did achieved what they meant to achieve. This is the closing pair of the book, and the synkrisis that closes it is one of the most considered moral verdicts in the Lives.

Key Overview

Two tyrannicides, two contexts. Dion (c. 408–354 BC) was a Sicilian Greek nobleman, brother-in-law of the tyrant Dionysius I, student of Plato, and the man who organized an expedition from Greece, landed at Syracuse in 357 BC, drove out Dionysius II, and was killed by his own subordinates four years later. The Syracusan Platonic project did not survive him. Marcus Brutus (85–42 BC) was a Roman senator who, on the Ides of March in 44 BC, led the conspiracy that killed Julius Caesar in the Theater of Pompey. The Republic the conspirators hoped to restore never returned; Brutus and Cassius were defeated at Philippi in 42 BC and both committed suicide. The imperial system Caesar had begun continued in modified form under his great-nephew Augustus.

The instructive asymmetry of motive. Plutarch builds the chapter on a careful contrast. Dion had personal grievances against Dionysius II — banishment, the forced remarriage of his wife, the confiscation of his estate. His act was partly revenge as well as principle. Brutus had been pardoned by Caesar after Pharsalus, elevated by Caesar to high office, and treated by Caesar as a personal friend. Brutus had no personal grievance. The synkrisis will weigh this difference carefully — and will find, on the question of unmixed motive, decisively for Brutus.

The genealogical inheritance. Marcus Brutus's name was not a coincidence. He was a descendant of the Lucius Junius Brutus of Chapter 4 — the man who had expelled the Tarquins and founded the Roman Republic in 509 BC. The family kept a bronze statue of the first Brutus in their home, and the second Brutus took the inherited identity seriously. The pairing with Dion thus closes a thematic arc that runs back to Chapter 4: the man who would kill a king to free his city.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Tyrannicide — The deliberate, justified killing of a sovereign on the grounds that the sovereign has betrayed the office. The chapter's announced theme.
  • Dion of Syracuse (c. 408–354 BC) — Brother-in-law of Dionysius I; Plato's most committed Sicilian student; leader of the 357 BC liberation of Syracuse.
  • Dionysius I and Dionysius II — Father and son tyrants of Syracuse; the philosophical project Dion ran failed under both.
  • Marcus Junius Brutus (85–42 BC) — Roman senator, descendant of Lucius Junius Brutus, leader of the Ides of March conspiracy.
  • Cassius — Brutus's brother-in-law and co-organizer of the conspiracy.
  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's closing comparative essay; the synkrisis of Dion and Brutus is the tenth and last in the book.
  • Plato's Seventh Letter — The primary additional source for Dion; Plato's own account of his Syracusan engagements.
  • The bronze statue of the first Brutus — Kept in the family home; the moral framework of Marcus Brutus's political career.

Important Passages

The introductory section quotes very little directly. What it foregrounds is the reception history. Dante's Inferno places Brutus and Cassius in the lowest circle of Hell — chewed in Satan's three mouths alongside Judas — on the political-theological reading that Caesarian imperium was providentially established and that to kill Caesar was to betray the order of Providence. The Renaissance humanist tradition reversed this: Shakespeare's Julius Caesar, drawing directly on Plutarch through North's English translation, calls Brutus "the noblest Roman of them all." The American Founders read Brutus enthusiastically as a model republican — Patrick Henry's "Give me liberty or give me death" is identified in the chapter as a Brutus citation.

The chapter is careful to note that neither reading is exactly Plutarch's own. Plutarch's verdict is more measured: he admires both the deed and the man without quite endorsing the political theory that the deed requires.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. State the chapter's announced theme in one sentence.
  2. Identify the two tyrannicides by name, dates, and city.
  3. Explain the genealogical link between Marcus Brutus and the first Brutus of Chapter 4.
  4. List Dion's three personal grievances against Dionysius II.
  5. What office had Caesar promised Brutus for 44 BC?
  6. Where in Dante's poem do Brutus and Cassius appear, and who is chewed alongside them?
  7. Whose translation of Plutarch did Shakespeare use for Julius Caesar?
  8. What text is the primary additional ancient source for Dion?
  9. Name the four chief ancient sources for Brutus, besides Plutarch.
  10. State the question Plutarch's synkrisis closes with.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch is careful to praise Brutus's "unmixed" motive over Dion's mixed one. Is the purer motive really the morally superior one? Or does Dion's personal grievance ground his action in something more concretely real than abstract principle?
  2. Dante condemns the tyrannicides; Shakespeare and the American Founders celebrate them. Both readings draw on Plutarch. What in Plutarch's own portrait makes both readings textually defensible?
  3. Tyrannicide is justified, in the chapter's framing, by the tyrant's betrayal of office. Who is competent to judge that betrayal has occurred? Plutarch does not answer this question, but the chapter's silence on it is itself instructive — argue what the silence implies.

Connections to Later Chapters

This is the last of Plutarch's ten pairings, but the chapter explicitly forecasts a return to its reception history in Chapter 12. The synkrisis form that closes Chapter 11 is the tenth synkrisis in the book; the writing prompt at the chapter's end asks the reader to compose a final synkrisis of their own and place it next to the Theseus–Romulus synkrisis written for Chapter 2 — the contrast between the two essays is the curricular point of the whole spine exercise. The pairing also looks back: Marcus Brutus's deliberate imitation of his ancestor closes a circuit that the founding scenes of Chapter 4 had opened. The book ends on the question of whether the political form Brutus's ancestor founded is one that Brutus's deed could in fact restore.
