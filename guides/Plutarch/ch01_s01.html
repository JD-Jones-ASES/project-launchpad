Study Guide: Who Was Plutarch?

This opening section introduces the man behind the Parallel Lives and lays out the moral conception of biography that will govern the rest of the book. Plutarch was born around 46 AD in Chaeronea, a small Greek town in Boeotia, and died in his mid-seventies around 120 AD. The seventy-some years between those dates frame everything he wrote — and they were lived in a world that was Greek by language and culture but Roman by political reality. Knowing who Plutarch was, where he stood between two civilizations, and why he chose biography as his form is the first key to reading him well.

Key Overview

  • A Greek under Roman rule. Greece had been a Roman province for almost two hundred years by the time Plutarch was born. He was a Roman citizen (Mestrius Plutarchus, after his patron Lucius Mestrius Florus) who lived under eight emperors from Nero to Hadrian, but his cultural identity was Greek.
  • A Platonist by training. He studied at the Academy in Athens under the philosopher Ammonius. He counted himself a follower of Plato, respecting Aristotle and sometimes quarreling with the Stoics.
  • A priest of Apollo. The center of his life was Delphi, where he served for roughly thirty years as one of the two senior priests at Apollo's oracle.
  • A prolific writer. His surviving works fill fifteen modern volumes. The two great projects are the Parallel Lives — forty-six biographies in twenty-three pairs, each closed by a synkrisis — and the Moralia, a vast collection of essays on ethical and other topics.
  • A moralist, not a historian. Plutarch writes biography for the moral education of writer and reader. His famous claim in the Life of Alexander is: "I am writing biography, not history."

Major Figures and Terms

  • Plutarch (Ploutarchos), c. 46–c. 120 AD: Greek biographer and moral philosopher. Born and died in Chaeronea, Boeotia. Studied at the Academy in Athens under Ammonius. Priest of Apollo at Delphi for roughly thirty years. Married Timoxena; they had five children.
  • Lucius Mestrius Florus: Roman senator and Plutarch's Roman patron. Plutarch took his patron's family name when he received Roman citizenship.
  • Ammonius: The Platonist teacher under whom Plutarch studied in Athens.
  • Moralia: Plutarch's collection of essays and dialogues; the title means "things relating to morals" or "ethical works."
  • Parallel Lives: Forty-six biographies arranged in twenty-three pairs (one Greek, one Roman), each closed by a comparative essay (synkrisis).
  • Synkrisis (Greek, "comparison"): The brief evaluative essay that closes each pairing of Lives, judging which life is greater on which axis.
  • Eusebius of Caesarea: The first great Christian historian; he read Plutarch carefully and learned from him how to write the biography of a holy man.

Important Passages

The preface to the Life of Aemilius Paulus is Plutarch's clearest statement of why he writes biography. He says the virtues of great men serve him "as a sort of looking glass, in which I may see how to adjust and adorn my own life." He compares each biography to "daily living and associating together" with a famous guest. The mirror image is the key: Plutarch reads great lives to learn how to live his own, and he writes them so that readers will do the same.

The opening of the Life of Alexander contains the most important sentence in the whole Parallel Lives: "I am writing biography, not history; and the most glorious exploits do not always furnish us with the clearest discoveries of virtue or vice in men; sometimes a matter of less moment, an expression or a jest, informs us better of their characters and inclinations than the most famous sieges, the greatest armaments, or the bloodiest battles whatsoever." This is a methodological claim. Battles and treaties show the public man; jokes, asides, and small acts of mercy or cruelty reveal the real one. The discipline the section asks you to bring to every Life that follows is to read for these small revealing moments.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Where was Plutarch born, and what was the political condition of Greece during his lifetime?
  2. What philosophical school did Plutarch identify with, and where did he study under Ammonius?
  3. What office did Plutarch hold at Delphi, and for how long?
  4. What are Plutarch's two great surviving works, and what does the title "Moralia" mean?
  5. According to the preface to the Life of Aemilius Paulus, why does Plutarch say he writes biographies?
  6. In the Life of Alexander, how does Plutarch distinguish biography from history?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch says the great public moments often show what someone wanted others to see, while small moments show what the person was. Test this on a public figure you know. What is the "small moment" you'd cite to characterize them, and why does it cut deeper than the famous one?
  2. The text calls Plutarch's moral seriousness a "deep affinity" with the later Christian biographical tradition, even though Plutarch was a pagan who never met a Christian. What do you think made his pagan method portable into a Christian context?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Plutarchan move announced here — watching for the small revealing moment — is the discipline every later chapter trains. The synkrisis you will read in Chapter 2 (Theseus and Romulus) and write yourself for the lost-comparison pairings (Chapters 5 and 8) is the natural extension of biography-as-mirror: holding two lives in your hand and saying which is heavier.
