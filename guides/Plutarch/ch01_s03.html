Study Guide: The Greek and Roman Worlds

To read Plutarch you need a mental scaffold — not a chronology to memorize but a frame to refer back to. This section supplies that frame. Plutarch's Lives cover almost a thousand years of Greek and Roman history, and the political vocabulary, geography, and literary culture of the two civilizations are different enough that a few minutes of orientation now saves much confusion later. The legendary material in the early Lives is not a defect — it is precisely where Plutarch trains you to weigh competing versions of a story and become a fellow inquirer rather than a passive recipient.

Key Overview

  • Two timelines run in parallel. Greek and Roman careers rarely overlap. Pairings are by kind of life (founder, lawgiver, orator), not by date.
  • Roughly one thousand years. From legendary founders (Theseus, Romulus, 13th–8th century BC) to the late Republic (Cicero, Brutus, Antony, first century BC).
  • Two political vocabularies. Greek (polis, demos, archon, boule, ekklesia, ephor, tyrannos) and Roman (res publica, consul, Senate, plebeian, tribune, dictator, imperium) are different worlds.
  • A few places to fix. Athens, Sparta, Macedon, Rome, Syracuse — Plutarch tells you where things happen as they happen, but a mental map helps.
  • Two literary cultures. Greek literature was already a millennium old before Plutarch's first Greek subject; Latin literature is comparatively young. Legendary layers are therefore thicker on both sides for the early Lives.
  • Weighing versions is the skill. Plutarch trades in "some say ... others say ..." formulas. Learning to weigh competing accounts is one of the chief skills this book trains.

Major Figures and Terms

Greek vocabulary:

  • polis (city-state): Basic unit of Greek political life — urban center plus farmland.
  • demos (the people): Body of male citizens collectively.
  • archon: Chief magistrate in Athens. Nine archons annually after Solon; the eponymous archon named the year.
  • boule (council): Administrative council — 400 under Solon, 500 under Cleisthenes.
  • ekklesia (assembly): Voting assembly of all male citizens at Athens; final authority on laws, war, peace.
  • ephor: One of five chief magistrates in Sparta; could overrule the kings.
  • tyrannos: Not "cruel ruler" by default — a man who seized power outside the legal constitution.

Roman vocabulary:

  • res publica (the public matter): Roman name for the state as the common affair of all citizens; source of "republic."
  • consul: Chief executive magistrate; two elected annually, each checking the other.
  • Senate: Standing council of ex-magistrates; its auctoritas (earned moral standing) guided Roman policy.
  • plebeian: Ordinary citizen, not a patrician — the central social division of the early Republic.
  • tribune of the plebs: Magistrate protecting plebeian interests; could veto any other magistrate's act.
  • dictator: Emergency magistrate with supreme authority for six months. Legal — Camillus held it five times.
  • imperium: Legal power of command, especially military. Consuls, praetors, and dictators held it; tribunes did not.

Places: Athens (Attica, central Greece, democracy from c. 508 BC); Sparta (Laconia, southern Peloponnese); Macedon (north of Greece proper, birthplace of Philip and Alexander); Rome (the Tiber, central Italy); Syracuse (east coast of Sicily, setting for Dion).

Important Passages

The pairing logic note at the head of the section says the first thing to notice is that Plutarch's Greek and Roman careers do not generally overlap. Theseus and Romulus share a legendary horizon; Pericles and Fabius, though paired, lived two centuries apart. Pairing is about kind of life, not chronology — a structural point that recurs in every chapter. The student who tries to read the pairings as chronologically synchronous misses the comparative game.

The trademark phrase of Plutarch's method appears at the close of this section: "some say ... others say ..." When Plutarch reports a story whose evidence is thin — most often in the early, legendary Lives — he reports rival versions and weighs them out loud. The text says this is "one of the great pleasures of reading him: he treats you as a fellow inquirer, not a passive recipient." The discipline this trains — weighing rival accounts of one event — is one of the most valuable transferable skills the book develops, and it carries directly into how you will write your own synkriseis.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is the difference between the Greek terms polis, demos, and ekklesia?
  2. What is a Roman tribune of the plebs, and what powers did the office carry?
  3. Define imperium. Which Roman magistrates held it, and which did not?
  4. What is res publica, and what English word derives from it?
  5. Why does the text say the legendary layers are thicker around early Lives (Theseus, Romulus) than around later ones (Pericles, Fabius)?
  6. What is the Plutarchan formula "some say ... others say ..." doing in the text, and what reading skill does it train?

Discussion Questions

  1. The Greek tyrannos and the Roman dictator are both exceptional offices — but one is by definition outside the constitution, the other inside it. What does that difference say about how the two cultures imagined emergency power?
  2. The Roman Senate had no power to pass laws, yet the text calls its decline "the political tragedy" of Plutarch's late-republican Lives. How can the loss of an advisory body be a tragedy? What kind of authority is auctoritas?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Greek vocabulary recurs immediately in Chapter 2 (Theseus and Romulus as founders) and in the Spartan Lives of Chapters 3 (Lycurgus and Numa) and beyond. The Roman vocabulary becomes central in Chapter 4 (Brutus the first consul), in the dictatorial career of Camillus (Chapter 5), and in the late-republican collapse traced through Cicero, Pompey, and Caesar in the second half of the book. Carry this scaffold forward — every later chapter assumes it.
