Study Guide: Background — Reform from Within

Chapter 4 of the Plutarch pair introduces a new type of political founder. The lawgivers of Chapter 3 — Lycurgus at Sparta and Numa at Rome — designed their cities from outside the existing order, taking power from a position of crisis or vacancy. Solon of Athens and Publius Valerius Publicola of Rome did something different. They were already prominent men in their cities, members of the existing aristocracy, and they reformed by persuading their peers rather than by displacing them. Plutarch wants us to read their parallel projects as one historical movement across two cities and a century: the moment when sovereignty relocated from a single ruler to a set of laws and offices any qualified citizen could hold. That move is what we still call republicanism.

Key Overview

The chapter establishes four organizing themes the reader should watch for across the two Lives.

First, the refusal of one-man rule. Both Solon and Publicola were explicitly offered the chance to make themselves sole rulers. Both refused. Solon's image — that tyranny is "a very fair spot, but it has no way down from it" — captures the irreversibility that made absolute power, in his view, the wrong solution.

Second, the legal scaffolding. Each man built institutions designed to outlast him: courts of appeal, annual magistracies, councils that prepared legislation, mechanisms that gave the ordinary citizen a voice.

Third, the discipline of leaving. Solon, once his laws were enacted, asked to travel for ten years so the Athenians could not pressure him for reinterpretations. Publicola, accused of monarchic ambition because his house overlooked the forum, had it demolished overnight. Both men understood that the public office is not the man.

Fourth, the figure of Brutus the Liberator. The Life of Publicola is also our principal source on Lucius Junius Brutus — the founder of the Roman Republic in the strict sense. He should not be confused with the Marcus Brutus of Chapter 11, the assassin of Caesar.

Major Figures and Terms

Solon — Athenian poet, merchant, statesman; archon in 594 BC; one of the Seven Sages of archaic Greece.

Publius Valerius Publicola — Roman patrician aristocrat, leading conspirator against Tarquin, four-time consul of the early Republic.

Lucius Junius Brutus — kinsman of the Tarquins who led the 509 BC revolt; first consul; ancestor of (not identical with) the Marcus Brutus of Chapter 11.

Tarquin the Proud — last Roman king; expelled after his son Sextus raped Lucretia.

archon — chief magistrate of Athens; nine annual archons after Solon, the eponymous archon giving his name to the year.

seisachtheia — "the shaking-off-of-burdens"; Solon's debt cancellation and abolition of debt-bondage.

Pentacosiomedimni, Hippeis, Zeugitae, Thetes — the four Solonian property classes, graded by annual yield of land.

Areopagus — ancient Athenian council of former archons; under Solon, the guardian of the laws.

consul, lictor, fasces, provocatio, quaestor — the institutional vocabulary of the early Roman Republic.

Important Passages

Solon's description of Athens before the reforms, quoted in this section: "the city seemed to be in a truly dangerous condition, and no other means for freeing it from disturbances and settling it, to be possible but a despotic power." This is the precise word — despotic — that Solon refused.

Plutarch's framing of the political style: the established men, "when they could have closed ranks, opened the door." This sentence is worth memorizing as a summary of the entire chapter.

Livy's framing of Brutus: "the consular authority was made annual, and the origin of liberty you may date from this period." The contrast with Plutarch's emphasis on Publicola as institutional architect is the central historiographical question of the Roman side.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What does Plutarch mean by "reform from within," and how does the pattern differ from the lawgivers of Chapter 3?
  2. Why was Solon, specifically, the man both factions in Athens could agree on?
  3. What is the difference between the two Brutuses, and why does the chapter raise the warning?
  4. Identify the four narrative watchpoints the chapter names for the reader.
  5. Why is the Greek side of this chapter "unaccompanied by a major surviving witness," and what do we have instead?

Discussion Questions

  1. Is "reform from within" inherently more conservative than reform from outside, or can it be more radical because the established men carry their peers with them?
  2. Solon and Publicola are separated by 85 years and a sea. Is it useful to read them as one political moment, as Plutarch does, or does the synthesis paper over real differences?
  3. Plutarch and Livy give different weight to Brutus and Publicola in the Roman founding. Which historian has the better instinct, and why?

Connections to Later Chapters

The republican refusal of one-man rule will be tested by Themistocles, Pericles, and finally Caesar. The figure of Brutus the Liberator returns in Chapter 11, where Marcus Brutus consciously reenacts his ancestor's deed by killing Caesar. The Solonian distinction between property classes will be the scaffold for every later Athenian reform, including the popular courts that Pericles will open in Chapter 6.
