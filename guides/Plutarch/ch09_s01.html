Study Guide: Background — Words as Political Action

Chapter 9 is a deliberate hinge. In Chapter 8 we read two men who broke free political systems by the sword. In Chapter 9 we read two men who tried to save them with the spoken word. Demosthenes and Cicero face the structural problem at the heart of any constitutional regime in crisis: when the man with the army arrives, what good is talk?

Key Overview

The chapter's announced theme is the orator against tyranny: the man whose only weapon is his voice, who faces a political opponent with armies and treasure, and who must use speech alone to save his city. The orator works in the assembly and the court; he persuades or he loses; he cannot, like a general, command obedience. He is also, structurally, a critic of power. Demosthenes attacks Philip of Macedon; Cicero attacks Catiline, then (cautiously) Caesar, then (intemperately) Mark Antony. The orator's natural enemy is the man with the army, and when the words fail, the orator usually dies.

What makes the pair fit is a shared political shape. A free city (Athens, then Rome) is being threatened by a great-man political-military figure (Philip, then Antony) who would end its constitutional freedom. The orator is the principal civic voice resisting the rise of personal rule, and the orator's speeches are the principal civic record of the resistance. Both men lose in immediate political terms; both leave behind speeches read continuously ever since. The tension between immediate political failure and long-term cultural success is a recurring Plutarchan concern.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Demosthenes (384–322 BC) — the greatest orator in the Greek tradition; spent his career from about 350 BC onward warning Athens about Philip of Macedon. The Philippics are his most celebrated speeches. Died in 322 BC, taking poison to escape arrest by the Macedonians.
  • Cicero (106–43 BC) — the greatest Roman orator and a central political figure of the late Republic. Exposed and suppressed the Catiline conspiracy as consul in 63 BC; opposed Caesar cautiously and Antony intemperately. Died in 43 BC, proscribed by Antony, killed on a litter while fleeing toward the coast.
  • Philip of Macedon — father of Alexander (Ch. 8). The structural opponent of Demosthenes.
  • Mark Antony — Caesar's lieutenant, then proscribing triumvir. The structural opponent of Cicero.
  • Philippics — Demosthenes's speeches against Philip, and Cicero's speeches against Antony, explicitly modeled on the Greek originals.
  • De Officiis ("On Duties") — Cicero's moral philosophy treatise, a standard schoolbook from the eighth century to the nineteenth.
  • Renaissance humanists — those who recovered Demosthenes from Byzantine libraries and treated him as the Greek counterpart of Cicero.

Important Passages

  • The shared political shape: "A free city (Athens, then Rome) is being threatened by a great-man political-military figure (Philip, then Antony) who would end its constitutional freedom. The orator is the principal civic voice resisting the rise of personal rule."
  • The self-construction: Demosthenes "rehearsing with pebbles in his mouth, training his lungs by running uphill while speaking, practicing before a mirror" — and the underground study where he shaved one half of his head so he could not leave.
  • The chapter's framing of the two deaths: "Demosthenes's death (poison in the temple of Poseidon) is sober and prepared. Cicero's (stretching his neck out to the assassins from a litter) is more pathetic."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Why does Plutarch place Demosthenes and Cicero in parallel, and what do they share structurally?
  2. What does Plutarch mean by calling the orator "structurally a critic of power"?
  3. How is Demosthenes's self-construction as an orator a Plutarchan moral story?
  4. What relationship does the chapter draw between immediate political failure and long-term cultural influence?
  5. Why is the chapter's treatment of Cicero so important to the later Christian humanist tradition?

Discussion Questions

  1. Is the orator-against-power model a viable political form, or does the chapter quietly suggest it is doomed once the armed man arrives?
  2. The chapter previews that Plutarch's synkrisis turns on the difference between Demosthenes's austerity and Cicero's wit. Before reading the rest of the chapter, predict which Plutarch will reward — and why.
  3. The chapter ends by saying neither Life has a formal Second Witness because the speeches themselves are the primary evidence. What does it mean that the orator's evidence is his own performance?
  4. Cicero became the schoolbook of Christian Europe; Demosthenes had to be recovered by Renaissance humanists. What does this asymmetry of reception tell us about which orator the modern reader should weigh more heavily?

Connections to Later Chapters

This background section closes by pointing forward to Chapter 12, where Plutarch's introduction promises to return to the long Christian and humanist reception of Cicero — and through him, of Demosthenes. The pair of Lives in Chapter 9 is not just a comparison of two ancient orators; it sets up one of the longest reception threads in the whole work. The Life of Demosthenes (Section 2) will give us the self-made orator and the death in the temple; the Life of Cicero (Section 3) will give us the brilliant generalist and the death on the litter; the synkrisis (Section 4) will weigh them on seven distinct axes.
