Study Guide: Background — Constitutions, Religion, and the Engineered Order

Chapter 3 of Plutarch's Lives moves from founders to lawgivers. Where Theseus and Romulus called their cities into existence, Lycurgus of Sparta and Numa Pompilius of Rome made theirs stand. They are remembered not for battles won but for the legal, social, and religious framework they installed — a framework that outlasted them by centuries. This first section sets up the conceptual problem the chapter will work on: what is a lawgiver, what did each of these two men engineer, and on what axes will Plutarch compare them?

Key Overview

The section establishes four ideas that govern the whole chapter:

  1. The lawgiver as a type. A lawgiver (Greek nomothetes, Latin legum lator) is a single mind imposing a constitutional shape on a people who had been living unsystematically. He comes after the founder and tells the new community what kind of community it will be. Solon (the subject of Chapter 4) is another. Moses fits the structural pattern as well, though he received rather than invented his laws.

  2. The pathos of the lawgiver. Both Lycurgus and Numa succeed by binding the future. They must persuade a free people to accept restraints they themselves will not live to enforce. This is why their personal self-restraint matters so much in Plutarch's telling — both surrendered power they could have kept.

  3. The two engineered orders. Lycurgus engineered a society of equals: iron money, equal plots, common tables, and the agoge (the Spartan public education). Numa engineered a society of pious citizens: priesthoods, festivals, the Vestal Virgins, the Pontifex Maximus, the Salii, the Fecials, and the sacred calendar.

  4. The three axes of comparison. Plutarch tells us to watch for coercion vs. persuasion, war vs. peace, and education vs. religion as the cement that holds a constitution together. The synkrisis at the chapter's end will turn principally on the third.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Lycurgus of Sparta — Spartan lawgiver, probably eighth or seventh century BC. Brother of King Polydectes; declined the throne in favor of his nephew Charilaus; designed the Spartan constitution after travels abroad.
  • Numa Pompilius — Second king of Rome, traditionally 715–673 BC. A Sabine of Cures, elected by acclamation after Romulus's disappearance.
  • Nomothetes / legum lator — Greek and Latin technical terms for "lawgiver."
  • Agoge — Spartan public education from age seven; the cement of Lycurgus's constitution.
  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's formal comparison appended to a paired biography.
  • Temple of Janus — Roman shrine whose gates stood open in war, shut in peace; closed for the entirety of Numa's reign and only twice more in seven centuries.
  • Egeria — The nymph or goddess Numa claimed to consult nightly.
  • Delphi — The oracle Lycurgus consulted before announcing (and then after exiling himself to ratify) his constitution.

Important Passages

  • "A lawgiver succeeds by binding the future." The pathos of the figure: the discipline he creates must hold once he is gone.
  • "Where Lycurgus made warriors, Numa made worshippers." The two cement metaphors of the chapter — drill and prayer.
  • Plutarch on the chronological problem: "scarcely anything is asserted by one [historian] which is not called into question or contradicted by the rest." Both lawgivers stand at the edge of legend.
  • "A city's legend of its lawgiver is the city's self-portrait." What the cities became through believing in their lawgivers is what matters most.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How does Plutarch distinguish a founder from a lawgiver?
  2. What does the term nomothetes mean, and what is its Latin equivalent?
  3. Why does the lawgiver's task carry a particular pathos in Plutarch's telling?
  4. What kind of society did Lycurgus engineer, and what kind did Numa engineer?
  5. Name the three contrasts the chapter introduction tells the reader to watch for.
  6. What did Lycurgus put in place of gold and silver coin, and why?
  7. What does the state of the gates of the temple of Janus signify, and how long did they remain shut under Numa?
  8. Who is the Second Witness for the Roman side of this chapter, and why is there no equivalent for the Greek side?
  9. To what divine source did each lawgiver appeal for authority?
  10. According to Plutarch, what is the cement of Lycurgus's constitution, and what is the cement of Numa's?

Discussion Questions

  • Plutarch says the city's legend of its lawgiver is the city's self-portrait. What does that imply about how we should read ancient biography that cannot be historically verified?
  • Both lawgivers claim divine authority for their reforms. Is divine sanction a tool of persuasion, of self-deception, or of something else?
  • Lycurgus had to fight for his reforms (and lost an eye); Numa was begged to accept the throne. Does the difference in starting conditions help explain why their cements were so different?

Connections to Later Chapters

The chapter's introduction sets up a contrast that runs through the rest of the Plutarch unit. Solon in Chapter 4 will be another nomothetes who designs but does not enforce. The contrast between Lycurgus's durable institutional discipline and Numa's evaporating peace foreshadows Plutarch's deeper claim that education is the cement of any constitution — a claim the synkrisis in section 3.4 will argue explicitly.
