Study Guide: How to Read This Book

You now know who Plutarch was, what the Parallel Lives are, and how the Greek and Roman worlds map onto a timeline. This last section of the introduction is the operating manual for the book itself: the shape of each chapter, the colored boxes that mark different kinds of text, the Second Witness feature, and — most important — the synkrisis exercise that is the spine of every chapter from 2 through 11. Read this section once carefully; you will refer back to it again and again.

The Four-Section Chapter

Chapters 2 through 11 share an identical four-part shape. Internalize it now and you will know where you are in any chapter the moment you open it.

  • Section X.1 — Background and Context. Sets the stage. Why did Plutarch pair these two men? What is the chapter's theme? Read this first; it makes everything that follows easier to enter.
  • Section X.2 — The Greek Life. The Greek member of the pair. Mostly Plutarch's own words (in terracotta-colored boxes), wrapped in short introductions and analyses.
  • Section X.3 — The Roman Life. Identical structure, for the Roman counterpart.
  • Section X.4 — The Comparison. Plutarch's surviving synkrisis (in a bronze-colored box) plus analysis. In Chapters 5 and 8 (where the synkrisis is lost), this section instead frames the absence. Always, X.4 ends with the writing prompt: your own synkrisis.

After Section X.4 comes a set of about fifteen questions (and, in the teacher's edition, an answer key). Chapters 1 and 12 are different — they are the introduction and conclusion and contain no extracted primary text.

Reading the Colored Boxes

The book uses six box colors. Learning what each color means is a small but real reading skill — your eye should know what kind of text it is looking at before you even read the first word.

  • Terracotta (rust-orange): Primary text. Plutarch's own words in the Dryden–Clough translation. Read these carefully; the surrounding prose is commentary on what is in them.
  • Bronze: The synkrisis sections. Plutarch's surviving comparisons plus your own writing prompts. These are the heart of the book.
  • Sage green: "Second Witness" boxes — excerpts from Livy and Thucydides on the same events Plutarch describes.
  • Aegean blue: Definitions and person boxes — biographical sketches of figures who appear but do not get their own Life.
  • Amber: End-of-chapter questions.
  • Olive green: In the teacher's edition only — answer keys and teaching notes.

The Second Witness

Plutarch is rarely the only ancient source on his subjects, and one of the book's distinctive features is the chance to compare him against a second voice. For Roman subjects in Chapters 2–5, that voice is Livy (Titus Livius, c. 59 BC – AD 17), whose vast Ab Urbe Condita is our chief source for early Rome — the founding legends, the kings, the early Republic. For Greek subjects in Chapters 6 and 7 (Pericles, Alcibiades), that voice is Thucydides (c. 460 – c. 400 BC), an Athenian general turned historian who knew several of these men personally and whose History of the Peloponnesian War is the most rigorously analytical of ancient histories.

When two ancient authors cover the same event, their differences are themselves educational. They show that ancient history is not a single transparent record but a tradition of competing versions, and they teach you to ask: which author am I reading? what biases would I expect? what does the comparison reveal? The Second Witness boxes (in sage green) let you perform this comparison in miniature, alongside the chapter that calls for it. You cannot skip them — the chapter questions and synkrisis prompts often ask you to compare the two accounts directly.

The Spine Exercise: Writing Synkrisis

At the end of every content chapter — ten times in all — you are asked to write a synkrisis. If you do nothing else, do this. Plutarch's own synkriseis follow a consistent shape, and a good student synkrisis can follow the same shape:

  1. Open with the axis. The axis is the specific question on which the two lives can be weighed — courage in adversity, loyalty to one's city, restraint in victory, capacity for friendship. Vague axes ("who was better?") produce vague essays. The narrower your axis, the sharper your judgment.
  2. Bring forward specific incidents. A synkrisis is won or lost by the incidents you cite. Adduce two or three specific moments from each life that bear on the axis. Brief paraphrase, not block quotation. Where you can name the moment ("when Theseus refused the throne of Athens ...", "when Romulus killed his brother ..."), name it.
  3. Render judgment. Plutarch's synkriseis are unfailingly evaluative. Yours should be too: which life shows the trait more fully, and why? Qualified judgments are fine ("Theseus was the more generous; but Romulus, the more decisive — and in a founder, decisiveness is the rarer gift"). Refusing to judge is not fine.
  4. Keep it short. Plutarch's surviving synkriseis run four to six pages even though the Lives they conclude are seventy. Your synkrisis should be 600–1,000 words — three to four pages, double-spaced. Brevity forces selection; selection sharpens judgment.

By the end of the book you will have written ten of these. The first two or three may feel awkward; the seventh, eighth, ninth will not. You will have developed a discipline that ancient and early-modern education prized above almost any other: weighing two lives and saying which is heavier.

What You Can Skip, and What You Cannot

  • You can skim the legendary material. The earliest Lives (Theseus, Romulus, Lycurgus, Numa) contain a great deal of legend. Read once; do not labor over every detail. The point is the moral biography, not the mythology.
  • You cannot skip the small revealing moments. Plutarch's art lives in the moment when a man's character flashes out — a laugh, a remark to his mother, the way he treats a captive. These moments are why we still read him and what the synkrisis exercises require you to notice.
  • You can skip footnotes on a first read. Most are vocabulary. If you know the term, skip; if you don't, look down. Don't let footnotes break the flow of a paragraph.
  • You cannot skip the Second Witness boxes. Their comparisons are tested by the chapter questions and the synkrisis prompts.
  • You can skip Chapter 12 until last. The conclusion (Plutarch's reception in Shakespeare, the American Founders, the Christian moral tradition) is more meaningful once you have read the ten content chapters.

One Last Thing

The text closes with a personal note worth keeping. Plutarch is one of the best companions a reader ever had — funny, observant, generous even toward men he disapproves of, and a teller of memorable stories. Many of his readers (the young John Adams, the young Hamilton, the young Lincoln, generations of American schoolchildren) credited him with forming their public characters. The work asks attention — slow reading, careful weighing, the writing of your own synkriseis — but the reward is one of the oldest in education: meeting some of the most interesting people who ever lived, in the company of one of their most observant readers, and being changed by the meeting.

Now turn the page. The first pairing is Theseus and Romulus.
