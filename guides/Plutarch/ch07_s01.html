Study Guide: Background — Brilliance and Betrayal

Chapter 7 of Plutarch: Lives in Parallel is the darkest of the political pairings. Alcibiades of Athens and Coriolanus of Rome are both men of extraordinary talent who served their cities brilliantly and then turned against them. Each, when cast out, took up arms with the enemy. Each came to the gates of his old home at the head of a foreign army. The two careers, the chapter says, are "different in texture but identical in shape: gifted, prominent, exiled, traitor."

Key Overview

The central question. Plutarch presses the central question of public life: how does a brilliant man go wrong? And, as a closely related question: when does the city's mistreatment of the brilliant man justify his betrayal of the city? The synkrisis at the end of the chapter will weigh both questions carefully, and the answer is not the same in both cases.

Two brilliant men. Alcibiades (c. 450 – 404 BC) was the most spectacular Athenian of the late fifth century: a ward of Pericles, a student of Socrates, a man of beauty, wealth, and rhetorical brilliance. He proposed and helped lead the Sicilian Expedition in 415 BC — the largest Greek military undertaking ever attempted — and was driven into exile by religious charges before the campaign was decided. He went to Sparta and gave them three pieces of strategic advice (send Gylippus to Syracuse; fortify Decelea permanently inside Attica; foment revolt among Athens's allies) that helped destroy Athenian power. Recalled, dismissed again, and finally driven to Persia and Phrygia, he died in foreign exile the same year Athens surrendered.

Coriolanus (fifth century BC, legendary date) was a patrician general of the early Republic. He earned his surname by the single-handed seizure of the Volscian city of Corioli. He returned to Rome a hero, ran for consul, and was defeated — the people would not vote for him because of his open disdain for them. In retaliation he proposed, during a famine, that the relief grain from Sicily be sold only at the old high price and only to plebs who would renounce the recently-won office of tribune of the people. The plebs threatened to murder him; the tribunes condemned him to exile in absentia. He went to the Volscians, the very enemy he had defeated, and within a year was at the gates of Rome at the head of a Volscian army. The Coriolanus story is partly legend (the dates do not quite fit the historical Volscian campaigns), but Plutarch tells it as historical biography.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Alcibiades — Athenian statesman and general (c. 450 – 404 BC); ward of Pericles; student of Socrates; proposer and partial leader of the Sicilian Expedition; later a defector to Sparta and Persia.
  • Coriolanus (Caius Marcius) — Roman patrician general of the early Republic; surnamed for the seizure of Corioli; later a Volscian commander.
  • The Sicilian Expedition (415 BC) — Athens's largest single naval and land operation, directed against Syracuse; annihilated after Alcibiades's defection.
  • The Herms — statues of Hermes at Athenian doorways, mutilated the night before the fleet sailed; the religious crisis used to charge Alcibiades.
  • Decelea — fort in Attica that, on Alcibiades's advice, the Spartans occupied permanently; bled Athens dry by interrupting agriculture and silver mining.
  • The tribunate of the people — Roman plebeian magistracy with veto power; Coriolanus proposed using the famine to roll it back.
  • Socrates and Volumnia — moral counterweights: the philosopher saved Alcibiades's life at Potidaea; the mother stopped Coriolanus's army at the gates of Rome.
  • North's translation (1579) — Sir Thomas North's English Plutarch, the conduit through which Shakespeare drew his Coriolanus.

Important Passages

The chapter does not quote at length, but it flags incidents to watch in the Lives proper. Two short stories of the boy Alcibiades — the wrestling-bite ("you bite like a woman" / "no, like a lion") and his lying in front of the loaded cart — are introduced as character-revealing in the Plutarchan sense. The chapter also points the reader toward Volumnia's great speech at the gates of Rome, the speech Shakespeare turned almost verbatim into Coriolanus V.iii.

For the Greek side, the chapter recommends Thucydides Book VI (the Sicilian Debate, where Alcibiades's case for the expedition is set beside Nicias's against it). For the Roman side, there is no comparable single passage; Livy Book II and Dionysius of Halicarnassus carry parallel narratives.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. State the shared shape that Plutarch finds in the careers of Alcibiades and Coriolanus.
  2. What is the central question Plutarch presses about brilliant men in this chapter?
  3. What were the three pieces of strategic advice Alcibiades gave the Spartans, and what effect did each have?
  4. What was the Herms incident and how was it used against Alcibiades?
  5. What did Coriolanus propose should be done with the Sicilian grain during the Roman famine?
  6. Who are the two moral counterweights identified in this chapter, and what is each meant to anchor?
  7. Define, in Plutarch's terms, the two styles of contempt for the multitude that distinguish Alcibiades from Coriolanus.
  8. Why is the chapter careful to note that Coriolanus's story is partly legend?
  9. What is the Shakespeare connection, and through what English translation does it run?
  10. Who is the Second Witness for the Greek side, and why is there no comparable witness for the Roman side?

Discussion Questions

  1. The chapter says Plutarch's biography of Alcibiades "handles the betrayal with a level of cool that is itself a moral comment." Is restrained narration ever a sharper moral judgment than indignation? Argue both sides.
  2. Which form of contempt for the multitude is more politically corrosive — Alcibiades's flattery or Coriolanus's open haughtiness? Take a position and defend it.
  3. The chapter foreshadows that Plutarch's synkrisis will not answer the central question the same way for both men. What about each man's situation might make the city's role in the betrayal more or less of an extenuation?

Connections to Later Chapters

This chapter inverts the Pericles–Fabius pair of Chapter 6. Where that pair set "the leader of opportunity" beside "the leader of emergency" and refused to rank them, Chapter 7 sets two leaders who could not be held by their cities at all, and asks why. The Alcibiades line runs forward into the politics of empire and ambition; the Coriolanus line runs forward into the Christian–Shakespearean reception that Chapter 12 will treat at length. The Volumnia scene and the refusal-to-show-wounds scene, both Plutarchan, are the building blocks of the modern English understanding of the proud, doomed hero.
