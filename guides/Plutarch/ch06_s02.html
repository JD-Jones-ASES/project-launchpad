Study Guide: The Life of Pericles

The Life of Pericles is Plutarch's portrait of the leader who made Athens the political, intellectual, and artistic capital of the Greek world. It is also unusual among Plutarch's biographies in the seriousness with which it treats education — Pericles is the man whose character was deliberately formed by philosophy, and Plutarch traces the influence of specific teachers on specific habits of mind. Below the elevated public manner, the Life sketches a careful political career: a man who entered public life only after his older rivals had cleared the stage, who chose the demos not from popular sentiment but from a strategic calculation, and whose authority over thirty years rested on rhetoric, restraint, and a deliberate distance from private life.

Key Overview

The intellectual formation. Three teachers shape Pericles. Damon, a music teacher who (in Plutarch's account) was really a political theorist using lessons as cover; he was eventually ostracized. Mnesiphilus, a Solonian mentor who taught "a sort of political shrewdness and practical sagacity." And Anaxagoras of Clazomenae, the philosopher who introduced Nous — mind, intelligence — into Greek thought. Anaxagoras gave Pericles "elevation and sublimity of purpose and of character," the serenity that defined his public manner.

The political rise. Pericles was cautious at first — he looked uncomfortably like the deposed tyrant Pisistratus, and Athenians watched for such resemblances. He served in the military, distinguished himself bravely, and entered politics only after Aristides died, Themistocles was ostracized, and Cimon was abroad. He allied himself with the demos in part for self-protective reasons: he could not match Cimon's private wealth, so he made the city itself the source of public benefits.

The Olympian style. Pericles withdrew from private social life. He went to the Assembly only for important business, and reserved himself, in Plutarch's image, "like the Salaminian galley, for great occasions." This deliberate distance was the engine of his authority for three decades.

The building program. Pericles rebuilt the Acropolis after its Persian destruction in 480 BC: the Parthenon, the Erechtheum, the Propylaea. They were largely paid for from the Delian League treasury, moved to Athens. His rival Thucydides son of Melesias accused him of adorning the city "like a vain woman with precious stones." Pericles answered that protection earned the right to use the funds as Athens saw fit.

The war and the speech. When the Peloponnesian War broke out in 431 BC, Pericles's strategy was characteristic: withdraw behind the Long Walls, let Spartan invasions burn out, rely on the navy. At the end of the first year he delivered the eulogy for the war dead. Thucydides reconstructed the speech as the Funeral Oration — the case for democracy as a way of life, and also a defense of the war.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Xanthippus — Pericles's father; victor at Mycale (479 BC).
  • Agariste — Pericles's mother; granddaughter of Cleisthenes.
  • Anaxagoras of Clazomenae — Pericles's philosophical teacher; nicknamed Nous.
  • Damon — political theorist masquerading as music teacher; ostracized.
  • Mnesiphilus — Solonian political mentor.
  • Cimon — wealthy aristocratic rival; maintained popularity by personal generosity.
  • Thucydides son of Melesias — political rival (not the historian); led the attack on the building program.
  • The Olympian — popular nickname for Pericles's godlike public manner.
  • Salaminian galley — Plutarch's image for Pericles's restraint: reserved for great occasions.
  • Long Walls — fortifications connecting Athens to Piraeus; backbone of Pericles's war strategy.
  • Funeral Oration — Thucydides II.34–46; the Second Witness for the Greek side.

Important Passages

On Anaxagoras (primary source): "But he that saw most of Pericles, and furnished him most especially with a weight and grandeur of sense, superior to all arts of popularity, and in general gave him his elevation and sublimity of purpose and of character, was Anaxagoras of Clazomenae; whom the men of those times called by the name of Nous, that is, mind, or intelligence." The same passage continues with the marketplace anecdote: a man abuses Pericles all day; Pericles silently completes his business; at dusk he orders a servant to take a light and escort the man safely home. Public composure under personal abuse, plus a small humane gesture — Pericles's distinctive style in a single scene.

On the Olympian style: Plutarch writes that Pericles "presented himself at intervals only, not speaking to every business, reserving himself, like the Salaminian galley, for great occasions."

On the building program: Pericles defended the use of Delian League funds on the grounds that if Athens protected the allies, she had the right to use the contribution for whatever purpose seemed good, including buildings that would endure "forever."

On his death: "In all my time no Athenian ever put on mourning because of me." Pericles wanted this — that his authority had never required the death of a citizen by political prosecution — to be the moral attached to his name.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Identify the three teachers Plutarch credits with shaping Pericles, and the contribution of each.
  2. Explain the nickname Nous and what it tells you about Anaxagoras's place in Greek thought.
  3. Why was Pericles initially afraid to enter public life?
  4. Why did Pericles ally with the popular party rather than the aristocrats?
  5. Name the three principal buildings of Pericles's Acropolis program.
  6. What was the political controversy over the use of Delian League funds?
  7. State Pericles's strategy for the Peloponnesian War in one sentence.
  8. What is the central political claim of the Funeral Oration?
  9. What does the Funeral Oration say about the relation of private freedom and public obligation?
  10. Quote Pericles's reported last words and explain what he wanted them to mean.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch presents Pericles's marketplace silence (and the servant with the light) as one of the great character vignettes of antiquity. Is the gesture genuinely humane, or is it part of the Olympian performance — and does it matter for political evaluation?
  2. The Funeral Oration is at once a magnificent statement of democratic principle and a war speech defending the leader's choice. How should a modern reader hold both readings at once?
  3. Pericles wanted to be remembered as the leader who never had a citizen put on mourning because of him. Plutarch notes that Pericles had prosecuted Cimon, had moved the ostracism of Thucydides son of Melesias, and had presided over the brutal reduction of Samos. Does the claim survive scrutiny?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Life of Alcibiades (Chapter 7) inherits a Pericles-shaped Athens — overstretched, imperial, brilliant, and brittle — and tracks what happens when the discipline of the Olympian style is replaced by Alcibiades's flamboyance. The synkrisis of Pericles and Fabius (6.4) judges the Periclean record against a very different Roman temperament. The Funeral Oration also haunts the later Greek Lives: every later Athenian leader has to live in its shadow.
