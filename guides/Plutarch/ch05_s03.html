Study Guide: The Life of Camillus

This study guide accompanies Section 5.3 of Plutarch: Lives in Parallel — the biography of Marcus Furius Camillus (c. 446 - 365 BC), the Roman dictator who captured Veii, refused to win a city by treachery, was driven into exile by ingratitude, and was recalled to deliver Rome from the Gauls. Plutarch's Camillus is the model Roman aristocratic statesman: brave, religious, scrupulous in method, generous to defeated enemies, willing to suffer ingratitude rather than seek revenge. The Life of Camillus is paired with substantial extracts from Livy Book V on the Gallic Sack.

Key Overview

A strange shape of office. Plutarch opens the Life by flagging a structural anomaly: Camillus was chosen dictator five times, triumphed four times, and was styled second founder of Rome — yet never held the consulship. The reason was political: the plebs were at dissension with the senate and refused to return consuls, electing military tribunes with consular power instead. The dictatorship — an emergency magistracy that suspended ordinary government for up to six months — remained available; the senate kept turning to Camillus for emergencies; each time he finished the task and laid the office down.

The siege of Veii. Veii was the largest Etruscan city, twelve miles north of Rome. The decisive siege began in 405 BC and dragged on for ten years — the longest war Rome had ever fought, and the first in which the Roman army wintered in the field. In 396 BC Camillus was named dictator and given the command. His troops dug a mining tunnel under the walls; the favorable prodigy of the strange rising of the Alban lake gave the Romans religious confidence. The city fell. From the high tower Camillus watched the pillaging, wept, raised his hands to heaven, and prayed that any necessary misfortune fall on his own head, sparing Rome. As he turned to leave he stumbled — and accepted the stumble as the small misfortune he had requested. Plutarch tells the moment to register Camillus's piety.

The schoolmaster of Falerii. The next campaign produced the most beautiful incident in the Life. Camillus was besieging Falerii. The Falerian children went out daily under their schoolmaster to play near the walls. The schoolmaster gradually led them further from the gates until he delivered the entire group — children of the noblest families — into the Roman camp. Camillus refused the bargain. "A great general should rely on his own virtue, and not on other men's vices." He had the schoolmaster stripped, bound, and given to the boys with rods. The children whipped their master back to the city. The Falerians sent ambassadors to Rome saying the Romans had taught them to embrace submission rather than liberty, because they were superior not in strength but in virtue. Falerii surrendered.

Exile. Camillus pressed two unpopular issues after Veii. He insisted that Apollo's tenth of the Veian booty be paid even after soldiers had pocketed it, making them contribute again. He opposed the tribunes' proposal that half the Roman population relocate to the empty Veii — which would have effectively split Rome. The tribunes were popular; Camillus blocked them; the people turned hostile. A probably-trumped-up accusation that he had appropriated Etruscan bronze gates triggered the crisis. Rather than face trial, Camillus chose exile, leaving for Ardea with a prayer that Rome would soon need him.

The Gallic Sack and the recall. A Gallic embassy was insulted, a Roman army was destroyed at the Allia, Rome was burned, and the Capitoline hill was besieged. Camillus, in Ardea, was the first to act — leading a night attack on the Gauls' camp before any Roman recall reached him. The Romans on the Capitol sent secretly to ask him to take command. He arrived as the ransom gold was being weighed against rigged Gallic weights, ordered the scales removed, and announced that "Romans recover their country with steel, not with gold." Livy's Second-Witness account adds the Capitoline geese, Brennus's "Vae victis" (Woe to the conquered) at the rigged scale, and the long oration against relocation that Plutarch only summarizes.

The second founding. Most of Rome had burned and the people were exhausted. The tribunes again proposed Veii. Camillus delivered a long speech on the holy ground of Rome — the temples, the founding rituals, the burial places of ancestors. The decisive moment was a centurion's casual remark, overheard by the senators, standard-bearer, fix your standard here. The people took it as an omen and resolved to stay. The rebuilding was hasty and disordered (Livy notes this is why later Rome had a confusing street plan), but the city was restored. Camillus was called the second founder.

The end. Camillus continued in public life into his eighties, held two more dictatorships, won victories over the Volscians and Aequians, and died in the plague of 365 BC, an old and honored man, with a public funeral.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Marcus Furius Camillus — Roman dictator (five times), second founder of Rome.
  • Veii — Largest Etruscan city, twelve miles north of Rome; captured by Camillus in 396 BC after a ten-year siege.
  • Brennus — Gallic chieftain who sacked Rome; in Livy, source of Vae victis.
  • Falerii — Etruscan ally city, scene of the schoolmaster incident.
  • Ardea — Latin city where Camillus lived in exile and from which he attacked the Gauls.
  • Alban Lake prodigy — The unexpected rising of a lake, taken as a favorable omen for the Roman siege of Veii.
  • Allia — River where the Roman army was destroyed before the Gallic Sack.
  • Capitol — The hill garrisoned during the Sack while the rest of Rome was abandoned; site of the famous geese.
  • Apollo's tenth — The vow Camillus had made before the Veian campaign; he insisted on enforcement after the soldiers had taken their share.
  • Military tribunes with consular power — Magistracy substituted for the consulship during the plebeian-senate quarrel.

Important Passages

  • A great general should rely on his own virtue, and not on other men's vices. — Camillus to the Falerian schoolmaster; the moral key to the Life.
  • We have been forced to revenge ourselves on the city of our unrighteous and wicked enemies. But if ... there be any calamity due, to counterbalance this great felicity, I beg that it may be ... fall ... upon my own head. — The prayer at the fall of Veii.
  • Romans recover their country with steel, not with gold. — Camillus on arriving at the ransom scene.
  • Standard-bearer, fix your standard here. — The overheard centurion remark that decided Rome's refusal to relocate.
  • The Romans, preferring justice before victory, had taught them rather to embrace submission than liberty. — The Falerian ambassadors to Rome.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What is singular about the shape of Camillus's offices?
  2. What role did the Alban lake prodigy play in the capture of Veii?
  3. What happened at the high tower when Veii fell, and what did Camillus's stumble mean?
  4. What is the schoolmaster of Falerii, and what line of Camillus crowns it?
  5. Why was Camillus exiled, and what was the immediate trigger?
  6. What did Camillus do in Ardea before the Roman recall reached him?
  7. What does Livy add to Plutarch's account of the Gallic Sack?
  8. What kept the Romans from abandoning Rome for Veii?

Discussion Questions

  1. The schoolmaster of Falerii is "the moral high water mark of the Life." Is it admirable in itself, or only in contrast with the choice Camillus refused? Could Plutarch have made the same case with a less neatly arranged scene?
  2. Camillus's prayer at the fall of Veii treats victory as a religious problem. Compare this to Themistocles's interpretation of the "walls of wood." Are they doing the same thing with religion, or different things?
  3. Plutarch presents Camillus as the model Roman aristocratic statesman. The text immediately concedes this is "the Roman moral self-image, not necessarily Roman moral fact." How should a reader hold both claims at once?
  4. The line Romans recover their country with steel, not with gold became one of the most-quoted in Roman memory. Is the line good policy, or only good rhetoric? What does the recovery of Rome actually owe to steel as opposed to to other factors?
  5. The second founding of Rome turns on a centurion's casual remark, taken as an omen. Is the omen the real cause, or only the cover story for a decision already made?
