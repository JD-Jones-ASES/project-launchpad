Study Guide: The Comparison

The synkrisis of Alcibiades and Coriolanus is among the longest and most carefully argued in the Lives, and its verdict is one of the most surprising in the book. Plutarch is working with two figures alike in fate (gifted, exiled, turned traitor) but opposite in style — the flatterer and the provoker, the serpent and the bear. By the count of axes, he tilts toward Alcibiades, even though on most readings of personal character Coriolanus is the more admirable man. The argument is political, not personal, and it cuts against an old Western preference.

Key Overview

Axis 1: Military ability. "Their military ones, we may say, incline the balance very decidedly upon neither side." Both displayed courage; both showed generalship. Alcibiades won more victories in independent command, which slightly favors him. Round one a draw, with the edge to Alcibiades.

Axis 2: Style of seeking power. Plutarch frames the contrast as the petulance and base flattery of Alcibiades versus the ungraciousness, pride, and oligarchical haughtiness of Marcius. His verdict: "A man who ingratiates himself by indulgence and flattery is hardly so censurable as one who, to avoid the appearance of flattering, insults." Flattery seeks power by lowering oneself; pride seeks power by lowering others. Round two unexpectedly to Alcibiades.

Axis 3: Provoking war. Alcibiades treacherously broke the Athens–Sparta peace, but in doing so gained Athens the alliance of Argos and Mantinea — a real strategic asset. Coriolanus stirred up the Volscian war by spreading false rumors, "simply to gratify anger." Plutarch finds the second worse than the first. Round three to Alcibiades.

Axis 4: Behavior in exile. Both turned on their cities; both led foreign armies. But Alcibiades relented when Athens recalled him; he came back and served. Coriolanus refused all embassies, all entreaties, all priests; he was moved only by his mother's intervention at the last hour. He acted not to reconcile but "to destroy and overthrow." Round four to Alcibiades.

Axis 5: Coriolanus's special problem. Plutarch develops this at length. The man who refuses to flatter the people because he despises them, and then resents the people for not honoring him, is contradicting himself. "He who least likes courting favor, ought also least to think of resenting neglect." The contradiction was in Coriolanus, not in Rome. Round five to Alcibiades.

Axis 6: Money. Alcibiades was famously corrupt — bribes, extravagance, luxury. Coriolanus "declined to receive [money], even when pressed upon him by his commanders as an honor." Personal incorruptibility favors Coriolanus, decisively. Round six to Coriolanus.

Axis 7: Temperance and personal life. Coriolanus was chaste, austere, devoted to his mother. Alcibiades was a famous debauch. Personal moral conduct favors Coriolanus, decisively. Round seven to Coriolanus.

The overall verdict. The count tilts toward Alcibiades. This is the genuine surprise of the synkrisis, because on most readings of personal character Coriolanus is the more admirable man — brave, austere, incorruptible, devoted to his mother. But Plutarch's argument is essentially political. The political question is which kind of leader does less damage to the community that produced him. Coriolanus, despite his personal virtues, did more damage, because his pride could not be appeased short of his city's destruction, where Alcibiades's vanity could always be re-flattered into service. The man who can be talked back is, politically, the safer figure.

The lesson it cuts against. Plutarch is saying that political life requires suppleness, and that even bad suppleness (Alcibiades's) is politically less destructive than principled rigidity (Coriolanus's). This cuts against the romantic Western admiration for the proud, austere, principled hero who refuses to compromise.

A complication. Alcibiades cost Athens her empire. Coriolanus cost Rome nothing in the end, because his mother turned him aside. The political damage was much less on the Roman side — a real fact the synkrisis does not weigh as heavily as it might.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Synkrisis — Plutarch's closing comparative essay; in Chapter 7 it is one of the longest and most carefully argued in the Lives.
  • The seven axes — military ability; style of seeking power; provoking war; behavior in exile; the special problem of resenting neglect; money; temperance.
  • "Flattery lowers oneself; pride lowers others" — Plutarch's analysis of Axis 2.
  • "He who least likes courting favor, ought also least to think of resenting neglect." — Plutarch's central political claim, the epigram of Axis 5.
  • Argos and Mantinea — alliances Athens gained from Alcibiades's breach of the peace; used by Plutarch on Axis 3.
  • The Christian–Shakespearean reading — the tradition (Shakespeare 1607, Eliot, Hazlitt) that reads Coriolanus more sympathetically than Plutarch does.

Important Passages

On flattery and pride (Axis 2): "A man who ingratiates himself by indulgence and flattery is hardly so censurable as one who, to avoid the appearance of flattering, insults."

On the contradiction of Coriolanus (Axis 5): "He who least likes courting favor, ought also least to think of resenting neglect."

On the political theory of the verdict: "Political life requires suppleness… even bad suppleness (Alcibiades's) is politically less destructive than principled rigidity (Coriolanus's)."

On the complication: "Alcibiades cost Athens her empire. Coriolanus cost Rome nothing in the end, because his mother turned him aside. The political damage was much less on the Roman side; this is a real fact that the synkrisis does not weigh as heavily as it might."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. State Plutarch's verdict on Axis 2 in his own words.
  2. Why does Plutarch award Axis 3 to Alcibiades despite calling his act treacherous?
  3. What distinguishes Alcibiades's response to recall from Coriolanus's response to the Roman embassies?
  4. State Plutarch's epigram on courting favor and resenting neglect.
  5. On which two axes does Coriolanus win the round, and why?
  6. What is the central political claim that decides Plutarch's overall verdict?
  7. Which Western tradition does the verdict cut against, and how?
  8. Identify the complication the chapter introduces about actual political damage.
  9. How does the Christian–Shakespearean tradition read Coriolanus differently from Plutarch?
  10. Name three modern readers (or critics) the chapter mentions in connection with the reception of Coriolanus.

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch judges flattery less destructive than pride because "flattery lowers oneself; pride lowers others." Is that the right principle for political life today? Argue with examples.
  2. The chapter says the verdict cuts against the Western preference for the proud, austere, incorruptible hero. Are there any political situations in which Coriolanus's rigidity would have served better than Alcibiades's suppleness?
  3. The synkrisis "does not weigh as heavily as it might" the fact that Coriolanus's actual damage was less than Alcibiades's. Should it have weighed actual damage more? Make the case for and against treating consequences as the decisive measure.

Connections to Later Chapters

The political claim that suppleness is safer than rigidity sets up the way later Roman Lives in the volume (Cato Minor, Cicero) will be read. The Christian–Shakespearean reception of Coriolanus is taken up at length in Chapter 12, which treats Shakespeare's reading of Plutarch and the way the modern English imagination has filtered the Lives through North's translation and the Shakespeare stage. The writing prompt at the close of this section — write your own synkrisis of Alcibiades and Coriolanus, 600 to 1,000 words, choose one of four axes, cite two incidents from each Life, render judgment — is one of the harder synkriseis in the book because both men are partly admirable and partly indefensible.
