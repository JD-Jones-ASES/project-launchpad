Study Guide: Background — Founding Legends and the Problem of Legendary Evidence

This section is the methodological doorway into Plutarch's Parallel Lives. Before introducing Theseus and Romulus as individual figures, Plutarch warns his reader that the ground beneath legendary biography is unstable, and he explains why he intends to write about these men anyway. Read this section carefully — every move Plutarch makes here (the geographer image, the "some say, others say" habit, the focus on moral self-portrait) returns throughout the book.

Key Overview

Plutarch begins the Lives with the oldest possible Greek and Roman pairing — the legendary founders of Athens and Rome. Theseus, in Athenian tradition, ruled in the second millennium BC, around the time of the Trojan War or earlier. Romulus, in Roman tradition, founded Rome in 753 BC. Neither figure can be historically verified.

Plutarch's solution to the evidentiary problem is to acknowledge it openly. He compares himself to a geographer drawing a map: the middle of the map is reliable, but at the edges the cartographer can only write that beyond this line nothing certain can be said. Plutarch is about to write biography at those edges, and he asks his readers — explicitly — to be "candid" and to "receive with indulgence the stories of antiquity."

The pairing is structural. Plutarch chooses Theseus because he is the namegiver of Athens, set against Romulus, the namegiver of Rome. The cities make the men comparable. Beyond that, the parallels run too deep to be coincidence: both men were born out of wedlock, both raised away from the city they would found or refound, both came to political power through the killing of a wrongdoer, both founded their city's first institutions, both ended in mysterious circumstances, and both were honored as gods after their deaths.

Plutarch does not think legendary biography is useless. He thinks it is the record of what a people thought their best self looked like — a city's moral self-portrait, painted through the figure of its founder. The character of Theseus the Athenians believed in (generous, restless, brave, sometimes careless) shaped what generations of actual Athenians took themselves to be.

Major Figures and Terms

Term Meaning
Theseus Legendary king of Athens; credited with killing the Minotaur and with the synoecism of Attica.
Romulus Legendary founder of Rome; son of Mars; killed his brother Remus during the founding quarrel.
Sosius Senecio Roman senator and consul; Plutarch's friend; the Parallel Lives is dedicated to him.
Synoecism The political unification of scattered villages into a single city-state. Theseus's claim to fame as a founder.
Polis The Greek city-state — the political form Theseus is said to have created for Attica.
Apollodorus Mythographer roughly contemporary with Plutarch; another preserver of the Greek legendary tradition for Theseus.
Livy The great Roman historian (Augustan age); the Second Witness for the Romulus side.
Quirinus The deified Romulus; the god into whom the founder was said to be transformed after his disappearance.
Skyros The island where Theseus was killed by King Lycomedes; later the site from which Cimon recovered his bones.

Important Passages

The geographer's map (opening of the Theseus). "As geographers, Sosius, crowd into the edges of their maps parts of the world which they do not know about, adding notes in the margin to the effect that beyond this lies nothing but sandy deserts full of wild beasts… so, in this work of mine… I might very well say of those that are farther off, 'Beyond this there is nothing but prodigies and fictions.'"

This image announces the methodological posture of the entire Lives. Read it slowly. Plutarch is not abandoning the project; he is naming its limits.

The appeal to candid readers. "Where it shall be found contumaciously slighting credibility, and refusing to be reduced to anything like probable fact, we shall beg that we may meet with candid readers." Plutarch asks for indulgence, not infallibility.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What does the geographer metaphor accomplish at the opening of the Theseus?
  2. Why does Plutarch pair Theseus with Romulus rather than another Greek figure?
  3. List four of the deep parallels Plutarch identifies between Theseus and Romulus.
  4. What does the phrase "some say, others say" tell us about Plutarch's method?
  5. Why is there a Second Witness for Romulus but not for Theseus?
  6. What are the five axes Plutarch instructs the reader to watch for in the Lives that follow?
  7. In what sense is a founder-legend a city's "moral self-portrait"?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch refuses both pretended certainty and total skepticism about legendary figures. Is this a defensible middle position, or does it allow him to have it both ways?
  2. The Athenian story of Theseus was not historically verifiable in Plutarch's day either — yet generations of Athenians lived by it. What is the relationship between a community's founding story and its present identity?
  3. The Roman tradition has Livy as a parallel narrative; the Greek tradition does not. What does it mean for our reading that Plutarch is, for Theseus, almost the only voice?
  4. Plutarch warns the reader at length about the unreliability of the material. Does this warning increase or decrease our trust in him as a biographer?
