Study Guide: The Roman Tribunes — Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus

Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus were brothers, the sons of a twice-consul father and of Cornelia, daughter of Scipio Africanus. Their mother — refusing remarriage even to a king of Egypt because she preferred the title "mother of the Gracchi" — is the moral center of both Lives. Tiberius, as tribune in 133 BC, pushed through an agrarian law that enforced an existing limit on private occupation of public land and was beaten to death on the Capitol. His brother Gaius, ten years later, attempted a broader program — grain dole, equestrian juries, Italian citizenship — and was killed in turn in 121 BC. Their two deaths opened a century of political violence at Rome.

Key Overview

  • The family. Tiberius (b. c. 163 BC) and Gaius (b. c. 154 BC) came from the most distinguished political family of mid-second-century Rome. Their mother Cornelia — daughter of the conqueror of Hannibal — raised them with extraordinary care.
  • The crisis. The constant wars of the previous century had bled the small farmer class. Wealthy senatorial families had moved in on the abandoned land — technically public land of the Roman state — and worked it with slave labor. The landless poor flowed into Rome; the citizen militia was running short of qualified recruits.
  • The Etrurian observation. On a journey through Etruria, Tiberius noticed that all the farm work was being done by slaves; the small Italian peasantry had disappeared. The image crystallized his sense of the demographic crisis.
  • The agrarian law. The lex Sempronia agraria enforced the existing 500-iugera limit on any one citizen's occupation of public land. Excess was recovered and redistributed in 30-iugerum plots to the landless, inheritable but not alienable. Holders kept 500 iugera plus 250 for each of their two oldest sons, and were compensated for improvements.
  • The deposition of Octavius. When fellow tribune Marcus Octavius vetoed every attempt to bring the bill to a vote, Tiberius forced a vote in the assembly to depose Octavius from the tribunate. The vote passed; the law passed; a commission of three (Tiberius, Gaius, and Appius Claudius) implemented redistribution.
  • The killing on the Capitol. On a hot day in 133 BC, with rumors that Tiberius meant to make himself king, the pontifex maximus Publius Scipio Nasica led a mob of senators armed with the broken legs of their own benches up the Capitol. They beat Tiberius and three hundred supporters to death and threw the bodies into the Tiber. It was the first political killing in Rome in three centuries.
  • Gaius's program (123–122 BC). Restoration of the agrarian commission; a monthly state-subsidized grain distribution; addition of equestrians to senatorial juries; colonies including one at the destroyed site of Carthage; reform of state-revenue contracts favoring equestrian tax-farmers; a proposal extending Roman citizenship to the Italian allies (the largest single political reform).
  • The senatorial counter. The Senate backed a rival tribune, Marcus Livius Drusus, who offered even more generous popular legislation to undercut Gaius's coalition. Gaius lost popular support and failed to win a third tribunate.
  • The senatus consultum ultimum. In 121 BC the Senate passed its first "final decree," authorizing the consuls to do whatever was necessary to preserve the Republic. Lucius Opimius raised an armed force; supporters were killed on the Aventine; Gaius escaped across the Tiber to the grove of Furrina, where his loyal slave killed him at his own order and then killed himself. Three thousand of his supporters were subsequently executed without trial.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus: Tribune 133 BC. Killed at thirty.
  • Gaius Sempronius Gracchus: Tribune 123 and 122 BC. Killed at about thirty-three.
  • Cornelia: Their mother. Daughter of Scipio Africanus. Moral center of both Lives.
  • Marcus Octavius: Fellow tribune whose veto Tiberius broke by deposition.
  • Appius Claudius: Tiberius's father-in-law and the third agrarian commissioner.
  • Publius Scipio Nasica: Pontifex maximus who led the senatorial mob that killed Tiberius.
  • Marcus Livius Drusus: The senatorial counter-tribune who out-bid Gaius's reforms.
  • Lucius Opimius: Consul of 121 BC who carried out the senatus consultum ultimum and ordered the post-fight executions.
  • Lex Sempronia agraria: Tiberius's agrarian law.
  • Ager publicus: Roman public land. The legal lever of the reform.
  • Senatus consultum ultimum: The Senate's "final decree" — its first use authorized the killing of Gaius.

Important Passages

The chapter's most chilling sentence describes the killing on the Capitol: the senators, "having torn the legs off their own benches in the Senate house as clubs, beat Tiberius and three hundred of his supporters to death." The image — patres conscripti improvising weapons from their own furniture to kill a sitting magistrate — captures the moral collapse the chapter wants to document.

Plutarch's verdict on the moral responsibility is generous and pointed: "The Gracchi were killed because they could not be defeated in the assembly. That, in any republican imagination, is not the fault of the man whose program won the votes." The moral weight falls on those who refused legal redress to a legal program — not on the Gracchi for proposing it. The brothers were not, in their own minds, revolutionaries. They were trying to fix a particular abuse with a particular legal remedy.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Who was Cornelia, and why does Plutarch place her at the center of both Lives?
  2. What did Tiberius see on his journey through Etruria, and how did it shape his program?
  3. What were the terms of the lex Sempronia agraria, and how did it treat existing holders?
  4. How did Tiberius break Octavius's veto, and why was the move politically momentous?
  5. How was Tiberius killed, and what does the manner of his death reveal about the senatorial class?
  6. Name three reforms in Gaius's broader program and describe his political coalition.
  7. What was the senatus consultum ultimum, and how did Gaius die?

Discussion Questions

  1. The brothers were not revolutionaries; they were enforcers of an existing law. Does that legal framing alter the moral case for or against them? Or is the political effect — escalation to violence — what really matters?
  2. Plutarch puts the moral responsibility for the violence on the senatorial class, not on the Gracchi. Is that the right place to put it? What is the counter-argument?
  3. Cornelia is described as the moral center of both Lives. Try to articulate what Plutarch thinks she contributed to her sons' formation that he would not have ascribed to a father.

Connections to Later Chapters

Section 10.4 (the joint synkrisis) weighs the Gracchi directly against Agis and Cleomenes on seven axes and ranks Tiberius the most virtuous of the four, with Gaius "far short of Cleomenes" in action and boldness. The Gracchan moment also opens forward into the broader Roman political collapse Plutarch will trace through the Lives of Marius, Sulla, Pompey, Crassus, and Caesar — the hundred-year civil violence that began with senators tearing the legs off their own benches.
