Study Guide: The Life of Solon

Solon stands at the threshold of Athenian political history. He was born around 638 BC into a family of moderate wealth and good lineage — descended, by his own claim, from Codrus, the last king of Athens. Because his father had ruined the family estate through generous giving, Solon went into the merchant trade, which exposed him to many foreign customs and which would later let him retreat from Athens when his work was done. Plutarch is interested in him as a poet first and a politician second: most of what we know about Solon survives through his own verses, which Plutarch quotes generously. Already counted as one of the Seven Sages of archaic Greece, Solon entered political life with a reputation that the office of archon did not create but only confirmed.

Key Overview

Five episodes structure the Life. The Salamis stratagem: Athens had outlawed any further attempt to take the island; Solon feigned madness, wore a cap that exempted him from the law, recited an inflammatory hundred-line poem in the marketplace, and so persuaded the city to repeal the law and reopen the campaign. The election to the archonship of 594 BC: a debt crisis had reduced many Athenians to bondage; both sides agreed that Solon, who had taken neither, was the only acceptable arbiter. The refusal of tyranny: offered absolute power openly, Solon declined, calling the tyrant's chair "a very fair spot, but it has no way down from it." The seisachtheia: a one-time cancellation of debts and a permanent abolition of debt-bondage, complicated by a scandal in which three of Solon's confidants exploited inside knowledge to enrich themselves before the law was announced. The departure and return: after his laws were complete, Solon asked leave to travel for ten years; he returned to find his cousin Pisistratus seizing the citadel.

The arc of the Life is moderate reform — adjustment of an existing society, refusing both the radical surgery of Lycurgus and the absolute power that would have made such surgery possible.

Major Figures and Terms

Solon — Athenian poet, merchant, archon in 594 BC; one of the Seven Sages.

Codrus — Solon's claimed ancestor, the last king of Athens.

Conon, Clinias, Hipponicus — three of Solon's friends who exploited inside knowledge of the seisachtheia to enrich themselves.

Pisistratus — Solon's cousin, who seized the citadel as tyrant in 561 BC after Solon's return.

Croesus — Lydian king to whom Solon delivered the maxim that no one's life can be called happy until it has ended.

seisachtheia — "the shaking-off-of-burdens"; debt cancellation and abolition of debt-bondage.

Pentacosiomedimni, Hippeis, Zeugitae, Thetes — the four property classes graded by annual yield.

Areopagus — council of former archons; under Solon, supreme guardian of the laws.

Important Passages

The opening of the debt-crisis section in Plutarch describes Athens as being "in a truly dangerous condition, and no other means for freeing it from disturbances and settling it, to be possible but a despotic power." This is the precise word Solon refused.

The seisachtheia is summed up in Solon's own line, which Plutarch quotes: "The mortgage-stones that covered her, by me / Removed,—the land that was a slave is free."

Solon's defense of his refusal of tyranny, also in his own verse: "That I spared my land, / And withheld from usurpation and from violence my hand, / And forbore to fix a stain and a disgrace on my good name, / I regret not; I believe that it will be my chiefest fame."

His verdict on his own legislation — that he had given Athens not the best possible laws but "the best they could receive" — is the formula that has defined moderate reform ever since.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. How did Solon use feigned madness to circumvent the Salamis law, and what does the episode reveal about his political style?
  2. Explain the seisachtheia. What did it cancel, what did it preserve, and why did it disappoint both rich and poor?
  3. How did the scandal involving Conon, Clinias, and Hipponicus nearly destroy Solon's standing, and how did he clear his name?
  4. Describe the four property classes and explain why the rights of the thetes mattered for later Athenian democracy.
  5. Why did Solon depart for ten years, and what did he find on his return?

Discussion Questions

  1. Solon's verdict that he gave Athens "the best they could receive" has been read as both modest realism and as a kind of confession of failure. Which reading is fairer to the Life?
  2. The disenfranchisement law against neutral citizens is among the strangest in archaic Greek legislation. Is Solon right that political crisis suspends the ordinary distinction between participating and abstaining?
  3. Plutarch is sympathetic to Solon and yet shows him outmaneuvered by Pisistratus at the end. What does the ending do to the moral reading of the Life?

Connections to Later Chapters

The popular courts that Pericles will open in Chapter 6 are built on the foundation Solon laid when he gave the thetes the right to sit as jurors. The Solonian vocabulary — property classes, council, Areopagus — remained the formal scaffolding of Athenian government for two centuries. The synkrisis in Section 4 will turn Solon's own saying about Croesus back on Solon himself.
