Study Guide: Plutarch and the Christian Moral Tradition

Plutarch was a pagan. He died around 120 AD; he was a Greek priest of Apollo at Delphi; he almost certainly never read a Christian text and possibly never met a Christian. And yet the Christian moral tradition is, in important ways, unthinkable without him. This section traces the line from Eusebius to Metaxas — two millennia of Christian readers absorbing Plutarchan method into their own tradition.

Key Overview

The reception begins with Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 263-339), the first great Christian historian and author of the Life of Constantine, the first sustained Christian biography of an emperor. Eusebius had read Plutarch carefully and consciously imitates his form: moral focus on character rather than event, attention to revealing small acts, honest weighing of virtues and vices. His most consequential concept — the praeparatio evangelica, "preparation for the Gospel" — gave Christians the framework for reading pagan moralists as partial witnesses whose truths Christian revelation would complete.

In the early medieval Latin West, direct access to Plutarch was lost because Greek was rarely read between roughly 600 and 1300 AD. But the Plutarchan habit was preserved indirectly: through Cicero (who absorbed Greek philosophical biography), through Boethius (whose Consolation echoes Plutarchan moralism), and through the lives of the saints, which adapted ancient biographical form to Christian subject matter.

The Renaissance recovery returned the full Lives to the West. Petrarch read what Plutarch he could find; by the late fifteenth century the Lives were in Greek manuscripts, printed by 1517, translated into Latin and the European vernaculars by mid-sixteenth century. Erasmus folded Plutarch into the Christian humanist curriculum. Montaigne made Plutarch (with Seneca) one of the two pillars of his essays. Sir Thomas North's 1579 introduction explicitly framed Plutarch as suitable Christian reading.

Modern Christian moral biography continues the same method: Newman's Lives of the English Saints (1844-45), Boswell's Life of Samuel Johnson (1791) — the closest modern English equivalent of a Plutarchan biography — and Metaxas's Bonhoeffer (2010).

Major Figures and Terms

  • Eusebius of Caesarea: first great Christian historian; author of the Life of Constantine and the Praeparatio Evangelica.
  • Praeparatio evangelica: "preparation for the Gospel" — the framework letting Christians read pagan moralists as partial witnesses.
  • Boethius: author of the Consolation of Philosophy, a medieval indirect channel for Plutarchan moralism.
  • Petrarch: launched the Renaissance recovery of ancient texts.
  • Erasmus (1466-1536): Dutch humanist who integrated Plutarch into Christian humanism.
  • Montaigne (1533-1592): made Plutarch one of his two pillars (with Seneca).
  • John Henry Newman: Anglican-turned-Catholic cardinal; wrote Lives of the English Saints (1844-45).
  • James Boswell: wrote the Life of Samuel Johnson (1791), the closest modern Plutarchan biography in English.
  • Eric Metaxas: wrote Bonhoeffer (2010), a modern Plutarchan moral biography.

Important Passages

  • "And yet the Christian moral tradition is, in important ways, unthinkable without him." — the section's thesis.
  • Eusebius's praeparatio evangelica: pagan philosophers were "preparing the ancient world for the fuller revelation that came in Christ."
  • North's introduction: "He maketh men virtuous, who would not else have been virtuous... he is so excellent a craftsman of life and manners that, taking pleasure in nothing more, ye need ask for no more."
  • "The line from Eusebius to Augustine to Erasmus to North to Newman to Metaxas is continuous."
  • "The kind of close moral attention Plutarch practiced is what the soul wants."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. Identify Plutarch's actual religion and the date of his death.
  2. Explain Eusebius's concept of the praeparatio evangelica and why it mattered for Christian reception of pagan authors.
  3. List the three medieval indirect channels that preserved Plutarchan method when Greek was unread in the Latin West.
  4. Why is Boswell's Life of Samuel Johnson called "the closest modern English equivalent of a Plutarchan biography"?
  5. State the chapter's final claim about why Christians have read Plutarch for two thousand years.

Discussion Questions

  1. Could a non-Christian reader accept the praeparatio evangelica framework, treating Plutarch as a "preparatory voice" without committing to a specific theology? What changes if you remove the theological frame?
  2. The saints' lives "share Plutarch's central intuition: that a holy person's character can be discerned through close attention to revealing acts." How does this differ from modern biography, which often emphasizes psychological development?
  3. Montaigne pairs Plutarch with Seneca; Erasmus folds Plutarch into Christian humanism; Newman applies the method to English saints. What do these three uses have in common, and what do they reveal about the flexibility of Plutarchan method?
  4. The chapter's final claim is that close moral attention is "what the soul wants." Try the experiment: think of someone whose life you have observed carefully over years. Does the act of careful observation change how you think of them?
