Study Guide: The Life of Numa Pompilius

Plutarch's Life of Numa is one of the gentlest biographies in the Lives. Numa is the king who never struck a man, never fought a battle, and yet transformed the city Romulus had made — by religious reform, daily ritual, and the patient softening of a warrior people. Read alongside Livy's parallel account, the Life provides the Roman side of Plutarch's lawgiver-pairing.

Key Overview

  1. The election. After Romulus disappeared, Romulus's Romans and Tatius's Sabines edged toward civil war. The senators ruled by rotation (the interregnum) while searching for a king acceptable to both. Numa Pompilius — a Sabine of Cures, age forty, famed for austere life and incorruptible justice — was their choice.

  2. The refusal. Numa initially refused. His refusal speech is a small masterpiece of self-knowledge: a city in need of a war-captain would only laugh at a king teaching worship and justice. The ambassadors and his own father insisted that public office was itself a religious service. He relented.

  3. The dismissal of the Celeres. Numa's first act was to dismiss Romulus's three-hundred-man bodyguard, saying he would not distrust those who had trusted him with the throne. The gesture set the tone for a reign of trust rather than force.

  4. Religion as politics. Numa's insight was that the early Romans were "hard and iron," shaped by perpetual war, and mere statute would not bend them. Religion would. He restructured worship, founded priesthoods, set the calendar, and built shrines, giving every day a sacred shape. Plutarch aligns Numa's religion with Pythagoras: aniconic (no images for 170 years), bloodless (flour, wine, the least costly offerings).

  5. The institutions. Numa founded the Pontifex Maximus, the Vestal Virgins, the Salii, and the Fecials; reformed the calendar from ten to twelve months; distinguished fas days from nefas; and kept the gates of the temple of Janus shut for his entire forty-three-year reign.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Numa Pompilius — Second king of Rome, a Sabine of Cures, traditional dates 715–673 BC.
  • Celeres — Romulus's three-hundred-man bodyguard, dismissed by Numa.
  • Egeria — The nymph or goddess Numa claimed to meet nightly in a sacred grove.
  • Pontifex Maximus — Chief priest, founded by Numa as head of the state religion.
  • Vestal Virgins — Six priestesses of Vesta, serving thirty years under a vow of chastity.
  • Salii — Twelve priests of Mars who danced with the ancilia in March.
  • Fecials — Priestly heralds with sole authority to declare war.
  • Fas / nefas — Days on which legal business was sacredly permitted or forbidden.
  • Temple of Janus — Doors open in war, shut in peace; shut for all forty-three years of Numa's reign.

Important Passages

  • "Every alteration of a man's life is dangerous to him; but madness only could induce one who needs nothing and is satisfied with everything to quit a life he is accustomed to." — Numa's refusal speech.
  • "I should but be, methinks, a laughing-stock . . . to a city whose needs are rather for a captain than for a king."
  • "Plato's expression of a city in high fever was never more applicable than to Rome at that time." — Plutarch on the Romans Numa inherited.
  • "Numa forbade the Romans to represent God in the form of man or beast." — The Pythagorean affinity.
  • Livy (Second Witness): Numa "pretends" to nightly meetings with Egeria so his innovations might gain credibility "without some fiction of a miracle."

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. From what city and people did Numa come, and how old was he at his election?
  2. What was the interregnum, and what political problem did it solve?
  3. Why did Numa initially refuse the kingdom, and what changed his mind?
  4. What was Numa's first political act as king, and what tone did it set?
  5. What did Numa understand about the early Roman temperament that determined his policy of religious reform?
  6. In what two ways did Plutarch align Numa's religion with the doctrine of Pythagoras?
  7. What was the service structure of the Vestal Virgins?
  8. What were the Fecials, and what religious procedure did they enforce before Rome could go to war?
  9. How long did the gates of the temple of Janus remain shut during Numa's reign?
  10. What happened to Numa's sacred books after his tomb was discovered in 181 BC?

Discussion Questions

  • Numa's strategy was to govern men by religion because law alone would not bind them. Is this an honest acknowledgment of human nature, cynical manipulation, or both?
  • Plutarch and Livy disagree about Numa's claims regarding Egeria. The Roman is more skeptical than the Greek. Why?
  • Numa's reign produced two generations of peace, yet the peace died with him. Was there any way he could have made it durable?

Connections to Later Chapters

The Life of Numa is the Roman half of the chapter's central pairing. The contrast with Lycurgus — coercion vs. persuasion, war vs. peace, education vs. religion — is argued explicitly in the synkrisis (section 3.4). Plutarch's interest in Numa as a philosopher-king prefigures Solon (Chapter 4). The aniconic, bloodless religion will reappear as a foil for the more ornate cults of Rome's expanding empire.
