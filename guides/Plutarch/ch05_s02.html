Study Guide: The Life of Themistocles

This study guide accompanies Section 5.2 of Plutarch: Lives in Parallel — the biography of Themistocles (c. 524 - c. 459 BC), the Athenian general whose strategic foresight, ruthlessness, and political theater saved Greece from Persia at Salamis, and whom Athens then drove into exile. Plutarch's Themistocles is brilliant, ambitious, ruthless, and far-sighted — the most Greek of the Greek lives in the book.

Key Overview

Obscure birth, vehement character. Themistocles was born in Athens around 524 BC to a respectable but undistinguished father and a mother some sources called foreign. Plutarch concedes the birth was "somewhat too obscure to do him honor" and immediately turns to the boy's personality. From childhood Themistocles spent his free hours composing speeches accusing or defending his classmates — what his schoolmaster diagnosed as the early signs of a man who would "be nothing small, but great one way or other, for good or else for bad." Plutarch endorses the diagnosis. Themistocles paid no attention to anything that would refine him in private virtue; his intelligence was practical, political, and aimed at the management of cities.

Marathon and the trophy. In 490 BC the young Themistocles watched Miltiades win the battle of Marathon against the Persian force sent by Darius. Most Athenians thought the war was over. Themistocles thought it the beginning of "far greater conflicts" — and was haunted, as Plutarch tells it, by Miltiades's victory monument. "The trophy of Miltiades would not let him sleep" is one of the most-quoted lines in the Lives. The image captures Themistocles exactly: a man whose ambition was triggered by the greatness of others.

The fleet decision. Athens then discovered a rich vein of silver at Laurium. The customary distribution would have made every citizen modestly richer for a year. Themistocles persuaded the assembly to divert the revenue into one hundred ships for a war against Aegina — a nearer-term rival than Persia, and therefore a politically usable pretext. Those one hundred ships became the fleet that won Salamis. Plato (as Plutarch quotes) complained that Themistocles had taken from the Athenians "the spear and the shield" and bound them "to the bench and the oar." The criticism was true and the strategy was decisive.

Evacuation and Salamis. When Xerxes marched on Greece in 480 BC, Athens could not be defended. Themistocles persuaded the city to evacuate — committing it to Minerva, sending women, children, and slaves to Troezen and Salamis. The ambiguous "walls of wood" oracle, which others read as a wooden palisade, he interpreted as the fleet. The interpretation provided religious sanction for abandoning the city. At Salamis his plan was to fight in narrow water where Persian numbers could not be deployed. When other Greek commanders wanted to retreat, he sent his personal slave Sicinnus to Xerxes with a false message: the Greeks were breaking. Xerxes sealed the strait. The Greek fleet had no choice but to fight, and won.

Exile and Persia. After Salamis, Themistocles rebuilt the Athenian walls against Spartan objections, fortifying Piraeus and anchoring Athens's future on the sea. He grew rich and overbearing. The Athenians ostracized him in the late 470s. He went to Argos, then, when medism accusations followed, fled to Persia, where Artaxerxes — son of the very Xerxes he had defeated — made him governor of three small Greek-speaking cities in Asia Minor. The dramatic tradition is that, ordered to lead Persian forces against Greece, he killed himself by drinking bull's blood. Plutarch tells the story seriously and notes its uncertainty.

Major Figures and Terms

  • Themistocles — Athenian general, c. 524 - c. 459 BC; architect of the navy, victor of Salamis, ostracized, died in Persia.
  • Miltiades — Athenian general at Marathon (490 BC); his victory monument haunted the young Themistocles.
  • Anaxagoras-style philosophical refinement — The text contrasts Themistocles's practical intelligence with the philosophical schooling he ignored.
  • Sicinnus — Themistocles's personal slave, sent with the false message to Xerxes that sealed the trap at Salamis.
  • Xerxes — Persian Great King; invaded Greece in 480 BC; watched Salamis from a throne on a hill and retreated overland after the defeat.
  • Artaxerxes — Xerxes's son; welcomed the exiled Themistocles and made him governor of three Greek-speaking cities in Asia Minor.
  • Laurium silver mines — Source of the revenue Themistocles diverted into the navy.
  • Piraeus — The new port Themistocles fortified, anchoring Athens's maritime future.
  • Ostracism — Athenian procedure that voted Themistocles into ten-year exile.

Important Passages

  • You, my boy, will be nothing small, but great one way or other, for good or else for bad. — The schoolmaster's diagnosis Plutarch endorses.
  • The trophy of Miltiades would not let him sleep. — Themistocles's reaction to Marathon, the line that captures his ambition.
  • He took away from the Athenians the spear and the shield, and bound them to the bench and the oar. — Plato (as Plutarch quotes) on the strategic shift Themistocles forced.
  • Set his machines to work, as in a theater, and employed prodigies and oracles. — Plutarch on the "walls of wood" interpretation, openly aware that his hero was doing political theater.
  • Many old men ... were left behind; and even the tame domestic animals could not be seen without some pity. — The evacuation scene, including the dog of Xanthippus.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What does Plutarch make of Themistocles's obscure birth?
  2. What did the schoolmaster predict, and how does Plutarch use the line?
  3. What was the haunting of "the trophy of Miltiades"?
  4. How did Themistocles get a hundred ships built out of the Laurium silver?
  5. How did he interpret the "walls of wood" oracle, and why politically?
  6. What did Sicinnus do at Salamis, and why was the false message decisive?
  7. What were Themistocles's main postwar actions in Athens, and how did they contribute to his ostracism?
  8. Where did he end his life, under whom, and by what death-traditions?

Discussion Questions

  1. Plutarch admires both Themistocles's strategy and his theatrical methods. Can the methods be defended on their own terms, or only by the success of the outcome?
  2. Themistocles's interpretation of the "walls of wood" oracle is described as politically decisive. Is religious cover for strategic decisions cynical, or does it serve a legitimate civic function?
  3. The Sicinnus episode is a deception practiced on the enemy. Is the moral status of military deception different from that of political deception in one's own city?
  4. The dog of Xanthippus is a tiny moment in a vast narrative. What does Plutarch achieve by including it?
  5. Themistocles ends his career as a Persian governor. Is his career best read as a tragedy of civic ingratitude, or as a strategist's final pivot to survival?
