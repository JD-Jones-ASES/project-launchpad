Study Guide: Background — Reform Politics in Sparta and Rome

Chapter 10 is Plutarch's only "double pair" — four Lives joined by a single closing synkrisis. Two Spartan kings, Agis IV and Cleomenes III, attempted in the third century BC to restore the Lycurgan order to a Sparta that had collapsed into oligarchy. Two Roman tribunes, Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus, attempted a parallel program at Rome a hundred years later. All four men were killed. Section 10.1 sets out the political background to both projects and tells the reader what to watch for in the four Lives that follow.

Key Overview

  • One project, four lives, two cultures. Across two generations and two cultures, Agis, Cleomenes, and the two Gracchi attempted the same essential political project: redistributing land and political power from a small wealthy class to a larger exploited one. All four were killed; all four programs were either reversed or imposed by violence; all four cities were significantly damaged in the process.
  • The Spartan collapse. By Agis's day the original 9,000 equal Lycurgan plots had collapsed into the hands of perhaps a hundred wealthy families. The citizen body had shrunk to a few hundred men, and most "Spartans" lacked the land qualification to attend the common mess.
  • The Roman crisis. Roman public land (the ager publicus), originally conquered and held in theory by the state, had been occupied by wealthy private holders who paid little rent and treated it as their own. Small farmers, displaced by these estates and by long professional wars, drifted to Rome as a landless urban populace.
  • Restorers vs. enforcers. Agis and Cleomenes thought of themselves not as innovators but as restorers — recalling Sparta to the constitution of Lycurgus. The Gracchi had no foundational constitution to restore; they were enforcers of a long-ignored statute that limited private occupation of public land.
  • A shared moral question. Plutarch's chapter asks: what happens when reformers attempt, by legal means, to redistribute resources captured by a small wealthy class over generations? Can such a program be carried through without violence? What kind of person becomes its public face, and what does the political system do to them?

Major Figures and Terms

  • Agis IV (c. 262–241 BC): Spartan king who proposed full cancellation of debts, land redistribution, and restoration of the agoge. Killed by ephoric faction before the program was finished.
  • Cleomenes III (c. 260–219 BC): Successor king who carried Agis's program through by a military coup. Defeated by Macedon at Sellasia in 222 BC; died an exile in Egypt.
  • Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus (133 BC): Roman tribune whose lex Sempronia agraria sought to enforce the existing legal limit on private occupation of public land. Beaten to death on the Capitol.
  • Gaius Sempronius Gracchus (123, 122 BC): Younger brother. Tribune twice with a broader program — grain dole, equestrian juries, Italian citizenship, Carthage colony. Killed in 121 BC.
  • Lycurgan constitution: The original Spartan order described in Chapter 3 — equal land plots, iron money, common tables, the agoge. The reference point for the Spartan reformers.
  • Ager publicus: Roman public land, conquered land legally belonging to the Roman state, in practice occupied by aristocratic holders. The legal lever the Gracchi tried to use.
  • Synkrisis: The brief comparative essay Plutarch writes at the end of a pairing. Here, uniquely, it judges four men in one essay.

Important Passages

The chapter's framing claim is plain: "Plutarch's only 'double pair' — four Lives joined by a single closing comparison — treats two Spartan kings and two Roman tribunes who attempted, two generations apart and two cultures apart, the same essential political project: to redistribute land and political power from a small wealthy class to a larger exploited one. All four were killed." This sentence sets the agenda for the whole chapter. The four Lives are not really independent biographies but pieces of a single comparative argument.

The other key claim is about the consequences of refusal. The killings of the Gracchi, the chapter notes, were "the first political killings in Rome since the early Republic. They opened a hundred years of escalating civil violence that ended only with the Augustan settlement." Sparta, similarly, never recovered from the failure of Cleomenes's reform. The chapter's quiet implication is that the political class's refusal of legal redress to a legal program produced the very violence the refusers wished to prevent.

Short-Answer Practice Questions

  1. What makes Chapter 10 unique among the Parallel Lives?
  2. What single political project links the four reformers?
  3. How had Sparta changed from the Lycurgan plan by Agis's day?
  4. What was the legal status of the Roman ager publicus that the Gracchi sought to redistribute?
  5. Why does the chapter call the Spartans "restorers" and the Gracchi "enforcers"?
  6. What was Agis's rhetorically decisive opening gesture?
  7. What political consequence does the chapter assign to the killings of the Gracchi?

Discussion Questions

  1. The chapter says Plutarch "does not condemn the projects" even though every project failed and every reformer was killed. What does Plutarch's sympathy tell you about how he understands political success?
  2. Compare the legal framing of the two reform projects. Is "restoring a foundational order" rhetorically stronger or weaker than "enforcing an existing statute"? Why?
  3. Imagine the senatorial class of either Sparta or Rome reading this chapter. Would the warning the chapter contains for them be intelligible? Why or why not?

Connections to Later Chapters

Section 10.2 takes up Agis and Cleomenes. Section 10.3 covers the two Gracchi. Section 10.4 contains Plutarch's joint synkrisis — the only one of its kind — which weighs all four men against one another on seven axes and closes with an unusually explicit four-way ranking. The Spartan story also leans backward into Chapter 3 (Lycurgus), since the reformers are continuators of that order. The Roman story leans forward into the political collapse Plutarch will track in Lives like Marius, Sulla, Pompey, and Caesar.
